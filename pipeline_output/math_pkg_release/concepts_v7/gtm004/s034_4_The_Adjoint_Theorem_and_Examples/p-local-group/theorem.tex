Let $P$ be a (possibly empty) family of primes. An integer $n$ is called a \text{$P$-number} if all prime factors of $n$ lie in $P$, and a \text{$P'$-number} if they lie outside $P$. A group $N$ is called \text{$P$-local} if the function $x \mapsto x^q$, $x \in N$, is bijective for all $P'$-numbers $q$. For abelian groups, $A$ is $P$-local if and only if it is uniquely divisible by $P'$-numbers, equivalently, $A$ is a $\mathbb{Z}_P$-module where $\mathbb{Z}_P$ is the ring of integers localized at $P$.
