A \textbf{classical solution} of the heat equation problem is a function $u(x, t)$, continuous for $x \in [0, \pi]$ and $t \geq 0$, satisfying

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial t} u(x, t) = \left( \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \right)^2 u(x, t), \quad x \in (0, \pi), \quad t > 0,$$

$$u(x, 0) = g(x), \quad \text{where } g \in C[0, \pi] \text{ is given},$$

and one of the following two sets of boundary conditions:

$$\text{(3)} \quad u(0, t) = u(\pi, t) = 0, \quad t \geq 0,$$

or

$$\text{(3)'} \quad \frac{\partial}{\partial x} u(0, t) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x} u(\pi, t) = 0, \quad t > 0.$$

This is in contrast to the \textit{distribution solution} for the periodic problem given by Theorem 3.1.
