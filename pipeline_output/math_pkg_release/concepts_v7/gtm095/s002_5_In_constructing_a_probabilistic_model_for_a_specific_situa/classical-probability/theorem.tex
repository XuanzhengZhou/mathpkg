In the \text{classical method of assigning probabilities}, all outcomes $\omega_1, \ldots, \omega_N$ of a finite sample space $\Omega$ are considered equally probable. For any event $A \in \mathcal{A}$,
$$\text{P}(A) = \frac{N(A)}{N(\Omega)},$$
where $N(A)$ denotes the number of elementary outcomes belonging to $A$. In this case, the computation of probabilities reduces to counting, and combinatorial methods play a fundamental role.
