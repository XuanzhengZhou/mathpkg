For $s > 1$, the equation

$$\zeta_k(s) = \prod_p \frac{1}{1 - \frac{1}{N(p)^s}}.$$

holds where $p$ runs through all distinct prime ideals $p$ of $k$.

To begin with, this product converges, since $\sum_p 1/N(p)^s$ converges as the constituent of the series for $\zeta_k(s)$. For a single factor we obtain a convergent series of positive terms

$$\frac{1}{1 - N(p)^s} = 1 + \frac{1}{N(p)^s} + \frac{1}{N(p^2)^s} + \cdots.$$

If we multiply these expressions in a purely formal way for all $p$, then we obtain a series with terms

$$\frac{1}{N(p_1^{\alpha_1} p_2^{\alpha_2} \cdots p_r^{\alpha_r})^s},$$

where each product of powers of prime ideals appears exactly once in the norm symbol. However, by the fundamental theorem we obtain each integral ideal of $k$ exactly once in this form, that is, all terms of the convergent series $\zeta_k(s)$ appear exactly once.
