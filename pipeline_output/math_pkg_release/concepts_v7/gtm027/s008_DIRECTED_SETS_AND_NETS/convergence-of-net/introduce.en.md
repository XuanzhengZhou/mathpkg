A net converges to a point if it is eventually in every neighborhood of that point. This definition directly generalizes the familiar notion of sequence convergence to arbitrary directed index sets. The convergence of nets depends on three ingredients: the function defining the net, the topology of the space, and the directing order. In a discrete space convergence is trivial (nets must be eventually constant), while in an indiscrete space every net converges to every point, illustrating that limits need not be unique in general topological spaces.
