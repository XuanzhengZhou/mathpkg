If $\mathfrak{J}$ and $\mathfrak{U}$ are topologies for $X$, then, following the convention for arbitrary families of sets, $\mathfrak{J}$ is \textbf{smaller} than $\mathfrak{U}$ and $\mathfrak{U}$ is \textbf{larger} than $\mathfrak{J}$ iff $\mathfrak{J} \subset \mathfrak{U}$. In other words, $\mathfrak{J}$ is smaller than $\mathfrak{U}$ iff each $\mathfrak{J}$-open set is $\mathfrak{U}$-open. In this case it is also said that $\mathfrak{J}$ is \textbf{coarser} than $\mathfrak{U}$ and $\mathfrak{U}$ is \textbf{finer} than $\mathfrak{J}$.

The discrete topology and the indiscrete topology are respectively the largest and the smallest topology for $X$. That is, every topology for $X$ is contained in the discrete topology and contains the indiscrete topology. If $\mathfrak{J}$ and $\mathfrak{U}$ are arbitrary topologies for $X$ it may happen that $\mathfrak{J}$ is neither larger nor smaller than $\mathfrak{U}$; in this case, following the usage for partial orderings, it is said that $\mathfrak{J}$ and $\mathfrak{U}$ are \textbf{not comparable}.
