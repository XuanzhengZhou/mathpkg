Let $\mathfrak{J}$ and $\mathfrak{U}$ be topologies for a set $X$. Then $\mathfrak{J}$ is \textbf{smaller} (or \textbf{coarser}) than $\mathfrak{U}$, and $\mathfrak{U}$ is \textbf{larger} (or \textbf{finer}) than $\mathfrak{J}$, iff $\mathfrak{J} \subset \mathfrak{U}$. Equivalently, $\mathfrak{J}$ is coarser than $\mathfrak{U}$ iff every $\mathfrak{J}$-open set is $\mathfrak{U}$-open. If neither $\mathfrak{J} \subset \mathfrak{U}$ nor $\mathfrak{U} \subset \mathfrak{J}$, the topologies are \textbf{not comparable}. The discrete topology is the largest topology for $X$, and the indiscrete topology is the smallest.
