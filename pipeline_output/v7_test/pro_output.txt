extracted/gtm-0073/gtm-0073_ch01_concepts/group-characterization-proposition/theorem.tex
```latex
\begin{proposition}
Let $G$ be a semigroup. Then $G$ is a group if and only if the following conditions hold:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] there exists an element $e \in G$ such that $ea = a$ for all $a \in G$ (left identity element);
\item[(ii)] for each $a \in G$, there exists an element $a^{-1} \in G$ such that $a^{-1} a = e$ (left inverse).
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}
```

Proposition 1.3 provides a useful characterization of groups in terms of a left identity and left inverses, which are weaker than the full group axioms. It shows that the existence of a left identity and left inverses is sufficient to guarantee a two-sided identity and two-sided inverses, making the structure a group. This proposition is often used to verify that a set with an associative binary operation is a group by checking only these weaker conditions.

**Proof of Proposition 1.3.** ($\Rightarrow$) Trivial. ($\Leftarrow$) Note that Theorem 1.2(i) is true under these hypotheses. $G \neq \emptyset$ since $e \in G$. If $a \in G$, then by (ii) $(aa^{-1})(aa^{-1}) = a(a^{-1}a)a^{-1} = a(ea^{-1}) = aa^{-1}$ and hence $aa^{-1} = e$ by Theorem 1.2(i). Thus $a^{-1}$ is a two-sided inverse of $a$. Since $ae = a(a^{-1}a) = (aa^{-1})a = ea = a$ for every $a \in G$, $e$ is a two-sided identity. Therefore $G$ is a group by Definition 1.1.
