4. Affine Planes

If $\mathcal{P}$ is a projective plane and $l$ is any line of $\mathcal{P}$ then let $\mathcal{P}^l$ be the set of points and lines of $\mathcal{P}$ obtained by deleting the line $l$ and all the points incident with it. $\mathcal{P}$ has a natural incidence, namely the incidence of $\mathcal{P}$. Clearly any two points of $\mathcal{P}^l$ are incident with a unique line of $\mathcal{P}^l$. This is readily seen as follows; let $X$ be any point of $l$ and let $m_1, m_2$ be any pair of distinct lines through $X$ such that neither is $l$. Then $m_1, m_2$ are lines of $\mathcal{P}^l$ and $m_1$ and $m_2$ do not intersect in $\mathcal{P}^l$. But clearly if $A$ is any point of $\mathcal{P}^l$ not on $m_1$, and if $m$ is the line joining $A$ to $X$ in $\mathcal{P}$, then $m$ is the only line of $\mathcal{P}^l$ through $A$ with the property that it does not intersect $m_1$.
