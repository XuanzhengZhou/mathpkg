A primary ideal generalizes the notion of a prime ideal: instead of requiring that one factor lies in the ideal, it only requires that some power of the second factor does. Every prime ideal is primary. The key theorem is that the radical of a primary ideal is always a prime ideal. In the ring \(\mathbb{Z}\), the primary ideals are precisely \((0)\) and \((p^n)\) for prime \(p\) and \(n \geq 1\). This provides the bridge from factorization of elements to primary decomposition of ideals.
