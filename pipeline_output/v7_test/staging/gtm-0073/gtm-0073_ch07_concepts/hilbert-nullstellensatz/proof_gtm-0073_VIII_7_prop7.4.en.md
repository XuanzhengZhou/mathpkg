If \(f \in \operatorname{Rad} I\), then \(f^m \in I\) for some \(m\). At any zero \((a_1, \ldots, a_n)\) of \(I\), \(0 = f^m(a_1, \ldots, a_n) = (f(a_1, \ldots, a_n))^m\), so \(f(a_1, \ldots, a_n) = 0\) since \(F\) is a field. Hence \(\operatorname{Rad} I \subset JV(I)\).

For the reverse inclusion, suppose \(f \in JV(I)\). Consider the ring \(K[x_1, \ldots, x_n, y]\) and the ideal \(I'\) generated by \(I\) and \(1 - yf\). The Rabinowitsch trick shows \(V(I') = \emptyset\) in \(F^{n+1}\). By Lemma 7.3, \(I'\) cannot be a proper ideal, so \(1 \in I'\). Clearing denominators yields a relation \(1 = \sum g_i(x)(1 - yf) + \sum h_j i_j\), and substituting \(y = 1/f\) and multiplying by a suitable power of \(f\) gives \(f^m \in I\) for some \(m \geq 1\).
