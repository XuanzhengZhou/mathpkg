Given a chain \(B_1 \subset B_2 \subset \cdots\) in \(B\), restrict \(f\) and \(g\) to obtain exact sequences \(0 \to A_i \to B_i \to C_i \to 0\). Then \(A_1 \subset A_2 \subset \cdots\) and \(C_1 \subset C_2 \subset \cdots\). By hypothesis, there exists \(n\) such that \(A_i = A_n\) and \(C_i = C_n\) for all \(i \geq n\). For each \(i \geq n\) there is a commutative diagram with exact rows and identity maps \(\alpha, \gamma\) and inclusion \(\beta_i\). The Short Five Lemma implies \(\beta_i\) is the identity, stabilizing the chain in \(B\). The descending case is analogous.
