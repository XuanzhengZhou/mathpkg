A primary decomposition expresses a submodule as a finite intersection of primary submodules, each belonging to a distinct prime ideal. This generalizes the factorization of integers into prime powers to the setting of modules over commutative rings. A reduced (irredundant) decomposition has no superfluous components and all associated primes are distinct. Among these primes, the minimal ones (with respect to inclusion) are isolated; the others are embedded. This mirrors the geometric distinction between irreducible components and embedded components of an algebraic variety.
