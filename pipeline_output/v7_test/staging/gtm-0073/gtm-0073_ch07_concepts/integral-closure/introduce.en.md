The set of all elements of an extension ring that are integral over the base ring is itself a subring, called the integral closure. To verify closure under subtraction and multiplication, one notes that if \(s\) and \(t\) are integral over \(R\), then both lie in \(R[s,t]\), which is finitely generated as an \(R\)-module. The integral closure is the largest integral extension of \(R\) contained in \(S\). An integral domain that equals its own integral closure in its quotient field is called integrally closed — a property fundamental to Dedekind domains and algebraic number theory.
