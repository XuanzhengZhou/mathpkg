Suppose that $(m, n) = 1$. Then if for each $a$ there exist two integers $\alpha$ and $\beta$ in $k(\sqrt{d})$ such that

$$a \equiv N(\alpha) \pmod{m} \quad \text{and} \quad a \equiv N(\beta) \pmod{n},$$

there is also an integer $\gamma$ in $k(\sqrt{d})$ for which

$$a \equiv N(\gamma) \pmod{mn}.$$

To see this we choose positive exponents $b, c$ such that

$$m^b \equiv 1 \pmod{n} \quad \text{and} \quad n^c \equiv 1 \pmod{m}$$

(say $b = \varphi(n)$ and $c = \varphi(m)$). Then

$$\gamma = n^c\alpha + m^b\beta$$

has the asserted properties.

As far as the prime 2 is concerned, we consider the group $\Re(2^a)$ for $a = 2$ or 3.
