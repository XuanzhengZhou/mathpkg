If the odd prime $p$ does not divide the discriminant $d$, then each rational integer relatively prime to $p$ is a norm residue mod $p$ for $k(\sqrt{d})$.

We distinguish two cases in the proof.

1. $p$ splits into two distinct factors $\mathfrak{p}$ and $\mathfrak{p}'$, of degree 1, in $k(\sqrt{d})$. Then there is a number $\pi$ in $k(\sqrt{d})$ which is divisible by $\mathfrak{p}$ but not by $\mathfrak{p}'$, and for each integer $\alpha$

$$N(\pi' \alpha + \pi) \equiv \pi'^2 \alpha \pmod{\mathfrak{p}}.$$

From this it follows that the rational numbers $N(\pi' \alpha + \pi)$ run through a complete system mod $\mathfrak{p}$, hence also mod $p$, if $\alpha$ runs through a complete system of residues mod $\mathfrak{p}$.
