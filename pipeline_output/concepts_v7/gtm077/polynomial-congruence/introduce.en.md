A congruence of integral polynomials modulo $n$ is defined coefficient-wise: two polynomials are congruent if each pair of corresponding coefficients is congruent modulo $n$. This is a stronger condition than the "functional" congruence $f(a) \equiv g(a) \pmod{n}$ for all $a$, as illustrated by the contrast between Fermat's theorem ($x^p \equiv x \pmod{p}$ as functions) and the fact that $x^p$ and $x$ are not congruent as polynomials modulo $p$.
