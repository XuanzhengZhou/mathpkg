If a polynomial $f(x)$, irreducible over $k$, has a common zero $x = \alpha$ with a polynomial $g(x)$ over $k$, then $f(x)$ is a divisor of $g(x)$ and hence all zeros of $f(x)$ are zeros of $g(x)$.

For $(f(x), g(x))$ is at least divisible by $x - \alpha$ and thus not $= 1$. On the other hand $f(x)$ has no factors over $k$ other than $cf(x)$. Consequently $(f(x), g(x)) = cf(x)$ and $f(x)|g(x)$.

In particular an irreducible polynomial over $k$, of degree $n$, has exactly $n$ distinct roots, since otherwise it would have a common zero with the derivative $f'(x)$, which is also a polynomial over $k$ but of degree $n - 1$, and hence it would have to divide $f'(x)$, which cannot be the case.
