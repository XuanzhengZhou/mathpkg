Let $l$ be a prime factor of $1 - \zeta$, which divides $1 - \zeta$ exactly to the $a$th power: $1 - \zeta = l^a l_1$. Suppose that $l$ does not divide $\mu$. Then $l$ splits into $l$ factors which are distinct from one another in $K(\sqrt{l}\mu; k)$ if the congruence

$$\mu = \xi^l (\bmod l^{a+1})$$

(82)

can be solved by a number $\xi$ in $k$. The ideal $l$ remains prime in $K$ if the congruence

$$\mu \equiv \xi^l (\bmod l^{a+1})$$

(83)

can indeed be solved, but (82) cannot be solved. Finally, $l$ becomes the $l$th power of a prime ideal in $K$, if the congruence (83) is also unsolvable.

I: The solvability of (82) is identical with the decomposition of $l$ into distinct factors in $K$. Namely from $l = \mathfrak{L} \cdot s\mathfrak{L} \cdots s^{l-1}\mathfrak{L}$, where the conjugates are distinct from one another, it follows as in the proof of Theorem 110 that every integer in $K$ is congruent to an integer in $k$ modulo every power of $\mathfrak{L}$. Thus to each rational integer $b$ there corresponds $\xi$ in $k$ such that

$$M - \xi \equiv 0 (\mathfrak{L}^b);$$

II: If $\mu = \xi^l(l^{al})$ is solvable, then we see in the same way that $A = \rho(M - \xi)$ is an integer in $K$ whose relative different is relatively prime to l. Consequently, by Theorem 114, l cannot be the $l$th power of a prime ideal in $K$, and hence, if (82) is unsolvable, l does not split into $l$ distinct factors, by l. Thus, by Theorem 117, l must also be a prime ideal in $K$.
