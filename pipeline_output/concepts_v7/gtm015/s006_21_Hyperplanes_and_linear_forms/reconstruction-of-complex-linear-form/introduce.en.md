This theorem provides the converse of Theorem 21.11: starting from any real-linear form $g$, the formula $f(x) = g(x) - i g(ix)$ yields a complex-linear form $f$ whose real part is exactly $g$. Together with 21.11, this establishes a natural bijection between $\mathbb{C}$-forms and $\mathbb{R}$-forms on a complex vector space. This bijection induces a correspondence between $\mathbb{C}$-hyperplanes and $\mathbb{R}$-hyperplanes, which is central to the complex Hahn-Banach theorem.
