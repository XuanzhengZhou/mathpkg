A subset $A$ of a topological space $X$ is said to be \text{rare} (in $X$) if its closure has no interior points, i.e., $\operatorname{int}(\overline{A}) = \varnothing$. {The classical term: ``$A$ is nowhere dense in $X$.''}

A subset $B$ of $X$ is said to be \text{meager} (in $X$) if it is the union of a countable family of rare subsets of $X$, i.e., $B = \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n$ with $A_n$ rare for all $n$. Otherwise, $B$ is said to be a \text{nonmeager} subset of $X$. {The classical term: ``$B$ is of the first category.''}
