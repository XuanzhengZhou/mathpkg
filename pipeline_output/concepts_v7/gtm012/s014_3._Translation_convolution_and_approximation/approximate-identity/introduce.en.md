An approximate identity (also called a summability kernel or Dirac sequence) is a sequence of nonnegative continuous $2\pi$-periodic functions $(\varphi_n)$ whose integrals are normalized to 1 and whose mass concentrates near 0 (and $2\pi$, by periodicity) as $n \to \infty$. Convolving an approximate identity with a function $u \in \mathcal{C}$ produces a weighted average of translates of $u$ that approximates $u$ increasingly well. This is the central tool for proving that smooth functions are dense in the continuous periodic functions, and it generalizes the classical Fejer kernel from Fourier series.
