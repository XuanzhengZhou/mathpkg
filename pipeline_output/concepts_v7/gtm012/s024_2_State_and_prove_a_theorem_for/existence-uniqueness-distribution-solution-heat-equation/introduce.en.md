Theorem 3.1 establishes that the heat equation admits a unique distribution solution for arbitrary periodic distribution initial data $G \in \mathcal{P}'$. The solution is remarkably regular: for every $t > 0$, $F_t$ is actually a smooth ($C^\infty$) function in both space and time. The proof uses Fourier series: uniqueness follows from the fact that the Fourier coefficients must satisfy $a_n(t) = b_n e^{-n^2 t}$, and existence is shown by verifying that these coefficients decay rapidly enough to define a smooth function for $t > 0$, thanks to the exponential factor $e^{-n^2 t}$ dominating any polynomial growth of $b_n$.
