The maximum principle for the heat equation asserts that a classical solution attains its maximum on the parabolic boundary of the domain — that is, either at the initial time $t=0$ or at the spatial endpoints $x=0, x=\pi$. The proof uses an auxiliary function $v(x,t) = u(x,t) - \varepsilon t$ and shows that an interior maximum would contradict the heat equation. This principle yields an a priori bound $|u(x,t)| \leq \sup |g|$, which directly implies uniqueness of classical solutions in Theorem 4.2. It also provides the key estimate needed for the proof of existence via the distribution solution.
