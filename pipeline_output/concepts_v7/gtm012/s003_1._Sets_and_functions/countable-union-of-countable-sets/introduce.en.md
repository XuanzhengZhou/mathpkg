Proposition 1.3 states that both finite and countably infinite unions of finite or countable sets remain finite or countable. The proof uses a diagonal enumeration argument: for each $A_j$, choose a surjection $f_j: \mathbb{Z}_+ \to A_j$, then construct a surjection from $\mathbb{Z}_+$ onto the union via the mapping $f(2^j(2k-1)) = f_j(k)$. This result is fundamental for establishing the countability of many mathematical structures, including the rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$.
