The Heine-Borel-Lebesgue theorem characterizes compact subsets of Euclidean $n$-space as exactly those that are both closed and bounded. The forward direction uses that a compact subset of a Hausdorff space is closed, and that compactness implies covering by finitely many spheres of radius one gives boundedness. The converse is proved by reducing to the one-dimensional case: a closed bounded interval in $\mathbb{R}$ is compact, and a product of such intervals (a cube) is compact by Tychonoff's theorem; any closed bounded subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$ embeds in such a cube.
