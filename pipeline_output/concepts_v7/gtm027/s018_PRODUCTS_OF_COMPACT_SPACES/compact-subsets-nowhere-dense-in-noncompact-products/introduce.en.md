This theorem reveals that compactness fails dramatically in infinite products of non-compact spaces: not only is the product not compact, but every compact subset is topologically negligible (nowhere dense). The proof argues by contradiction: if a compact subset $B$ had an interior point $x$, then $B$ would contain a basic open neighborhood of $x$ depending on only finitely many coordinates. Projecting to the remaining (infinitely many non-compact) coordinates would force those coordinate spaces to contain compact subsets with interior, contradicting their non-compactness. This result starkly contrasts with Tychonoff's theorem for products of compact spaces.
