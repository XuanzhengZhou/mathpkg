A set in a topological space is nowhere dense if its closure has empty interior — intuitively, the set is topologically "thin" in the sense that it does not contain any open set, not even after taking limits of its points. Equivalently, the complement of the closure is dense. Nowhere dense sets form the foundation of Baire category theory, and they appear in Kelley's Theorem 16, which shows that in a product of infinitely many non-compact spaces, every compact subset is nowhere dense.
