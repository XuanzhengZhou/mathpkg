This proposition observes that the extreme topologies on a group -- the discrete topology (every subset is open) and the indiscrete topology (only the empty set and the whole group are open) -- always make the group into a topological group. In both cases the continuity of $(x, y) \mapsto x \cdot y^{-1}$ is trivially satisfied: the discrete topology makes every function continuous, and the indiscrete topology provides only the whole group as a neighborhood. Standard examples include the real numbers under addition and the nonzero real numbers under multiplication, both with the usual Euclidean topology.
