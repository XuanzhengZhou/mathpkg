A topological space is locally compact when every point possesses at least one compact neighborhood. This property generalizes compactness while retaining many of its desirable features: every compact space is locally compact, every discrete space is locally compact, and the property is inherited by closed subspaces. Local compactness, when combined with the Hausdorff separation axiom, yields regularity and forms the foundation for the study of Tychonoff spaces and the one-point compactification.
