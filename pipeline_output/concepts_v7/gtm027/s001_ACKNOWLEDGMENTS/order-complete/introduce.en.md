A set is order-complete (or Dedekind-complete) if every non-empty subset that is bounded above possesses a least upper bound (supremum). This property is the defining characteristic of the real numbers, distinguishing them from the rationals. Theorem 0.9 shows that order-completeness with respect to upper bounds is equivalent to the corresponding statement for lower bounds: every non-empty subset with a lower bound has an infimum. Order-complete chains enjoy special extension properties for isotone functions.
