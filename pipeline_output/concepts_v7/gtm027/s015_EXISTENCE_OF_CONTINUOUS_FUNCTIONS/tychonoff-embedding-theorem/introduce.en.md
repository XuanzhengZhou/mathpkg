The Tychonoff embedding theorem gives a beautiful characterization: a topological space is Tychonoff if and only if it can be embedded as a subspace of a cube (a product of copies of $[0,1]$). The forward direction uses the family of all continuous functions to $[0,1]$ and the embedding lemma; the reverse direction uses the fact that cubes are Tychonoff and the property is hereditary. This theorem justifies the central role of Tychonoff spaces in general topology.
