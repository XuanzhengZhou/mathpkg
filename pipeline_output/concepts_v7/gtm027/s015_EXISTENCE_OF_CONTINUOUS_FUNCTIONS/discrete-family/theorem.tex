A family $\mathcal{A}$ of subsets of a topological space is \textbf{discrete} iff each point of the space has a neighborhood which intersects at most one member of $\mathcal{A}$. A discrete family is locally finite, and if $\mathcal{A}$ is discrete, then the family of closures of members of $\mathcal{A}$ is also discrete.

A family is \textbf{$\sigma$-locally finite} (resp. \textbf{$\sigma$-discrete}) iff it is the union of countably many locally finite (resp. discrete) subfamilies.
