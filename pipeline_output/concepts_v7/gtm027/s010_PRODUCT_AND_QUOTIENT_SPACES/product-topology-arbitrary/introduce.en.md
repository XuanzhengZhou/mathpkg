The product topology for an arbitrary (possibly infinite) Cartesian product generalizes the finite case. Its subbase consists of cylinder sets — those sets where a single coordinate is constrained to lie in an open set of the corresponding coordinate space. A base element is a finite intersection of such cylinders, meaning only finitely many coordinates are restricted. This is why the product topology is sometimes called the topology of pointwise convergence: a net converges precisely when each coordinate converges. Crucial to note is that an arbitrary product of open sets $\bigwedge\{U_a : a \in A\}$ is generally \emph{not} open in the product topology unless all but finitely many $U_a$ equal $X_a$.
