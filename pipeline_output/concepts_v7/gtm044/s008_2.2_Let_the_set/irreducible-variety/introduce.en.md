An irreducible variety is one that cannot be expressed as a proper union of two subvarieties. This is the geometric translation of the algebraic notion of an irreducible ideal, mediated by the inclusion-reversing correspondence between ideals and varieties. In the lattice of varieties $(\mathcal{V}, \subset, \cap, \cup)$, irreducible varieties are precisely the join-irreducible elements. Together with the descending chain condition on varieties (which follows from the a.c.c. on ideals), irreducibility guarantees that every variety admits a unique decomposition as a finite irredundant union of irreducible varieties.
