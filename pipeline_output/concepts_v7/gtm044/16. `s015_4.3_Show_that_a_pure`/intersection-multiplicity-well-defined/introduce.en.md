This theorem establishes the well-definedness of intersection multiplicity for two properly intersecting varieties. By independently perturbing all three objects ($V_1$, $V_2$, and the auxiliary linear space $L$) via small linear transformations, the intersection near $P$ splits into a fixed number of distinct points. The proof is analogous to that of Theorem 6.6 but uses Theorem 6.10 instead of the dimension-theoretic argument of Chapter III, working with the incidence variety in $\mathbb{C}^{3(n+1)^2} \times \mathbb{C}^{n+1}$.
