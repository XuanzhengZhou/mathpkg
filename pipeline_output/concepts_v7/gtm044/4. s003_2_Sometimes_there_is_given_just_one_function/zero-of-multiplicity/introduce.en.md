The multiplicity (or order) of a zero generalizes the notion of counting roots with repetition from polynomial algebra to the setting of analytic functions. If $f$ vanishes at $a$, the order $n$ is the unique nonnegative integer such that $f(X) / (X-a)^n$ is analytic and nonvanishing at $a$. This definition is fundamental for the argument principle, which counts zeros inside a contour weighted by their multiplicities, and for the local study of holomorphic mappings via the Weierstrass preparation theorem.
