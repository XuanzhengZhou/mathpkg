While dehomogenization followed by homogenization recovers the original polynomial ($D \circ H = \mathrm{id}$), the reverse composition $H \circ D$ is not the identity on homogeneous polynomials. A simple counterexample is $q(X_1, X_2) = X_1 X_2$: dehomogenizing at $X_1$ kills the $X_1$ factor, yielding $X_2$, and re-homogenizing gives $X_2$, not $X_1 X_2$. This asymmetry reflects the fact that dehomogenization loses information when the variable being set to $1$ appears as a factor in the homogeneous polynomial.
