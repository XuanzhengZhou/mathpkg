The implicit complex-analytic mapping theorem is the fundamental analytic tool for studying the local structure of complex algebraic varieties. Given $q$ complex-analytic functions vanishing at the origin, if their Jacobian matrix has constant rank $r$ near the origin, then the common zero set is locally the graph of a unique complex-analytic map from an $(n-r)$-dimensional subspace to an $r$-dimensional subspace. This theorem guarantees that near smooth points, complex algebraic varieties are complex manifolds. It is the complex-analytic analogue of the real implicit function theorem, but strengthened by the rigidity of complex-analytic functions (Hartogs' theorem). A proof is given later in Section IV,2.
