Dehomogenization is the reverse operation to homogenization: given a homogeneous polynomial $q$ in $n+1$ variables, setting one variable to $1$ yields a (generally non-homogeneous) polynomial in $n$ variables. Geometrically, this corresponds to taking the affine part of the projective variety defined by $q$, relative to the coordinate hyperplane $X_i = 0$ as the hyperplane at infinity. The operations $H$ and $D$ are not full inverses: while $D_i(H_i(p)) = p$ always holds, $H_i(D_i(q))$ may differ from $q$.
