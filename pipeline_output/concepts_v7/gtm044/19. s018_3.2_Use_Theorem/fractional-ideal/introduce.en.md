A fractional ideal generalizes the notion of an ideal to allow denominators. Given an integral domain $R$ with quotient field $K$, a fractional ideal is an $R$-submodule of $K$ that has a common denominator — every element can be expressed with a fixed denominator $d_0 \in R$. Ordinary ideals of $R$ are precisely the fractional ideals contained in $R$. For example, in $\mathbb{Q}$, the set of all multiples of $a/b$ by elements of $\mathbb{Z}$ is a fractional ideal of $\mathbb{Z}$. Fractional ideals form a group under multiplication when the ring is a Dedekind domain, providing the algebraic counterpart to the divisor group of a curve.
