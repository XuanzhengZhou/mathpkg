This definition completes the analogy between meromorphic functions and differential forms on an algebraic curve. Just as a nonzero function $f$ defines a principal divisor $\operatorname{div}(f)$ recording its zeros and poles with multiplicities, a differential $\omega$ defines a canonical divisor $\operatorname{div}(\omega)$. Theorem 6.18 shows that on a nonsingular plane curve of degree $d \geq 3$, every canonical divisor can be expressed as the sum of the divisor of a form $F$ of degree $d-3$ and a principal divisor. A fundamental invariant is $\deg(\operatorname{div}(\omega)) = 2g-2$, where $g$ is the genus of the curve.
