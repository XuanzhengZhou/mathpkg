The desingularization theorem for algebraic curves asserts that any irreducible algebraic curve in projective space is birationally equivalent to a plane curve whose only singularities (if any) are ordinary singularities. An ordinary singularity is locally a finite union of transversally intersecting smooth analytic arcs. This theorem is fundamental in the theory of algebraic curves because it allows one to reduce many problems to the study of plane curves with well-behaved singularities, serving as a compromise between the desire for a plane model and the desire for nonsingularity (which cannot always be achieved simultaneously in the plane).
