The $L^p$ spaces are fundamental objects in probability theory and functional analysis. For $p \geq 1$, $L^p(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ consists of all random variables with finite $p$-th absolute moment, equipped with the norm $\|\xi\|_p = (E|\xi|^p)^{1/p}$. The triangle inequality (Minkowski's inequality) makes $L^p$ a normed linear space after identifying random variables that are equal almost surely. For $0 < p < 1$, the function $\|\xi\|_p$ does not satisfy the triangle inequality and is not a norm, but $L^p$ is still a complete metric space under the metric $d(\xi, \eta) = E|\xi - \eta|^p$. The completeness of $L^p$ for $p \geq 1$ (Theorem 7) makes it a Banach space.
