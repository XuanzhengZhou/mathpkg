A random variable is the fundamental object of probability theory, formalizing the notion of a numerical outcome of a random experiment. The measurability condition $\{\omega : \xi(\omega) \in B\} \in \mathcal{F}$ ensures that when a probability measure $P$ is defined on $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$, it is meaningful to speak of the probability that the value of the random variable falls in any Borel set $B$. Discrete and simple random variables serve as building blocks for the general theory via approximation arguments.
