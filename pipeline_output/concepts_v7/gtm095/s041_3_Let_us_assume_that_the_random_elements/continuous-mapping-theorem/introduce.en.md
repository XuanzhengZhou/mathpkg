The Continuous Mapping Theorem (also known as the Mann–Wald theorem) guarantees that weak convergence (convergence in distribution) of random elements is preserved under almost-surely-continuous mappings. If $X_n \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} X$ and $h$ is a measurable function whose set of discontinuity points $\Delta_h$ has $\mathbb{P}_X$-measure zero, then $h(X_n) \xrightarrow{\mathcal{D}} h(X)$. This result is fundamental in probability and statistics, allowing, for example, the derivation of the convergence of $n(\overline{X}_n - m)^2/\sigma^2$ to a chi-squared distribution from the Central Limit Theorem. The proof relies on the "method of a single probability space" (Skorokhod representation).
