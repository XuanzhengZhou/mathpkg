An infinitely divisible distribution is a probability distribution that can be expressed, for every positive integer $n$, as the $n$-fold convolution of some other distribution. Equivalently, a random variable $T$ is infinitely divisible if for each $n \geq 1$ it can be represented as the sum of $n$ independent and identically distributed random variables. The normal, Poisson, Cauchy, gamma, and stable distributions are classical examples of infinitely divisible laws, whereas the uniform distribution on a bounded interval is not. This concept arises naturally when studying the class of all possible limit distributions for sums of independent random variables in a triangular array, where Theorem 1 of this section shows that the limit distributions are exactly the infinitely divisible ones.
