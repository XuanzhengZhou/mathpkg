Let $(\Omega^n, \mathcal{F}^n)_{n \geq 1}$ be a sequence of measurable spaces, and let $(P^n)_{n \geq 1}$ and $(\tilde{P}^n)_{n \geq 1}$ be sequences of probability measures with $P^n$ and $\tilde{P}^n$ defined on $(\Omega^n, \mathcal{F}^n)$, $n \geq 1$.

A sequence $(\tilde{P}^n)$ of measures is said to be \textbf{contiguous} to the sequence $(P^n)$, denoted $(\tilde{P}^n) \triangleleft (P^n)$, if for all $A^n \in \mathcal{F}^n$ such that $P^n(A^n) \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$, we have $\tilde{P}^n(A^n) \to 0$.

A sequence $(\tilde{P}^n)$ is said to be \textbf{entirely asymptotically separated} from $(P^n)$, denoted $(\tilde{P}^n) \triangle (P^n)$, if there exist a subsequence $n_k \uparrow \infty$ and sets $A^{n_k} \in \mathcal{F}^{n_k}$ such that $P^{n_k}(A^{n_k}) \to 1$ and $\tilde{P}^{n_k}(A^{n_k}) \to 0$.

These concepts are the natural asymptotic analogues of absolute continuity ($\tilde{P} \ll P$) and singularity ($\tilde{P} \perp P$) for sequences of measures.
