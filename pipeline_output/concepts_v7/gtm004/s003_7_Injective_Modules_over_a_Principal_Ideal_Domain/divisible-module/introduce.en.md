A divisible module over an integral domain is one in which every non-zero scalar can "divide" every element of the module. This concept is the algebraic dual of torsion-free and plays a central role in the characterization of injective modules over principal ideal domains. Classical examples include the additive group of rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$ and the quotient $\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$ as $\mathbb{Z}$-modules. Notably, non-trivial finitely generated abelian groups are never divisible, since they decompose as direct sums of cyclic groups.
