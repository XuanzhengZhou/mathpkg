In a category $\mathfrak{C}$ with zero object, a morphism $\mu: J \to K$ is called a \text{kernel} of $\sigma: K \to L$ if:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] $\sigma \mu = 0$;
\item[(ii)] If $\sigma \tau = 0$ for some $\tau: Z \to K$, then $\tau$ factorizes uniquely as $\tau = \mu \tau_0$ with $\tau_0: Z \to J$.
\end{enumerate}
The kernel is essentially unique, and $\mu$ is necessarily a monomorphism by virtue of the uniqueness of $\tau_0$.
