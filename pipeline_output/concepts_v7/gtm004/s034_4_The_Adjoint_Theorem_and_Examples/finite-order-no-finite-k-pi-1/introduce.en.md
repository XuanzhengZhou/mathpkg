This theorem states a fundamental obstruction in algebraic topology: if the fundamental group $\pi$ contains a nontrivial element of finite order, then any Eilenberg--MacLane space $K(\pi, 1)$ must be infinite-dimensional. The proof uses group cohomology: first, the cyclic group $C_m$ of order $m \geq 2$ has nonzero integral homology in all odd dimensions, so its $K(C_m, 1)$ is infinite-dimensional. The general case follows because the chain complex of the universal cover of $K(\pi, 1)$ serves as a free $C_m$-resolution when $C_m \leq \pi$.
