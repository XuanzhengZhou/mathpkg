A \text{category} $\mathfrak{C}$ consists of three pieces of data:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(1)] A class of objects $A, B, C, \ldots$
\item[(2)] For each pair of objects $A, B$ of $\mathfrak{C}$, a set $\mathfrak{C}(A, B)$ of morphisms from $A$ to $B$.
\item[(3)] For each triple of objects $A, B, C$ of $\mathfrak{C}$, a law of composition
$$\mathfrak{C}(A, B) \times \mathfrak{C}(B, C) \to \mathfrak{C}(A, C).$$
\end{enumerate}
These data satisfy the following axioms:
\begin{description}
\item[A1:] The sets $\mathfrak{C}(A_1, B_1)$, $\mathfrak{C}(A_2, B_2)$ are disjoint unless $A_1 = A_2$, $B_1 = B_2$.
\item[A2:] Given $f: A \to B$, $g: B \to C$, $h: C \to D$, then $h(gf) = (hg)f$ (Associative law of composition).
\item[A3:] To each object $A$ there is a morphism $1_A: A \to A$ such that, for any $f: A \to B$, $g: C \to A$,
$$f 1_A = f, \quad 1_A g = g.$$
\end{description}
If $f \in \mathfrak{C}(A, B)$, we write $f: A \to B$ or $A \xrightarrow{f} B$, and call $A$ the domain and $B$ the codomain. The composition of $f: A \to B$ and $g: B \to C$ is written $g \circ f$ or $g f$.
