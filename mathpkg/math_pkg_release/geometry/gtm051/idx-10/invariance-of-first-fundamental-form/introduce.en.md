The first fundamental form $I$ of a surface measures lengths and angles on tangent vectors using the Euclidean inner product of $\mathbb{R}^3$. This proposition establishes two natural invariance properties. First, $I$ is unchanged when the surface is composed with a rigid Euclidean isometry $B(x) = Rx + x_0$, because the orthogonal part $R$ preserves the inner product. Second, $I$ transforms naturally under a change of parameters $\phi$, with the form pulled back to the new parameter domain by the derivative $d\phi$. These invariances confirm that $I$ is a genuine geometric object.
