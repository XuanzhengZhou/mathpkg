The text states that this result was proved by E. Hopf; the proof is not provided in the section but is cited as a known theorem. The flat torus satisfies $K \equiv 0$ (see the second example in (6.7)), and by (6.5.6 (i)) the flat torus is free of conjugate points. Hopf's contribution is the converse direction: if a torus with a Riemannian metric has no conjugate points, then $K \equiv 0$. The proof relies on the Gauss-Bonnet theorem (6.3.5), which gives $\int_M K\,dM = 0$ for any metric on a surface homeomorphic to a torus, together with an analysis of Jacobi fields to rule out the possibility of $K$ taking both positive and negative values while maintaining the absence of conjugate points.
