The Theorema Egregium (Latin for "Remarkable Theorem") is a foundational result in differential geometry proved by Carl Friedrich Gauss. It states that the Gauss curvature of a surface, which is initially defined using the second fundamental form (an extrinsic quantity depending on how the surface is embedded in $\mathbb{R}^3$), can in fact be expressed solely in terms of the first fundamental form and its derivatives. This means Gauss curvature is an intrinsic property of the surface, independent of the particular embedding. The theorem opened the door to the study of intrinsic geometry of surfaces and ultimately to Riemannian geometry.
