The Plateau problem asks for the existence of a minimal surface (soap film) spanning a given closed curve in space. Named after the 19th-century physicist Joseph Plateau who studied soap films experimentally, the problem was solved affirmatively by T. Rado and J. Douglas in 1930, earning Douglas one of the first Fields Medals. The solution minimizes area among all surfaces with the given boundary and serves as a cornerstone of the calculus of variations in geometric analysis. Generalizations include seeking surfaces of prescribed constant or variable mean curvature spanning a given curve.
