**3.9.3 Weingarten surfaces.** A surface $f: U \to \mathbb{R}^3$ is called a **Weingarten surface** (or $W$-surface) if there exists a smooth function $\varphi: U \to \mathbb{R}$ with $d\varphi \neq 0$ such that the principal curvatures $\kappa_1(u) \geq \kappa_2(u)$ satisfy
$$\varphi(\kappa_1(u), \kappa_2(u)) = 0.$$

For example, surfaces with $H = \text{constant}$ or $K = \text{constant}$ are $W$-surfaces.

Prove:

(i) On a $W$-surface, the principal curvatures satisfy
$$\kappa_{1,1}\kappa_{2,2} - \kappa_{1,2}\kappa_{2,1} = 0.$$

(ii) The ellipsoid of revolution,
$$f(u, v) = (a \cos u \cos v, a \cos u \sin v, b \sin u),$$
with $0 < a \leq b$, is a $W$-surface satisfying $\kappa_1 = c\kappa_2^5$.

(iii) A $W$-surface is not a canal surface if and only if its caustic surfaces consist of asymptotic curves.
