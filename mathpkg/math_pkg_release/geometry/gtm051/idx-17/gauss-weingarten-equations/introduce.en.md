The Gauss-Weingarten equations are the fundamental formulas of classical surface theory, expressing the second partial derivatives $f_{ik}$ of a surface parametrization and the first partial derivatives $n_i$ of the unit normal vector in terms of the tangent basis $\{f_1, f_2\}$ and the normal $n$. The coefficients $\Gamma_{ik}^l$ appearing in the tangential components are the Christoffel symbols, which are intrinsic quantities computable solely from the first fundamental form $g_{ij}$ and its first derivatives. The normal component involves the second fundamental form $h_{ik}$, which measures how the surface bends in the ambient space. Together, these equations encode both the intrinsic and extrinsic geometry of a surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$.
