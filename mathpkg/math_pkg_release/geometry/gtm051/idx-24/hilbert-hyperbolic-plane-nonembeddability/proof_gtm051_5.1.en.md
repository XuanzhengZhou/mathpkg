The proof of Hilbert's theorem is not given in full detail in this section. The text notes that this result was proved by David Hilbert in 1901. A standard proof uses the Gauss-Bonnet theorem and the fact that a complete surface of constant negative curvature would have to contain a geodesic circle of arbitrarily large radius; applying the Gauss-Bonnet theorem to such a geodesic disc leads to a contradiction with the embedding in $\mathbb{R}^3$. The pseudosphere of (4.4.5) provides an example of a surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$ with constant negative Gauss curvature $-1/r^2$, but it is not complete, thus illustrating that the completeness hypothesis is essential to Hilbert's theorem.
