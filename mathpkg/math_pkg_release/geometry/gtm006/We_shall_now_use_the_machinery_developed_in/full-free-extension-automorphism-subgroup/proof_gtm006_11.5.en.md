Obviously it is only necessary to prove the lemma when $\mathcal{B}$ is a full free one-step extension of $\mathcal{A}$. Let $\beta$ be a collineation of $\mathcal{A}$ and let $P$ be any element of $\mathcal{B}$ which is not in $\mathcal{A}$. Since $\mathcal{B}$ is a free one-step extension of $\mathcal{A}$, there are exactly two elements $x, y$ of $\mathcal{A}$ which are incident with $P$ in $\mathcal{B}$. The fullness of the extension implies that $x^\beta$ and $y^\beta$, which clearly have no common element in $\mathcal{A}$, meet in $\mathcal{B}$. Defining $P^\beta$ as the intersection of $x^\beta$ with $y^\beta$ and defining $X^\beta = X^\beta$ for all $X$ in $\mathcal{A}$ extends $\beta$ to be a collineation $\beta$ of $\mathcal{B}$, and the lemma is proved. (Note that we need both fullness and freeness to prove this lemma; it is easy to give examples where the lemma is false without both of these assumptions.)
