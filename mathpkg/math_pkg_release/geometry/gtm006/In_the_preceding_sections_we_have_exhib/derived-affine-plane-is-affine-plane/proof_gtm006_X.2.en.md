Certainly $D_{\mathcal{S}'}(\mathcal{A})$ has $n^4$ points, since its points are those of $\mathcal{A}$. Each of its lines is either a line of $\mathcal{A}$, in which case it has exactly $n^2$ points, or is an affine Baer subplane of $\mathcal{A}$, which also has $n^2$ points.

Let $X$ and $Y$ be any two distinct points of $\mathcal{A}$ (or of $D_{\mathcal{S}'}(\mathcal{A})$). If $XY$ meets $\ell_{\infty}$ in $\mathcal{S}'$, then $X$ and $Y$ are on the line $XY$ of $D_{\mathcal{S}'}(\mathcal{A})$ and are in no Baer subplane containing $\mathcal{S}$; if $XY$ meets $\ell_{\infty}$ in $\mathcal{S}$ then the line $XY$ of $\mathcal{A}$ is not a line of $D_{\mathcal{S}'}(\mathcal{A})$, while the unique Baer subplane containing $X$, $Y$ and $\mathcal{S}$ is. So $X$ and $Y$ are on a unique line of $D_{\mathcal{S}'}(\mathcal{A})$.

Thus we have shown that $D_{\mathcal{S}'}(\mathcal{A})$ is a set of $n^4$ points such that each line contains $n^2$ points and every pair of distinct points is on a unique line, and so, by Exercise 3.14, $D_{\mathcal{S}'}(\mathcal{A})$ is an affine plane of order $n^2$.
