*Proof.* Let $X, Z, J$ be three non-collinear points of $\mathcal{U}$. Since $X^{\beta}$ and $Z^{\beta}$ are both absolute lines, neither contains $J$ (otherwise $J$ would be on an absolute line through $X$ or $Z$, contradicting the fact that an absolute line meets $\mathcal{U}$ only at its pole). Thus $Y = X^{\beta} \cap Z^{\beta}$ is not collinear with any two of $X, Z, J$, so $X, Y, Z, J$ form a frame. Choosing coordinates $X = \langle (1,0,0) \rangle$, $Y = \langle (1,1,0) \rangle$, $Z = \langle (0,0,1) \rangle$, $J = \langle (1,1,1) \rangle$ and imitating the proof of Theorem 2.36, one determines the entries of the matrix $A$ representing $\beta$. The conditions $X^{\beta} = \langle (0,1,0) \rangle$ (the line through $Y$ and $Z$) and $Z^{\beta} = \langle (1,1,0) \rangle$ force the matrix to have the stated anti-diagonal form. The relation $b + c + c^{\phi} = 0$ follows from the fact that $J \in \mathcal{U}$ imposes $J A J'^{\phi} = 0$. The explicit equation for membership in $\mathcal{U}$ is then derived from the condition $P A P'^{\phi} = 0$. $\square$
