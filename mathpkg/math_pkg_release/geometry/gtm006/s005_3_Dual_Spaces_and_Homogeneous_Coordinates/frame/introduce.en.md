In projective geometry, a frame plays the role analogous to that of a basis in vector space theory. Given a projective geometry $\mathcal{P}(V)$ of geometric dimension $n$, a frame is an ordered set of $n+2$ points with the property that no $n+1$ of them lie in a common hyperplane. While $n+1$ points coming from a basis of $V$ determine the basis only up to scalar multiples, adding the $(n+2)$-nd point fixes the basis uniquely up to a common scalar factor, enabling a consistent coordinatization of the geometry.
