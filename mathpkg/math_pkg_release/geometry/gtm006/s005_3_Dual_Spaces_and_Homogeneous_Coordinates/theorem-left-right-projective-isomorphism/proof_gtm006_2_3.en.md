The method of proof of Theorem 2.8 can be used with obvious and simple modifications. The key difference is that when working with a right vector space $W$, scalar multiplication is written on the right, and the semi-linear transformation will involve an anti-isomorphism $\phi : K \to F$ (i.e., $\phi(ab) = \phi(b)\phi(a)$) rather than an isomorphism.

One selects frames as in Theorem 2.8, constructs the bijection $\phi : K \to F$ using the geometric incidence structure, and verifies that $\phi$ is an anti-isomorphism by the same geometric constructions (the non-commutativity of the skewfields manifests as a reversal of the multiplication order in the coordinate computations). The isomorphism from $\mathcal{P}(V)$ onto $\mathcal{P}(W)$ is then induced by a semi-linear transformation composed with the anti-isomorphism $\phi$. $\square$
