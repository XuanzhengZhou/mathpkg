The Bruck-Ryser-Chowla theorem provides a powerful necessary condition for the existence of finite projective planes. It states that if a plane of order $n$ exists with $n \equiv 1$ or $2 \pmod{4}$, then $n$ must be representable as the sum of two squares, or satisfy a certain diophantine equation involving odd prime divisors of the square-free part of $n$. This theorem rules out infinitely many orders, including $n = 6, 14, 21, 22, \ldots$, and is one of the most celebrated non-existence results in finite geometry.
