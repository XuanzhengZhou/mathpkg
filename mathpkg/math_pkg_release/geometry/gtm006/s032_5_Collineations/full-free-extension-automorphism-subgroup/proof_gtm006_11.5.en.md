It suffices to prove the lemma when $\mathcal{B}$ is a full free one-step extension of $\mathcal{A}$. Let $\beta$ be a collineation of $\mathcal{A}$ and let $P$ be any element of $\mathcal{B}$ that is not in $\mathcal{A}$. Since $\mathcal{B}$ is a free one-step extension of $\mathcal{A}$, there are exactly two elements $x, y$ of $\mathcal{A}$ that are incident with $P$ in $\mathcal{B}$. The fullness of the extension implies that $x^\beta$ and $y^\beta$, which clearly have no common element in $\mathcal{A}$, meet in $\mathcal{B}$. Define $P^\beta$ as the intersection of $x^\beta$ with $y^\beta$, and define $X^\beta = X^\beta$ for all $X$ in $\mathcal{A}$. This extends $\beta$ to a collineation $\beta$ of $\mathcal{B}$, and the lemma is proved.

(Note that both fullness and freeness are required for this lemma; it is easy to give examples where the lemma is false without both of these assumptions.)
