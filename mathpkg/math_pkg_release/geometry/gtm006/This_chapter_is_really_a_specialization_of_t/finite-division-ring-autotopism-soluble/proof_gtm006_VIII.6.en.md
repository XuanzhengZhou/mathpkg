By the corollary to Lemma 8.14, $\Delta$ is soluble if and only if $\Delta_1$ is soluble. The subgroup $\Delta_2$ of $\Delta_1$ (where $P, Q, R$ are linear, not merely semi-linear) is normal in $\Delta_1$ with elementary abelian $2$-group quotient. Using the Feit-Thompson theorem (or more elementary arguments applicable to this special case), the structure of $\Delta_2$ is analyzed via its involutions.

If $\mathcal{P}(D)$ has even order and no Baer subplanes, then by Lemma 8.16, any involution in $\Delta_2$ must be of the form $(A, A, I), (A, 1, A),$ or $(I, A, A)$. These form a normal elementary abelian $2$-subgroup whose centralizer in $\Delta_2$ has no involutions, hence (by Feit-Thompson) is soluble.

If $\mathcal{P}(D)$ has odd order and the dimension of $D$ over some semi-nucleus is odd, then by Lemma 8.17, $\Delta_1$ contains no Baer involutions. Again, by Lemma 8.16, all involutions are of the three special homology types, and the same argument shows $\Delta_1$ is soluble.

In both cases, $\Delta_1$ is soluble, hence $\Delta$ is soluble. $\square$
