**Exercise 9.11.** Show that the quaternions, as defined in (b) above, could also have been defined as a four-dimensional vector space $Q$ over the reals, with the basis elements $1, i, j, k$ and multiplication defined by the rules $1x = x1 = x$ for all $x \in Q$; $ij = -ji = k$; $jk = -kj = i$; $ki = -ik = j$; $i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = -1$; if $x$ is in the reals then $x$ commutes with each of $i, j, k$.
