The proof involves verifying that the collection of sets $\mathcal{B}(a, b, c)$ for varying parameters satisfies the axioms of a derivation set. Specifically, one must check that any two points determine a unique such Baer subplane and that the intersection properties with lines behave correctly.

Let $X = (\alpha_1 a + b, \beta_1 a + c)$ and $Y = (\alpha_2 a + b, \beta_2 a + c)$ be any two points of $\mathcal{B}(a, b, c)$. If $\alpha_1 = \alpha_2$, then $X$ and $Y$ are on a line whose slope lies in $F$, and the line contains exactly $q$ points of $\mathcal{B}(a, b, c)$. If $\alpha_1 \neq \alpha_2$, then by properties (d) and (e) of the quasifield structure, the elements $\alpha, \beta, \delta$ in $R$ satisfying certain relations are all in $F$, and there exists $\lambda \in F$ such that $X$ and $Y$ lie on the line $[\lambda, k]$ meeting $\ell_{\infty}$ in the derivation set $\mathcal{S}$. The counting argument then shows that each such line contains exactly $q$ points of $\mathcal{B}(a, b, c)$, establishing that $\mathcal{B}(a, b, c)$ is indeed a Baer subplane and that the collection of such subplanes forms a derivation set. $\square$
