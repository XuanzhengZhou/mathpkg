Suppose $\theta$ is an isomorphism from $\mathcal{P}^l$ onto $\mathcal{P}^{l'}$. Define $\alpha$ on $\mathcal{P}$ by: for any point $X$ not on $l$, set $X^\alpha = X^\theta$; for any point $Y$ on $l$, pick any line $m$ through $Y$ with $m \neq l$, and define $Y^\alpha = m^\theta \cap l'$. One verifies that $\alpha$ is well-defined (independent of the choice of $m$) and is an isomorphism of $\mathcal{P}$ mapping $l$ to $l'$.

Conversely, if $\alpha$ is an isomorphism of $\mathcal{P}$ with $l^\alpha = l'$, then the restriction of $\alpha$ to the points and lines of $\mathcal{P}^l$ gives an isomorphism from $\mathcal{P}^l$ to $\mathcal{P}^{l'}$.
