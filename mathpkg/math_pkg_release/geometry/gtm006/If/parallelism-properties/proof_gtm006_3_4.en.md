Suppose $l \parallel h$ with $l \neq h$. If there were a point $A$ on both $l$ and $h$, then we would have two distinct lines through $A$ not intersecting a third line $m$ (where $m$ is any line intersecting neither $l$ nor $h$ in the same parallel class). This contradicts axiom (ii) of the definition of an affine plane, which guarantees a unique parallel through any point to a given line. Therefore $l$ and $h$ are disjoint.

The second claim follows immediately from axiom (ii) in the definition of an affine plane: given any line $m$ and a point $A$ not on $m$, there is a unique line through $A$ that does not intersect $m$, i.e., a unique line through $A$ parallel to $m$.
