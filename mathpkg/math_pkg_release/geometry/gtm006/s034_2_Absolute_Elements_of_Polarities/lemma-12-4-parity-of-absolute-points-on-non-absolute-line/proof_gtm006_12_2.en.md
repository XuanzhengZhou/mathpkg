If $X$ is any point of $m$ then, since $m$ is not absolute, $X^\alpha \neq m$. For any $X$ on $m$ define $X^\theta$ by $X^\theta = m \cap X^\alpha$; then $(X^\theta)^\theta = (X^\theta)^\alpha \cap m = (X^\alpha) \cap m \cap m = X \cdot m^\alpha \cap m = X$. Thus $\theta$ is an involutory permutation of the points of $m$ and, if $t$ is the number of points fixed by $\theta$, then $n + 1 - t \equiv 0 \pmod{2}$, i.e. $t \equiv n + 1 \pmod{2}$. But $X^\theta = X$ if and only if $X$ is on $X^\alpha$, so that $t$ is also equal to the number of absolute points of $\alpha$ on $m$, and the lemma is proved.
