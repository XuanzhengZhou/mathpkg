Bézout's theorem is a cornerstone of algebraic geometry, discovered by the French mathematician Étienne Bézout (1730–1783). It states that for two algebraic curves in the complex projective plane defined by polynomials with no repeated factors and no common factor, the total number of intersection points counted with multiplicity equals the product of their degrees. This elegant result generalizes the fundamental theorem of algebra (the case where one curve is a line) and provides the algebraic foundation for counting intersections of plane curves. The theorem requires working in projective space to account for points at infinity, and the no-common-factor condition ensures the intersection is finite.
