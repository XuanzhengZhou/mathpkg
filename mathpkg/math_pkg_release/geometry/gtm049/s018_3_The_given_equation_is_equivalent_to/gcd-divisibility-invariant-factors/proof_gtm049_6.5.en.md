\textbf{Part (b)(i).} Assume $[d] = [m_j] + [m_k]$, the ideal generated by a greatest common divisor of $m_j$ and $m_k$. Then there exist $u, v \in R$ such that $d = um_j + vm_k$. Consider the ordered basis $(b_1, \ldots, b_t)$ of the free module. Define
$$b_j' = vb_j - ub_k, \quad b_k' = \frac{m_j}{d} b_j + \frac{m_k}{d} b_k,$$
and $b_i' = b_i$ for $i \notin \{j, k\}$. The transformation matrix relating $(b_i')$ to $(b_i)$ has determinant $v \cdot \frac{m_k}{d} + u \cdot \frac{m_j}{d} = \frac{vm_k + um_j}{d} = 1$, so $(b_i')$ is also a basis. Moreover,
$$m_j b_j' + m_k b_k' = m_j(vb_j - ub_k) + m_k\left(\frac{m_j}{d}b_j + \frac{m_k}{d}b_k\right) = \left(m_j v + \frac{m_k m_j}{d}\right)b_j + \left(-m_j u + \frac{m_k^2}{d}\right)b_k.$$

Actually, compute directly:
$$m_j b_j + m_k b_k = d\left(\frac{m_j}{d} b_j + \frac{m_k}{d} b_k\right) = d b_k'.$$
Thus $d b_k'$ belongs to the submodule generated by $b_j, b_k$ (since it equals $m_j b_j + m_k b_k$), and conversely $b_k'$ generates a submodule containing $d b_k'$. Hence the ideal generated by $d$ equals the annihilator of $b_k'$ modulo the relations, so $d$ has the same invariant factor length as $m_k$. Therefore $xd = m_k$ for some invertible $x \in R$, and $m_k \mid m_j$.

\textbf{Part (b)(ii).} Let $d$ be a greatest common divisor of $m_k$ and $n_k$, and write $d = um_k + vn_k$ with $u, v \in R$. The module has two sets of relations:
$$\sum_{i=1}^{k-1} m_i b_i + m_k b_k = 0, \quad \sum_{i=1}^{k-1} n_i b_i + n_k b_k = 0.$$
Multiply the first relation by $u$ and the second by $v$, then add:
$$\sum_{i=1}^{k-1} (um_i + vn_i) b_i + (um_k + vn_k) b_k = 0,$$
which simplifies to
$$\sum_{i=1}^{k-1} (um_i + vn_i) b_i + d b_k = 0.$$
This shows that $d$ annihilates the class of $b_k$ in the quotient by the first $k-1$ generators. By Exercise 5 (which states that if an element annihilates a generator in such a presentation, it is an associate of the invariant factor), we conclude $m_k = xd$ for some invertible $x \in R$. Hence $m_k \mid n_k$.
