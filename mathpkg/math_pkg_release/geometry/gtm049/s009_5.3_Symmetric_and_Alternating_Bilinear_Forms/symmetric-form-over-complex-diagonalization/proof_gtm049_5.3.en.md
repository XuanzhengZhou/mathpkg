**PROOF.** By the corollary to Theorem 3, there exists an ordered basis $(a_1, \ldots, a_n)$ such that

$$(\sigma; (a_i)) = \operatorname{diag}(x_1, \ldots, x_r, 0, \ldots, 0),$$

with $x_i \neq 0$ for $i = 1, \ldots, r$. Let $y_i^2 = x_i^{-1}$ for $i = 1, \ldots, r$ (possible since every complex number has a square root). Then with respect to $(y_1 a_1, \ldots, y_r a_r, a_{r+1}, \ldots, a_n)$, the matrix of $\sigma$ is $\begin{pmatrix} I_r & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.
