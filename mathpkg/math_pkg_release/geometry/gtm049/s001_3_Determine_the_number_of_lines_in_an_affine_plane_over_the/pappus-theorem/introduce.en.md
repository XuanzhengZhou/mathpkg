Pappus' theorem is a classical result in projective geometry, dating back to Pappus of Alexandria (c. 340 AD). It states that if two triads of points lie on two distinct coplanar lines, then the three intersection points formed by cross-joining the points are collinear. Together with Desargues' theorem, Pappus' theorem plays a centrally important role in the synthetic foundations of projective geometry. Unlike Desargues' theorem, the proof of Pappus' theorem requires the commutative law of multiplication in the ground field.
