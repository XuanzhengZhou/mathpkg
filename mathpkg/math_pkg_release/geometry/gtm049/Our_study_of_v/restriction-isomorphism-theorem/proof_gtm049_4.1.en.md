We recall from Chapter I, Theorem 1, Corollary 2 that there always exist subspaces $M$ such that $V = M \oplus \operatorname{Ker} f$. Any vector $v$ in $V$ is uniquely expressible in the form $v = a + b$ where $a \in M$ and $b \in \operatorname{Ker} f$. By the definition of $\operatorname{Ker} f$, $vf = af$ and so $Vf = Mf$. Further, $af = 0$ implies that $a \in M \cap \operatorname{Ker} f = \{0\}$. Thus the restriction of $f$ to $M$ is an isomorphism of $M$ onto $Mf = Vf$.

The last part follows immediately from Theorem 2 of Chapter I.
