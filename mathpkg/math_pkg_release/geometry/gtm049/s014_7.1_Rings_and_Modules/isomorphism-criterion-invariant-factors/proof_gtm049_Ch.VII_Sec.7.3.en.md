This result is an immediate consequence of Theorems 1 and 2 together with Proposition 4.

By Theorem 1, any finitely generated $F[X]$-module $M$ can be decomposed as a direct sum of cyclic submodules
$$M = [a_1] \oplus \cdots \oplus [a_k]$$
with $\mathfrak{a}(a_1) \subset \mathfrak{a}(a_2) \subset \cdots \subset \mathfrak{a}(a_k)$, where the ideals $\mathfrak{a}(a_i)$ (the invariant factors) are uniquely determined by $M$.

If two finitely generated $F[X]$-modules $M$ and $N$ have the same invariant factors, then both are isomorphic to the same direct sum of cyclic modules $[a_1] \oplus \cdots \oplus [a_k]$ with the prescribed annihilator ideals, hence $M \cong N$. Conversely, if $M \cong N$, then by the uniqueness part of Theorem 1 they must have the same invariant factors.

For finitely generated torsion modules, Theorem 2 gives the decomposition into elementary divisors:
$$M = \bigoplus_{i=1}^{t} \bigoplus_{j=1}^{k_i} [a_{ij}]$$
where $\mathfrak{a}(a_{ij})$ is generated by a power of an irreducible polynomial $p_i$, and these ideals are uniquely determined by $M$. The same reasoning as above shows that two such modules are isomorphic precisely when they have the same elementary divisors (the same $t$, $k_i$, and powers $p_i^{n_{ij}}$).
