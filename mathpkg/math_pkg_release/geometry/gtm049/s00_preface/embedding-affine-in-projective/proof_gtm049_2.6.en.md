(1) If $S = a+M \in \mathcal{A}(c+H)$, then $[S] = [a]+M$ and $c+H = a+H$ (by Lemma 1). If $xa+m = a+h$, then $(x-1)a = h-m \in H$ where $a \notin H$ (since $c \notin H$), and so $x=1$ and $h=m$. Hence $[S] \cap (c+H) = S$. This shows that the mapping $\varphi$ is one-one and also, incidentally, that the inverse of $\varphi$ is the mapping $S\varphi \rightarrow (S\varphi) \cap (c+H)$.

(2) If $P$ is a subspace of $V$ not contained in $H$, then $P+H=V$, since $H$ is a hyperplane in $V$, and so $\dim(P \cap H) = \dim P - 1$.

(3)–(5) follow from the properties of $[ \cdot ]$ on cosets and subspaces.

(6) If $S = a + M$, then $\dim [S] = \dim M + 1$, since $[a] \cap M = 0$. Hence $\operatorname{pdim} [S] = \dim S$.

(7) If $S = a + M$, then $[S] \cap H = M$. For if $xa + m = h$ we must have $x = 0, m = h$. The result now follows at once from the definition of parallel cosets.
