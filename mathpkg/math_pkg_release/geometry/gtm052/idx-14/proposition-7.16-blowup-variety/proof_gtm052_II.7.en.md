**Proof.** (a) Since $X$ is integral, the sheaf $\mathcal{S} = \bigoplus_{d \geqslant 0} \mathcal{I}^d$ is a sheaf of integral domains on $X$, so $\tilde{X}$ is also integral. By (7.10), $\pi$ is proper; in particular, $\pi$ is separated and of finite type, so $\tilde{X}$ is also separated and of finite type over $k$. Hence $\tilde{X}$ is a variety.

(b) Since $\mathcal{I} \neq 0$, the corresponding closed subscheme $Y$ is not all of $X$, so the open set $U = X - Y$ is nonempty. By Proposition 7.13(b), $\pi$ induces an isomorphism from $\pi^{-1}(U)$ to $U$, so $\pi$ is birational. Properness follows from (7.10), and proper morphisms between varieties are closed, hence surjective onto their image; since $\pi$ is birational and proper, it is surjective.

(c) If $X$ is quasi-projective, writing $X$ as a locally closed subscheme of some $\mathbf{P}_k^N$ and using the relative notion of Proj, one obtains that $\tilde{X}$ is also quasi-projective and $\pi$ is projective. The projective case follows similarly. $\square$
