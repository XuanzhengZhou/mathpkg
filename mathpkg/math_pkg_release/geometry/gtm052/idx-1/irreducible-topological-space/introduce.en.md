A topological space (or subset) is irreducible if it cannot be written as a union of two proper closed subsets. This is the algebro-geometric analogue of connectedness, but stronger: in the Zariski topology, irreducible spaces correspond to prime ideals. For instance, $\mathbf{A}^1$ is irreducible because its only proper closed subsets are finite sets, while the field $k$ is infinite. Irreducibility is hereditary to nonempty open subsets and preserved under taking closures.
