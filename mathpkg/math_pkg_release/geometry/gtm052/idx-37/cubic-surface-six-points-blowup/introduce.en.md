The main result of Section 4: the projective plane $\mathbf{P}^2$ blown up at six points in general position (no three collinear, not all six on a conic) is isomorphic to a nonsingular cubic surface in $\mathbf{P}^3$. The isomorphism is given by the linear system of plane cubics through the six points. This theorem establishes a fundamental bridge between the birational geometry of $\mathbf{P}^2$ and the projective geometry of cubic surfaces, enabling the study of the 27 lines and other rich geometry.
