Let $P \in C$ and $P' \in C'$ be singular points of (reduced) curves lying on surfaces. The singularities are \textit{equivalent} if, after finitely many blowups, the strict transforms become nonsingular and the configurations of the exceptional curves and the strict transforms at the preimages of $P$ and $P'$ are isomorphic. This equivalence relation is weaker than analytic isomorphism: analytically isomorphic singularities are equivalent, but not conversely (Ex. 3.6).

For a double point $P$, the equivalence class is determined by:
\begin{itemize}
\item the number $n$ of blowups required to make the strict transform $\tilde{C}$ nonsingular;
\item at the last step, whether $\tilde{C}$ meets the exceptional curve $E$ transversally in two distinct points (node, type (a)) or is tangent to $E$ in one point (cusp, type (b)).
\end{itemize}
In this case the equivalence classification coincides with the analytic classification: up to analytic isomorphism, any plane double point is given by $y^2 = x^r$ for some $r \geqslant 2$, with $n = \lfloor r/2 \rfloor$ and type (a) for $r$ even, type (b) for $r$ odd.
