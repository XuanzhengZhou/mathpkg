An exceptional curve of the first kind is a curve on an algebraic surface that behaves exactly like the exceptional divisor produced by a monoidal transformation (blow-up at a point): it is isomorphic to the projective line and has self-intersection number \(-1\). The terminology originates from the classical Italian school of algebraic geometry, where such curves were studied in the context of Castelnuovo's contraction criterion. The defining numerical condition \(Y \cong \mathbf{P}^1, Y^2 = -1\) is both necessary (any exceptional curve of a blow-up satisfies it) and, by Castelnuovo's theorem, sufficient to guarantee that the curve can be contracted to a nonsingular point.
