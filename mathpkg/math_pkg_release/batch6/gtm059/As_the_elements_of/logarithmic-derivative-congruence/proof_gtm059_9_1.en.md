The proof is similar to that of DL 3. We know that

$$
\lambda_i(u^{m-1})(X) = u^{m-1} \lambda_i(u)
$$

so by the chain rule,

$$
\lambda_i(u)^{m-1}(X) = u \cdots
$$

We have the representations

$$
a = g(s) = g[s]^{(n+1)} = g_n(s)
$$

where

$$
g(s) = g[s]^{n+1} X.
$$

Since $a$ is assumed to be a unit, the power series has a unit as leading coefficient, and thus both power series can be used to compute the logarithm. The rest of the proof then follows immediately from the chain rule and the definition of the logarithmic derivative.
