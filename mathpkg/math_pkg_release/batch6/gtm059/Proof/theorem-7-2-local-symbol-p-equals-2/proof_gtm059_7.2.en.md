Since $A_n(1) = 0$ for $p = 2$ (the trace of the logarithmic derivative vanishes at the identity), it follows that $A_n(a(1)) = a(1)$ up to a unit factor. This shows that $[x, a]$ is well-defined modulo $\mathbb{Z}_2$ and lies in the appropriate conductor. The proof reduces to showing that the expression is well-defined in the special Lie algebra $L^2$ associated to the formal group.

We may therefore assume that the conductor is a unit in the relevant module; in this case we follow the same arguments as in the proof of Theorem 6.1 (the general case for odd $p$), using Lemma 6.3 instead of Theorem 6.2 to account for the different ramification at $p = 2$. Each step of the argument from the odd $p$ case remains valid when the appropriate adjustments for wild ramification are made. The key point is that the factor $1/2$ compensates for the failure of the trace map to be surjective at the level of integral elements when $p = 2$, ensuring that the resulting symbol takes values in $\frac{1}{2}\mathbb{Z}_2/\mathbb{Z}_2$ rather than $\mathbb{Z}_2/\mathbb{Z}_2$.
