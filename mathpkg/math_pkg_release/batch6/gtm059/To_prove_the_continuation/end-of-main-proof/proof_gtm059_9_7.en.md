The theorem has already been proved when $x$ is a power of $n$, or when $y$ is (by the preceding results in Theorems 5.1-6.2 and Lemma 6.3). We may therefore assume that $x$ is a unit with $1 \ne n \ne 0$.

Under this assumption, we reduce the theorem to the result of the preceding section as follows. The condition $k \ge |s| + 21$ (or the appropriate bound when $p = 2$) ensures that the valuations are large enough to apply the interchangeability results of Theorem 6.2 and Lemma 6.3. Specifically, these bounds guarantee that the error terms arising from the difference between formal group addition and ordinary addition are negligible with respect to the relevant symbol computations.

The reduction to the unit case, combined with the previously established results for the case where $x$ is a power of $n$, completes the proof of the main theorem. The condition that $[x, w_n]$ lies in $i^{n+1}$ for $1 \le i \le k$ follows from the valuation bounds and the structure of the Lubin-Tate tower.
