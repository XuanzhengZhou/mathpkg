The proof follows the same pattern as for Gauss sums over finite fields. One computes $|S(\chi, \lambda)|^2$ by expanding the product and changing variables. The orthogonality of characters eliminates the cross terms when $\chi$ is primitive, yielding the value $m$. The Fourier inversion formula then follows from the standard character orthogonality relations on the finite abelian group $\mathbb{Z}(m)^\times$, with the Gauss sum providing the normalization constant. The absolute value $1$ property of the normalized Gauss sum follows directly from $|S(\chi, \lambda)|^2 = m$.
