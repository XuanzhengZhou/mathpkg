\textbf{Proof.} The proof follows Kubert's method by induction on the number of prime factors of $N$. Let $U(N)$ be the universal distribution generated by the symbols $[x]$ for $x \in \frac{1}{N}\mathbb{Z}/\mathbb{Z}$ modulo the distribution relations. Let $A_N$ be the subgroup of $U(N)$ generated by the elements $[x]$ with $x \in T_N$.

We first show that the elements $[x]$ for $x \in T_N$ generate $U(N)$. Let $N = \prod p_i^{e_i}$ and write $p = p_1$. Consider an arbitrary generator $[y] \in U(N)$ with partial fraction decomposition $y = \sum a_i/p_i^{e_i}$. We proceed by induction on the number of nonzero components $a_i$.

If some $a_i = 0$, the element belongs to a level with fewer prime factors, and we are done by induction. Assume $a_1$ is prime to $p$ and $a_1 \neq 0$. Apply the distribution relation with $d = p$ to the element $[py]$:
$$p \cdot [py] = \sum_{pz = py} [z] \pmod{A_N}.$$
This relation expresses $[y]$ in terms of elements with smaller $p$-component multiplied by $p$, modulo elements in $A_N$. By iteration, we reduce to the case where $a_1 = 0$, completing the induction.

For the second part, we show there are no relations among the generators $[x]$ for $x \in T_N$ beyond those defining the universal distribution. We exhibited a natural distribution (from cyclotomic units) in Section 8 whose rank equals $|T_N|$. Since $U(N)$ is generated by $|T_N|$ elements, and the exhibited distribution provides a lower bound of $|T_N|$ for the rank, the generators must be free. The counting argument uses the fact that $|T_N| = \prod_i (p_i^{e_i} - p_i^{e_i-1}) = \varphi(N)$.

For the final step concerning the partial fraction decomposition with respect to multiple prime factors, we apply the same induction argument to each prime factor in succession. Subtracting the appropriate distribution relations yields:
$$\sum_{i=1}^{r} t_i \left(\frac{P_i^{\dagger}}{P_i + 1}\right) = 0 \pmod{A_N}.$$
Repeating the procedure on each component of the partial fraction decomposition yields the desired result inductively on the number of prime factors. $\square$
