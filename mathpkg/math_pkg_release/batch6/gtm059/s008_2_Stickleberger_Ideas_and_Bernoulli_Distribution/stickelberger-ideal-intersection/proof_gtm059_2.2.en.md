First, we prove that for any prime $j \geq 2$, we have $R^2 = R_c$ and $J^2 = J_c$. This is a property due to Macza and Couture-Smortt. Indeed, from the definitions of the Stickelberger elements, we have

$$
e^2(c^2) - c^2 p^2 = \sum_{j=1}^{k} E_{k^2}(J).
$$

From this relation, the prime-by-prime analysis of the Stickelberger ideal shows that an element belongs to $R^{(\infty)}$ if and only if its $p$-component lies in $R$ for every prime $p$. The equality $R_k \cap R = R^{(\infty)}$ then follows by checking the condition locally at each prime and using the $p$-adic properties of the Bernoulli polynomials established in the preceding lemmas. Part (ii) follows by applying the same local analysis to the element $Q(c)$ under the stated condition.
