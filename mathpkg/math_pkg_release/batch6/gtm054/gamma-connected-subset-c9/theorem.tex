Let $\Gamma$ be $m$-connected. Let $A, Z \subset V$ such that $A \cap Z = \varnothing$ and $|A|, |Z| \leq m$. Let there exist functions $g: A \rightarrow \mathbb{N} + \{0\}$ and $h: Z \rightarrow \mathbb{N} + \{0\}$ such that

$$\sum_{a \in A} g(a) = m = \sum_{z \in Z} h(z).$$

Then $\Gamma$ admits an openly-disjoint $m$-family $\mathcal{F}$ of AZ-paths such that for each $a \in A$ and $z \in Z$, precisely $g(a)$ of the paths in $\mathcal{F}$ contain $a$ and precisely $h(z)$ of the paths contain $z$.

Observe carefully what the above proposition does not say. It does not imply that for some given $a_0 \in A$ and $z_0 \in Z$, the family $\mathcal{F}$ includes an $a_0z_0$-path. Such a condition is of a fundamentally different nature, and demands much more from a graph, as the next result indicates.

We shall say that $\Gamma$ satisfies the condition $O_n (n = 1, 2, \ldots)$ if $|V| \geq 2n$ and, given $\{a_1, \ldots, a_n, z_1, \ldots, z_n\} \in \mathcal{P}_{2n}(V)$, there exists an openly-disjoint $n$-family $\{\Pi_1, \ldots, \Pi_n\}$ of paths such that $\Pi_i$ is an $a_iz_i$-path ($i = 1, \ldots, n$). Clearly $O_n$ implies $O_m$ if $n > m$.
