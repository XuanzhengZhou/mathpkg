Assume $\Gamma$ is of Type III. By F26, it is a graph. Since it has $4$ vertices each of valence $3$, it must be $K_4$, which is isocovalent since it has a unique planar imbedding.

Assume $\Gamma$ is of Type V. It is also a graph by F26. Furthermore, $\mathcal{L}(\Gamma)$ is even, and hence by A13, $\Gamma$ is bipartite, say with respect to $\{V_1, V_2\}$. Since $\Gamma$ is isovalent, $|V_1| = |V_2|$. Thus $\Gamma$ is a subgraph of $K_{4,4}$. Since $\nu_1(\Gamma) = 12 = \nu_1(K_{4,4}) - 4$, $\Gamma$ must be obtained by deleting $4$ edges from $K_{4,4}$ which reduces the valence of each vertex by $1$. This can be done in essentially one way, yielding the cube (F21).

Assume $\Gamma$ is of Type IV. Let $\Theta$ be Platonic and let $\Theta \perp \Gamma$. We have just shown that $\Theta$ is the cube. Since the cube has no cocycle consisting of two edges, $\Gamma$ has no cycle of length $2$ and is therefore a graph. Hence $\Gamma$ is a subgraph of $K_6$. Since $\nu_1(\Gamma) = \nu_1(K_6) - 3$, $\Gamma$ must be obtained by deleting three edges from $K_6$ in such a way as to reduce the valence of each vertex by $1$. This can be done in only one way (up to system-isomorphism) yielding the octahedron (F20). One may easily verify that the cube and the octahedron have each only one planar imbedding and hence are isocovalent.
