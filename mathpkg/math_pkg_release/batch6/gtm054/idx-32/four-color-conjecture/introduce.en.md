The Four-Color Theorem (originally the Four-Color Conjecture) asserts that every planar graph can be vertex-colored using at most four colors such that no two adjacent vertices share the same color. Equivalently, any map drawn on the plane (or sphere) can be colored with at most four colors so that no two regions sharing a boundary of positive length have the same color. The theorem was proved in 1976 by Kenneth Appel and Wolfgang Haken using a computer-assisted exhaustive case analysis, marking a landmark achievement in combinatorics and the first major proof to rely heavily on computer verification.
