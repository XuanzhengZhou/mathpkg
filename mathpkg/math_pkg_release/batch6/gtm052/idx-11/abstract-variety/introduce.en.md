Definition 4.10 introduces the modern notion of an abstract variety: an integral, separated scheme of finite type over an algebraically closed field $k$. This generalizes the quasi-projective varieties of Chapter I by removing the requirement of an embedding into projective space. The concept was invented by Weil to construct the Jacobian variety of a curve, which initially could only be realized as an abstract, not projective, variety. From this point onward in Hartshorne, "variety" means abstract variety.
