Corollary 7.8 recovers the classical Bézout theorem: for two distinct curves $Y, Z$ in the projective plane $\mathbf{P}^2$ of degrees $d$ and $e$, the sum of their intersection multiplicities at all intersection points equals $de$. A point in $\mathbf{P}^2$ has Hilbert polynomial 1 and hence degree 1, so the general formula from Theorem 7.7 specializes to the classical form. The intersection multiplicity defined via the homogeneous coordinate ring coincides with the earlier local definition for plane curves.
