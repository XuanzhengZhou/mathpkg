This classification uses the discrete data of multiplicities at singular points and all infinitely near singular points to categorize curve singularities on surfaces. Unlike analytic isomorphism, which requires local analytic equivalence, this classification captures only the configuration of infinitely near multiplicities. For double points, the classification reduces to the number $n$ of blow-ups needed for resolution and whether the final strict transform meets the exceptional divisor transversally (node) or tangentially (cusp). For double points, this classification surprisingly coincides with analytic isomorphism.
