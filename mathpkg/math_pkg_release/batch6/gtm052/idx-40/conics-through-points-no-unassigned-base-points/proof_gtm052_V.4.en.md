Clearly it is sufficient to consider the case $r = 4$. First suppose $P_1, P_2, P_3, P_4$ are all ordinary points. Let $L_{ij}$ denote the line containing $P_i$ and $P_j$. Then $\mathfrak{d}$ contains $L_{12} + L_{34}$ and $L_{13} + L_{24}$. Since no three of the $P_i$ are collinear, the intersection of these two divisors consists of the points $P_1, P_2, P_3, P_4$ with multiplicity 1 each, so there are no unassigned base points.

Now suppose $P_2$ is infinitely near $P_1$. In this case $\mathfrak{d}$ contains $L_{12} + L_{34}$ and $L_{13} + L_{14}$. (Here $L_{12}$ denotes the line through $P_1$ with the tangent direction given by $P_2$.) This intersection again is just $\{P_1, P_2, P_3, P_4\}$, so there are no further base points.
