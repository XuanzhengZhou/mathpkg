It is sufficient to consider the case $r = 7$. We show first that if $P_1, \ldots, P_7$ are all ordinary points, then $\mathfrak{d}$ has no unassigned ordinary base points. For this, for each point $Q$ not equal to any $P_i$, we exhibit a cubic curve containing $P_1, \ldots, P_7$ but not $Q$.

Case 1. Suppose three points $P_1, P_2, P_3$ lie on a line $L^*$ with $Q$. The points $P_4, P_5, P_6, P_7$ are not all collinear, so we may assume $P_4, P_5, P_6$ are not collinear. Then the conic $\Gamma_{12456}$ through $P_1, P_2, P_4, P_5, P_6$, together with the line $L_{37}$ through $P_3$ and $P_7$, forms a cubic containing $P_1, \ldots, P_7$ but not $Q$. Indeed, if $Q \in \Gamma_{12456}$, then this conic contains $L^*$, so it is reducible, contradicting that $P_4, P_5, P_6$ are not collinear. If $Q \in L_{37}$, then $P_7 \in L^*$, contradicting that $P_1, P_2, P_3, P_7$ are not collinear (no 4 collinear).
