We use the fact that $Q$ is isomorphic to $\mathbf{P}_k^1 \times_k \mathbf{P}_k^1$ (I, Ex. 2.15). Let $p_1$ and $p_2$ be the projections of $Q$ onto the two factors. Then as in the proof of (6.6), one obtains $\text{Cl} Q \cong \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}$.

One can also define a restriction homomorphism $\text{Cl} \mathbf{P}^3 \rightarrow \text{Cl} Q$ by intersecting divisors $D = \sum n_i Y_i$ on $\mathbf{P}^3$ with $Q$, for divisors whose components do not contain $Q$. Since any divisor on $\mathbf{P}^3$ is linearly equivalent to one whose prime divisors do not contain $Q$ by (6.4), we obtain a well-defined homomorphism $\text{Cl} \mathbf{P}^3 \rightarrow \text{Cl} Q$. If $H$ is the hyperplane $w = 0$, then $H \cap Q$ consists of the two lines $x = w = 0$ and $y = w = 0$, one in each family, so $H \cap Q$ is of type $(1,1)$ in $\text{Cl} Q = \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}$.
