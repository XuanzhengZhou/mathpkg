To show $\pi^*$ is injective, suppose $D \in \text{Div} X$, and $\pi^*D = (f)$ for some $f \in K(t)$. Since $\pi^*D$ involves only prime divisors of type 1, $f$ must be in $K$. For otherwise we could write $f = g/h$, with $g,h \in K[t]$, relatively prime. If $g,h$ are not both in $K$, then $(f)$ will involve some prime divisor of type 2 on $X \times \mathbf{A}^1$. Now if $f \in K$, it is clear that $D = (f)$, so $\pi^*$ is injective.

To show that $\pi^*$ is surjective, it suffices to show that any prime divisor of type 2 on $X \times \mathbf{A}^1$ is linearly equivalent to a linear combination of prime divisors of type 1. Let $Z \subseteq X \times \mathbf{A}^1$ be a prime divisor of type 2. Localizing at the generic point of $X$, we get a prime divisor in $\text{Spec } K[t]$, which corresponds to a prime ideal $\mathfrak{p} \subseteq K[t]$. This is principal, so let $f$ be a generator. Then $f \in K(t)$, and the divisor of $f$ consists of $Z$ plus perhaps something purely of type 1. It cannot involve any other prime divisors of type 2. Thus $Z$ is linearly equivalent to a divisor purely of type 1.
