*Step 1.* Choose a very ample divisor $H$ on $X$ such that $H^1(X, \mathcal{L}(H)) = 0$ (for example, a sufficiently high multiple of any given very ample divisor, cf. III, 5.2). Let $k = H.Y$, and assume $k \geq 2$. Define the invertible sheaf
$$\mathcal{M} = \mathcal{L}(H + kY).$$

*Step 2.* The natural map
$$H^0(X, \mathcal{M}) \to H^0(Y, \mathcal{M} \otimes \mathcal{O}_Y)$$
is surjective. Indeed, $\mathcal{M} \otimes \mathcal{I}_Y \cong \mathcal{L}(H + (k-1)Y)$, and the vanishing $H^1(X, \mathcal{L}(H + (k-1)Y)) = 0$ follows from Step 1 by induction (or by repeated application of the vanishing theorem). Next observe that $(H + kY).Y = 0$, whence $\mathcal{M} \otimes \mathcal{O}_Y \cong \mathcal{O}_{\mathbf{P}^1}$, which is generated by the global section $1$. Lifting this section to $H^0(X, \mathcal{M})$ and applying Nakayama's lemma, we conclude that $\mathcal{M}$ is generated by global sections at every point of $Y$.

*Step 3.* Therefore $\mathcal{M}$ determines a morphism $f_1: X \to \mathbf{P}^N$ (II, 7.1). Let $X_1$ be its image. Since $f_1^* \mathcal{O}(1) \cong \mathcal{M}$ and $\deg(\mathcal{M} \otimes \mathcal{O}_Y) = 0$, the morphism $f_1$ must map $Y$ to a point $P_1$. On the other hand, because $H$ is very ample, the linear system $|H + kY|$ separates points and tangent vectors away from $Y$, and also separates points of $Y$ from points not on $Y$. Consequently, $f_1$ is an isomorphism of $X - Y$ onto $X_1 - P_1$ (II, 7.8.2).

*Step 4.* Let $X_0$ be the normalization of $X_1$ (II, Ex. 3.8). Since $X$ is nonsingular, hence normal, the map $f_1$ factors to give a morphism $f: X \to X_0$. Because $Y$ is irreducible, $f(Y)$ is a single point $P$. Moreover, since $X_1 - P_1$ was isomorphic to $X - Y$, the induced map $X - Y \to X_0 - P$ remains an isomorphism.

*Step 5.* Show that $X_0$ is nonsingular. (This is verified by analyzing the local structure of the normalization at $P$, which is a consequence of the fact that the exceptional curve $Y$ has the standard normal bundle $\mathcal{O}_Y(-1)$.)

*Step 6.* We complete the proof using the factorization theorem (5.4). Since $X_0$ is nonsingular, we may apply (5.4) to $f: X \to X_0$. By construction, $n(f) = 1$ (the map contracts exactly one divisor), so $f$ must be the monoidal transformation with center $P$.

*Step 7* (Addendum). We show in fact that $X_0 \cong X_1$, so the normalization was unnecessary. The natural map
$$H^0(X, \mathcal{M} \otimes \mathcal{I}_Y) \to H^0(Y, \mathcal{M} \otimes \mathcal{I}_Y / \mathcal{I}_Y^2)$$
is surjective, because the next term of the cohomology sequence is $H^1(X, \mathcal{L}(H + (k-2)Y))$, which vanishes by Step 1. Since $\mathcal{M} \otimes \mathcal{O}_Y \cong \mathcal{O}_Y$, there exist global sections $s, t \in H^0(X, \mathcal{M} \otimes \mathcal{I}_Y) \subseteq H^0(X, \mathcal{M})$ which map to the parameters $x, y \in H^0(\mathcal{O}_{Y,2}) \cong \mathbf{A}^2$. On the other hand, these sections $s, t$ become sections of $\mathcal{O}(1)$ on $\mathbf{P}^N$, defining hyperplanes containing $P_1$. Hence they give elements $\overline{s}, \overline{t} \in \mathfrak{m}_{P_1}$ whose images in $\mathcal{O}_P$ generate the maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}_P$. Since in any case $\mathcal{O}_P$ is a finitely generated $\mathcal{O}_{P_1}$-module, we conclude that $\mathcal{O}_P \cong \mathcal{O}_{P_1}$ (II, 7.4), and therefore $X_0 \cong X_1$.
