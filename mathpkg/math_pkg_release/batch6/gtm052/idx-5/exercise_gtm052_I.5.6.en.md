\textbf{Blowing Up Curve Singularities.}

\begin{enumerate}
\item[(a)] Let $Y$ be the cusp or node of (Ex. 5.1). Show that the curve $\tilde{Y}$ obtained by blowing up $Y$ at $O = (0,0)$ is nonsingular (cf. (4.9.1) and (Ex. 4.10)).
\item[(b)] We define a \textbf{node} (also called \textit{ordinary double point}) to be a double point (i.e., a point of multiplicity 2) of a plane curve with distinct tangent directions (Ex. 5.3). If $P$ is a node on a plane curve $Y$, show that $\varphi^{-1}(P)$ consists of two distinct nonsingular points on the blown-up curve $\tilde{Y}$. We say that "blowing up $P$ resolves the singularity at $P$".
\item[(c)] Let $P \in Y$ be the tacnode of (Ex. 5.1). If $\varphi: \tilde{Y} \to Y$ is the blowing-up at $P$, show that $\varphi^{-1}(P)$ is a node. Using (b) we see that the tacnode can be resolved by two successive blowings-up.
\item[(d)] Let $Y$ be the plane curve $y^3 = x^5$, which has a "higher order cusp" at $O$. Show that $O$ is a triple point; that blowing up $O$ gives rise to a double point (what kind?) and that one further blowing up resolves the singularity.
\end{enumerate}

Note: We will see later (V, 3.8) that any singular point of a plane curve can be resolved by a finite sequence of successive blowings-up.
