In Serre's approach, the theorem is first proved on $\mathbf{P}^1$ by explicit calculation of residues in terms of Laurent expansions at each point. The sum of residues of any rational differential form on $\mathbf{P}^1$ is shown to be zero by direct computation.

The general case is then obtained by using a finite morphism $f: X \to \mathbf{P}^1$ and studying the relationship between residues on $X$ and on $\mathbf{P}^1$. For a rational differential form $\tau$ on $X$, the pushforward $f_* \tau$ (or the trace map on differentials) relates the residues on $X$ to those on $\mathbf{P}^1$, and the vanishing of the sum of residues on $\mathbf{P}^1$ implies the same on $X$.

In Tate's approach, the residue theorem follows directly from the construction of the residue map using trace maps on infinite-dimensional vector spaces. Once one has the theory of residues, the duality theorem for $X$ can be proved by a method of Weil using repartitions (adeles). We refer to the lucid expositions of Serre and Tate for the details of this classic story.
