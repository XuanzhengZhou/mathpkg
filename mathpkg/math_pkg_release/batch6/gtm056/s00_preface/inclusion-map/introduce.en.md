The inclusion map $i \colon A \hookrightarrow B$ is the canonical embedding of a subset $A$ into its superset $B$, defined by $i(x) = x$ for all $x \in A$. It is the simplest example of an injective function and is uniquely determined once the subset relation $A \subseteq B$ is fixed. Inclusion maps are fundamental in topology and algebra for relating subspaces to ambient spaces, and they serve as the building blocks for many universal constructions such as pushouts, pullbacks, and exact sequences.
