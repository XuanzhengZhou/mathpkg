By the Tietze theorem, it suffices to check Tietze I and II. For Tietze I (adding a redundant relator $s$), the new row in the Alexander matrix is a linear combination of existing rows (since $s$ is a consequence of $\mathbf{r}$, and its derivatives are linear combinations of the derivatives of the $r_i$ after applying $\gamma$). For Tietze II (adding a new generator $y$ with relation $y\xi^{-1}$), the Alexander matrix gains a new column with the derivative of the new relation; the new matrix is equivalent via operations (iv) and (v) to the old matrix augmented with an appropriate unit, preserving elementary ideals.
