A non-autonomous Lagrangian system differs from an autonomous one by allowing the Lagrangian function to depend explicitly on time. Formally, the Lagrangian is defined on $TM \times \mathbb{R}$ rather than on $TM$ alone. This generalization is essential for describing mechanical systems with time-dependent constraints or external forces. In a natural non-autonomous system, both the kinetic energy $T$ (which becomes a time-dependent Riemannian metric on the configuration manifold) and the potential energy $U$ may vary with time. An important example is a bead on a rotating circle, where the time-dependent constraint leads to an effective potential with centrifugal contributions.
