Denote by $\alpha$ the angle of the orbit with a meridian. The velocity component in the azimuthal direction is $r\dot{\varphi}$, so

$$r\dot{\varphi} = |v| \sin \alpha,$$

where $|v|$ is the magnitude of the velocity vector.

For a free particle on the surface (no potential), the Lagrangian coincides with the kinetic energy. Since the Lagrangian is autonomous, the energy $H = L = T$ is conserved. Therefore $|v| = \text{const}$.

The coordinate $\varphi$ is cyclic (the Lagrangian does not depend on $\varphi$ due to rotational symmetry), so the conjugate momentum

$$p_\varphi = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{\varphi}} = r^2 \dot{\varphi}$$

is conserved.

Combining these conservation laws:

$$p_\varphi = r^2 \dot{\varphi} = r \cdot r\dot{\varphi} = r \cdot |v| \sin \alpha = \text{const}.$$

Since $|v|$ is constant, we obtain

$$r \sin \alpha = \text{const}.$$

This relationship shows that the motion takes place in the region $|\sin \alpha| \leq 1$, i.e., $r \geq r_0 \sin \alpha_0$. The inclination of the orbit from the meridian increases as the radius $r$ decreases. When the radius reaches the smallest possible value $r = r_0 \sin \alpha_0$, the orbit is reflected and returns to the region with larger $r$.
