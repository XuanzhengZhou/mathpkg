Noether's theorem is one of the most profound results in mathematical physics, establishing a fundamental link between continuous symmetries and conservation laws. If a Lagrangian system admits a one-parameter group of diffeomorphisms that preserve the Lagrangian (i.e., a continuous symmetry), then there exists a corresponding first integral -- a quantity conserved along the trajectories of the equations of motion. In local coordinates, this conserved quantity takes the form $I = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{\mathbf{q}}} \cdot \frac{d}{ds}h^s(\mathbf{q})|_{s=0}$. The theorem unifies diverse conservation laws such as conservation of momentum (from translational symmetry), angular momentum (from rotational symmetry), and energy (from time-translation symmetry in the extended phase space).
