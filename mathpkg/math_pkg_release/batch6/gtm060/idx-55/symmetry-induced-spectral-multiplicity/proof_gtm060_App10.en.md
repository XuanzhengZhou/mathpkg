Let the quadratic form (potential energy) be invariant under the orthogonal transformation $g$ of order 3. For any principal axis $\xi$ (eigenvector of the quadratic form with respect to the kinetic energy metric), applying $g$ yields another principal axis $g\xi$ with the same eigenvalue, since the quadratic form is $g$-invariant.

Two possibilities arise: either $g\xi = \xi$ (the axis is fixed), or $\xi$ and $g\xi$ are linearly independent. In the second case, the plane spanned by $\xi$ and $g\xi$ consists entirely of principal axes with the same eigenvalue, so the multiplicity is at least 2. The space spanned by $\xi, g\xi, g^2\xi$ is invariant under $g$ and is either 2-dimensional ($g$ acts by $120^\circ$ rotation) or 3-dimensional. In the 3-dimensional case, the sum $\xi + g\xi + g^2\xi$ is $g$-invariant and provides a principal axis, with the other two axes spanning a $g$-invariant plane of equal eigenvalues.

For the specific case of three identical particles on a line, the configuration space $\mathbb{R}^6$ decomposes orthogonally: the invariant lines are spanned by $\xi_1 + \xi_2 + \xi_3$ and $\eta_1 + \eta_2 + \eta_3$ (symmetric modes), and the invariant planes are their orthogonal complements (non-symmetric modes where the $120^\circ$ rotation sends each eigenvector to an independent eigenvector with the same frequency). The decomposition yields two independent symmetric characteristic oscillations and four non-symmetric ones, divided into two pairs of equal eigenvalue.

The same conclusion holds for any symmetric interaction law: decompose the configuration space into the orthogonal sum of the $g$-invariant subspace and its orthogonal complement; the potential energy splits as a sum of quadratic forms on each invariant subspace, and the characteristic oscillations are found separately in each.
