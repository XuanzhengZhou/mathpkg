D'Alembert's principle, stated in equilibrium form, reduces dynamical problems to problems of static equilibrium. By adding the inertia forces $-m_i \ddot{\mathbf{x}}_i$ to the actual forces acting on the system, the instantaneous configuration becomes an equilibrium configuration in the sense of virtual work. This elegant reformulation transforms the equations of motion into a condition that the sum of all forces (real and inertial) performs zero work on any virtual variation, unifying statics and dynamics within a single variational framework.
