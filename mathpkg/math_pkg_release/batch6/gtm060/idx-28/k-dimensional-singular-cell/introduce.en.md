A $k$-dimensional singular cell is the fundamental domain of integration on a manifold. It consists of a standard convex polyhedron $D$ in $\mathbb{R}^k$, a smooth parametrization $f: D \to M$, and a choice of orientation. The map $f$ may have self-intersections or folds — the cell is called "singular" precisely because $f(D)$ need not be a smooth embedded submanifold. Cells serve as the generators of chains, which are formal integer-linear combinations used as generalized domains of integration.
