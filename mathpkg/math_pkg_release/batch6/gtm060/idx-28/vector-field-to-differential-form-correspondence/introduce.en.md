In an oriented Euclidean three-dimensional space, the metric and orientation furnish the tangent space with inner product and triple scalar product structures. Using these, every vector field $A$ on a manifold gives rise to two differential forms: a 1-form $\omega_A^1$ defined by taking the inner product with $A$, and a 2-form $\omega_A^2$ defined by taking the triple scalar product with $A$. This correspondence is coordinate-independent and provides the bridge between classical vector calculus (gradient, curl, divergence) and the modern language of differential forms.
