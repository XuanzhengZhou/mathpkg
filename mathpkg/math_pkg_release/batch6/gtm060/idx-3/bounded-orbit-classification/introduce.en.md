Bertrand's theorem is a remarkable classification result in classical mechanics: the only central potentials for which every bounded orbit is a closed curve are the isotropic harmonic oscillator ($U \propto r^2$) and the Newtonian/Coulomb potential ($U \propto -1/r$). The proof proceeds by studying the apocentral angle $\Phi_{\text{cir}}$ for nearly circular orbits. One finds $\Phi_{\text{cir}} = \pi/\sqrt{\alpha + 2}$ for power-law potentials $U \propto r^\alpha$. Requiring $\Phi_{\text{cir}}$ to be independent of radius forces $\Phi$ to be constant. The limit $E \to \infty$ then forces $\Phi = \pi/2$, giving $\alpha = 2$ or $\alpha = -1$.
