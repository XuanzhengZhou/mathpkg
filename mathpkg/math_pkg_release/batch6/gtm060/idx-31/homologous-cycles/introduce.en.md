Two cycles $a$ and $b$ on a manifold $M$ are homologous if their difference forms the boundary of a higher-dimensional chain. Introduced by Poincaré, this equivalence relation partitions cycles into homology classes. A cycle homologous to zero is precisely a boundary. The invariance of integrals of closed differential forms over homologous cycles is the fundamental principle linking de Rham cohomology with singular homology, ultimately leading to de Rham's theorem.
