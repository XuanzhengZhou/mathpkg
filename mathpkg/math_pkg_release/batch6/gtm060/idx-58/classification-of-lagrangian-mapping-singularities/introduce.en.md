This classification theorem is the Lagrangian analogue of Whitney's theorem for general smooth maps, but the two classifications differ significantly. Lagrangian mappings form a much more restricted class (they come from generating functions), so singularities that are not typical for general mappings (like $A_4, D_4$ in dimension 3) become typical in the Lagrangian setting. Moreover, reducing a mapping to normal form via arbitrary diffeomorphisms of pre-image and image does not guarantee that the equivalence can be realized by a symplectic diffeomorphism — Lagrangian equivalence is a strictly finer notion. The theorem has direct applications to geometric optics (caustics of light), the calculus of variations (conjugate points), and the asymptotics of oscillatory integrals.
