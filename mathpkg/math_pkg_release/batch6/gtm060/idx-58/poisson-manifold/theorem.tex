A **Poisson manifold** is a smooth manifold $M$ equipped with a Poisson structure — a Lie algebra structure $\{\cdot, \cdot\}$ on $C^\infty(M)$ satisfying the Leibniz rule, such that for each $a \in C^\infty(M)$ the operator $\{a, \cdot\}$ is a derivation (and thus corresponds to a Hamiltonian vector field $\theta_a$).

A Poisson manifold decomposes into **symplectic leaves** — equivalence classes under the relation: two points are equivalent if they can be joined by a path consisting of segments of integral curves of Hamiltonian vector fields. Each leaf is a symplectic manifold, and the Poisson bracket restricts to the standard symplectic Poisson bracket on each leaf. The leaves may have different dimensions and are immersed submanifolds of $M$.

A classical example is the dual $\mathfrak{g}^*$ of a Lie algebra, with the Lie-Poisson bracket
$$\{f, g\}(\xi) = \xi([df_\xi, dg_\xi]),$$
whose symplectic leaves are the coadjoint orbits.
