Lagrangian singularities describe where the projection from phase space to configuration space fails to be a local diffeomorphism. When an $n$-dimensional Lagrangian submanifold of a $2n$-dimensional phase space is projected onto configuration space, the rank of the derivative may drop at certain points, creating singularities. The image of these singular points forms caustics — visible contours that are fundamental in geometric optics, wave asymptotics, and the calculus of variations. Arnold classifies these singularities by their stability under small Lagrangian perturbations, yielding a hierarchy of normal forms $A_k$, $D_k$, $E_k$ that govern the typical behavior of caustics in low dimensions.
