Galilean invariance of Newton's equation encapsulates the physical consequences of Galileo's principle of relativity. Requiring the equations of motion to be invariant under the Galilean group forces the force law to depend only on relative positions and relative velocities between particles, and to transform equivariantly under rotations. In particular, the force cannot depend on absolute time, absolute position, or absolute velocity. These constraints dramatically restrict the possible forms of mechanical laws and are foundational to the Lagrangian and Hamiltonian formulations of mechanics.
