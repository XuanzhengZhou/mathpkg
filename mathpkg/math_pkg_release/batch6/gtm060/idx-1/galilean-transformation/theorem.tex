Galilean transformations are affine transformations of $A^4$ which preserve intervals of time and the distance between simultaneous events. In the galilean coordinate space $R \times R^3$, every galilean transformation can be written in a unique way as the composition of a rotation, a translation, and a uniform motion $g = g_1 \circ g_2 \circ g_3$.

Three examples of galilean transformations of galilean coordinate space $R \times R^3$:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Uniform motion with velocity $v$}:
$$g_1(t, x) = (t, x + vt) \quad \forall t \in R, x \in R^3.$$

\item \textbf{Translation of the origin}:
$$g_2(t, x) = (t + s, x + s) \quad \forall t \in R, x \in R^3.$$

\item \textbf{Rotation of the coordinate axes}:
$$g_3(t, x) = (t, Gx), \quad \forall t \in R, x \in R^3,$$
where $G: R^3 \rightarrow R^3$ is an orthogonal transformation.
\end{enumerate}

The dimension of the galilean group is equal to $3 + 4 + 3 = 10$.
