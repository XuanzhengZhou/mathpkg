Newton's principle of determinacy states that the entire future (and past) motion of a mechanical system is uniquely determined by its state at any single instant of time — specifically, by the positions and velocities of all its constituent points. This principle justifies the use of differential equations to describe mechanical systems: given the initial positions and velocities, a unique trajectory exists. It is the classical expression of determinism in physics and forms the conceptual foundation for Newton's second law as a second-order ordinary differential equation.
