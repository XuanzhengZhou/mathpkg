Liouville's theorem is the cornerstone of integrable Hamiltonian dynamics. It states that for a system with $n$ degrees of freedom, knowing $n$ independent first integrals in involution is sufficient to reduce the system to quadratures — each such integral reduces the order of the system by two rather than one. Moreover, when the common level set of the integrals is compact and connected, it is necessarily an $n$-dimensional torus, and the motion on it is conditionally periodic with constant angular velocities. This result underpins the theory of action-angle variables and explains why so many classical mechanical systems (Kepler problem, rigid body motion, geodesics on ellipsoids) are explicitly solvable.
