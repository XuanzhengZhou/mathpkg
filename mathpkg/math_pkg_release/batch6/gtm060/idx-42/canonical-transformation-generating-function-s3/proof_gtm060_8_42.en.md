**Proof.** The proof of this theorem is almost the same as the one carried out above in the particular case $k = n$. We need only verify that the determinant

$$\det\left( \frac{\partial(P_i, Q_j)}{\partial(p_i, p_j)} \right)$$

is not zero for one of the $2^n$ sets $(P_i, Q_j, \mathbf{q})$.

Consider the differential of the transformation $g$ at the point $(\mathbf{p}_0, \mathbf{q}_0)$. By identifying the tangent space to $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ with $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$, we can consider $dg$ as a symplectic transformation $S: \mathbb{R}^{2n} \to \mathbb{R}^{2n}$.

From the discussion in Section 41, at least one of the $2^n$ null coordinate planes is transverse to $SP$. This means that one of the $2^n$ determinants is nonzero, as was to be shown.
