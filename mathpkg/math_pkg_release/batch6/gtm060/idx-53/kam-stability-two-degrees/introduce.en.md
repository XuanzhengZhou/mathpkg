This corollary of KAM theory provides a fundamental stability result: for Hamiltonian systems with two degrees of freedom, the action variables remain eternally close to their initial values under small perturbations. This follows from the topological fact that two-dimensional invariant tori partition three-dimensional energy level manifolds, preventing phase curves from escaping the gaps between tori. In contrast, for $n \geq 3$ degrees of freedom, $n$-dimensional tori do not divide $(2n-1)$-dimensional energy manifolds, so action variables may drift -- a phenomenon known as Arnold diffusion. This result explains the qualitative difference between two and three (or more) degrees of freedom in nearly-integrable systems.
