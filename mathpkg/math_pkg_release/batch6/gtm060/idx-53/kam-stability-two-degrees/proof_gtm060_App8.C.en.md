For $n = 2$ degrees of freedom, the phase space has dimension $2n = 4$, and an energy level manifold $H = \text{const}$ is $(2n - 1 = 3)$-dimensional. The invariant tori preserved by the KAM theorem are $n = 2$ dimensional, i.e., surfaces of dimension 2 embedded in a 3-dimensional energy manifold.

A 2-dimensional surface divides a 3-dimensional manifold (in the same way that a closed curve divides a surface, or a point divides a line). Therefore, a phase curve that begins in a gap between two invariant tori cannot cross either torus (by uniqueness of solutions of Hamilton's equations) and thus remains forever confined between them.

Since the tori correspond to fixed values of the action variables $I = (I_1, I_2)$, and the gaps between tori are small (the measure of the complement of the union of invariant tori is small when the perturbation is small), the action variables along any trajectory remain forever close to their initial values. This holds for all initial conditions -- both those on the preserved tori (non-resonant, comprising most of the phase space) and those in the resonant gaps.

In contrast, for $n \geq 3$, the $n$-dimensional invariant tori do not divide the $(2n-1)$-dimensional energy manifold (just as points do not divide a plane, or lines do not divide 3-space), so the "gaps" are connected and Arnold diffusion becomes possible.
