Let $q_{t+s} \in \Phi_{q_0}(t + s)$. Then there exists a path from $q_0$ to $q_{t+s}$ along which the time of travel of light equals $t + s$, and there is none shorter. Look at the point $q_t$ on this path, to which light travels in time $t$. The wave front $\Phi_{q_0}(t + s)$ and the front $\Phi_{q_t}(s)$ must be tangent at the point $q_{t+s}$. In fact, if they crossed each other, then it would be possible to reach some points of $\Phi_{q_0}(t + s)$ from $q_t$ in time less than $s$, and therefore from $q_0$ in time less than $s + t$. This contradicts the definition of $\Phi_{q_0}(t + s)$. Hence the fronts $\Phi_{q_t}(s)$ and $\Phi_{q_0}(t + s)$ are tangent at the point $q_{t+s}$, as was to be proved.
