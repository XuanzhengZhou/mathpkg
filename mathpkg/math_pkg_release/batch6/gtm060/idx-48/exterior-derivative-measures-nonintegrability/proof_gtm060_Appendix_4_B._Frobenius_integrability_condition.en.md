We show that the difference in heights of the two points obtained as a result of motions along the two sides of the parallelogram equals the integral of the $1$-form $\omega$ over the four sides of the parallelogram, up to a quantity of third order with respect to the sides.

The height of the rise of an integral curve along any path of length $\varepsilon$ emanating from the origin has order $\varepsilon^2$, since at the origin the plane of the field is horizontal. Therefore, the integrals of the $2$-form $d\omega$ over all four vertical areas over the sides of the parallelogram, bounded by the integral curves and the horizontal plane, have order $\varepsilon^3$ if the sides are of order $\varepsilon$.

The integrals of the form $\omega$ along integral curves are exactly equal to zero. By Stokes' formula, the increase in height along the integral curve lying over any side of the parallelogram equals the integral of the $1$-form $\omega$ along this side up to a quantity of third-order smallness. The theorem follows directly from the definition of exterior differentiation.

The $2$-form is well-defined independent of the coordinate system: if $\omega$ is replaced by $f\omega$ with $f(0) \neq 0$, then $d(f\omega) = f d\omega + df \wedge \omega$, which on the horizontal plane differs from $d\omega$ only by multiplication by the nonzero constant $f(0)$.
