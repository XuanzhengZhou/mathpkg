Consider a mapping $A$ obtained as the Poincaré return map of a Hamiltonian system near a resonant periodic trajectory. Its truncation $A_0$ (coming from the Birkhoff normal form truncated to leading resonant order) has saddle fixed points connected by separatrices forming the sides of an equilateral triangle (for resonance of order $3$).

For the full mapping $A$, each unstable fixed point $M$ has:
\begin{itemize}
\item A stable separatrix $\Gamma^+$ through $M$: points approaching $M$ under forward iteration $A^n$, $n \to +\infty$.
\item An unstable separatrix $\Gamma^-$ through the neighboring fixed point $N$: points approaching $N$ under backward iteration $A^n$, $n \to -\infty$.
\end{itemize}

The curves $\Gamma^+$ and $\Gamma^-$ both lie near the line connecting $M$ and $N$ from the truncated system, but they **do not coincide**. This is the phenomenon of **splitting of separatrices**. The magnitude of splitting is exponentially small in the frequency deviation $\varepsilon$: it is of order $e^{-1/\varepsilon^{n/4}}$ for a resonance of order $n$.

The intersections of $\Gamma^+$ and $\Gamma^-$ form a complicated network — a "lattice, tissue, or grid with infinitely fine mesh" (Poincaré) — giving rise to homoclinic tangles and chaotic dynamics.
