In the principal axis basis, the kinetic energy is:

$$T = \frac{1}{2}(I_1 \Omega_1^2 + I_2 \Omega_2^2 + I_3 \Omega_3^2).$$

Now take the angular velocity directed along a principal axis, say $\Omega = \Omega \mathbf{e}_1$ (so $\Omega_1 = \Omega$, $\Omega_2 = \Omega_3 = 0$). By the theorem on kinetic energy about an axis:

$$T = \frac{1}{2} I_{\mathbf{e}_1} \Omega^2.$$

Comparing with the principal-axis expression $T = \frac{1}{2} I_1 \Omega^2$, we obtain $I_1 = I_{\mathbf{e}_1}$. The argument is identical for $I_2 = I_{\mathbf{e}_2}$ and $I_3 = I_{\mathbf{e}_3}$.
