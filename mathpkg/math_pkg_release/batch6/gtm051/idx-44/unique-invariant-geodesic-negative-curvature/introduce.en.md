When the Gauss curvature of a surface is strictly negative, the invariant geodesic associated to a fundamental group element is unique up to a shift in the parameter. The proof uses the Gauss-Bonnet theorem: if two distinct invariant geodesics existed, they would form a nondegenerate geodesic quadrilateral whose interior angle sum would be $2\pi$ (since the covering transformation is an isometry mapping one edge to another), contradicting the Gauss-Bonnet bound of $<2\pi$ for negatively curved surfaces.
