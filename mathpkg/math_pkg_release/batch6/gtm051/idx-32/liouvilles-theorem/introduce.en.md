Liouville's theorem provides a complete characterization of geodesics on a surface $(U, g)$ endowed with a Liouville line element $ds^2 = (A(u) + B(v))(du^2 + dv^2)$. The function $\Phi(\dot{c}(t))$ is a first integral of the geodesic equation, constant along any geodesic. This theorem generalizes Clairaut's theorem (4.5.1) for surfaces of revolution, where $A(u)$ or $B(v)$ reduces to a single-variable function, to the broader class of Liouville metrics.
