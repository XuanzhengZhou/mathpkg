This definition extends the notion of differentiability from Euclidean spaces to manifolds. Given differentiable manifolds $M$ and $N$, a continuous map $F: M \to N$ is called differentiable if its local coordinate representation $v_\beta \circ F \circ u_\alpha^{-1}$ is differentiable as a map between open subsets of Euclidean spaces, for every pair of charts $(u_\alpha, M_\alpha)$ on $M$ and $(v_\beta, N_\beta)$ on $N$. The definition is well-posed because the chart transition maps are diffeomorphisms, ensuring that differentiability does not depend on the particular choice of atlases. A curve $c: I \to M$ is differentiable precisely when its coordinate representations are differentiable functions of a real variable.
