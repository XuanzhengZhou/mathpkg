There exists a surface $f: \mathbb{R} \times {]-1,1[} \to \mathbb{R}^3$ whose first and second fundamental forms are given by
$$g_{ik} = \delta_{ik},$$
$$h_{ik} = P_{ik}(u,v) \, e^{-((1 \pm v)/u)^2},$$
with
$$P_{11} = \frac{1}{1 \pm v}, \quad P_{12} = \mp \frac{u}{(1 \pm v)^2}, \quad P_{22} = \frac{u^2}{(1 \pm v)^3},$$
using the upper sign for $u \geq 0$ and the lower sign for $u \leq 0$. This surface satisfies:
1. The $h_{ik}$ are differentiable.
2. $h_{11}h_{22} - h_{12}^2 = 0$, hence $K = 0$.
3. The Gauss and Codazzi-Mainardi equations hold.
4. By the fundamental theorem of surface theory, such a surface exists and is unique up to isometry.
5. Nevertheless, this surface is **not** a developable surface near $(0,0)$: there is no change of variables $\phi: V \to U' \subset U$ with $(0,0) \in U'$ such that $f \circ \phi(s,t) = sX(t) + c(t)$.
