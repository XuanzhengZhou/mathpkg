In 1901, David Hilbert proved a landmark result in differential geometry: the complete hyperbolic plane $H_r^2$ of constant negative Gauss curvature $-1/r^2$ cannot be isometrically immersed as a $C^2$ surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$. This answered a natural question about whether every abstract Riemannian surface arises from an embedding in Euclidean space. Despite this global obstruction, every point of $H_r^2$ admits a local isometric embedding -- indeed, the pseudosphere provides a surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$ with constant negative curvature $-1/r^2$, though it is not complete. Hilbert's theorem highlights the essential distinction between local and global differential geometry.
