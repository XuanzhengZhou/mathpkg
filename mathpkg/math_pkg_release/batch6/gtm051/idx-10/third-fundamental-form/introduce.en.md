The third fundamental form $III$ of a surface is defined using the derivative of the Gauss map (the unit normal $n$) alone, in contrast to the second fundamental form which mixes $dn$ with $df$. As a bilinear form on $T_u \mathbb{R}^2$, it is given by $(X, Y) \mapsto dn_u X \cdot dn_u Y$. When viewed on the tangent space $T_u f$, it can be expressed as $III(X, Y) = L_u X \cdot L_u Y$, where $L_u$ is the Weingarten map. The three fundamental forms $I$, $II$, and $III$ are related: they satisfy the classical identity $III - 2H \cdot II + K \cdot I = 0$, where $H$ and $K$ are the mean and Gauss curvatures.
