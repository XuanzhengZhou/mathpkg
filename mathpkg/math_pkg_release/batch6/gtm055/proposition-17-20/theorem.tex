Let $\mathcal{E}$ be a Banach space and let $K$ be a compact subset of $\mathbb{C}$. The mapping

$$\Phi \rightarrow \int_{\alpha} \Phi(\zeta) d\zeta$$

is a bounded linear transformation of $\mathcal{C}(K; \mathcal{E})$ into $\mathcal{E}$ for each fixed finite formal sum $\alpha$ of rectifiable arcs lying in $K$, the norm of the transformation being less than or equal to the length of $\alpha$. If $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are two such formal sums, then

$$\int_{\alpha + \beta} \Phi(\zeta) d\zeta = \left( \int_{\alpha} + \int_{\beta} \right) \Phi(\zeta) d\zeta, \quad \Phi \in \mathcal{C}(K; \mathcal{E})$$

and if $T$ is any bounded linear transformation of $\mathcal{E}$ into a second Banach space $\mathcal{F}$, then

$$T \left( \int_{\alpha} \Phi(\zeta) d\zeta \right) = \int_{\alpha} T(\Phi(\zeta)) d\zeta, \quad \Phi \in \mathcal{C}(K; \mathcal{E})$$

The fundamental theorem of all complex analysis is the Cauchy–Goursat theorem (see Theorem 5.7 and Problem 5O). It is, consequently, gratifying to learn that the Cauchy–Goursat theorem is likewise valid for Banach space valued mappings.
