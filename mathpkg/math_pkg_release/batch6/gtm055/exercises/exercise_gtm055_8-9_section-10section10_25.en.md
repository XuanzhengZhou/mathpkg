More integration theory

In this chapter we treat some additional important topics in the theory of measure and integration. The first of these concerns another way that new measures can be constructed from old ones. If $(X, S)$ and $(Y, T)$ are measurable spaces, then a set of the form $E \times F$, where $E \in S$ and $F \in T$, is called a measurable rectangle in $X \times Y$. The $\sigma$-ring of subsets of $X \times Y$ generated by the collection of all measurable rectangles is denoted by $S \times T$, and the space $X \times Y$ equipped with the product $\sigma$-ring $S \times T$ is a measurable space called the product of $(X, S)$ and $(Y, T)$.

Example A. If $X$ and $Y$ are topological spaces, and if $B_X$ and $B_Y$ denote the $\sigma$-rings of Borel subsets of $X$ and $Y$, respectively, then $B_X \times B_Y$ is, in general, a subring of the $\sigma$-ring $B$ of Borel subsets of $X \times Y$. If $X$ and $Y$ both satisfy the second axiom of countability, then $B_X \times B_Y = B$. (In this connection see Problem A.)
