Proof. Suppose $\rho$ is a given invariant metric on $\mathcal{E}$, and let $v$ be defined as in (1). Then $v(x) > 0$ for all $x \neq 0$, and $v(-x) = \rho(-x, 0) = \rho(0, x) = \rho(x, 0) = v(x)$ for all $x$ in $\mathcal{E}$, because $\rho$ is invariant and symmetric. Likewise, if $x$ and $y$ are any two vectors in $\mathcal{E}$, then

$$v(x + y) = \rho(x + y, 0) \leq \rho(x + y, y) + \rho(y, 0) = v(x) + v(y).$$

This shows that $v$ is a value on $\mathcal{E}$. That the metric defined by $v$ on $\mathcal{E}$ coincides with $\rho$ follows at once from (1).

The topology induced by the metric defined by a value $v$ on a (real or complex) linear space $\mathcal{E}$ will also be said to be induced by $v$. The following elementary but important results concern this topology on $\mathcal{E}$ and the corresponding product topology on $\mathcal{E} \times \mathcal{E}$. (The reader must take care to distinguish between this notion of induced topology and the inversely induced topology introduced in Chapter 3 (see page 43).)
