The positive and negative variations $\mu_+$ and $\mu_-$ of a signed measure $\mu$ on a measurable space $(X, S)$ are measures on $(X, S)$, one of which at least is finite, and $\mu = \mu_+ - \mu_-$ setwise on $S$. Moreover, $\mu_+$ and $\mu_-$ are minimal in this respect in the sense that if $v_1$ and $v_2$ are any two measures on $(X, S)$ such that $\mu = v_1 - v_2$, then there exists a finite measure $\delta$ on $(X, S)$ such that $v_1 = \mu_+ + \delta$ and $v_2 = \mu_- + \delta$. If $\mu$ is finite or $\sigma$-finite, then so are $\mu_+$ and $\mu_-$.

Example J. Let $g$ be a real-valued function of bounded variation on a closed interval of real numbers $[a, b]$, $a \leq b$. (See Problems 1I, 1J, and 1K for definitions and terminology.) Then there exists a unique signed measure $\mu_g$ on the $\sigma$-ring of Borel subsets of $[a, b]$ satisfying the conditions

$$\mu_g((s, t)) = g(t-) - g(s+), \quad a \leq s < t \leq b,$$

$$\mu_g(\{a\}) = g(a+) - g(a), \quad \mu_g(\{b\}) = g(b) - g(b-).$$
