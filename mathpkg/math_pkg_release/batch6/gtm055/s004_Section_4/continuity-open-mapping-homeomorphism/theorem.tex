Let $X$ and $Y$ be topological spaces and $f: X \to Y$ a mapping.

$f$ is \textbf{continuous at} $x_0 \in X$ if for every neighborhood $W$ of $f(x_0)$ in $Y$ there is a neighborhood $V$ of $x_0$ in $X$ such that $f(V) \subset W$.

$f$ is \textbf{continuous} if it is continuous at every point of $X$, equivalently if $f^{-1}(U)$ is open in $X$ for every open set $U$ in $Y$, or equivalently if $f^{-1}(F)$ is closed in $X$ for every closed set $F$ in $Y$.

$f$ is an \textbf{open mapping} if $f(U)$ is open in $Y$ whenever $U$ is open in $X$.

$f$ is a \textbf{homeomorphism} of $X$ onto $Y$ if $f$ is a one-to-one mapping of $X$ onto $Y$ such that both $f$ and $f^{-1}$ are continuous, i.e., such that $f$ is both continuous and open. Two topological spaces are \textbf{homeomorphic} if there exists a homeomorphism of one of them onto the other.
