A measurable space $(X, \mathcal{S})$ consists of a set $X$ equipped with a $\sigma$-ring $\mathcal{S}$ that contains $X$ itself, making $\mathcal{S}$ a $\sigma$-algebra. The elements of $\mathcal{S}$ are the measurable subsets of $X$. This minimal structure is the foundation on which measures, measurable functions, and the entire theory of integration are built. The requirement that $X \in \mathcal{S}$ ensures that the whole space is measurable, which is equivalent to $\mathcal{S}$ being closed under complements.
