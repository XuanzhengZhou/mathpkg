The intersection of a nonempty family of rings [resp. $\sigma$-rings] is itself a ring [resp. $\sigma$-ring]. If $\mathcal{C}$ is any collection of subsets of a set $X$, then there exists a smallest ring [resp. $\sigma$-ring] of subsets of $X$ that contains $\mathcal{C}$, namely, the intersection of the collection of all those rings [resp. $\sigma$-rings] of subsets of $X$ that contain $\mathcal{C}$.

This ring [resp. $\sigma$-ring] is called the \textit{ring [resp. $\sigma$-ring] generated by $\mathcal{C}$} and is denoted by $\mathcal{R}(\mathcal{C})$ [resp. $\mathcal{S}(\mathcal{C})$].

The ring $\mathcal{R}(\mathcal{C})$ can be described directly in a fairly straightforward manner; the general description of the $\sigma$-ring $\mathcal{S}(\mathcal{C})$ requires a transfinite procedure.
