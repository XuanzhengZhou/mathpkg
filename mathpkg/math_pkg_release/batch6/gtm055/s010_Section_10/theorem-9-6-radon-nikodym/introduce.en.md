The Radon--Nikodym theorem is one of the central results of measure theory. It provides a converse to the elementary fact that indefinite integrals are absolutely continuous: under the hypothesis of $\sigma$-finiteness, every measure $
u$ that is absolutely continuous with respect to $\mu$ arises as an indefinite integral of a nonnegative measurable density function $f = d
u/d\mu$. This derivative is unique up to $\mu$-almost everywhere equality. The theorem underpins the theory of conditional expectation in probability and justifies the Leibniz-like notation $d
u = f \, d\mu$ for changing measures.
