Mutual singularity is the antithesis of equivalence for measures. Two measures $\mu$ and $
u$ are singular, written $\mu \perp 
u$, if they are supported on disjoint measurable sets. In other words, there exists a measurable partition $X = A \cup B$ such that $\mu$ is concentrated on $A$ and $
u$ is concentrated on $B$. Singular measures represent the extreme opposite of absolute continuity: a measure cannot be both absolutely continuous and singular with respect to another nontrivial measure. The Lebesgue decomposition theorem expresses any $\sigma$-finite measure as the sum of an absolutely continuous part and a singular part.
