If $\langle \mathcal{E}, \mathcal{F} \rangle$ is a dual pair and $M$ is an arbitrary subset of $\mathcal{E}$, then $(0(M^0))^0 = M^0$. If $A$ is an absolutely convex subset of $\mathcal{E}$, then $0(A^0)$ coincides with the closure of $A$ in the weak topology. In particular, if $A$ is an absolutely convex set in $\mathcal{E}$ that is closed in the weak topology, then $0(A^0) = A$.

If $\langle \mathcal{E}, \mathcal{F} \rangle$ is a dual pair, then the polar $M^0$ of an arbitrary subset $M$ of $\mathcal{E}$ is absolutely convex. It is natural to enquire when $M^0$ is also absorbing, or, what comes to the same thing, when the Minkowski function of $M^0$ is a pseudonorm on $\mathcal{F}$ (cf. Example 14F and the discussion preceding Proposition 14.7). The answer to this question involves a very important concept in the general theory of locally convex spaces.
