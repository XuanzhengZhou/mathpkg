Example K. If $U$ is a nonempty open subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$, then the topology of $D$-convergence introduced in Problem 11V turns the linear space $\mathcal{C}^{(\infty)}(U)$ into a Frechét space. Indeed, the results of Problem 11V show that $\mathcal{C}^{(\infty)}(U)$ is a metrizable locally convex topological linear space in the topology of $D$-convergence. To see that $\mathcal{C}^{(\infty)}(U)$ is also complete with respect to this topology, we employ Proposition 14.9 and note that if a sequence $\{f_p\}_{p=1}^{\infty}$ in $\mathcal{C}^{(\infty)}(U)$ is Cauchy with respect to all of the pseudonorms $\sigma_{K,m}$ that induce the topology of $D$-convergence, then every one of the derived sequences $\{D^{k_1}, \ldots, D^{k_n}f_p\}_{p=1}^{\infty}$ is convergent to some continuous limit in the topology of uniform convergence on compact subsets of $U$. But from this, via an easy mathematical induction, we conclude that if $\lim_p f_p = f$, then $\lim_p D^{k_1}, \ldots, D^{k_n}f_p = D^{k_1}, \ldots, D^{k_n}f$ for all nonnegative integers $k_1, \ldots, k_n$. (The central element in this argument is the fact, established in advanced calculus, that if a sequence $\{g_n\}$ of continuously differentiable functions of a single real variable converges uniformly to a limit $g$ on an open interval $(a, b)$, and if the derived sequence $\{g'_n\}$ is uniformly Cauchy on $(a, b)$, then $\{g'_n\}$ converges uniformly to $g'$ on $(a, b)$.
