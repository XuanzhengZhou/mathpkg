Suppose now, to go the other way, that $u, v \in \mathcal{C}^{(1)}(\Delta')$. Then, as is shown in advanced calculus (theorem on the total differential), there exist real-valued functions $\varepsilon', \varepsilon'', \eta',$ and $\eta''$, defined on the disc

$$D' = \{(x, y) : (x - x_0)^2 + (y - y_0)^2 < R\}$$

and tending to zero as $(x, y) \to (x_0, y_0)$, such that

$$u(x, y) - u(x_0, y_0) = [u_x(x_0, y_0) + \varepsilon'(x, y)](x - x_0)$$

$$+ [u_y(x_0, y_0) + \varepsilon''(x, y)](y - y_0)$$

and

$$v(x, y) - v(x_0, y_0) = [v_x(x_0, y_0) + \eta'(x, y)](x - x_0)$$

$$+ [v_y(x_0, y_0) + \eta''(x, y)](y - y_0)$$

identically on $D'$. If, in addition, the Cauchy–Riemann equations are satisfied at $(x_0, y_0)$ then straightforward calculation shows that

$$f(\lambda) - f(\lambda_0) = u_x(x_0, y_0)(\lambda - \lambda_0) + iv_x(x_0, y_0)(\lambda - \lambda_0)$$

$$+ [\varepsilon'(x, y) + i\eta'(x, y)](x - x_0)$$

$$+ [\varepsilon''(x, y) + i\eta''(x, y)](y - y_0).$$

Hence $f'(\lambda_0)$ exists (and equals $u_x(x_0, y_0) + iv_x(x_0, y_0)$), and the result follows.
