Let $X = (X, \leq)$ be a partially ordered set.

An element $x_0 \in X$ is \textit{maximal} [resp. \textit{minimal}] in $X$ if there exists no element $x \in X$ such that $x > x_0$ [resp. $x < x_0$].

If $E \subset X$ and $x_0 \in X$ such that $x \leq x_0$ for every $x \in E$, then $x_0$ is an \textit{upper bound} of $E$ and $E$ is \textit{bounded above}. If also $x_0 \leq y$ for every upper bound $y$ of $E$, then $x_0$ is the \textit{supremum} (or \textit{least upper bound}) of $E$, denoted $x_0 = \sup E$.

The dual notions of \textit{lower bound}, \textit{bounded below}, and \textit{infimum} (or \textit{greatest lower bound}), denoted $\inf E$, are defined analogously by reversing the inequalities.

A partially ordered set is \textit{bounded} if it is bounded both above and below.

For finite subsets $\{x_1, \ldots, x_n\}$ of a partially ordered set $X$, write
$$x_1 \vee \cdots \vee x_n = \sup\{x_1, \ldots, x_n\}, \quad x_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge x_n = \inf\{x_1, \ldots, x_n\}.$$
