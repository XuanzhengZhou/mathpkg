Suppose given a linear space $\mathcal{E}$, a directed set $\Lambda$ of indices, and a monotone increasing net $\{M_\lambda\}_{\lambda \in \Lambda}$ of linear submanifolds of $\mathcal{E}$ (in the inclusion ordering) such that $\mathcal{E} = \bigcup_\lambda M_\lambda = \sum_\lambda M_\lambda$. Suppose also that each $M_\lambda$ is equipped with a locally convex linear topology, and that these topologies are all coherent in the sense that $M_\lambda$ is topologically a subspace of $M_{\lambda'}$ as well as a linear submanifold of $M_{\lambda'}$ whenever $\lambda \leq \lambda'$ in $\Lambda$ (cf. Example 3L). Then the finest locally convex linear topology on $\mathcal{E}$ with respect to which all of the inclusion mappings $i_\lambda : M_\lambda \rightarrow \mathcal{E}$ are continuous (the indiscrete topology on $\mathcal{E}$ is one such) is called the inductive limit topology on $\mathcal{E}$, and $\mathcal{E}$ equipped with this topology is known as the inductive limit of the net $\{M_\lambda\}$.

The notion of inductive limit introduced here is more restrictive than the usual definition, and corresponds to what many authors refer to as the strict inductive limit.

It should be remarked that this inductive limit topology may equally well be described as the finest locally convex linear topology on $\mathcal{E}$ with the property that the relative topology induced by it on each of the submanifolds $M_\lambda$ is refined by the given topology on $M_\lambda$.
