If $\bar{\lambda} \in \mathcal{P}(K)$, then the subalgebra of $\mathcal{C}(K)$ generated by polynomials and $\bar{\lambda}$ is self-conjugate and strongly separating (it contains the functions $\lambda$ and $\bar{\lambda}$, which separate points, and the constant function $1$). By the Stone-Weierstrass theorem (Theorem 18.5), this algebra is dense in $\mathcal{C}(K)$. But since $\bar{\lambda} \in \mathcal{P}(K)$, this algebra equals $\mathcal{P}(K)$, hence $\mathcal{P}(K) = \mathcal{C}(K)$. Conversely, if $\mathcal{P}(K) = \mathcal{C}(K)$, then $\bar{\lambda} \in \mathcal{C}(K) = \mathcal{P}(K)$. The argument for $\mathcal{A}(K)$ is identical.
