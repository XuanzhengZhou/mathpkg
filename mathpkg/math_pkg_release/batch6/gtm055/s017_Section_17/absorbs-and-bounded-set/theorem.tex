If $M$ and $N$ are subsets of a linear space $\mathcal{E}$, then $M$ is said to \textbf{absorb} $N$ if there exists a positive number $k$ large enough so that $N \subset \lambda M$ for every $\lambda$ such that $|\lambda| \geq k$. A subset $A$ of $\mathcal{E}$ is absorbing (in the sense of Chapter 14) if and only if it absorbs every singleton.

A subset $B$ of a separated locally convex space $\mathcal{E}$ is \textbf{bounded} if it is absorbed by every neighborhood of the origin in $\mathcal{E}$.
