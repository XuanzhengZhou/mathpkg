As $(a)$ ranges over all principal fractional ideals, the numbers $w(x)$ form a multiplicative group. It suffices to prove that this group is finite, since any algebraic integer whose Galois conjugates all have absolute value $1$ and which belongs to a finite group must be a root of unity. The finiteness follows from two observations: the numbers have bounded degree (since they lie in the cyclotomic field), and the product of $w(x)$ and its complex conjugate equals the norm $N(x)$, which is an ordinary integer. By the Plancherel formula and Fourier inversion on the finite group $(\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z})^\times$, one shows that the Fourier coefficients are integers, implying finiteness of the set of possible values.
