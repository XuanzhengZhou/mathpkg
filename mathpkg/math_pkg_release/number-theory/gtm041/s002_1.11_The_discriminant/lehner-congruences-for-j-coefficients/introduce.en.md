In 1949, Joseph Lehner discovered that the Fourier coefficients $c(n)$ of $12^3 J(\tau)$ satisfy remarkable divisibility properties: $c(5n)$ is divisible by 25, $c(7n)$ by 7, and $c(11n)$ by 11. He also found congruences for higher powers of 5, 7, and 11, and later extended these results to the primes 2 and 3. These congruences are early examples of the deep $p$-adic properties of modular form coefficients, foreshadowing the theory of Serre and Swinnerton-Dyer on congruences for modular forms.
