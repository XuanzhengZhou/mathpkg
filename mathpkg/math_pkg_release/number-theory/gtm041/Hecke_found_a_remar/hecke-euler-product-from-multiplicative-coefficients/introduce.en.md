Hecke discovered that if the Fourier coefficients $c(n)$ of a modular form satisfy a certain multiplicative convolution identity, the associated Dirichlet series $\varphi(s) = \sum c(n)n^{-s}$ factors as an Euler product over primes. The multiplicative property $c(m)c(n) = \sum_{d|(m,n)} d^{2k-1}c(mn/d^2)$ implies, for each prime $p$, the recurrence $c(p)c(p^n) = c(p^{n+1}) + p^{2k-1}c(p^{n-1})$, which in turn yields the power series identity $(1 - c(p)x + p^{2k-1}x^2) \sum_{n=0}^\infty c(p^n)x^n = 1$. Substituting $x = p^{-s}$ gives the Euler factor $(1 - c(p)p^{-s} + p^{2k-1-2s})^{-1}$. This theorem bridges the additive Fourier theory of modular forms with the multiplicative theory of $L$-functions.
