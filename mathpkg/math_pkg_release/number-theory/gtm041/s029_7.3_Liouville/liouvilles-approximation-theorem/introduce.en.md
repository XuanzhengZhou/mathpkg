Liouville's approximation theorem is a fundamental result in Diophantine approximation that provides a lower bound on how well an algebraic number can be approximated by rational numbers. Specifically, if $\theta$ is a real algebraic number of degree $n \geq 2$, there exists a constant $C(\theta) > 0$ such that $|\theta - h/k| > C(\theta)/k^n$ for all rationals $h/k$ with $k > 0$. This theorem is historically significant as the first result that allowed proving certain numbers are transcendental, by showing that numbers which are too well approximated by rationals cannot be algebraic.
