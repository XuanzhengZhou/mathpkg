The three-square theorem characterizes exactly which positive integers can be expressed as $x_1^2 + x_2^2 + x_3^2$ with $x_i \in \mathbf{Z}$. The only obstruction is that $n$ cannot be of the form $4^a(8b-1)$. The proof combines the Hasse-Minkowski local-global principle (Theorem 8) with the Davenport-Cassels lemma (Lemma B), which bridges the gap between rational and integral representations for the form $X_1^2 + X_2^2 + X_3^2$. This is a classical result going back to Legendre and Gauss.
