Not every formal combination $(n, d, \varepsilon)$ corresponds to an actual quadratic form. For $n = 2$, one cannot have simultaneously $d = -1$ and $\varepsilon = -1$. For $n = 1$, one must have $\varepsilon = 1$. For $n \geq 3$, all combinations are realizable. The proof constructs forms explicitly using diagonal forms.
