Since $V$ and $V'$ are isomorphic, we can suppose that $V = V'$. Moreover, by applying Theorem 2, we are reduced to the case where $U$ is nondegenerate. We argue then by induction on $\dim U$.

If $\dim U = 1$, $U$ is generated by a nonisotropic element $x$; if $y = s(x)$, we have $y \cdot y = x \cdot x$. One can choose $\varepsilon = \pm 1$ such that $x + \varepsilon y$ is not isotropic; otherwise, we would have

$$2x \cdot x + 2x \cdot y = 2x \cdot x - 2x \cdot y = 0$$

which would imply $x \cdot x = 0$. Choose such an $\varepsilon$, and let $H$ be the orthogonal complement of $z = x + \varepsilon y$; we have $V = kz \;\hat{\oplus}\; H$. Let $\sigma$ be the "symmetry with respect to $H$", i.e., the automorphism of $V$ which is the identity on $H$ and which changes $z$ to $-z$. Since $x - \varepsilon y$ is contained in $H$, we have

$$\sigma(x - \varepsilon y) = x - \varepsilon y \quad\text{and}\quad \sigma(x + \varepsilon y) = -x - \varepsilon y,$$

hence $\sigma(x) = -\varepsilon y$, and the automorphism $-\varepsilon\sigma$ extends $s$.

If $\dim U > 1$, we decompose $U$ in the form $U_1 \hat{\oplus} U_2$, with $U_1, U_2 \neq 0$. By the inductive hypothesis, the restriction $s_1$ of $s$ to $U_1$ extends to an automorphism $\sigma_1$ of $V$; after replacing $s$ by $\sigma_1^{-1} \circ s$, one can thus suppose that $s$ is the identity on $U_1$. Then the morphism $s$ carries $U_2$ into the orthogonal complement $U_1^0$ of $U_1$, and one applies the inductive hypothesis to $U_1^0$.
