The proof proceeds by constructing an element $e_x = e'_1 + x e'_2$ (with $x \in k$) that together with $e_1$ generates a nondegenerate plane. One computes:

$$e_x \cdot e_x = e'_1 \cdot e'_1 + x^2(e'_2 \cdot e'_2).$$

We must choose $x^2$ distinct from $-(e'_1 \cdot e'_1)/(e'_2 \cdot e'_2)$. Moreover, for $e_x$ to generate with $e_1$ a nondegenerate plane, it is necessary and sufficient that

$$(e_1 \cdot e_1)(e_x \cdot e_x) - (e_1 \cdot e_x)^2 \neq 0.$$

Making this explicit, taking into account hypothesis iii), the left hand side equals $-2x(e_1 \cdot e'_1)(e_1 \cdot e'_2)$. Hypothesis iii) implies $e_1 \cdot e'_i \neq 0$ for $i = 1, 2$. Thus $e_x$ verifies the conditions if and only if $x \neq 0$ and $x^2 \neq -(e'_1 \cdot e'_1)/(e'_2 \cdot e'_2)$. This eliminates at most three values of $x$; if $k$ has at least 4 elements, one can find such an $x$.

There remains the case $k = \mathbf{F}_3$ (the case $k = \mathbf{F}_2$ is excluded because $\operatorname{char}(k) \neq 2$). But then all nonzero squares are equal to 1 and hypothesis iii) can be written $(e_1 \cdot e'_1)(e'_i \cdot e'_i) = 1$ for $i = 1, 2$; the expression $(e'_1 \cdot e'_1)/(e'_2 \cdot e'_2)$ is thus equal to 1, and to realize the condition $x^2 \neq 0, -1$, it suffices to take $x = 1$.

Having chosen $e_x = e'_1 + x e'_2$ verifying the conditions, since $e_x$ is not isotropic, there exists an element $e'_2$ orthogonal to $e_1$ in the plane generated by $e_1, e_x$ such that $e_1, e'_2$ form an orthogonal basis of that plane. The basis $(e_1, e'_2, e_3, \dots, e_n)$ is then contiguous to $e$ (they share $e_1$) and has one more element in common with $e'$ than $e$ does. Repeating this process yields the desired chain.
