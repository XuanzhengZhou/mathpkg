This is a translation of Theorem 1 into the language of quadratic forms. Given $f$, let $(V,Q)$ be the associated quadratic module. By Theorem 1, $V$ admits an orthogonal basis $(e_1, \dots, e_n)$ with respect to which $Q(x) = a_1 x_1^2 + \cdots + a_n x_n^2$ where $a_i = e_i \cdot e_i$. Translating back, $f \sim a_1 X_1^2 + \cdots + a_n X_n^2$. The rank is the number of $i$ with $a_i \neq 0$, which equals $\dim V - \dim \operatorname{rad}(V)$, i.e., the rank defined earlier. The discriminant of $f$ is $a_1 \cdots a_n$ (modulo squares), and it is nonzero exactly when all $a_i \neq 0$, i.e., when the radical is trivial.
