For $\Re(s) > 1$, the Euler product for $L(s,\chi)$ yields
$$\log L(s,\chi) = \sum_{n,p} \chi(p)^n / n p^{ns},$$
where $\log$ is taken with the principal branch that vanishes as $s \to +\infty$ on the real axis. Splitting this into $n = 1$ and $n \geq 2$ terms:
$$\log L(s,\chi) = f_\chi(s) + F_\chi(s),$$
where $F_\chi(s)$ remains bounded as $s \to 1^+$ (the $n \geq 2$ terms converge absolutely).

Since $L(1,\chi) \neq 0$ and $\chi \neq 1$ (Theorem 1), $\log L(s,\chi)$ remains bounded as $s \to 1^+$. Hence $f_\chi(s) = \log L(s,\chi) - F_\chi(s)$ also remains bounded.
