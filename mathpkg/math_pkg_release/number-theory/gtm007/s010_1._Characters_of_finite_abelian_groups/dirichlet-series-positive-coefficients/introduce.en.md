Proposition 7 (also known as Landau's theorem for Dirichlet series) states that if a Dirichlet series with non-negative coefficients has an analytic continuation past a point on the real axis, then the series actually converges beyond that point. In other words, the abscissa of convergence of such a series is exactly the location of its first singularity on the real axis. This is the key tool Dirichlet used to prove $L(1,\chi) \neq 0$: the product $\zeta_m(s) = \prod_\chi L(s,\chi)$ has non-negative coefficients, so if it were holomorphic everywhere, it would converge everywhere — but it does not.
