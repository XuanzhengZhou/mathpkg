(a) Apply $T(n)$ to $f$ and compare the coefficients of $q = e^{2\pi iz}$. By Corollary 1 of Proposition 12, the coefficient of $q$ in $T(n)f$ is $\gamma(1) = c(n)$. On the other hand, since $T(n)f = \lambda(n)f$, the coefficient of $q$ in $\lambda(n)f$ is $\lambda(n)c(1)$. Hence
$$c(n) = \lambda(n)c(1) \quad \text{for all } n \geq 1.$$
If $c(1) = 0$, then $c(n) = 0$ for all $n \geq 1$, which would mean $f$ is identically zero (since $f$ is a modular form with all Fourier coefficients zero). This contradicts the hypothesis that $f$ is not identically zero. Therefore $c(1) \neq 0$.

(b) If we normalize $f$ so that $c(1) = 1$, the relation $c(n) = \lambda(n)c(1)$ becomes $c(n) = \lambda(n)$ for all $n \geq 1$.
