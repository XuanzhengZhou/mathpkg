# Proof of Theorem 81: Bound on Number of Prime Ideal Factors

**Theorem.** If a rational prime $p$ factors in the ring of integers of an
algebraic number field $K$ of degree $n$ as

$$p = \mathfrak{p}_1 \mathfrak{p}_2 \cdots \mathfrak{p}_r$$

(into not necessarily distinct prime ideals), then $r \leq n$.

**Proof.** Taking norms of both sides and using the multiplicativity of the
ideal norm (Theorem 79):

$$N(p) = N(\mathfrak{p}_1) N(\mathfrak{p}_2) \cdots N(\mathfrak{p}_r).$$

For a principal ideal generated by a rational integer, the ideal norm equals
the absolute value of the field norm. Since $p$ is rational,
$N(p) = |N_{K/\mathbb{Q}}(p)| = p^n$, where $n = [K : \mathbb{Q}]$ is the
degree of the field.

Each $N(\mathfrak{p}_i)$ is a positive rational integer greater than $1$
(since $\mathfrak{p}_i$ is a proper ideal). Therefore

$$N(\mathfrak{p}_1) N(\mathfrak{p}_2) \cdots N(\mathfrak{p}_r) = p^n.$$

Since each $N(\mathfrak{p}_i) \geq 2$ (in fact each is a prime power
$p^{f_i}$ with $f_i \geq 1$), the number of factors $r$ cannot exceed $n$.

This establishes a fundamental connection between the degree of the field and
the factorization of rational primes: if a rational prime decomposes into $k$
ideal factors, then the degree of the field is at least $k$.
