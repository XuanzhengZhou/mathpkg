In the rational number field the introduction of this symbol would be superfluous, since there the number $\mu$ can always be assumed to be free of unnecessary square factors. But in other fields, where the class number is even, $\mu$ can have accessory square factors which can not be avoided.

With the help of the symbol $Q$, the zeta-function of $K$ can be expressed through that of $k$ and an additional series, as was shown in §49 for the quadratic fields. For if $P$ denotes a prime ideal of $K$ then, in the notation of Theorem 108, the relative norm with respect to $k$ is $N_k(P) = p$ or $p^2$ and $N(P) = n(P)$ or $n(P^2)$, where $p$ is the prime ideal in $k$ which is divisible by $P$. In the infinite product

$$\zeta_K(s) = \prod_P \frac{1}{1 - N(P)^s}$$

we extract those factors which can be derived from all prime divisors $P$ of a fixed $p$. For these factors we then have

$$\prod_P (1 - N(P)^s) = (1 - n(P)^s)(1 - Q(\mu, p)n(P)^s)$$

and hence

$$\zeta_K(s) = \zeta_k(s)Z(s)$$

$$Z(s) = \prod_P \frac{1}{1 - Q(\mu, p)n(P)^s} = \sum_a \frac{Q(\mu, a)}{n(a)^s}.$$
