# Proof of Theorem 44: Cyclicity of $\Re(p^\alpha)$ for Odd Primes

**Theorem 44.** If $p$ is an odd prime, then the group of residue classes modulo each power $p^\alpha$ is cyclic.

*Proof.* The order of this group is $h = \varphi(p^\alpha) = p^{\alpha-1}(p-1)$. We may assume $\alpha \geq 2$ (the case $\alpha = 1$ is the well-known cyclicity of the multiplicative group of a finite field). The primes dividing $h$ are $p$ and the prime divisors $q$ of $p-1$. By the basis theorem for finite abelian groups, $\Re(p^\alpha)$ is cyclic if and only if all basis numbers are equal to $1$.

**Step 1: The basis number belonging to $p$ is $1$.**

Let $e$ be the basis number which belongs to $p$ in $\Re(p^\alpha)$. Then $p^e$ is the number of solutions of

$$a^p \equiv 1 \pmod{p^\alpha} \tag{21}$$

which are incongruent modulo $p^\alpha$. By Fermat's theorem, each such $a$ satisfies $a \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$. We assume $a \neq 1$ and write $a = 1 + u p^m$, where $p^m$ is the highest power of $p$ dividing $a-1$; hence

$$m \geq 1, \quad (u, p) = 1. \tag{22}$$

From (21) it follows that

$$(1 + u p^m)^p \equiv 1 \pmod{p^\alpha}. \tag{23}$$

Expand the $p$-th power by the binomial theorem:

$$(1 + u p^m)^p = 1 + \binom{p}{1} u p^m + \binom{p}{2} u^2 p^{2m} + \cdots + \binom{p}{p} u^p p^{pm}.$$

For a prime $p$, all binomial coefficients $\binom{p}{k}$ for $k = 1, 2, \ldots, p-1$ are divisible by $p$. The first non-trivial term is $p \cdot u p^m = u p^{m+1}$. Each subsequent term $\binom{p}{k} u^k p^{km}$ for $k \geq 2$ contains a factor $p$ from the binomial coefficient and at least $p^{2m}$ from the power, giving at least $p^{2m+1}$. Since $m \geq 1$, we have $2m+1 \geq 3 \geq m+2$.

Therefore

$$(1 + u p^m)^p \equiv 1 + u p^{m+1} \pmod{p^{m+2}}.$$

For this to be congruent to $1$ modulo $p^\alpha$, we must have $m+1 \geq \alpha$, i.e., $m \geq \alpha-1$. Thus $a = 1 + u p^m$ with $m \geq \alpha-1$, meaning $a \equiv 1 \pmod{p^{\alpha-1}}$. Among such numbers, there are at most $p$ residue classes that are incongruent modulo $p^\alpha$ (namely $1, 1+p^{\alpha-1}, 1+2p^{\alpha-1}, \ldots, 1+(p-1)p^{\alpha-1}$).

Therefore $p^e \leq p$, so $e \leq 1$, hence $e = 1$.

**Step 2: The basis numbers belonging to the primes $q \mid (p-1)$ are also $1$.**

By Theorems 23 and 24, the elements of the group of residue classes modulo $p^\alpha$ can be represented in the form

$$A \cdot B$$

where $B$ runs through the $p-1$ classes with $B^{p-1} = 1$, and $A$ runs through the $p^{\alpha-1}$ classes with $A^{p^{\alpha-1}} = 1$. We thus need only verify that the subgroup of the $B$'s is cyclic.

Let $a$ be a primitive root modulo $p$. Since

$$a \equiv a^p \equiv a^{p^2} \equiv \cdots \equiv a^{p^{\alpha-1}} \pmod{p},$$

the number $b = a^{p^{\alpha-1}}$ is also a primitive root modulo $p$. Hence the numbers

$$b, b^2, \ldots, b^{p-1}$$

are pairwise incongruent modulo $p$, and therefore *a fortiori* pairwise incongruent modulo $p^\alpha$. Moreover, their $(p-1)$-th powers satisfy

$$(b^k)^{p-1} = (b^{p-1})^k \equiv 1^k = 1 \pmod{p^\alpha}$$

since $b^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p^\alpha}$ by Euler's theorem. Thus the group of classes $B$ is cyclic of order $p-1$, generated by the residue class of $b$.

**Conclusion:** Since all basis numbers are equal to $1$, the group $\Re(p^\alpha)$ is cyclic. $\square$
