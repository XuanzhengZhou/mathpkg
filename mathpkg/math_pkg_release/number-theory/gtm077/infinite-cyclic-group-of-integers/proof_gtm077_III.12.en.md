# Proof: The Integers as an Infinite Cyclic Group

The set of all integers (positive, negative, and zero) under composition by addition forms an infinite Abelian group $\mathfrak{G}$. We verify this and establish its cyclic structure.

**Group structure.** For integers $a, b$:
- $a + b$ is an integer (closure);
- $a + b = b + a$ (commutativity);
- $a + (b + c) = (a + b) + c$ (associativity);
- Cancellation holds: $a + b = a' + b \implies a = a'$;
- For each $a, b$ there exists $x = b - a \in \mathbb{Z}$ with $a + x = b$ (solvability of $a + x = b$).

The unit element is $0$: $a + 0 = a$ for all $a$.

**Cyclic nature.** The group is obtained by composing the element $1$ with itself: every integer $n \in \mathbb{Z}$ can be written as $n \cdot 1$ (where $n \cdot 1$ denotes $1$ added to itself $n$ times, interpreted as $0$ when $n = 0$, and as $(-1)$ added $|n|$ times when $n < 0$). Thus $\mathfrak{G}$ is the cyclic group generated by $1$.

**Torsion-free.** If $m \cdot a = 0$ for $m \neq 0$ and $a \in \mathfrak{G}$, then $ma = 0$ in $\mathbb{Z}$ forces $a = 0$. Hence $\mathfrak{G}$ contains no element of finite order other than the identity. This means $\mathfrak{G}$ is a torsion-free group with one basis element, i.e., an infinite cyclic group.

**Subgroup structure.** As a restatement of Theorem 2 from Chapter I in group-theoretic language: every subgroup of an infinite cyclic group is again cyclic. Concretely, the subgroups are precisely the modules $k\mathbb{Z}$ for $k \geq 0$, each consisting of all multiples of $k$. The subgroup $k\mathbb{Z}$ has index $k$ in $\mathfrak{G}$, and the factor group $\mathfrak{G}/k\mathbb{Z}$ is the cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}/k\mathbb{Z}$ of order $k$. $\square$
