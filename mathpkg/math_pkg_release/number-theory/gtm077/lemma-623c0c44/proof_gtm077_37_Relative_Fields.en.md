# Proof of Lemma (a)

If $\beta$ belongs to $\mathfrak{A}$, then, provided

$$\mathfrak{a} = (\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_q)_k \quad \text{and} \quad \mathfrak{A} = (\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_q)_K,$$

$\beta$ also belongs to $\mathfrak{a}$.

*Proof.* If an equation

$$\beta = \sum_h \Gamma_h \alpha_h$$

with integers $\Gamma_h$ in $K$ holds, then the equations

$$\beta = \sum_h \Gamma_h^{(i)} \alpha_h \quad (i = 1, 2, \ldots, m)$$

with the corresponding relative conjugates also hold. Multiplying these $m$ equations together and applying the fundamental theorem on symmetric functions, we obtain an expression for $\beta^m$ as a polynomial in the $\alpha_h$ with coefficients which are symmetric integral expressions in the $\Gamma_h^{(i)}$, hence integers in $k$. These coefficients $\gamma$, as symmetric integral expressions in the $\Gamma_h^{(i)}$, are integers in $k$. For $x_h = \alpha_h$ $(h = 1, 2, \ldots, q)$ we thus obtain a number from the ideal $\mathfrak{a}^m$. Consequently $\beta^m/\mathfrak{a}^m$ is an integer; hence $\beta/\mathfrak{a}$ is also an integer, that is, $\beta$ belongs to $\mathfrak{a}$.
