If $p$ is a prime which ramifies, then by definition the divisor $(p,*)$ satisfies $(p,*)^2 \sim I$, so the square of its class is the principal class. By the definition of an ambiguous class, this means the class of $(p,*)$ is ambiguous.

When $D > 0$, the divisor $(-1,*)$ also lies in an ambiguous class; this follows from the fact that $(-1,*)^2 = (1,*) = I$, since $N(-1) = 1$ and the divisor of $-1$ is principal.

Since the product of divisors whose classes are ambiguous yields a divisor whose class squares to the product of squares, which is also principal, any product $(p_1,*)(p_2,*)\cdots(p_k,*)$ of such divisors also lies in an ambiguous class.
