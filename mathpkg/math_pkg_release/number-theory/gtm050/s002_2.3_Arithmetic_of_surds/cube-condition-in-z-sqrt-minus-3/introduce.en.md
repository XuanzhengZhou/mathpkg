In the study of Fermat's Last Theorem for exponent $n = 3$, one encounters the Diophantine condition $p^2 + 3q^2 = \text{cube}$. Using the arithmetic of surds, this condition can be rewritten in the ring $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-3}]$: since the norm $N(p + q\sqrt{-3}) = p^2 + 3q^2$ is multiplicative, the requirement that $p^2 + 3q^2$ be a cube suggests that $p + q\sqrt{-3}$ itself might be a cube, i.e., $p + q\sqrt{-3} = (a + b\sqrt{-3})^3$ for some integers $a, b$. Euler attempted to prove this necessity using unique factorization in $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-3}]$, but his proof was fallacious because unique factorization does not hold in this ring.
