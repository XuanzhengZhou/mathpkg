This theorem, Statement (4) in Edwards' exposition of Euler's proof, is the central result in establishing Fermat's two-square theorem. It states that if two integers $a$ and $b$ are coprime, then every divisor of their sum of squares $a^2+b^2$ can itself be represented as a sum of two squares. The proof employs the method of infinite descent: assuming a counterexample exists, one constructs a strictly smaller counterexample, leading to a contradiction. This theorem, combined with the fact that every prime $4n+1$ divides some $a^2+b^2$ with coprime $a,b$, directly implies Fermat's theorem.
