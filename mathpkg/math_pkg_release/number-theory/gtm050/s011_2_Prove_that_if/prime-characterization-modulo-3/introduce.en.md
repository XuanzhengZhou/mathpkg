This proposition classifies primes with respect to representability by the quadratic form $a^2+3b^2$. Any prime $p \neq 3$ is either of the form $3n+1$ or $3n+2$ by the division algorithm modulo 3. Primes congruent to 2 modulo 3 cannot be represented as $a^2+3b^2$, because if $a^2+3b^2$ is not divisible by 3 then $a$ must not be divisible by 3, which forces $a^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$, and hence $a^2+3b^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{3}$. This simple modular obstruction shows that only primes of the form $3n+1$ (and the prime 3 itself) can possibly be represented in this quadratic form, which is a crucial step in Euler's analysis for the $n=3$ case of Fermat's Last Theorem.
