From the main theorem, if $Nh(\alpha) = p$ is prime, then $p \equiv 0$ or $1 \pmod{\lambda}$. The case $p = \lambda$ gives $p \equiv 0 \pmod{\lambda}$. In the case $p \neq \lambda$, the theorem establishes $p \equiv 1 \pmod{\lambda}$ through the existence of an integer $k$ satisfying $k^\lambda \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$ with $k \not\equiv 1 \pmod{p}$. By Fermat's little theorem, the multiplicative order of $k$ modulo $p$ divides $p-1$, and since this order is $\lambda$, we obtain $\lambda \mid p-1$, i.e., $p \equiv 1 \pmod{\lambda}$.
