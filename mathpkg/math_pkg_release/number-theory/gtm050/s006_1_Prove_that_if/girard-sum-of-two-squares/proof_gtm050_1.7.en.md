The necessity of Girard's conditions is proved by the lemma that if a prime $p = 4n+3$ divides $x^2 + y^2$, then $p$ divides both $x$ and $y$ (see Exercise 2 of Section 1.8). This implies primes of the form $4n+3$ must appear with even exponent in any number representable as a sum of two squares.

The sufficiency reduces, via the Brahmagupta-Fibonacci identity, to proving that every prime of the form $4n+1$ can be written as a sum of two squares. Fermat stated he could prove this rigorously. A complete proof of this theorem -- that every prime $p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$ is a sum of two squares -- is given in Sections 2.4 and 2.6 of the book.
