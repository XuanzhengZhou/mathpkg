Albert Girard (1595-1632) was the first to give necessary and sufficient conditions for a number to be representable as a sum of two squares, preceding Fermat's earliest known writings on the subject. The necessity of Girard's conditions can be reformulated as: if the quotient of a number by the largest square it contains is divisible by a prime of the form $4n+3$, then the number cannot be written as a sum of two squares. The sufficiency reduces, via the Brahmagupta-Fibonacci identity, to proving every prime of the form $4n+1$ is a sum of two squares, a theorem Fermat claimed to have proved rigorously.
