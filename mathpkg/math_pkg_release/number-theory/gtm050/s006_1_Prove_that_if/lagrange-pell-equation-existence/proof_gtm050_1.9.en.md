Lagrange's proof that Pell's equation $y^2 - Ax^2 = 1$ always has solutions for any non-square $A$ is outlined in the exercises following this section. The key idea is to show that the continued fraction expansion of $\sqrt{A}$ is periodic, and the convergents of this continued fraction provide solutions to Pell's equation.

Specifically, the proof shows that among the infinitely many equations $p^2 - Aq^2 = k$ produced by the cyclic method, the values of $k$ are bounded, so by the pigeonhole principle a value of $k$ must repeat. Using the multiplicative identity, one can then combine two occurrences of the same $k$ to produce a solution with $k = 1$.

Lagrange's proof (circa 1770) was the first to establish the completeness of the method that Wallis, Brouncker, and the ancient Indian mathematicians had used without proving it always terminates.
