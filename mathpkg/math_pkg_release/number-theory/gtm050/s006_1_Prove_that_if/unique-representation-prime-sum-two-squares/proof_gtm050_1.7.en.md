The proof that a prime $p = 4n+1$ has a unique representation as a sum of two squares (up to order and signs) is given in Sections 2.4 and 2.6. The proof relies on the fact that $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ is a unique factorization domain and that $p$ factors as $p = (a+bi)(a-bi)$ in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$, where the Gaussian prime factors are uniquely determined up to units.
