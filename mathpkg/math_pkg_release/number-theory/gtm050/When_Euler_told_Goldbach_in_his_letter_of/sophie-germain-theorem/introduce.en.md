Sophie Germain's Theorem is one of the first significant general results toward Fermat's Last Theorem. It provides a sufficient condition for Case I of Fermat's Last Theorem to hold for a given odd prime exponent $n$: the existence of an auxiliary prime $p$ such that (1) the congruence $x^n + y^n + z^n \equiv 0 \pmod{p}$ forces one of the variables to be divisible by $p$, and (2) $x^n \equiv n \pmod{p}$ has no solution. This theorem unified and generalized earlier ad-hoc arguments (such as those for $n = 5$ using $p = 11$) and was a crucial stepping stone toward Kummer's later theory of ideal factors and regular primes.
