This theorem generalizes the special case $n = 5$ (where $2 \cdot 5 + 1 = 11$ is prime) to any odd prime $n$ for which $2n + 1$ is also prime. The proof follows exactly the same algebraic argument as the $n = 5$ case, using the factorization of $x^n + y^n$ and the fact that the fifth powers modulo $2n + 1$ (or more precisely $n$-th powers) are limited to a small set of residues. Primes $p$ of the form $p = 2n + 1$ with $n$ prime are now called Sophie Germain primes, and this theorem shows that for such primes $n$, Fermat's Last Theorem reduces to proving the impossibility under the extra condition that one variable is divisible by $n$.
