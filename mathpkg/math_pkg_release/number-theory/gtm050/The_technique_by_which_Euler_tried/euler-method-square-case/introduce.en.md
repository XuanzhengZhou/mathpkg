Before tackling the case of cubes, Euler studied the simpler case of $x^2 + cy^2$ being a square. He stated that if the product of two numbers is a square, then either each factor is a square, or the factors are squares multiplied by the same number. While provable for ordinary integers (see Section 1.4 of the book), Euler freely applied this principle to numbers of the form $x + y\sqrt{-c}$. By setting $x + y\sqrt{-c} = (p + q\sqrt{-c})^2$, he derived the parametric formulas $x = cq^2 - p^2$, $y = 2pq$. However, the argument is not generally valid, as the relativity assumption on $p$ and $q$ does not guarantee the conclusion.
