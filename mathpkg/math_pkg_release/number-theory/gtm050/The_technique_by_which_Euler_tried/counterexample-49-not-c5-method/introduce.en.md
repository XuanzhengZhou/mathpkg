The example $49 = 2^2 + 5 \cdot 3^2$ provides a concrete counterexample to the generality of Euler's method for the square case. Euler's parametric formulas $x = cq^2 - p^2$, $y = 2pq$ cannot represent this solution because the resulting system of equations has no integer (or even rational) solution. This demonstrates that the natural assumption that $p$ and $q$ are relatively prime does not imply that $cq^2 - p^2$ and $2pq$ are relatively prime, and thus Euler's analogical extension of integer factorization principles to surds is not generally valid. The failure occurs for $c = 5$, while $c = 3$ (the case relevant to Fermat's Last Theorem) happens to work.
