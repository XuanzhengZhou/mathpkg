Euler employed an analogical reasoning principle: he assumed that if the product $AB$ of two relatively prime surds is a cube, then each factor must itself be a cube. This principle is provable for ordinary integers using unique factorization (as shown in Section 1.4 of the book), but Euler extended it to numbers of the form $a + b\sqrt{-3}$ without justification. In modern terms, the principle relies on unique factorization in the ring $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-3}]$, which happens to hold for this particular ring but fails for general $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-c}]$. Euler himself noted failures of his method for certain values of $c$, demonstrating his awareness of the limits of the analogy.
