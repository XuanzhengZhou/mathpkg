Let $A, B$ be elements of a commutative ring. If $AB$ is a cube, and if $A$ and $B$ are relatively prime (share no common non-unit factors), then it is asserted by analogy with the integers that $A$ and $B$ must themselves be cubes.

For integers $A, B \in \mathbb{Z}$, this statement can be proved rigorously. Euler applied the same principle to numbers of the form $A, B \in \mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-3}]$, treating them as if they satisfied unique factorization. While the conclusion is true in the special case $x^2 + 3y^2$ (i.e., for the ring $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-3}]$), the proof is different from the integer case and the statement is false for general rings $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-c}]$.
