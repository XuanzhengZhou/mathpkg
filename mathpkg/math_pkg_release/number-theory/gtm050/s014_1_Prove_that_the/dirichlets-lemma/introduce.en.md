Dirichlet's Lemma provides the crucial step in his proof that the first case of Fermat's Last Theorem for exponent $n = 5$ is impossible. The lemma gives sufficient conditions under which an element $P + Q\sqrt{5}$ of the quadratic integer ring $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{5}]$ whose norm $P^2 - 5Q^2$ is a fifth power can be expressed as a perfect fifth power $(A + B\sqrt{5})^5$. The key necessary condition turns out to be $5 \mid Q$, which Dirichlet proved is also sufficient when $P$ and $Q$ are relatively prime and of opposite parity. This lemma circumvents the failure of unique factorization in $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{5}]$, where the presence of units like $9 + 4\sqrt{5}$ (from Pell's equation $9^2 - 5 \cdot 4^2 = 1$) would otherwise allow non-trivial unit factors to appear.
