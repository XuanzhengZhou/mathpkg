Fermat's Last Theorem for exponent $n = 3$ states that the cubic Diophantine equation $x^3 + y^3 = z^3$ admits no solution in positive integers. This is the first nontrivial case of Fermat's famous conjecture beyond the Pythagorean triples ($n=2$). Leonhard Euler gave the first proof, using Fermat's method of infinite descent. Euler's original proof contained a subtle gap concerning unique factorization in the quadratic ring $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-3}]$, but his overall strategy can be corrected using arguments he himself developed for other problems. This theorem is historically significant as the starting point for the deep connection between Fermat's Last Theorem and algebraic number theory.
