**Proof.** Let $\{x, y\} \in Q_i$. Of the $n_i$ $i$th associates of $x$, precisely $p_{ik}^i$ of them are $k$th associates of $y$, for each $k = 1, \ldots, m$. Since $y$ itself is an $i$th associate of $x$ and $y$ is an $i$th associate of itself only in the sense that the pair $\{y, y\}$ is not considered (the relations $Q_i$ partition unordered pairs of distinct vertices), the $n_i - 1$ $i$th associates of $x$ other than $y$ are partitioned according to their associate class with $y$. Hence

$$\sum_{k=1}^{m} p_{ik}^i = n_i - 1.$$

If $j \neq i$, then of the $n_j$ $j$th associates of $x$, precisely $p_{jk}^i$ are $k$th associates of $y$, for each $k = 1, \ldots, m$. Thus

$$\sum_{k=1}^{m} p_{jk}^i = n_j.$$
