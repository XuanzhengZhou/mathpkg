Craig's Theorem establishes the equivalence of three ways to characterize the complexity of a theory's axiomatization. It shows that a theory is recursively axiomatizable if and only if it can be given an elementary (i.e., very effectively computable) axiom set, which in turn is equivalent to the set of Gödel numbers of its theorems being recursively enumerable. The proof constructs an elementary axiom set by closing the r.e. enumeration under iterated conjunctions of a given sentence, ensuring the Gödel numbers grow large enough. This result is a logical analogue of the equivalence between r.e. sets and domains of computable functions.
