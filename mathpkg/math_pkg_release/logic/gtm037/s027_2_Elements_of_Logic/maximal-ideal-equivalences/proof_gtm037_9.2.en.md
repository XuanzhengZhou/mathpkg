$(i) \Rightarrow (ii)$: Assume $I$ is maximal. Suppose $x \cdot y \in I$ but $x \notin I$ and $y \notin I$. Then the ideal generated by $I \cup \{x\}$ would be a proper extension of $I$, contradicting maximality. (Specifically, the ideal generated by $I \cup \{x\}$ consists of all $z$ such that $z \leq i + x$ for some $i \in I$; this would be proper since $y \notin I$ and $x \cdot y \in I$.)

$(ii) \Rightarrow (iii)$: Since $x \cdot -x = 0 \in I$, condition $(ii)$ implies $x \in I$ or $-x \in I$.

$(iii) \Rightarrow (iv)$: Define $f: A \to 2$ by $f(x) = 0$ if $x \in I$ and $f(x) = 1$ if $x \notin I$ (i.e., $-x \in I$). This is a well-defined homomorphism with kernel $I$, so $\mathfrak{A}/I \cong 2$.

$(iv) \Rightarrow (i)$: If $\mathfrak{A}/I \cong 2$, then $A/I$ has exactly two elements, so $I$ is proper and there can be no ideal strictly between $I$ and $A$, because any such ideal would correspond to an intermediate quotient, which is impossible when the quotient has only two elements.
