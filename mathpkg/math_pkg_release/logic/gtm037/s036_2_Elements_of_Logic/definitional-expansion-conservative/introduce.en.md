This theorem establishes that introducing defined symbols into a theory does not increase its deductive power with respect to the original language. If $(\Gamma', \mathcal{L}')$ is a definitional expansion of $(\Gamma, \mathcal{L})$, then any $\mathcal{L}$-sentence provable from $\Gamma'$ was already provable from $\Gamma$.

The proof constructs, for any model $\mathfrak{A}$ of $\Gamma$, an expansion $\mathfrak{A}'$ to $\mathcal{L}'$ by interpreting each new symbol according to its defining formula. This shows that $\Gamma'$ cannot prove anything about the original language that was not already a consequence of $\Gamma$.
