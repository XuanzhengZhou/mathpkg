A weak general logic is an abstract notion of a logical system, formalized as a class of quadruples consisting of a first-order language $\mathcal{L}$ (with no operation symbols and finitely many relation symbols), a set $\Gamma$ of sentences, an $\mathcal{L}$-structure $\mathfrak{A}$, and a sentence $\varphi \in \Gamma$ such that $\mathfrak{A}$ satisfies $\varphi$. The key requirement is that the set of sentences $\Gamma$ is uniquely determined by the language $\mathcal{L}$ alone. This definition provides the foundation for Lindström's characterization of first-order logic: any logic extending first-order logic that satisfies compactness and the downward Löwenheim-Skolem theorem must be equivalent to first-order logic.
