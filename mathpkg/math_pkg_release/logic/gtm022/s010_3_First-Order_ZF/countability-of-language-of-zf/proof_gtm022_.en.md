The language $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{S})$ is independent of the choice of the infinite set $V$ of variables used. For if $V_0$ is a countable subset of $V$, and $p \in P(V, \mathcal{R})$ has $\text{var}(p) = \varnothing$, then $p \in P(V_0, \mathcal{R})$. The result follows on recalling that there are no individual constants in the construction of $\mathcal{S}$. We may therefore suppose that $V$ is countable. Since $\mathcal{R} = \{\mathcal{I}, \in\}$, it follows that $P(V, \mathcal{R})$ is countable, and hence $\mathcal{S}$ is countable.
