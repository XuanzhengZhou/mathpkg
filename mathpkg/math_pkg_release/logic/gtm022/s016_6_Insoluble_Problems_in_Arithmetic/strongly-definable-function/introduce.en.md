Strong definability is the formal analog of representability in a first-order theory extending recursive arithmetic $N_0$. A partial function $f: U \to \mathbb{N}$ is strongly definable when there exists a formula $p(x,y)$ such that the theory proves $p(m,n)$ precisely when $f(m)=n$. This notion bridges computability theory and formal arithmetic, and is the key technical device used to prove that every partial recursive function can be represented within sufficiently strong arithmetical theories, leading ultimately to Godel's incompleteness results.
