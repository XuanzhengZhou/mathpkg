Theorem 1.16 establishes the fundamental equivalence between two seemingly unrelated concepts: a function is recursive (Turing-computable) if and only if it is diophantine (its graph can be defined by the existence of solutions to a polynomial equation). One direction---diophantine implies recursive---follows from the sequence number function and the algebraic characterisation of recursive functions. The converse requires showing that the class of diophantine functions is closed under the operations of composition, primitive recursion, and minimalisation, a deep result that underlies the negative solution to Hilbert's Tenth Problem: since there exist recursively enumerable but non-recursive sets, there can be no algorithm to decide solvability of arbitrary Diophantine equations.
