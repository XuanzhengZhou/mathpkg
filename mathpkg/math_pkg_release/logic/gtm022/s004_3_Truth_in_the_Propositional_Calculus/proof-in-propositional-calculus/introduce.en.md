A formal proof in the propositional calculus $\text{Prop}(X)$ is a finite sequence of propositions ending in the desired conclusion, where each step is either an axiom (from the three axiom schemes $\mathcal{A}_1, \mathcal{A}_2, \mathcal{A}_3$) or an assumption from $A$, or follows from two earlier steps by the single rule of inference, modus ponens (from $p$ and $p \Rightarrow q$ deduce $q$). This definition captures the syntactic side of logic: a proposition $q$ is deducible from assumptions $A$, written $A \vdash q$, if there exists a proof of $q$ from $A$. The three axiom schemes formalize the underlying logical assumptions common to every mathematical system, while modus ponens is the sole rule for deriving new statements.
