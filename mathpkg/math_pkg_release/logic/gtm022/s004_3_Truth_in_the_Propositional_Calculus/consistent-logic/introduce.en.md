A logic is consistent if the false proposition $F$ (the algebraic zero element, interpretable as "false") is not provable from the empty set of assumptions. In symbols, $\mathcal{L}$ is consistent if $\not\vdash F$. Consistency is the syntactic counterpart of the requirement that a logic should not derive a contradiction. In the propositional calculus $\text{Prop}(X)$, consistency is equivalent to the statement that the axioms are not self-contradictory under modus ponens.
