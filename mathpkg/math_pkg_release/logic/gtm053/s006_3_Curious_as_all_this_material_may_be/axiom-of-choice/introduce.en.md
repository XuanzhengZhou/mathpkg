The Axiom of Choice asserts that for any nonempty set $x$, there exists a choice function $y$ defined on $x$ that selects one element from each nonempty member $u \in x$. In other words, given any family of nonempty sets, one can simultaneously choose one element from each set in the family.

This axiom is the final axiom of ZFC (Zermelo-Fraenkel set theory with Choice). The belief that this axiom is true in the von Neumann universe $V$ is, as Manin notes, at least as justified as the belief in the existence of $V$ itself. After heated controversies in the early 20th century, the Axiom of Choice has become a standard working tool accepted by virtually all mathematicians. It is essential for many fundamental results, including the existence of bases for arbitrary vector spaces, Tychonoff's theorem, and the well-ordering principle.
