A formula in the language of arithmetic is called a $\Sigma_0$-formula (or $\Pi_0$-formula) if all its quantifiers are restricted (bounded).

For $n \geq 1$, a formula is $\Sigma_n$ if it has the form $\exists x_1 \forall x_2 \cdots Q x_n \, P$ where $P$ is $\Sigma_0$ and the quantifiers alternate $n$ times starting with $\exists$. A formula is $\Pi_n$ if it has the form $\forall x_1 \exists x_2 \cdots Q x_n \, P$ where $P$ is $\Sigma_0$ and the quantifiers alternate $n$ times starting with $\forall$.

A set $S \subseteq \mathbf{Z}^+$ is in $\Sigma_n$ (resp. $\Pi_n$) if it is definable by a $\Sigma_n$-formula (resp. $\Pi_n$-formula). The classes $\Sigma_n$ and $\Pi_n$ form the arithmetic hierarchy.
