The Strong Lefschetz Principle provides three equivalent ways to check the truth of a first-order sentence in the language of arithmetic over algebraically closed fields: it holds in the complex numbers $\mathbf{C}$, it holds in every algebraically closed field of characteristic zero, or it holds in the algebraic closure $\tilde{F}_p$ of the finite field $F_p$ for all but finitely many primes $p$. This principle formalizes the classical Lefschetz principle used by algebraic geometers, which states that algebro-geometric statements proven over $\mathbf{C}$ also hold over any abstract algebraically closed field of characteristic zero.
