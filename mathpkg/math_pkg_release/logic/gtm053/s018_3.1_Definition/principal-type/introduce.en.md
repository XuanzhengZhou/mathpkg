A principal type (also called an isolated type) is an $n$-type that is generated by a single formula $P(\bar{x})$: every formula in the type is a logical consequence of $P(\bar{x})$ modulo $T$. Principal types are always realized in every model of $T$ — the formula $P(\bar{x})$ guarantees the existence of a witness. The contrast between principal and nonprincipal types is the subject of the Omitting Types Theorem.
