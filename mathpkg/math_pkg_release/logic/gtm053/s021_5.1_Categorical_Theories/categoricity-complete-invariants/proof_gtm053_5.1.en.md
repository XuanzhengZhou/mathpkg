By definition, $\operatorname{Th}(\mathcal{A})$ is the set of all $L$-sentences true in $\mathcal{A}$. Suppose $\operatorname{Th}(\mathcal{A})$ is $\mu$-categorical. Then any $L$-structure $\mathcal{B}$ of cardinality $\mu$ with $\mathcal{B} \models \operatorname{Th}(\mathcal{A})$ is isomorphic to $\mathcal{A}$. Thus the pair $(\operatorname{Th}(\mathcal{A}), \mu)$ uniquely determines $\mathcal{A}$ up to isomorphism. In the language of invariants, this means that the first-order theory together with the cardinality forms a complete set of invariants: if two structures share the same invariants (same theory, same cardinality $\mu$), they are isomorphic. This is the strongest possible classification one can hope for in model theory, and it motivates why categoricity in power is the central notion in Shelah's classification program.
