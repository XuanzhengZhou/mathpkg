Suppose $A$ is unital without loss of generality.

(i) It is clear that $A_+$ is invariant under multiplication by (real) scalars $\geq 0$; thus it is a cone of vertex 0 (II, 1). To show $A_+$ is convex, it must be proved that $x_1, x_2 \in A_+$ implies $x_1 + x_2 \in A_+$.

Let $x_1, x_2$ and $t_1, t_2$ ($\in \mathbb{R}_+$) satisfy $t_j \geq \|x_j\|$ and $\|t_j e - x_j\| \leq t_j$ ($j = 1, 2$) in accordance with the lemma above. Then we have

$$\|(t_1 + t_2)e - (x_1 + x_2)\| \leq \|t_1e - x_1\| + \|t_2e - x_2\| \leq t_1 + t_2,$$

which implies $x_1 + x_2 \in A_+$ by part (b) of the lemma. This part also implies $A_+$ to be closed. Finally (b) of the lemma implies $\|x_1 + x_2\| e - (x_1 + x_2) \in A_+$, hence

$$\|x_1 + x_2\| e - x_1 = (\|x_1 + x_2\| e - (x_1 + x_2)) + ((x_1 + x_2) - x_1) \in A_+.$$

Another application of part (b) of the lemma shows that $\|x_1 + x_2\| \geq \|x_1\|$. By Chapter V, 3.1, $A_+$ is normal.

(ii) Let $x \in A_{\mathrm{sa}}$. Using Theorem 2.2, Corollary 1 again, the C*-algebra generated by $x$ and $e$ is isomorphic with $C(\sigma(x))$. But $\sigma(x) \subset \mathbb{R}$, and every real-valued continuous function on a compact subset of $\mathbb{R}$ can be expressed as the difference of two non-negative continuous functions. This shows that $x$ is the difference of two elements of $A_+$, so $A_+$ generates $A_{\mathrm{sa}}$.
