The implications $(a) \Rightarrow (b)$, $(a) \Rightarrow (c)$, $(a) \Rightarrow (d)$ are straightforward consequences of the definitions and are omitted.

---

**$(b) \Rightarrow (a)$:** This is immediate from Theorem 11.1. Take $(Y, d)$ to be the scalar field $\mathbb{K}$ with its usual metric. Let $X$ be an equicontinuous, $\sigma(E', E)$-compact subset of $E'$ (such a set forms a fundamental system of neighborhoods of $0$ in $E'_{c}$). The evaluation map identifies $E$ with a subspace of $\mathcal{C}_{\mathbb{K}}(X)$. Condition (b) says each sequence in $H$ has a $\sigma(E, E')$-cluster point in $E$, which is precisely a cluster point in $\mathbb{K}^X$ for the pointwise convergence topology. Theorem 11.1 then yields that $H$ is relatively compact in $\mathbb{K}^X$ and its closure lies in $\mathcal{C}_{\mathbb{K}}(X)$, hence in $E$. Thus $H$ is relatively weakly compact.

---

**$(c) \Rightarrow (a)$:** Note first that $H$ is bounded in $E$. Otherwise, there would exist a continuous real linear form $u$ on $E$ and a sequence $\{x_n\}$ in $H$ such that $u(x_n) > n$ for all $n \in \mathbb{N}$. The sets

$$H_n = \{x \in E : u(x) \geq n\}$$

form a decreasing sequence of closed convex subsets of $E$. Each $H_n$ meets $H$ (since $x_n \in H \cap H_n$), yet $\bigcap_n H_n = \varnothing$, contradicting (c). Hence $H$ is bounded.

Now consider $H$ as a subset of $\mathcal{C}_{\mathbb{K}}(X)$ with $X$ as in the previous step, and denote by $\overline{H}$ the closure of $H$ in $\mathbb{K}^X$. It suffices to show $\overline{H} \subset \mathcal{C}_{\mathbb{K}}(X)$. Assume some $g \in \overline{H}$ is not continuous on $X$. Construct, exactly as in the first part of the proof of Theorem 11.1, a sequence $\{t_0, t_1, t_2, \ldots\}$ in $X$ and a sequence $\{f_1, f_2, \ldots\}$ in $H$ such that the relations (1), (2), (3) of that proof are satisfied. Now let $H_n$ be the closed convex hull of $\{f_\nu : \nu \geq n\}$ in $E$. By (c), $\bigcap_n H_n \neq \varnothing$; pick $h \in \bigcap_n H_n$. Then $h$ satisfies the same inequalities as the cluster point used in the proof of Theorem 11.1 (replacing $f_m$ by suitable $f_m' \in H_m$), leading to the same contradiction. Hence $\overline{H} \subset \mathcal{C}_{\mathbb{K}}(X)$, and by Theorem 11.1, $H$ is relatively compact in $\mathcal{C}_{\mathbb{K}}(X)$, i.e., relatively weakly compact.

---

**$(d) \Rightarrow (b)$:** Since $H$ is bounded, each sequence $\{x_n\}$ in $H$ has a $\sigma(E'', E')$-cluster point $x^* \in E''$ by Theorem 5.4 (Alaoglu–Bourbaki). By (d), $x^* \in E$. It suffices to show that the restriction of $x^*$ to any equicontinuous, $\sigma(E', E)$-compact disk $X \subset E'$ is continuous. Suppose it is not; we may assume $0 \in X$ and that $x^*$ is discontinuous at $0$.

Let $B$ be an equicontinuous, $\sigma(E', E)$-compact disk in $E'$, and let $E'_C$ be the Banach space generated by the gauge of $B$. The mapping $x' \mapsto f$ where $f(x) = \langle x, x' \rangle$ for $x \in B$ is a norm isomorphism $\psi$ of $E'_C$ into $\mathcal{C}(B)$. By Theorem 7.9, the transpose $\psi'$ maps $\mathcal{C}(B)'$ onto the bidual $(E'_C)''$; in particular, each $x \in E$ is the image under $\psi'$ of a suitable $\mu \in \mathcal{C}(B)'$.

Let $\{x_n'\}$ be an equicontinuous sequence in $E'$ converging pointwise to $0$ on $E$ but such that $\langle x^*, x_n' \rangle \not\to 0$ (possible by the discontinuity assumption). Setting $f_n = \psi(x_n')$, the sequence $\{f_n\}$ is uniformly bounded on $B$. Since each $\mu \in \mathcal{C}(B)'$ is a linear combination of positive Radon measures on $B$, Lebesgue's dominated convergence theorem implies $\lim_n \mu(f_n) = 0$ for all $\mu \in \mathcal{C}(B)'$. Hence

$$\lim_n \langle x, x_n' \rangle = \lim_n \langle \psi'(\mu), x_n' \rangle = \lim_n \mu(f_n) = 0$$

for every $x = \psi'(\mu) \in E$. This contradicts the assumption that $x^*$ is discontinuous on $X$, completing the proof.
