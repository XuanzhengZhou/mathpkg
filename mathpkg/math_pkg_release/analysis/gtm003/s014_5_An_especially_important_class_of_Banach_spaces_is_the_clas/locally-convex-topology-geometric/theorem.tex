If $E$ is a vector space, a locally convex topology on $E$ can geometrically be defined by selecting a filter base $\mathfrak{V}$ in $E$, consisting of radial, convex, circled sets and such that $V \in \mathfrak{V}$ implies $\frac{1}{2}V \in \mathfrak{V}$. Since $\frac{1}{2}V + \frac{1}{2}V = V$ by the convexity of each $V$, the corollary of Chapter I, Proposition 1.2 implies that $\mathfrak{V}$ is a $0$-neighborhood base for a unique locally convex topology.

Conversely, every locally convex topology on $E$ can be so defined; for example, the family of all closed, convex, circled $0$-neighborhoods is a base at $0$.
