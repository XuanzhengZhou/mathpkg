Let $E$ and $E_{\alpha} (\alpha \in \mathbf{A})$ be vector spaces over $K$, let $g_{\alpha}$ be a linear mapping of $E_{\alpha}$ into $E$, and let $\mathfrak{T}_{\alpha}$ be a locally convex topology on $E_{\alpha} (\alpha \in \mathbf{A})$. The \textit{inductive topology} on $E$ with respect to the family $\{(E_{\alpha}, \mathfrak{T}_{\alpha}, g_{\alpha}): \alpha \in \mathbf{A}\}$ is the finest locally convex topology for which each of the mappings $g_{\alpha} (\alpha \in \mathbf{A})$ is continuous on $(E_{\alpha}, \mathfrak{T}_{\alpha})$ into $E$.

The class $\mathcal{T}$ of locally convex topologies on $E$ for which all $g_{\alpha}$ are continuous is nonempty (it contains the trivial topology); the upper bound $\mathfrak{T}$ of $\mathcal{T}$ in the lattice of topologies on $E$ is a locally convex topology for which all $g_{\alpha}$ are continuous, hence $\mathfrak{T}$ is the inductive topology. As an upper bound, $\mathfrak{T}$ is also a projective topology with respect to the family $\mathcal{T}$ and the identity map on $E$. $\mathfrak{T}$ need not be separated, even if all $\mathfrak{T}_{\alpha}$ are separated.
