The result follows immediately from the definition of the product topology and the normality condition. A neighborhood base of $0$ in $L = \prod_\alpha L_\alpha$ is given by sets of the form $U = \prod_\alpha U_\alpha$, where $U_\alpha$ is a $0$-neighborhood in $L_\alpha$ and $U_\alpha = L_\alpha$ for all but finitely many $\alpha$. Since $[U] = \prod_\alpha [U_\alpha]$, the condition $\mathfrak{U} = [\mathfrak{U}]$ in $L$ is equivalent to $\mathfrak{U}_\alpha = [\mathfrak{U}_\alpha]$ in each $L_\alpha$. Thus $C$ is normal in $L$ if and only if each $C_\alpha$ is normal in $L_\alpha$.
