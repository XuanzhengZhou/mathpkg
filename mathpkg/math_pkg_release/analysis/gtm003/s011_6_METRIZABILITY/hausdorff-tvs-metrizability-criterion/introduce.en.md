A Hausdorff topological vector space is metrizable precisely when the origin has a countable neighborhood base. The theorem goes further: from any countable 0-neighborhood base one can construct a real-valued function $x \mapsto |x|$ on the space that behaves like a norm---it is subadditive, satisfies $|\lambda x| \leq |x|$ for $|\lambda| \leq 1$, and vanishes only at zero---and whose associated translation-invariant metric $(x, y) \mapsto |x - y|$ generates both the topology and the t.v.s. uniformity. This function is sometimes called an $F$-norm or pseudo-norm. The construction is non-trivial and uses the dyadic rational weights of finite subsets of the neighborhoods to ensure the triangle inequality.
