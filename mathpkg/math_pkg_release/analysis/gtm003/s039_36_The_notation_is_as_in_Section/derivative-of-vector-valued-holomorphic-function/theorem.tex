Let $E$ be a locally convex space over $\mathbb{C}$ and let $G \subset \mathbb{C}$ be an open set. A function $f: G \rightarrow E$ is called \emph{holomorphic} at $\zeta \in G$ if the limit

$$\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(\zeta + h) - f(\zeta)}{h}$$

exists in $E$, and the linear form

$$x' \mapsto \left\langle \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(\zeta + h) - f(\zeta)}{h}, x' \right\rangle$$

is $\sigma(E', E)$-continuous. The unique element $x(\zeta) \in E$ representing this linear form is called the \emph{derivative} of $f$ at $\zeta$, and is usually denoted by $f'(\zeta)$.
