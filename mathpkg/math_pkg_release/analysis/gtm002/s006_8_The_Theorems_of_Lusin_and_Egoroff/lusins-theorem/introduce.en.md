Lusin's theorem establishes the precise relationship between measurability and continuity: a function is Lebesgue measurable exactly when it can be made continuous by removing a set of arbitrarily small measure. This is one of the cornerstones of real analysis, showing that measurable functions, while potentially very irregular, are "nearly continuous" in the sense of measure. The theorem complements Theorem 8.1, which gives the analogous characterization for the property of Baire using first-category sets instead of small-measure sets.
