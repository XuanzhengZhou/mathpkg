An algebraic number is a complex number that can be expressed as a root of a non-zero polynomial equation with integer coefficients. This concept generalizes rational numbers (which are algebraic of degree 1) and includes many irrational numbers such as $\sqrt{2}$. The set of algebraic numbers forms a field and is countable, making them sparse among the real numbers. The existence of transcendental numbers -- real numbers that are not algebraic -- was first established by Cantor via a cardinality argument.
