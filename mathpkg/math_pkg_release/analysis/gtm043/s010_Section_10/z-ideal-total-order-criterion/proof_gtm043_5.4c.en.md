By the order criterion (5.4(a)), $I(f) \geq 0$ if and only if $f$ is nonnegative on some zero-set of $I$. To establish total order, it suffices that every element be comparable with $0$. Hence $C/I$ is totally ordered if and only if for every $f \in C(X)$, either $f$ or $-f$ is nonnegative on some zero-set of $I$ — that is, $f$ does not change sign on some zero-set of $I$.

By Theorem 2.9, a $z$-ideal $I$ is prime if and only if every zero-set of $I$ meets every pair of completely separated zero-sets. Equivalently, for every $f$, there is a zero-set of $I$ on which $f$ does not change sign. Thus $C/I$ is totally ordered if and only if $I$ is prime.
