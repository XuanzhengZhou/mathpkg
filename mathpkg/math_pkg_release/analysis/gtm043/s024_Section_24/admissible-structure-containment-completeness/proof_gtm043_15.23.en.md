Any Cauchy $z$-filter relative to $\mathcal{E}$ is certainly a Cauchy $z$-filter relative to $\mathcal{D}$. Therefore, if $X$ is complete in $\mathcal{D}$, every Cauchy $z$-filter in $\mathcal{E}$ is Cauchy in $\mathcal{D}$ and hence fixed, so $X$ is complete in $\mathcal{E}$.

As an example, consider the structure $\mathcal{C}$ on $\mathbf{R}$. Since $\mathcal{C}(\mathbf{R})$ contains the metric $\psi_i$, it contains the standard uniform structure. Since $\mathbf{R}$ is complete in this structure, it is also complete in $\mathcal{C}$. (This gives another proof that $\mathbf{R}$ is realcompact.) In this example, $\mathcal{C}$ is far larger than the metric structure: every function in $C(\mathbf{R})$ is uniformly continuous in $\mathcal{C}$ (into $\mathbf{R}$ with the standard structure).
