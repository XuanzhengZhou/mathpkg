This follows directly from the one-one correspondence in 10.9(a). If $C(X)$ is a homomorphic image of $C(Y)$, then there exists a homomorphism $\mathfrak{s}: C(Y) \to C(X)$ whose image is all of $C(X)$. Since the image contains the constants on $X$, by 10.9(a) there exists a unique continuous mapping $\tau: X \to vY$ inducing $\mathfrak{s}$. When $Y$ is compact, $vY = \beta Y = Y$, so $\tau$ maps $X$ into $Y$. Moreover, since $\mathfrak{s}$ is surjective, $\tau$ must be an embedding (a homeomorphism onto its image). Conversely, if $Y$ contains a copy of $X$, the restriction map $C(Y) \to C(X)$ is a surjective homomorphism.
