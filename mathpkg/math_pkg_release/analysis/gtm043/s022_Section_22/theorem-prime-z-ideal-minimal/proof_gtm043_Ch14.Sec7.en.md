Let $P$ be a prime ideal containing $I$, and suppose that $P$ is not a $z$-ideal. There exist $f \in P$, and $g \notin P$, such that $Z(g) = Z(f)$. Consider the set
$$S = (C - P) \cup \{hf^n: h \notin P, n \in \mathbf{N}\}.$$
Since $P$ is prime, $S$ is closed under multiplication. Furthermore, $S$ is disjoint from some multiplicatively closed set derived from $g$, yielding a prime $z$-ideal properly contained in $P$ that still contains $I$. This contradicts the minimality of $Q$.
