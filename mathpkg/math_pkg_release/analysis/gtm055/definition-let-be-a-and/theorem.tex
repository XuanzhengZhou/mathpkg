Let $X$ be a set, and for each $\gamma$ in an index set $\Gamma$ let $f_{\gamma}$ be a mapping of $X$ into a topological space $Y_{\gamma}$. Then the coarsest topology on $X$ making all of the mappings $f_{\gamma}$ continuous is the topology inversely induced on $X$ by the family $\{f_{\gamma}\}_{\gamma \in \Gamma}$. (That such a topology always exists is an immediate consequence of Problem G. When no confusion is possible, this topology will simply be said to be induced by the family $\{f_{\gamma}\}$.)

If $\{f_{\gamma}\}$ is a family of mappings of a set $X$ into a family $\{Y_{\gamma}\}$ of topological spaces, as in the foregoing definition, and if $\mathcal{T}$ denotes the topology induced on $X$ by the family $\{f_{\gamma}\}$, then it is clear that a subbase for $\mathcal{T}$ is given by the collection of all sets of the form $f_{\gamma}^{-1}(U)$, where $U$ is an open set in $Y_{\gamma}$ and $\gamma$ runs through $\Gamma$. (More generally, if, for each index $\gamma$, $\mathcal{S}_{\gamma}$ is a subbase for the topology on $Y_{\gamma}$, then the set of all inverse images $f_{\gamma}^{-1}(W)$, $W \in \mathcal{S}_{\gamma}$, $\gamma \in \Gamma$, constitutes a subbase for $\mathcal{T}$.) Consequently a base for $\mathcal{T}$ is given by the collection of all sets of the form

$$f_{\gamma_{1}}^{-1}(U_{1}) \cap \cdots \cap f_{\gamma_{n}}^{-1}(U_{n})$$

where each $U_{i}$ is an open set in $Y_{\gamma_{i}}$ (which may be required to belong to a specified base for $Y_{\gamma_{i}}$) and $\{\gamma_{1}, \ldots, \gamma_{n}\}$ is an arbitrary finite subset of $\Gamma$.
