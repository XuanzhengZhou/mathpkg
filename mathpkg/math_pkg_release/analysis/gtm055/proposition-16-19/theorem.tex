If $\langle \mathcal{E}, \mathcal{F} \rangle$ is a dual pair, then, according to Proposition 16.19, there is a coarsest admissible polar topology on $\mathcal{F}$, viz., the topology induced by $\mathcal{E}$. There is likewise a finest admissible polar topology on $\mathcal{F}$, namely the topology of uniform convergence on all the weakly bounded subsets of $\mathcal{E}$. (The collection of all weakly bounded subsets of $\mathcal{E}$ is readily perceived to be admissible.) This topology is frequently called the strong topology on $\mathcal{F}$. Every admissible polar topology on $\mathcal{F}$ with respect to the dual pair $\langle \mathcal{E}, \mathcal{F} \rangle$ lies between the topology induced by $\mathcal{E}$ and the strong topology.

Example V. Let $\mathcal{E}$ be a Banach space with dual $\mathcal{E}^*$, and consider the dual pair $\langle \mathcal{E}^*, \mathcal{E} \rangle$. According to Problem 15Q a subset of $\mathcal{E}^*$ is bounded in the topology induced by $\mathcal{E}$ (i.e., is weak* bounded) if and only if it is bounded in norm. Hence the strong topology on $\mathcal{E}$ is identified as the topology of uniform convergence on the (norm) bounded subsets of $\mathcal{E}^*$. Thus the strong topology on $\mathcal{E}$ (with respect to the dual pair $\langle \mathcal{E}^*, \mathcal{E} \rangle$) is simply the norm topology.
