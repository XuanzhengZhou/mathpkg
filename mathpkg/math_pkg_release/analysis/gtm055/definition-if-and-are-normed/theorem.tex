If $\mathcal{E}$ and $\mathcal{F}$ are normed spaces, then an invertible element of $\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{E}, \mathcal{F})$ is also known as an equivalence between $\mathcal{E}$ and $\mathcal{F}$, and $\mathcal{E}$ and $\mathcal{F}$ are said to be equivalent if there exists an equivalence between them.

It is clear that the inverse of an equivalence and the product of two equivalences are also equivalences so that this relation possesses the defining properties of an equivalence relation.

Example N. Two finite dimensional Banach spaces are equivalent if and only if they possess the same dimension (see Problem 11J).

Example O. Let $\mathcal{E}$ be a linear space and let $\|\|_1$ and $\|\|_2$ be two norms on $\mathcal{E}$. Then $\|\|_1$ and $\|\|_2$ are equivalent norms (Ex. 11B) if and only if the identity mapping on $\mathcal{E}$ is an equivalence between $\mathcal{E}$ equipped with $\|\|_1$ and $\mathcal{E}$ equipped with $\|\|_2$.
