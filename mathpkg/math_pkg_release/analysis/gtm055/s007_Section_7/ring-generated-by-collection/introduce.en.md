For any collection $\mathcal{C}$ of sets, there exists a unique smallest ring $\mathcal{R}(\mathcal{C})$ (resp. $\sigma$-ring $\mathcal{S}(\mathcal{C})$) containing $\mathcal{C}$, obtained by intersecting all rings (resp. $\sigma$-rings) that contain $\mathcal{C}$. The generated ring has a constructive description via finite Boolean operations on elements of $\mathcal{C}$, while the generated $\sigma$-ring requires a transfinite construction, reflecting the essential role of countable operations in measure theory.
