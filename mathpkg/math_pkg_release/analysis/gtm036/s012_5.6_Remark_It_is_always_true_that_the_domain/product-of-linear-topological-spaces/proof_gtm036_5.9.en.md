The proof requires only a direct application of the definitions and the preceding results.

First, the product $\prod_{t \in A} E_t$ is a linear space under coordinate-wise addition and scalar multiplication. The product topology is generated by sets of the form $\prod_{t \in A} U_t$ where each $U_t$ is open in $E_t$ and $U_t = E_t$ for all but finitely many $t$.

To verify that addition is continuous: let $(x,y) \mapsto x+y$. A neighborhood of $x+y$ in the product contains a basic open set $V = \prod_t V_t$ with $V_t$ open neighborhoods of $x_t + y_t$ in $E_t$, and $V_t = E_t$ for $t \notin F$ (finite). By continuity of addition in each $E_t$, for $t \in F$ choose neighborhoods $U_t, W_t$ of $x_t, y_t$ with $U_t + W_t \subset V_t$. For $t \notin F$, set $U_t = W_t = E_t$. Then $\prod_t U_t$ and $\prod_t W_t$ are neighborhoods of $x$ and $y$ whose sum is contained in $V$.

Similarly, scalar multiplication $(a,x) \mapsto ax$ is continuous: for a basic neighborhood $\prod_t V_t$ of $ax$, with $V_t$ neighborhoods of $a x_t$, choose a neighborhood $N$ of $a$ in the scalar field and neighborhoods $U_t$ of $x_t$ such that $N \cdot U_t \subset V_t$ for $t \in F$, and $U_t = E_t$ otherwise. Then $N \times \prod_t U_t$ maps into the given neighborhood.

The statement about decomposition into a topological product follows from Lemma 5.10.
