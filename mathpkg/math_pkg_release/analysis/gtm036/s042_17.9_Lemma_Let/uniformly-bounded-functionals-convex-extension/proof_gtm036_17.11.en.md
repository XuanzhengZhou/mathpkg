Without loss of generality, one can assume that the sequence $\{f_n\}$ is in the polar $C^\circ$ in $E^*$ of $C$. Assume that for some $x$ in $\langle C \rangle^-$ it is false that $\lim_{n} f_n(x) = 0$. Then, if necessary by taking a subsequence, we can assume that, for some positive number $e$, $|f_n(x)| > e$ for all $n$. For each $i$, there is an $x_i$ in $\langle C \rangle$ such that $|f_n(x_i)| > e$ for each $n \leq i$. It is possible to choose $x_i$ in the form:

$$x_i = \frac{1}{\operatorname{card}(I_i)} \sum \{x_a : a \in I_i\},$$

where $I_i$ is a finite index set and $x_a \in C$ for each $a$ in $I_i$. Let

$$I = \bigcup \{I_i : i = 1, 2, \cdots\}, \quad A_k = \{a : a \in I \text{ and } |f_k(x_a)| > e/2\}.$$

Define $m$ as the counting measure on the finite subsets of $I$ restricted to the algebra generated by the $A_k$. Since $|f_n(x_i)| > e$ and $x_i$ is an average over $I_i$, it follows that $m(A_k) \geq \operatorname{card}(I_k)/2$ for large $k$, bounding $m(A_k)$ below by a positive constant. By Lemma 17.9, extract a subsequence $\{A_{k_j}\}$ such that every finite intersection has positive counting measure. Choose $a$ belonging to all $A_{k_j}$; then $|f_{k_j}(x_a)| > e/2$ for all $j$, contradicting that $f_n(x_a) \to 0$ for $x_a \in C$. Hence the assumed failure cannot hold, and $\lim_n f_n(x) = 0$ for all $x \in \langle C \rangle^-$.
