If the space is metrizable, then it is pseudo-metrizable and the metric $d$ satisfies $d(x,y)=0 \implies x=y$, which immediately yields the Hausdorff property: for $x \neq y$, let $r = d(x,y)/2 > 0$; the open balls $B(x,r)$ and $B(y,r)$ are disjoint neighborhoods.

Conversely, suppose the space is pseudo-metrizable by $d$ and Hausdorff. If $x \neq y$, the Hausdorff property gives disjoint open sets separating them. Since the pseudo-metric topology is generated by open $d$-balls, there exist $r_x, r_y > 0$ with $B(x,r_x)$ and $B(y,r_y)$ disjoint. This forces $d(x,y) \geq \min(r_x, r_y) > 0$, so $d(x,y)=0$ implies $x=y$, and $d$ is a metric.
