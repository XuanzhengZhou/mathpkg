The projective topology on $F$ is, by definition, the weakest topology making every $g_t$ continuous. Each $g_t$ vanishes on $N$, so each $g_t$ factors through the quotient $F/N$ via a map $\tilde g_t: F/N \to F_t$ with $g_t = \tilde g_t \circ \pi$, where $\pi: F \to F/N$ is the canonical projection.

The projective topology on $F$ is generated by the seminorms $p_t \circ g_t$ where $p_t$ ranges over the continuous seminorms of $F_t$. Since $g_t$ factors through $F/N$, these seminorms depend only on the coset in $F/N$. Hence the topology on $F$ is the pullback under $\pi$ of the projective topology on $F/N$ determined by $\{\tilde g_t\}$, together with the trivial (indiscrete) topology on the kernel $N$. This is precisely the product topology of $(F/N, \text{projective})$ and $(N, \text{trivial})$.

In particular, if $N \neq \{0\}$, the space $F$ is not Hausdorff, since points in the same $N$-coset cannot be separated. The assumption $N = \{0\}$ is therefore necessary and sufficient for the projective topology to be Hausdorff.
