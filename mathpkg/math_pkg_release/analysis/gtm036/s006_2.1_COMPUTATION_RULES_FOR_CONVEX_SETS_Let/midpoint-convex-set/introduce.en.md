A set is midpoint convex if it contains the midpoint of any two of its points. This is a weaker condition than full convexity, which requires containing the entire segment between any two points. Midpoint convexity arises naturally in the study of measurable functions (where convexity follows from midpoint convexity plus measurability) and in the theory of functional equations. For subsets of the real line, a midpoint convex set that is either open or closed is automatically convex.
