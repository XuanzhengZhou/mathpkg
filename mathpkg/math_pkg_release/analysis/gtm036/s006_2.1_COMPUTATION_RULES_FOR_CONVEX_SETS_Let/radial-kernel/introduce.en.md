The radial kernel of a subset $A$ of a linear space (also called the algebraic interior or core) consists of those points $x$ in $A$ from which one can move a small distance in any direction while staying inside $A$. Formally, $x$ is in the radial kernel if for every $z$ in the space there exists $r > 0$ such that $x + tz \in A$ for all $0 \leq t < r$. The radial kernel is a purely algebraic analogue of the topological interior, requiring no topology on the space. For convex sets the radial kernel exhibits particularly nice behavior, as shown in Theorem 2.2.
