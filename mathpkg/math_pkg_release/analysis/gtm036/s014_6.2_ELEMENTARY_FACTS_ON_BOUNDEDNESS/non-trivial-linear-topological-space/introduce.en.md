A linear topological space is called non-trivial (or not indiscrete) if its topology is not the indiscrete topology; that is, there exists at least one proper, non-empty open set. This definition is introduced to avoid degenerate cases in the statement of Theorem 6.10 on products of pseudo-metrizable spaces, since the indiscrete topology would trivially satisfy pseudo-metrizability conditions without reflecting the behavior of the coordinate spaces.
