For a discrete measure space $X$ with counting measure on finite or countable subsets, the general $L^p$ duality theorem applies. The space $l^p(X)$ is the $L^p$ space of this measure. For $1 < p < \infty$, the result follows directly from part (a) of the $L^p$ duality theorem.

For $p = 1$, the theorem follows from part (b) with the observation that on a discrete space with the stated $\sigma$-ring, locally measurable functions with respect to the counting measure are precisely the functions whose restriction to every countable set is measurable, i.e., all functions. However, functions that are bounded locally almost everywhere are those bounded on every countable set, which is $l^\infty(X)$.

The remark about non-measurability: if $X$ is uncountable and $\mathcal{S}$ consists of countable subsets, then the counting measure on $\mathcal{S}$ is not localizable (see Halmos [4] §31, (9)), and $L^\infty$ in the sense defined here yields functions that are locally measurable but not necessarily measurable in the usual sense.
