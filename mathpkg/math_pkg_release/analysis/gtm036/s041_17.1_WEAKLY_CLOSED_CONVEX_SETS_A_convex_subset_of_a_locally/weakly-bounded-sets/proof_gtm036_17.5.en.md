Each $\mathcal{T}$-bounded set is weakly bounded because $\mathcal{T}$ is stronger than $w$. Suppose that $B$ is a weakly bounded set and that $U$ is a $\mathcal{T}$-neighborhood of $0$; it can be assumed that $U$ is convex, circled and $\mathcal{T}$-closed, and hence that $U$ is weakly closed. It follows that $U$ is the polar of $U^0$, where $U^0$ is the polar of $U$ in $E^*$ (see computation rules 16.3). The Banach-Alaoglu theorem 17.4 shows that the polar $U^0$ is $w(E^*, E)$-compact. The polar of $B$ in $E^*$ is $w(E^*, E)$-closed and radial at $0$, and it follows from the absorption theorem 10.1 (or its corollary 10.2) that $B^0$ absorbs $U^0$. Finally, taking polars in $E$, it is clear that $U$ absorbs $B^{00}$ and therefore absorbs the subset $B$ of $B^{00}$.
