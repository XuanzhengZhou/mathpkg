The proof is left to the reader. One must verify the sub-mean-value inequality for $\varphi'$ at every point in $G$. For $z \in G \setminus \bar{B}(a; r)$, the inequality holds because $\varphi' = \varphi$ and $\varphi$ is subharmonic. For $z \in B(a; r)$, one uses harmonicity of $\varphi'$ together with the fact that $\varphi' \leq \varphi$ on the disk (by the Maximum Principle, since $\varphi - \varphi'$ is subharmonic and vanishes on the boundary). The sub-mean-value inequality for $\varphi'$ then follows from that of $\varphi$ and the mean value property of harmonic functions.
