This corollary follows from the preceding result (Corollary 3.5) that $M(G) \cup \{\infty\}$ is a complete metric space, together with the fact that limits of analytic functions are analytic (Weierstrass's theorem). 

From the discussion preceding the corollaries: if each $f_n$ is analytic then $\frac{1}{f_n}$ has no zeros in $B(a; r)$. By Corollary 2.6 to Hurwitz's Theorem, either $\frac{1}{f} \equiv 0$ or $\frac{1}{f}$ never vanishes. In the case where $f(a) = \infty$, $\frac{1}{f}$ has at least one zero, which forces $f \equiv \infty$ in $B(a; r)$. Otherwise $f$ is analytic. Thus any limit point of $H(G) \cup \{\infty\}$ in $C(G, \mathbb{C}_\infty)$ must belong to $H(G) \cup \{\infty\}$, establishing closedness.
