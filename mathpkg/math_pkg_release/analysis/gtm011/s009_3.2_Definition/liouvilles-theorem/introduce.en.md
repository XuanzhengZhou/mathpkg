Liouville's Theorem states that any bounded entire function must be constant. This is a striking result: while polynomials of positive degree grow without bound as $|z| \to \infty$, entire functions might naively be thought to include bounded non-constant examples (like $\sin z$ along the real line). Liouville's Theorem shows this is impossible in the complex plane — the only bounded functions analytic everywhere on $\mathbb{C}$ are constants. The proof is a direct application of Cauchy's Estimate, demonstrating the power of integral formulas in complex analysis.
