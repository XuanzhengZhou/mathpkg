This theorem extends the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus from real-valued to complex-valued continuous functions on a compact interval. Every continuous $f: [a,b] \to \mathbb{C}$ admits a primitive (antiderivative) $F$ such that $F' = f$, and the definite integral evaluates as $F(b) - F(a)$. The proof reduces to the real case by decomposing $f$ into its real and imaginary parts: if $g$ and $h$ are primitives of $\operatorname{Re} f$ and $\operatorname{Im} f$, then $F = g + ih$ is a primitive of $f$, and the equality of integrals follows from the definition of the complex-valued integral.
