If $\mu$ is a totally $\sigma$-finite measure on a $\sigma$-algebra $\mathbf{S}$ and $\nu$ is a totally $\sigma$-finite signed measure on $\mathbf{S}$ such that $\nu \ll \mu$, then there exists a finite valued measurable function $f$ on $X$ such that
$$\nu(E) = \int_E f \, d\mu$$
for every measurable set $E$. The function $f$ is unique in the sense that if also $\nu(E) = \int_E g \, d\mu$, $E \in \mathbf{S}$, then $f = g \; [\mu]$.

The function $f$ is not asserted to be integrable; a necessary and sufficient condition that $f$ be integrable is that $\nu$ be finite. The notation $\int f \, d\mu$ implicitly asserts that either the positive part or the negative part of $f$ is integrable, corresponding to the fact that either the upper or the lower variation of $\nu$ is finite.
