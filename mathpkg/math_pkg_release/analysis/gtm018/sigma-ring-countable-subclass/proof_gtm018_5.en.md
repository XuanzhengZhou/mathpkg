Consider the union of all those $\sigma$-subrings of $\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{S}(\mathbf{E})$ which are generated by countable subclasses of $\mathbf{E}$. Let $\mathbf{C}$ denote this union. We claim $\mathbf{C}$ is a $\sigma$-ring containing $\mathbf{E}$, which forces $\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{S}(\mathbf{E})$, proving the theorem.

The key observation is: the class of all sets belonging to $\sigma$-rings generated by countable subclasses of $\mathbf{E}$ is closed under countable unions and differences. If $A, B \in \mathbf{C}$, then $A \in \mathbf{S}(\mathbf{D}_1)$ and $B \in \mathbf{S}(\mathbf{D}_2)$ for some countable subclasses $\mathbf{D}_1, \mathbf{D}_2 \subset \mathbf{E}$. Then $\mathbf{D}_1 \cup \mathbf{D}_2$ is a countable subclass of $\mathbf{E}$, and since $\mathbf{S}(\mathbf{D}_1) \subset \mathbf{S}(\mathbf{D}_1 \cup \mathbf{D}_2)$ and similarly for $\mathbf{D}_2$, both $A - B$ and $A \cup B$ belong to $\mathbf{S}(\mathbf{D}_1 \cup \mathbf{D}_2) \subset \mathbf{C}$. For countable unions, if $\{E_i\} \subset \mathbf{C}$ with each $E_i \in \mathbf{S}(\mathbf{D}_i)$ for countable $\mathbf{D}_i \subset \mathbf{E}$, then $\bigcup_i E_i \in \mathbf{S}(\bigcup_i \mathbf{D}_i)$, and $\bigcup_i \mathbf{D}_i$ is a countable subclass of $\mathbf{E}$. Thus $\mathbf{C}$ is indeed a $\sigma$-ring.

Every $E \in \mathbf{E}$ belongs to $\mathbf{S}(\{E\})$, which is generated by the singleton (countable) subclass $\{E\} \subset \mathbf{E}$, so $\mathbf{E} \subset \mathbf{C}$. Therefore $\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{S}(\mathbf{E})$, and every set in $\mathbf{S}(\mathbf{E})$ belongs to some $\sigma$-ring generated by a countable subclass of $\mathbf{E}$.
