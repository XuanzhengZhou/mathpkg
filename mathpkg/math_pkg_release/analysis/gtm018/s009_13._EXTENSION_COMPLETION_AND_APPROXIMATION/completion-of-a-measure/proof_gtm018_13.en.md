If $E \in \mathbf{S}$, $N \subset A \in \mathbf{S}$, and $\mu(A) = 0$, then

$$E \cup N = (E - A) \Delta [A \cap (E \cup N)]$$

and

$$E \Delta N = (E - A) \cup [A \cap (E \Delta N)]$$

show that $\bar{\mathbf{S}}$ may also be described as the class of all sets of the form $E \cup N$. Since this implies $\bar{\mathbf{S}}$ is closed under countable unions and symmetric differences, it is a $\sigma$-ring. If $E_1 \Delta N_1 = E_2 \Delta N_2$, then $E_1 \Delta E_2 = N_1 \Delta N_2$ is a null set, so $\mu(E_1) = \mu(E_2)$, showing $\bar{\mu}$ is well-defined. Completeness follows from the definition.
