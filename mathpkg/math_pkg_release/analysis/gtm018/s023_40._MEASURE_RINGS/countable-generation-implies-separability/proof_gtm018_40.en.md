Let $\{E_n\}$ be a sequence of sets in $\mathbf{S}$ such that $\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{S}(\{E_n\})$. Because of the $\sigma$-finiteness of $\mu$, there is no loss of generality in assuming that $\mu(E_n) < \infty$ for every $n = 1, 2, \cdots$. Since the ring generated by $\{E_n\}$ is also countable (by 5.C), we may assume that the class $\{E_n: n = 1, 2, \cdots\}$ is a ring.

It follows from 13.D that, for every $E$ in $\mathbf{s}(\mu)$ and for every positive number $\epsilon$, there exists a positive integer $n$ such that $\rho(E, E_n) < \epsilon$. Since this means that a countable set is dense in $\mathbf{s}(\mu)$, the proof of the theorem is complete.
