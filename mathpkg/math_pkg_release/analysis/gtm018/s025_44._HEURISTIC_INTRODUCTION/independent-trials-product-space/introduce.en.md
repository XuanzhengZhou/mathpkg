When performing two independent trials of the same experiment, the analytic description is the Cartesian product of the original probability space with itself. If a single trial is described by $(X, \mathbf{S}, \mu)$, then two independent trials correspond to the product space $(X \times X, \mathbf{S} \times \mathbf{S}, \mu \times \mu)$. Independence of the trials means that for events $E$ from the first trial and $F$ from the second, $\mu(E \cap F) = \mu(E)\mu(F)$. The outcome of the compound experiment is a pair $(x_1, x_2)$ in the product space.
