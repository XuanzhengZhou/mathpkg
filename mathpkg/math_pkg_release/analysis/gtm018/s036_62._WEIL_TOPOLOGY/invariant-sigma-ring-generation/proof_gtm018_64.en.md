**Theorem A.** Since $xE \in \mathbf{T}$ for every $x$ in $X$ and every $E$ in $\mathbf{E}$ (by definition of invariant $\sigma$-ring), it follows that $\mathbf{T}_0 \subset \mathbf{T}$; it is sufficient therefore to prove that $\mathbf{T}_0$ is invariant. Let $x_0$ be any fixed element of $X$. The class of all those Baire sets $F$ for which $x_0F \in \mathbf{T}_0$ is a $\sigma$-ring; since, for every $x$ in $X$ and every $E$ in $\mathbf{E}$, $x_0(xE) = (x_0x)E \in \mathbf{T}_0$, it follows that this $\sigma$-ring contains $\mathbf{T}_0$. We have proved, in other words, that if $F \in \mathbf{T}_0$, then $x_0F \in \mathbf{T}_0$. Thus $\mathbf{T}_0$ is invariant, so $\mathbf{T} \subset \mathbf{T}_0$, and therefore $\mathbf{T} = \mathbf{T}_0$. $\blacksquare$
