For the first distributive law: $x \in E \cap (F \cup G)$ means $x \in E$ and $(x \in F \text{ or } x \in G)$. By the distributive property of "and" over "or", this is equivalent to $(x \in E \text{ and } x \in F)$ or $(x \in E \text{ and } x \in G)$, i.e. $x \in (E \cap F) \cup (E \cap G)$.

For the second law: $x \in E \cup (F \cap G)$ means $x \in E$ or $(x \in F \text{ and } x \in G)$. By the distributive property of "or" over "and", this is equivalent to $(x \in E \text{ or } x \in F)$ and $(x \in E \text{ or } x \in G)$, i.e. $x \in (E \cup F) \cap (E \cup G)$.

The extended forms are proved similarly by logical distributivity applied to arbitrary quantifications.
