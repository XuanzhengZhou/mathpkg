This is an easily verified theorem related to the generation of Borel sets and Baire sets (cf. 5.2 and 5.3). The class of all finite, disjoint unions of proper differences $C - D$ (with $C, D \in \mathbf{C}$ and $D \subset C$) forms a ring because the difference of two such finite disjoint unions can again be expressed as a finite disjoint union of proper differences. The $\sigma$-ring generated by this ring coincides with $\mathbf{S}$ because every open $\sigma$-bounded set can be expressed as a countable union of compact sets, and the operations of taking proper differences, finite disjoint unions, and countable unions suffice to generate the full Borel $\sigma$-ring. The same reasoning applies to $\mathbf{C}_0$ and $\mathbf{S}_0$.
