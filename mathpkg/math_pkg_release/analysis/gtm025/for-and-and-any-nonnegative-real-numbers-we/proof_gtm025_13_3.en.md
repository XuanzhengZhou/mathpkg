For $u \in [0, \infty[$, define $\varphi(u) = u^{p-1}$; $\varphi$ is continuous and strictly increasing, $\lim \varphi(u) = \infty$, and $\varphi(0) = 0$. The inverse $\psi$ of $\varphi$ is given by $\psi(v) = v^{\frac{1}{p-1}}$. We have $\Phi(a) = \int_0^a \varphi(u) du = \int_0^a u^{p-1} du = \frac{a^p}{p}$ and $\Psi(b) = \int_0^b \psi(v) dv = \int_0^b v^{\frac{1}{p-1}} dv = \frac{b^{p'}}{p'}$ [the Lebesgue integral and the Riemann integral agree on Riemann integrable functions (12.51.f)]. The corollary follows at once from (13.2).
