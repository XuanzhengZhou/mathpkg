in $\mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in W_x \subset U_i \cap V_\eta \subset U \cap V$. Let $\mathcal{A} = \{W_x : x \in U \cap V\}$. Then $\mathcal{A} \subset \mathcal{B}$ [$\mathcal{A}$ may be void!] and $U \cap V = \cup \mathcal{A} \in \mathcal{O}$. Thus $\mathcal{O}$ is closed under the formation of finite intersections. According to (i) $X$ is in $\mathcal{O}$, and, since $\varnothing \subset \mathcal{B}$, we have $\varnothing = \cup \varnothing \in \mathcal{O}$. This proves that $\mathcal{O}$ is a topology for $X$. Clearly $\mathcal{B}$ is a base for $\varnothing$.

The function $(x, y) \rightarrow |x - y|$ defined on $R \times R$ is an obvious distance-function. An important although special class of topological spaces are those in which the topology can be defined from a reasonable distance-function. The axiomatic definition follows.
