# Proof of Rudin Analyticity Theorem

**Theorem 10.3 (Rudin).** Let $\mathcal{L}$ be an algebra of continuous functions on the closed unit disk $D$ such that

(a) The function $z$ is in $\mathcal{L}$.

(b) $\mathcal{L}$ satisfies a maximum principle relative to $\Gamma = \partial D$:

$$|G(x)| \leq \max_{\Gamma} |G|, \quad \text{for all } x \in D,\; G \in \mathcal{L}.$$

Then $\mathcal{L} \subseteq A(D)$, i.e., every function in $\mathcal{L}$ is analytic on the interior $\mathring{D}$.

**Proof.** Let $\mathfrak{A}$ be the uniform closure of $\mathcal{L}$ on $D$. Then $\mathfrak{A}$ still satisfies (a) and (b), because uniform limits preserve both the algebra structure and the maximum principle inequality.

Put $B = \mathfrak{A}|_\Gamma$, the restriction of $\mathfrak{A}$ to the unit circle $\Gamma$. Because of the maximum principle (b), the restriction map is isometric, so $B$ is closed under uniform convergence on $\Gamma$. By condition (a), $z \in \mathfrak{A}$, hence $z|_\Gamma \in B$. Moreover, every element of $\mathfrak{A}_0 = A(D)|_\Gamma$ (the disk algebra restricted to $\Gamma$) belongs to $B$, since $\mathfrak{A}_0$ is generated by $z|_\Gamma$ and constants. Thus

$$\mathfrak{A}_0 \subseteq B \subseteq C(\Gamma),$$

and Theorem 10.2 applies: either $B = \mathfrak{A}_0$ or $B = C(\Gamma)$.

Consider the map $\varphi: B \to \mathbb{C}$ defined by

$$\varphi(g) = G(0),$$

where $G \in \mathfrak{A}$ is the unique function with $G|_\Gamma = g$. Uniqueness follows from the maximum principle (b): if $G_1, G_2 \in \mathfrak{A}$ agree on $\Gamma$, then $G_1 - G_2$ vanishes on $\Gamma$, and by (b) its maximum modulus on $D$ is $0$, so $G_1 = G_2$ on $D$.

The map $\varphi$ is a nonzero complex homomorphism of $B$. It is not evaluation at any point of $\Gamma$, because evaluation at a point $p \in \Gamma$ would send the function $z$ to $p$, whereas $\varphi(z) = 0$. Since every complex homomorphism of $C(\Gamma)$ is point evaluation (by the Gelfand–Mazur theory for $C(\Gamma)$), $B$ cannot equal $C(\Gamma)$. Hence $B = \mathfrak{A}_0$.

Now fix an arbitrary $G \in \mathfrak{A}$. Its restriction $g = G|_\Gamma$ lies in $B = \mathfrak{A}_0$, so $g$ extends to an analytic function $\tilde{G}$ on $\mathring{D}$, continuous on $D$. Both $G$ and $\tilde{G}$ belong to $\mathfrak{A}$ and agree on $\Gamma$, so by the maximum principle (b), $G = \tilde{G}$ on $D$. Thus $G$ is analytic on $\mathring{D}$, i.e., $G \in A(D)$. Since $\mathcal{L} \subseteq \mathfrak{A} \subseteq A(D)$, we have $\mathcal{L} \subseteq A(D)$. ∎
