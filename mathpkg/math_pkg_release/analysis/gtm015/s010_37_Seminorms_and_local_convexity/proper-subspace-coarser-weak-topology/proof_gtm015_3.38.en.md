# Proof of Proper subspace of the dual yields a strictly coarser weak topology

Let $E, F$ be vector spaces over $\mathbb{K}$ with a duality $B$ between them. If $F_0$ is a linear subspace of $F$ such that the relations $x \in E$, $B(x, y) = 0$ for all $y \in F_0$ imply $x = \theta$ (i.e., $F_0$ is total over $E$), and if $F_0 \neq F$, then $\sigma(E, F_0)$ is strictly coarser than $\sigma(E, F)$.

*Proof.* Since $F_0 \subset F$, the topology $\sigma(E, F_0)$ is generated by a subset of the seminorms that generate $\sigma(E, F)$, hence $\sigma(E, F_0) \subset \sigma(E, F)$. To show the inclusion is strict, note that if $F_0 \neq F$, there exists $y \in F \setminus F_0$. The linear form $B^y$ is $\sigma(E, F)$-continuous. If it were $\sigma(E, F_0)$-continuous, then by (38.8) there would exist $y_1, \ldots, y_n \in F_0$ such that

$$|B(x, y)| \leq |B(x, y_1)| + \cdots + |B(x, y_n)|$$

for all $x \in E$. This would imply, by (21.17), that $B^y$ is a linear combination of $B^{y_1}, \ldots, B^{y_n}$, and since the mapping $y \mapsto B^y$ is injective, $y$ would belong to the span of $\{y_1, \ldots, y_n\} \subset F_0$, contradicting $y \notin F_0$. Hence $B^y$ is not $\sigma(E, F_0)$-continuous, so the topologies differ.
