This theorem gives three equivalent descriptions of a complex hyperplane: (a) as a level set of a nonzero $\mathbb{C}$-linear form, (b) as the intersection of two $\mathbb{R}$-hyperplanes whose underlying maximal $\mathbb{R}$-subspaces are related by multiplication by $i$, and (c) as a translate of the intersection $M_0 \cap iM_0$ of a maximal $\mathbb{R}$-subspace with its $i$-multiple. This result shows that every $\mathbb{C}$-hyperplane is the intersection of two $\mathbb{R}$-hyperplanes, which is the geometric mechanism underlying the extension of the Hahn-Banach theorem from real to complex spaces.
