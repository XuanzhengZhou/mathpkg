An element $x$ of a ring $R$ is \textbf{left quasi-regular} in case $1 - x$ has a left inverse in $R$. It is \textbf{right quasi-regular} in case $1 - x$ has a right inverse in $R$, and \textbf{quasi-regular} in case $1 - x$ has a (two-sided) inverse in $R$. An element of $R$ can be left quasi-regular but not right quasi-regular. A subset of $R$ is left quasi-regular (resp. quasi-regular) in case each of its elements is left quasi-regular (resp. quasi-regular). This notion generalizes nilpotence: if $x \in R$ with $x^n = 0$, then
$$(1 + x + \cdots + x^{n-1})(1 - x) = 1 = (1 - x)(1 + x + \cdots + x^{n-1}),$$
so $x$ is quasi-regular.
