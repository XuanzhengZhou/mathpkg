A subset $I$ of a ring $R$ is a (two-sided) ideal of $R$ if it is an additive subgroup such that for all $x \in I$ and all $a, b \in R$,
$$axb \in I.$$
The subsets $\{0\}$ and $R$ are ideals of $R$, called the trivial ideals. Any ideal of $R$ other than $R$ itself is a proper ideal. The ideal $\{0\}$ (denoted simply by $0$) is the zero ideal. An ideal $I$ equals $R$ if and only if $1 \in I$. Moreover, if $a \in R$ is left invertible (or right invertible), then $R$ is the only ideal containing $a$.
