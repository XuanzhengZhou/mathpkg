For (1), recall that $R^* = \operatorname{Hom}_R(R, U)$ is isomorphic to ${}_R U_S$ as an $(R, S)$-bimodule (4.5). For each ideal $I$ of $R$, its annihilator $r_{R^*}(I)$ is an $(R, S)$-submodule of $R^*$. Conversely, for any $(R, S)$-submodule $V$ of $R^*$, its left annihilator $l_R(V)$ is a two-sided ideal of $R$. By Proposition 24.5, these maps give a lattice anti-isomorphism between the lattice of left ideals of $R$ and the lattice of $(R, S)$-submodules of $R^*$. Restricting to those submodules that are $(R, S)$-submodules (i.e., two-sided) yields an isomorphism between the lattices of two-sided ideals of $R$ and $S$ (via the isomorphism $R^* \cong {}_R U_S$ and symmetry).

(2) follows from (1) together with the fact that the center can be identified as the set of elements acting as $R$-module endomorphisms of ${}_R R$, which correspond to bimodule endomorphisms of ${}_R U_S$.

(3) Since $U$ is an injective cogenerator, it cogenerates every module. For a finitely generated module $M$, the evaluation $\sigma_M$ can be shown to be an isomorphism using the fact that ${}_R U$ is injective and cogenerates, together with exactness arguments.

(4) is a consequence of the lattice anti-isomorphism in Proposition 24.5 and the characterizations of projectivity and injectivity in terms of submodule lattices.
