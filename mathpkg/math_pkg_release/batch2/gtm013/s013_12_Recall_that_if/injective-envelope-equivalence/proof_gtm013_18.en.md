(a) $\Rightarrow$ (b) and (c): Use injectivity of $E$ to obtain $g$ extending the appropriate map; then use (5.13) to see that $g$ is monic because $i$ is essential.

(b) $\Rightarrow$ (a): By Theorem (18.10) there exists an injective envelope $f: M \to Q$. By (b) there exists a monomorphism $g: E \to Q$ with $f = g i$. Since $E$ is injective, this monomorphism splits: $Q = \operatorname{Im} g \oplus K$. Since $f$ is essential, $K = 0$, so $g$ is an isomorphism, and $i$ is an injective envelope.

(c) $\Rightarrow$ (a): By Theorem (18.10) let $f: M \to F$ be an injective envelope. By (c) (applied to the essential monomorphism $f$) there exists a monomorphism $g: F \to E$ with $g f = i$. Since both $f$ and $i$ are essential, $g$ must be an isomorphism. Thus $E \cong F$ and $i$ is an injective envelope.
