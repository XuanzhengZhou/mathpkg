A module $M$ over a commutative integral domain is called $R$-divisible if multiplication by every nonzero scalar $a$ is surjective; that is, for every element $m \in M$ and every nonzero $a \in R$, one can solve $ax = m$ for $x \in M$. Over $\mathbb{Z}$, divisible modules are precisely divisible abelian groups, such as $\mathbb{Q}$ and $\mathbb{Z}_{p^\infty}$.
