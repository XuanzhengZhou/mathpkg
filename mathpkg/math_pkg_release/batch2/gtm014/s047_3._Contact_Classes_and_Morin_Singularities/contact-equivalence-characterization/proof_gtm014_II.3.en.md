The statement is established through the chain of equivalences connecting the contact of submanifold germs (Definition 3.1), the local ring of contact $\mathcal{R}_{A,B}$ (Definition 3.2), and the definition of contact equivalence of map germs via their graphs (Definition 3.8). By Definition 3.8, two map germs $f, g: (X, p) \to (Y, q)$ are contact equivalent if and only if $\operatorname{graph} f$ and $\operatorname{graph} g$ have the same contact with $X \times \{q\}$ as submanifold germs of $X \times Y$ near $(p, q)$.

From Definition 3.1, $\operatorname{graph} f$ and $\operatorname{graph} g$ have the same contact with $A = X \times \{q\}$ if and only if there exists a germ of a diffeomorphism $\phi: (X \times Y, (p,q)) \to (X \times Y, (p,q))$ such that $\phi|_{X \times \{q\}} = \text{id}_{X \times \{q\}}$ and $\phi(\operatorname{graph} f) = \operatorname{graph} g$.

By Lemma 3.7, the local ring $\mathcal{R}_f(p)$ can be identified with $C_p^\infty(\mathbf{R}^n)/(f_1, \ldots, f_m)$ where $f_1, \ldots, f_m$ are the coordinate functions of $f$, and similarly for $g$. By Lemma 3.6, the ideal $\mathcal{I}_{(p,q)}(X \times \{q\}, \operatorname{graph} f)$ equals the ideal generated by the coordinate functions of $f$. It follows that $\mathcal{R}_f(p) \cong \mathcal{R}_{X \times \{q\}, \operatorname{graph} f}$.

Thus $\operatorname{graph} f$ and $\operatorname{graph} g$ have the same contact with $X \times \{q\}$ if and only if $\mathcal{R}_{X \times \{q\}, \operatorname{graph} f} = \mathcal{R}_{X \times \{q\}, \operatorname{graph} g}$, which is equivalent to $\mathcal{R}_f(p) = \mathcal{R}_g(p)$. The forward direction (same contact $\Rightarrow$ same local ring) follows from the invariance noted after Definition 3.2. The converse direction uses the algebraic structure of the local ring to reconstruct the contact equivalence.
