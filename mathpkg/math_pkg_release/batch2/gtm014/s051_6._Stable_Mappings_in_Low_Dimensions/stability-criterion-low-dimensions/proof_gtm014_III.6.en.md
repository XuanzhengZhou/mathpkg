That the $S_{1_k}$ singularities which are listed are the only ones that can occur is clear from Corollary 3.14. In §5, exercises 11 and 12 indicate how to prove analogous results for the $S_2$ singularities on the list above. $S_3$ singularities are, of course, removable as long as $\dim X < 9$.

In particular, for dimensions $\leq 4$, a stable map can only exhibit the above singularities; and, being stable, it must exhibit them transversely. The necessity is a consequence of Mather's characterization of stability: infinitesimal stability implies stability, and infinitesimal stability forces singularities to appear transversely. The sufficiency follows from verifying directly via Mather's criterion that the canonical forms for the singularities in the list (given in Theorems 4.1 and 5.10) do indeed represent infinitesimally stable germs.
