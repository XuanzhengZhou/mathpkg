The intrinsic derivative, introduced by Porteous, is a fundamental tool for differentiating maps between vector bundles. Given a vector bundle homomorphism $\rho: E \to F$ over a manifold $X$, the usual derivative $(d\rho)_p$ maps $T_p X$ into $\operatorname{Hom}(E_p, F_p)$. The intrinsic derivative refines this by further restricting the domain to the kernel $K_p = \operatorname{Ker} \rho(p)$ and projecting the codomain onto the cokernel $L_p = \operatorname{Coker} \rho(p)$, yielding a map $(D\rho)_p: T_p X \to \operatorname{Hom}(K_p, L_p)$. This construction is independent of the choice of local trivializations and plays a central role in the Thom-Boardman stratification of singularity types. A special case appeared in Chapter II, Definition 6.5, where for a real-valued function $f$, the intrinsic derivative at a critical point recovers the Hessian.
