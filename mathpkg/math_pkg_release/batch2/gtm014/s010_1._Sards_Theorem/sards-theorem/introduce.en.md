Sard's Theorem is one of the fundamental results in differential topology. It states that for any smooth map between smooth manifolds, the set of critical values — points in the target that are images of points where the differential fails to be surjective — has Lebesgue measure zero. A generalized version holds for $C^k$ maps when $k > \max(\dim Y - \dim X, 0)$. Sard's Theorem underpins transversality theory: it guarantees that regular values are dense, and thus that generic preimages are well-behaved submanifolds (by the Preimage Theorem). When $\dim X < \dim Y$, the result follows directly from the fact that the image of $X$ under $f$ already has measure zero. The proof reduces to a local Euclidean statement and proceeds by induction on the domain dimension.
