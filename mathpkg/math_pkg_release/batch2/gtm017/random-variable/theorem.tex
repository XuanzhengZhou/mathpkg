A numerical valued function $X(w)$ on the space $\Omega$ of sample points is called a \text{random variable}. Thus $X(w)$ represents an observable in the experiment.

A random variable $X(w)$ generates a field (sigma-field) $\mathfrak{F}_X$ of events generated by events of the form $\{w \mid X(w) = a\}$ where $a$ is any number. The field consists of events which are unions of events of the form $\{w \mid X(w) = a\}$. The probability function $P$ on the events of this field $\mathfrak{F}_X$ is called the \text{probability distribution} of $X(w)$.

$n$ random variables $X_1(w), \ldots, X_n(w)$ defined on a probability space are said to be \text{independent} if the fields (sigma-fields) $\mathfrak{F}_{X_1}, \ldots, \mathfrak{F}_{X_n}$ generated by them are independent.
