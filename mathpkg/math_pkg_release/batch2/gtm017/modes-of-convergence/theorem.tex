For a sequence of random variables $X_n(w)$:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Convergence almost everywhere} (with probability one):
  $X(w) = \lim_{n \to \infty} X_n(w)$ except on a set of measure zero.

\item \textbf{Convergence in probability}:
  $\lim_{n \to \infty} P(|X_n - X| > \varepsilon) = 0$ for every $\varepsilon > 0$.

\item \textbf{Convergence in mean square}:
  $\lim_{n \to \infty} E|X_n - X|^2 = 0$.
\end{itemize}

Convergence almost everywhere implies convergence in probability. The converse does not hold generally.
\end{itemize}
