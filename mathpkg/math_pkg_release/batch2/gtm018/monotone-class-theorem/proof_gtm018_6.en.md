Let $\mathbf{R}$ be a ring. Set $\mathbf{M} = \mathbf{M}(\mathbf{R})$, the monotone class generated by $\mathbf{R}$, and $\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{S}(\mathbf{R})$, the $\sigma$-ring generated by $\mathbf{R}$.

Since a $\sigma$-ring is a monotone class (Theorem A) and $\mathbf{S}(\mathbf{R}) \supset \mathbf{R}$, it follows that

$$\mathbf{S}(\mathbf{R}) \supset \mathbf{M} = \mathbf{M}(\mathbf{R}).$$

To prove the reverse inclusion, we show that $\mathbf{M}$ is a $\sigma$-ring; then $\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{R}) \supset \mathbf{S}(\mathbf{R})$ follows since $\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{R}) \supset \mathbf{R}$.

For any set $F$, define

$$\mathbf{K}(F) = \{E \in \mathbf{M}: E - F \in \mathbf{M}, \; F - E \in \mathbf{M}, \; E \cup F \in \mathbf{M}\}.$$

Because of the symmetric roles of $E$ and $F$ in the definition, the relations $E \in \mathbf{K}(F)$ and $F \in \mathbf{K}(E)$ are equivalent.

If $\{E_n\}$ is a monotone sequence of sets in $\mathbf{K}(F)$, then

$$\lim_n E_n - F = \lim_n (E_n - F) \in \mathbf{M},$$

$$F - \lim_n E_n = \lim_n (F - E_n) \in \mathbf{M},$$

$$F \cup \lim_n E_n = \lim_n (F \cup E_n) \in \mathbf{M},$$

so that if $\mathbf{K}(F)$ is not empty, it is a monotone class.

If $E \in \mathbf{R}$ and $F \in \mathbf{R}$, then by the definition of a ring, $E - F \in \mathbf{R}$, $F - E \in \mathbf{R}$, and $E \cup F \in \mathbf{R} \subset \mathbf{M}$, so $E \in \mathbf{K}(F)$. Since this holds for every $E \in \mathbf{R}$, we have $\mathbf{R} \subset \mathbf{K}(F)$, and therefore, since $\mathbf{M}$ is the smallest monotone class containing $\mathbf{R}$,

$$\mathbf{M} \subset \mathbf{K}(F).$$

Hence if $E \in \mathbf{M}$ and $F \in \mathbf{R}$, then $E \in \mathbf{K}(F)$, and therefore $F \in \mathbf{K}(E)$. Since this holds for every $F \in \mathbf{R}$, we obtain

$$\mathbf{M} \subset \mathbf{K}(E)$$

for every $E \in \mathbf{M}$. The validity of this relation for every $E \in \mathbf{M}$ is equivalent to the assertion that $\mathbf{M}$ is closed under the formation of differences and finite unions, i.e., that $\mathbf{M}$ is a ring.

Since $\mathbf{M}$ is a monotone class and a ring, Theorem A implies $\mathbf{M}$ is a $\sigma$-ring. Therefore $\mathbf{M} \supset \mathbf{S}(\mathbf{R})$, and combining with the first inclusion yields $\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{R}) = \mathbf{S}(\mathbf{R})$.
