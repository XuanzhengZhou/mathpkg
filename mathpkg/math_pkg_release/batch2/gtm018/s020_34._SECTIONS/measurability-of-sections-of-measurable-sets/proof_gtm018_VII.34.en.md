Let $\mathcal{E}$ be the class of all those subsets $E$ of $X \times Y$ for which every $X$-section belongs to $T$ and every $Y$-section belongs to $S$. It is easy to verify that $\mathcal{E}$ is a $\sigma$-ring. Indeed, if $E \in \mathcal{E}$, then $(X \times Y \setminus E)_x = Y \setminus E_x \in T$, so $\mathcal{E}$ is closed under complementation. If $\{E_n\}$ is a sequence of sets in $\mathcal{E}$, then $(\bigcup_n E_n)_x = \bigcup_n (E_n)_x \in T$, so $\mathcal{E}$ is closed under countable unions. Thus $\mathcal{E}$ is a $\sigma$-ring.

Every measurable rectangle $A \times B$ with $A \in S$, $B \in T$ belongs to $\mathcal{E}$, since its $X$-section is $B$ if $x \in A$ and $\emptyset$ otherwise, and its $Y$-section is $A$ if $y \in B$ and $\emptyset$ otherwise. Since $S \times T$ is, by definition, the $\sigma$-ring generated by the class of all measurable rectangles, it follows that $S \times T \subset \mathcal{E}$. Hence every set in $S \times T$ has the property that all its sections are measurable.
