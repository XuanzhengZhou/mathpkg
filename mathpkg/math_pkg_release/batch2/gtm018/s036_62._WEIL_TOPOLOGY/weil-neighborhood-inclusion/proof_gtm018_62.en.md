**Theorem B.** It is trivial that $N \in \mathbf{N}$ by definition. To see that $N \subset A$, observe that if $x \in N$, then $\rho(xE, E) < \varepsilon < 2\mu(E)$, which implies that $xE \cap E \neq 0$. Indeed, if $xE \cap E = 0$, then $\mu(xE \Delta E) = \mu(xE) + \mu(E) \geq 2\mu(E)$ (since $\mu(xE) = \mu(E)$ for a measurable group with invariant measure, and generally $\mu(xE)$ is comparable to $\mu(E)$). The condition $xE \cap E \neq 0$ is equivalent to $x \in EE^{-1} = A$. Hence $N \subset A$. $\blacksquare$
