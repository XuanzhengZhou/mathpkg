If $(X, S, \mu)$ is a measurable group and if $E$ and $F$ are measurable sets, then there exist two sequences $\{x_n\}$ and $\{y_n\}$ of elements of $X$ and a sequence $\{A_n\}$ of measurable sets such that (a) the sequences $\{x_nA_n\}$ and $\{y_nA_n\}$ are disjoint sequences of subsets of $E$ and $F$ respectively, and (b) at least one of the two measurable sets,
$$
E_0 = E - \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} x_nA_n \quad \text{and} \quad F_0 = F - \bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} y_nA_n,
$$
has measure zero. (Hint: if either $E$ or $F$ has measure zero, the assertion is trivial. If both have positive measure, use the measure-preserving transformations $S$ and $T$ to compare the product measure.)
