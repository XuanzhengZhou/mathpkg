If an experiment is analytically described by a measure space $(X, \mathbf{S}, \mu)$, then the compound experiment consisting of two independent trials of the given experiment is described by the Cartesian product measure space
$$(X \times X, \; \mathbf{S} \times \mathbf{S}, \; \mu \times \mu).$$
The independence of trials is reflected in the fact that for any events $E$ from the first trial and $F$ from the second,
$$\mu(E \cap F) = \mu(E) \, \mu(F).$$
This construction generalizes to $n$ independent trials as the $n$-fold product space.
