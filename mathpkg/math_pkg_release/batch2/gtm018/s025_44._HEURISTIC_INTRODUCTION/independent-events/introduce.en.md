Two events $E$ and $F$ are independent if $\mu_F(E) = \mu(E)$, meaning that knowledge that $F$ has occurred does not change the probability assigned to $E$. Equivalently, $E$ and $F$ are independent if and only if $\mu(E \cap F) = \mu(E)\mu(F)$, a symmetric condition. This concept generalizes the intuitive idea that the outcome of one trial does not affect the outcome of another. For a fair die, the events $E = \{2,4,6\}$ (even) and $F = \{1,2\}$ (less than 3) happen to be independent, since $\mu(E)=1/2$, $\mu(F)=1/3$, and $\mu(E \cap F)=\mu(\{2\})=1/6 = (1/2)(1/3)$.
