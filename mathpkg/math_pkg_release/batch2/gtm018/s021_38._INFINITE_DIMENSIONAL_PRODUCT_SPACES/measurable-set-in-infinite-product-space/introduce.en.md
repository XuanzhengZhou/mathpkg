In an infinite product of totally finite measure spaces each normalized to total measure 1, measurability is defined via the $\sigma$-ring (in fact, a $\sigma$-algebra) generated by all measurable rectangles. This $\sigma$-algebra, denoted $\prod_{i=1}^\infty \mathbf{S}_i$, is the natural infinite-dimensional analog of the product $\sigma$-ring from the finite case. The normalization $\mu_i(X_i)=1$ is essential: it ensures that the finite-dimensional projection measures are compatible and that the infinite product measure can be consistently defined. This construction is fundamental to probability theory, where the factor spaces represent independent random variables each taking values in a probability space.
