Theorem A characterizes measurable $\{1,\dots,n\}$-cylinders in an infinite product space: a set $E$ is a measurable cylinder depending only on the first $n$ coordinates if and only if it can be written as $E = A \times X^{(n)}$ where $A$ is a measurable subset of the finite product $X_1 \times \cdots \times X_n$. This representation theorem is fundamental because it establishes a correspondence between measurable sets in the infinite product $\sigma$-algebra that depend on only finitely many coordinates and measurable sets in the corresponding finite-dimensional product. It justifies the definition of the set function $\mu$ on the algebra of finite-dimensional measurable cylinders, which is the first step in constructing the infinite product measure.
