A $J$-cylinder is a subset of an infinite product space whose membership depends only on coordinates indexed by a fixed set $J$. When $J = \{1,\dots,n\}$, a $J$-cylinder takes the form $A \times X^{(n)}$ where $A \subset X_1 \times \cdots \times X_n$. Cylinder sets generalize measurable rectangles to the infinite-dimensional setting: they generate the product $\sigma$-algebra and serve as the domain of the set function $\mu$, which is first defined on the algebra of finite-dimensional measurable cylinders and then extended to a countably additive measure via Theorem B. The geometric picture is that a $\{1,\dots,n\}$-cylinder imposes constraints only on the first $n$ coordinates while leaving the tail coordinates unrestricted.
