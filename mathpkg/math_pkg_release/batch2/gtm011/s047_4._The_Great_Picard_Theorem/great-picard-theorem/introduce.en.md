The Great Picard Theorem is a fundamental result in complex analysis that dramatically strengthens the Casorati-Weierstrass Theorem. While Casorati-Weierstrass guarantees that the image of any deleted neighborhood of an essential singularity is dense in the complex plane, the Great Picard Theorem asserts a much stronger conclusion: in any deleted neighborhood, the function assumes every complex value infinitely often, with at most a single exceptional value. This "Picard exceptional value" cannot be eliminated; for instance, $e^{1/z}$ has an essential singularity at $z = 0$ and never assumes the value $0$, but assumes every other complex value infinitely often in any neighborhood of the origin.
