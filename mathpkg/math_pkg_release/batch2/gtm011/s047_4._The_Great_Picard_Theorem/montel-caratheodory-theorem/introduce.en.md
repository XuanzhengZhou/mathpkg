The Montel-Carathéodory Theorem states that the family of all analytic functions on a region that omit the two values $0$ and $1$ is a normal family in the sense of Montel — that is, every sequence in the family contains a subsequence converging uniformly on compact subsets (allowing $\infty$ as a limit in the extended complex plane $\mathbb{C}_\infty$). This theorem is a direct consequence of Schottky's Theorem and Montel's Theorem on locally bounded families. It serves as the critical tool in proving the Great Picard Theorem: given an essential singularity and two omitted values, one can rescale to obtain a sequence of functions omitting $0$ and $1$, then the normality conclusion forces the original function to either be bounded (removable singularity) or have a pole, yielding a contradiction.
