A function $f: G \to \mathbb{C}$ is analytic if $f$ is continuously differentiable on $G$.

It follows readily, as in calculus, that sums and products of functions analytic on $G$ are analytic. Also, if $f$ and $g$ are analytic on $G$ and $G_1$ is the set of points in $G$ where $g$ doesn't vanish, then $f/g$ is analytic on $G_1$.

Since constant functions and the function $z$ are clearly analytic it follows that all rational functions are analytic on the complement of the set of zeros of the denominator.

Moreover, the usual laws for differentiating sums, products, and quotients remain valid.

2.4 Chain Rule. Let $f$ and $g$ be analytic on $G$ and $\Omega$ respectively and suppose $f(G) \subset \Omega$. Then $g \circ f$ is analytic on $G$ and

$$(g \circ f)'(z) = g'(f(z))f'(z)$$

for all $z$ in $G$.
