This definition introduces the fundamental classification of singularities in complex analysis. An isolated singularity at $z = a$ means the function is analytic in a punctured neighborhood of $a$ but not at $a$ itself. A removable singularity is the mildest type: the function can be redefined (or extended) at $a$ to become analytic on the full disk. The classic example is $\frac{\sin z}{z}$ at $z = 0$, which has a removable singularity. In contrast, $\frac{1}{z}$ and $\exp\frac{1}{z}$ have non-removable isolated singularities at $z = 0$ — they are respectively a pole and an essential singularity.
