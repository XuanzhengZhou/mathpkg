This proposition gives a precise epsilon-characterization of the order $\lambda$ of an entire function. The upper bound $|f(z)| < \exp(|z|^{\lambda+\epsilon})$ for large $|z|$ follows directly from the definition of $\lambda$ as an infimum. The lower bound $|f(z)| \geq \exp(|z|^{\lambda-\epsilon})$ on arbitrarily large $|z|$ shows that $\lambda$ is sharp: the growth cannot be bounded by any exponent strictly smaller than $\lambda$. This is an immediate consequence of the observation that if $|f(z)| < \exp(|z|^a)$ and $b > a$, then $|f(z)| < \exp(|z|^b)$.
