Let $\gamma$ and $G$ be as defined in Exercise 1(a). Show that
$$n(\gamma; z) = \pm 1$$
if $z$ is inside $\gamma$ and $n(\gamma; z) = 0$ if $z \notin G^-$.

Remarks. It is not necessary to assume that $\gamma$ has such a point $a$ as above; each part of this exercise remains true if $\gamma$ is only assumed to be a simple closed rectifiable curve. Of course, we must define what is meant by the inside of $\gamma$. This is difficult to obtain. The fact that a simple closed curve divides the plane into two pieces (an inside and an outside) is the content of the Jordan Curve Theorem. This is a very deep result of topology.
