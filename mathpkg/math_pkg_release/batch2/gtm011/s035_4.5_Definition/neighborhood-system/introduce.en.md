A neighborhood system on a set $X$ assigns to each point $x \in X$ a collection $\mathcal{N}_x$ of subsets satisfying three axioms: each neighborhood contains the point; the intersection of two neighborhoods contains a neighborhood; and neighborhoods of different points are compatible via condition (c). This structure mimics the system of open balls in a metric space and provides an alternative foundation for defining a topology. Condition (c) is crucial for ensuring that the induced topology has the desired properties; without it, the collection of closed balls in a metric space would satisfy (a) and (b) but fail to generate a topology in which neighborhoods are open.
