If $f: G \to \mathbb{R}$ and $a \in G^{-}$ or $a = \infty$, then the limit superior of $f(z)$ as $z$ approaches $a$, denoted by $\limsup_{z \to a} f(z)$, is defined by

$$\limsup_{z \to a} f(z) = \limsup_{r \to 0+} \{ f(z): z \in G \cap B(a; r) \}$$

(If $a = \infty$, $B(a; r)$ is the ball in the metric of $\mathbb{C}_{\infty}$.) Similarly, the limit inferior of $f(z)$ as $z$ approaches $a$, denoted by $\liminf_{z \to a} f(z)$, is defined by

$$\liminf_{z \to a} f(z) = \liminf_{r \to 0+} \{ f(z): z \in G \cap B(a; r) \}.$$

It is easy to see that $\lim f(z)$ exists and equals $\alpha$ iff $\alpha = \limsup_{z \to a} f(z) = \liminf f(z)$.

If $G \subset \mathbb{C}$ then let $\partial_{\infty} G$ denote the boundary of $G$ in $\mathbb{C}_{\infty}$ and call it the extended boundary of $G$. Clearly $\partial_{\infty} G = \partial G$ if $G$ is bounded and $\partial_{\infty} G = \partial G \cup \{\infty\}$ if $G$ is unbounded.
