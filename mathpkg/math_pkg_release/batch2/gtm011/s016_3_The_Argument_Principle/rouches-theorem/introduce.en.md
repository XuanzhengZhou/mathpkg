Rouché's Theorem provides a powerful method for comparing the number of zeros and poles of two meromorphic functions inside a contour. If the strict triangle inequality $|f(z) + g(z)| < |f(z)| + |g(z)|$ holds on the boundary circle, then $Z_f - P_f = Z_g - P_g$. A common special case is when $|f(z) - g(z)| < |f(z)|$ on $\gamma$, which implies $Z_f = Z_g$ (when $f$ and $g$ are analytic). The theorem is widely used to locate zeros of analytic functions—for instance, it gives an elegant proof of the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra by comparing a polynomial $p(z)$ to $z^n$ on a sufficiently large circle.
