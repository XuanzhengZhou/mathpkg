A family $\mathcal{F}$ of continuous functions from $G$ to $\Omega$ is called normal if every sequence in $\mathcal{F}$ possesses a subsequence converging to some function in $C(G, \Omega)$. The limit function need not belong to $\mathcal{F}$ itself, unlike the definition of sequential compactness. This classical notion originates in complex analysis and plays a fundamental role in the proofs of Montel's theorem, the Riemann mapping theorem, and related results concerning compactness in spaces of analytic functions.
