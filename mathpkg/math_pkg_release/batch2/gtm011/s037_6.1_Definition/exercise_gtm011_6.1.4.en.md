Let $T = \{z : |z| = 1\} \times \{z : |z| = 1\}$; then $T$ is a torus. (This torus is homeomorphic to the usual hollow doughnut in $\mathbb{R}^3$.) If $\omega$ and $\omega'$ are complex numbers such that $\operatorname{Im}(\omega/\omega') \neq 0$ then $\omega$ and $\omega'$, considered as elements of the vector space $\mathbb{C}$ over $\mathbb{R}$, are linearly independent. So each $z$ in $\mathbb{C}$ can be uniquely represented as $z = t\omega + t'\omega'$; $t, t'$ in $\mathbb{R}$. Let $G = \{t\omega + t'\omega' : 0 \leq t, t' < 1\}$ and define the map $p$ of $\mathbb{C}$ onto $T$ as in the previous problem. Show that $p(G) = T$ and $p$ is one-one on $G$. Use this to put an analytic structure on $T$; show this gives an uncountable number of incompatible complex structures on $T$.
