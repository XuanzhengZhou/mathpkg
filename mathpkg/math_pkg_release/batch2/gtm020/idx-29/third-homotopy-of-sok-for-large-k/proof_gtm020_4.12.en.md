First compute $\pi_3(SO(4))$. From the quaternionic description (Proposition 12.7), the maps $r, s: S^3 \to SO(4)$ are given by $r(x)y = xyx^{-1}$ and $s(x)y = xy$. These satisfy relation (12.10): $[c_5(x)] = -[r(x)] + 2[s(x)]$ in $\pi_3(SO(4))$.

From Theorem 10.4 (the homotopy exact sequence of the Stiefel fibration), there is an exact sequence:
$$\pi_4(S^4) \xrightarrow{\partial} \pi_3(SO(4)) \xrightarrow{} \pi_3(SO(5)) \to 0.$$
The image of the boundary map $\partial$ is generated by $-[r(x)] + 2[s(x)]$. Therefore:
$$\pi_3(SO(5)) \cong \operatorname{coker} \partial.$$
The cokernel is isomorphic to $\mathbf{Z}$, giving $\pi_3(SO(5)) = \mathbf{Z}$.

For $k \geq 5$, the inequality $3 \leq k-2$ holds, so by the stability properties from Section 4 (Proposition 4.1),
$$\pi_3(SO(5)) \cong \pi_3(SO(6)) \cong \cdots \cong \pi_3(SO(k)),$$
hence $\pi_3(SO(k)) = \mathbf{Z}$ for all $k \geq 5$.
