Second, we prove the theorem under hypothesis (H2). We assume the theorem is true for all $$Y$$ with $$\dim Y < n$$. This is the case for $$n = 0$$ because $$X = Y$$ and $$A = B$$. We let $$Y$$ be of dimension $$n$$. By the inductive hypothesis we have a cross section $$s'$$ of $$\xi|[(Y \times 0) \cup (Y_{n-1} \times I)]$$ with $$s'|[(Y \times 0) \cup (X \times I)] = s$$. We let $$C$$ be an $$n$$-cell of $$Y$$ with attaching map $$u_C: I^n \rightarrow Y$$. The bundle $$(u_C \times 1_I)^*(\xi)$$ over $$I^n \times I$$ is locally trivial, and since $$I^n \times I$$ is compact, we can dissect $$I^n \times I$$ into equal cubes $$K \times [(i-1)/k, i/k]$$ of length $$1/k$$ for $$1 \leq i \leq k$$ such that $$(u_C \times 1_I)^*(\xi)$$ is trivial over each of these cubes. By (5.10) the cross section $$s'$$ defines a cross section $$\sigma'$$ of $$(u_C \times 1_I)^*(\xi)|[(I^n \times 0) \cup (\partial I^n \times I)]$$. Applying the inductive hypothesis to $$\sigma'$$ with respect to $$I^n \times [0, 1/k]$$, we can assume that $$\sigma'$$ is defined on each $$\partial K \times [0, 1/k]$$ making up $$(I^n)_{n-1} \times [0, 1/k]$$.
