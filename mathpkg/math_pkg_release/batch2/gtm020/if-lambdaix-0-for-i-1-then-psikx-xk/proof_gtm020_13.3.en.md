We have $\lambda_t(x) = 1 + tx$ and $(d/dt)\lambda_t(x) = x$. Then $\psi_{-t}(x) = -tx/(1 + tx)$ or $\psi_t(x) = tx/(1 - tx) = \sum_{k \geq 1} x^k t^k$. Since $\psi_t(x) = \sum_{k \geq 1} \psi^k(x)t^k$, we have $\psi^k(x) = x^k$.

2.4 Remark. From a universal formula relating symmetric functions we have $\psi^k(x) = s_k^n(\lambda_1(x), \ldots, \lambda_n(x))$ for $n \geq k$, where $x_1^k + \cdots + x_n^k = s_k^n(\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_n)$ and $\sigma_i$ are the elementary symmetric functions; see Proposition (1.8) of the next chapter.
