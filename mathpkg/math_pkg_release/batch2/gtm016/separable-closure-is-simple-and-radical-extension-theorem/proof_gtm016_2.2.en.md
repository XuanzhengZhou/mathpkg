If $k$ is finite, $K = k(s)$ and $K/k$ is separable, so $k_{\text{sep}} = K$. If $k$ is infinite, $\operatorname{Hom}_k(K, k_{\text{Alg}})$ is finite. Choose $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_n$ and let $s$ be such that the values $\sigma_i(s)$ are all distinct. Then the minimum polynomial of $s$ has degree $n$, and one shows $k_{\text{sep}} = k(s)$.
