Let $f$ be the frequency of a photon measured by an instantaneous observer $(z, Z)$. The \textbf{electromagnetic radiation} is classified into the following frequency ranges:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Radio waves}: $f < 10^9$ s$^{-1}$
\item \textbf{Microwaves, millimeter radiation, infrared}: $10^9 \leq f \leq 3 \times 10^{14}$ s$^{-1}$
\item \textbf{Visible light (optical radiation)}: $3 \times 10^{14} \leq f \leq 10^{15}$ s$^{-1}$
\item \textbf{Ultraviolet radiation}: $f > 10^{15}$ s$^{-1}$ (and below X-rays)
\item \textbf{X-rays and gamma rays}: at still larger frequencies
\end{itemize}

Light that is red has a smaller $f$ (and thus larger $\lambda$) than light that is blue or violet. The term \textbf{radiation}, \textbf{electromagnetic radiation}, or simply \textbf{light} is used generically to refer to all ranges.
