A future-pointing lightlike vector $Y$ (representing a "particle of light" or photon) admits a canonical decomposition $Y = e(Z - U)$ relative to an instantaneous observer $(z, Z)$. Here $U \in Z^\perp$ is the unit spatial direction from which the light appears to arrive, and $e > 0$ encodes the photon's energy (and color -- larger $e$ means bluer light). The Newtonian angle between two light particles reduces to the Euclidean angle between their direction vectors: $\cos\theta = g(U, U')$. This decomposition is fundamental for analyzing observed properties of light in general relativity, including aberration and redshift (Chapter 5).
