This definition formalizes the notion of particle collisions in general relativity and the conservation laws that govern them. A collision event is a spacetime point where particle world-lines begin or end. The definition requires that total 4-momentum and total electric charge are each conserved across every collision—the sum over incoming particles equals the sum over outgoing particles. These conservation laws are of great physical importance because they yield predictions even when the detailed microscopic mechanism of a collision is not understood. For example, Pauli postulated the neutrino in 1930–31 precisely to balance 4-momentum conservation (part (a)) in beta decay, and the neutrino was confirmed experimentally over twenty-five years later.
