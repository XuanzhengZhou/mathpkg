Let $E \subset \mathbb{R}$ be an interval, which may be infinite, and $\gamma: E \rightarrow M$ a map. $\gamma$ will always be understood to be $C^\infty$ in the following sense: there exists an open set $\hat{E} \subset \mathbb{R}$ containing $E$ and a $C^\infty$ map $\hat{\gamma}: \hat{E} \rightarrow M$ such that $\hat{\gamma}|_E = \gamma$. Such a $C^\infty$ map $\gamma: E \rightarrow M$ is called a \textbf{curve} in $M$.

We denote the inclusion function $E \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ by $s, t$, or $u$ and the distinguished vector field on $E$ by $d/ds$, and so on. For example, $du(d/du) = 1$. For each $u \in E$, $\gamma_* u$ denotes the tangent vector at $\gamma u$; thus $\gamma_* u = [\gamma_*(d/du)](u) \in M_{\gamma u}$.
