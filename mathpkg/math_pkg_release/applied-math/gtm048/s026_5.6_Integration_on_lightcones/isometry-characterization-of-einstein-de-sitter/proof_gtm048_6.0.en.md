*Proof.* Let $	ilde{\psi}$ be a Euclidean isometry from $\mathbb{R}^3$ to $\mathbb{R}^3$ and define $\psi$ as in the proposition. Clearly $\psi$ is then a diffeomorphism. With $h = \sum_{ho=1}^{3} du^ho \otimes du^ho$ on $\mathbb{R}^3$ we have $	ilde{\psi}^*h = h$; thus $\psi^*h = h$, where $h$ is now regarded as a tensor on $M$. Moreover, $\psi^*du^4 = du^4$. Thus $\psi^*g = g$ and $\psi$ is an isometry.

Conversely, let $\chi: M 	o M$ be an isometry and designate the level surface $u^4 = 1$ by $\mathcal{B}$. We may regard $\mathcal{B}$ together with the Riemannian metric induced on $\mathcal{B}$ by $g$ as $(\mathbb{R}^3, h)$. Moreover, if we denote the restriction of $\chi$ to $\mathcal{B}$ by $	ilde{\psi}$, $	ilde{\psi}$ is a diffeomorphism from $\mathcal{B}$ to $\mathcal{B}$, by property (a) that isometries preserve the cosmological time slices. Since $\chi$ is an isometry, $(	ilde{\psi})^*h = h$ and $	ilde{\psi}$ is a Euclidean isometry on $\mathcal{B}$. Define $\psi$ as before, then $\chi$ and $\psi$ are isometries of $M$ which agree on $\mathcal{B}$. We will show they agree everywhere, thus completing the proof.

Let $\gamma$ be an inextendible integral curve of $\partial_4$ which intersects $\mathcal{B}$ at $x$. Then both $\chi \circ \gamma$ and $\psi \circ \gamma$ are inextendible integral curves of $\partial_4$ passing through $\chi(x) = \psi(x)$. By uniqueness of integral curves, they coincide. Since every point of $M$ lies on such an integral curve, $\chi = \psi$ on all of $M$.
