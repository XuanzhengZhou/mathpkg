There exists a neighborhood $U$ of the observer's event $z$ such that, for the instantaneous observer $(z, Z)$, spacetime curvature within $U$ is negligible compared to empirical inaccuracies (see Sections 2.1.2 and 6.1.3b). Within such a neighborhood, the spacetime is effectively flat --- that is, locally Minkowskian. In Minkowski spacetime, the rest space of the instantaneous observer is a flat $3$-dimensional Euclidean submanifold $\mathbb{R}^3 \subset M_2$ parallel to $Z^\perp$.

Under the additional condition that all relative speeds relevant to the distance measurements are negligible compared to $1$ (i.e., non-relativistic regime), both the luminosity distance $d_L$ (defined by $4\pi f = L/d_L^2$) and the angular diameter distance $d_A$ (defined by $\Omega = A/d_A^2$) reduce to the ordinary Euclidean distance within this rest space $\mathbb{R}^3$. This is because:

1. **For $d_L$:** In flat spacetime with negligible relative speeds, the relativistic correction factors (redshift, time dilation) that enter the relation between apparent luminosity $f$ and absolute luminosity $L$ become trivial, reducing $4\pi f = L/d_L^2$ to the classical inverse-square law where $d_L$ coincides with the Euclidean distance.

2. **For $d_A$:** In flat space, the apparent angular size $\Omega$ of an object of intrinsic cross-sectional area $A$ is given by the elementary geometric relation $\Omega = A/d_A^2$, where $d_A$ is simply the Euclidean distance to the object.

Thus, within the neighborhood $U$, one may identify $d_L = d_A = d_N$ where $d_N$ denotes the Newtonian (Euclidean) distance in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

The quantitative bound $d_L < 10^8$ years ensures that the observational region is sufficiently small that the accumulated effects of spacetime curvature remain below the threshold of empirical detectability, so the identification $d_L = d_A = d_N$ holds to well within the limits of contemporary empirical accuracy.
