S. Lie showed that every Poisson manifold is locally (in the neighborhood of a point where the dimension of the symplectic leaves is locally constant, for example, in the neighborhood of a generic point, where the rank is locally maximal) decomposable into the product of a symplectic leaf and a complementary space on which all Poisson brackets are zero.

On such a neighborhood, one may introduce coordinates $p_i, q_i, c_k$ such that $p_i$ and $q_i$ have the usual symplectic Poisson brackets:

$$\{p_i, q_j\} = \delta_{ij}, \quad \{p_i, p_j\} = \{q_i, q_j\} = 0,$$

while the Poisson bracket of each $c_k$ with any function is equal to zero:

$$\{c_k, f\} = 0 \quad \text{for all } f.$$

In physics, the coordinates $p_i, q_i$ are called **Clebsch variables**, while the $c_k$ are called **Casimir functions**.
