Under a Galilean boost with velocity $\mathbf{v}$, coordinates transform as $(t, \mathbf{x}) \mapsto (t, \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{v}t)$. The positions become $\tilde{\varphi}_i(t) = \varphi_i(t) + \mathbf{v}t$, velocities become $\dot{\tilde{\varphi}}_i = \dot{\varphi}_i + \mathbf{v}$, and accelerations are unchanged: $\ddot{\tilde{\varphi}}_i = \ddot{\varphi}_i$. Invariance requires the force to satisfy:

$$\mathbf{F}_i(\{\varphi_j + \mathbf{v}t\}, \{\dot{\varphi}_j + \mathbf{v}\}) = \mathbf{F}_i(\{\varphi_j\}, \{\dot{\varphi}_j\}).$$

Combined with translation invariance (dependence on $\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_k$), this forces $\mathbf{F}_i$ to depend on velocities only through differences $\dot{\mathbf{x}}_j - \dot{\mathbf{x}}_k$, since adding the same vector $\mathbf{v}$ to all velocities must leave the force unchanged.
