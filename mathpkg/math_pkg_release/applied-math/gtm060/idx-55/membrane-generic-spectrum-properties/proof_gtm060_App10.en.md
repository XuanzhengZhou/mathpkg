The kinetic energy $T = \frac{1}{2} \int_D u_t^2 \, dx$ defines a Euclidean (inner product) structure on the configuration space of displacements $u$. The potential energy $U = \frac{1}{2} \int_D (\nabla u)^2 \, dx$ is a quadratic form on this space. The characteristic frequencies are the eigenvalues of $U$ with respect to the Euclidean structure defined by $T$.

By Theorem 1, the codimension of the set of forms with a double eigenvalue is 2, independent of the dimension of the space. Assuming transversality of the manifold of membrane quadratic forms to the submanifold of forms with multiple eigenvalues, the same dimension-counting arguments apply. A generic one-parameter family (codim 2 avoided) has simple spectrum. A two-parameter family generically intersects the double-eigenvalue set at isolated points. The codimension for a triple eigenvalue is $2+3=5$, for four-fold $5+5=10$, etc., following from the parameter count in the proof of Theorem 1.

The claim that the space of simple-spectrum membranes is connected follows from the fact that a submanifold of codimension 2 does not separate the ambient space. Rigorous justification of these arguments for infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces was given by K. Uhlenbeck (Amer. J. Math. 98 (1976), 1059-1078).
