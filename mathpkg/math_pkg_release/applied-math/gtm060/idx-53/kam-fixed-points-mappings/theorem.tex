Consider a canonical (symplectic) mapping of a $2n$-dimensional phase space having a fixed point at the origin. Assume all $2n$ eigenvalues of the linearization at the fixed point have modulus 1 and satisfy no low-order resonance relations:
$$\lambda_1^{k_1} \cdots \lambda_n^{k_n} = 1, \qquad |k_1| + \cdots + |k_n| \leq 4,$$
where the $2n$ eigenvalues are $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n, \bar{\lambda}_1, \ldots, \bar{\lambda}_n$.

After disregarding terms of higher than third order in the Taylor series, the mapping can be written in Birkhoff normal form:
$$(\tau, \varphi) \to (\tau, \varphi + \alpha(\tau)), \qquad \alpha(\tau) = \frac{\partial S}{\partial \tau},$$
where $S = \sum \omega_k \tau_k + \frac{1}{2} \sum \omega_{kl} \tau_k \tau_l$ and $\tau_k = (p_k^2 + q_k^2)/2$, $p_k = \sqrt{2\tau_k} \cos \varphi_k$, $q_k = \sqrt{2\tau_k} \sin \varphi_k$.

The nondegeneracy condition
$$\det |\omega_{kl}| \neq 0$$
guarantees the existence of $n$-dimensional invariant tori (close to $\tau = \text{const}$) forming a set of almost full measure in a sufficiently small neighborhood of the fixed point.

If $n = 1$, the invariant tori become invariant circles. J. Moser first proved stability for plane mappings under the assumption of 333-times differentiability; this was later reduced to 6 derivatives by Moser and Russmann.
