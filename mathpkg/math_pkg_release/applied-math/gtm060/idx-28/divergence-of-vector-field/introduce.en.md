The divergence of a vector field $A$ at a point $x$ measures the infinitesimal outward flux per unit volume: it is the limit of the flux through the boundary of a small parallelepiped divided by its volume, as the parallelepiped shrinks to the point. This quantity, which depends only on $x$ and not on the choice of the test parallelepiped, represents the "source density" of the field. The divergence serves as the motivating example for the general concept of exterior differentiation: just as divergence converts a 2-form (flux) into a 3-form (source density), the exterior derivative $d$ converts a $k$-form into a $(k+1)$-form.
