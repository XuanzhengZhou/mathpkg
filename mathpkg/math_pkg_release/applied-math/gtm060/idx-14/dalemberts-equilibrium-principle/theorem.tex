If the forces of inertia $-m_i \ddot{\mathbf{x}}_i(t)$ are added to the acting forces on a mechanical system, then the position $\mathbf{x}(t)$ becomes an equilibrium position. Equivalently, at each instant $t$, the sum of the work of the actual forces and the inertia forces on any virtual variation is zero:

$$\sum_i \left( -m_i \ddot{\mathbf{x}}_i + \mathbf{f}_i, \xi_i \right) = 0, \quad \forall \{\xi_i\} \in TM_{\mathbf{x}}.$$

In the Newtonian formulation with potential forces $\mathbf{f}_i = -\partial U / \partial \mathbf{x}_i$, this becomes

$$\sum_i \left( -m_i \ddot{\mathbf{x}}_i - \frac{\partial U}{\partial \mathbf{x}_i}, \xi_i \right) = 0,$$

which is exactly the D'Alembert-Lagrange equation.
