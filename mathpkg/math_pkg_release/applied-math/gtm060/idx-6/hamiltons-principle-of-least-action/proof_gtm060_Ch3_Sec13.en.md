As shown in the theorem on Newton's equations as Euler–Lagrange equations, with $L = T - U$, the Euler–Lagrange equations

$$\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{\mathbf{r}}_i} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{r}_i} = 0$$

are equivalent to Newton's equations of motion. By the characterization of extremals via the Euler–Lagrange equation, the physical trajectory is precisely an extremal of the action functional $\int L\, dt$. This is the content of Hamilton's principle. The fact that in many cases the extremum is a minimum (rather than merely a stationary point) follows from the positive definiteness of the kinetic energy quadratic form, which ensures the second variation is positive.
