From the instantaneous angular velocity theorem, $\dot{q} = [\omega, q]$ for all $q \in k$. Points $q$ on the line spanned by $\omega$ satisfy $q = \lambda \omega$ for some $\lambda$, so $[\omega, q] = [\omega, \lambda \omega] = 0$, giving $\dot{q} = 0$. Thus the line through $0$ in the direction of $\omega$ consists of points with zero velocity — this is the instantaneous axis of rotation.

For any point $q$ not on this axis, the velocity $\dot{q} = [\omega, q]$ is orthogonal to both $\omega$ and $q$, hence perpendicular to the axis. The magnitude of the velocity is $|\dot{q}| = |\omega| \cdot |q| \cdot \sin \theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between $q$ and $\omega$, which equals $|\omega| \cdot d$ where $d$ is the perpendicular distance from $q$ to the axis. Thus the velocity magnitude is proportional to the distance from the axis.
