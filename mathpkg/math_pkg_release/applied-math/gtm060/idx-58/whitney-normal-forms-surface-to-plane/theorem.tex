By slightly deforming any smooth mapping from a surface to a plane, one can arrange that in a neighborhood of any point, the mapping is either nonsingular, or structurally similar to the projection of a sphere onto a plane near the equator, or structurally similar to the projection of the surface $x = yz - z^3$ onto the $(x,y)$ plane.

In suitable local coordinates $(x_1, x_2)$ on the pre-image surface and $(y_1, y_2)$ on the image plane, the mapping takes one of the following **normal forms**:

1. **Nonsingular point:**
   $$y_1 = x_1, \quad y_2 = x_2$$

2. **Fold** (as on the equator of the sphere):
   $$y_1 = x_1^2, \quad y_2 = x_2$$

3. **Tuck (cusp)** — a tuck with a cusp on the apparent contour:
   $$y_1 = x_1 x_2 - x_1^3, \quad y_2 = x_2$$

These are the only structurally stable singularities for smooth mappings from a 2-manifold to a 2-manifold. Whitney (1955) proved that all more complicated singularities are removable by a small perturbation.
