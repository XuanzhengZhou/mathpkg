A Poisson structure generalizes the classical Poisson bracket of Hamiltonian mechanics to manifolds where the bracket may be degenerate. While symplectic manifolds correspond to nondegenerate Poisson brackets, general Poisson manifolds can have "Casimir functions" (whose brackets with everything vanish) and symplectic leaves of varying dimensions. Arnold introduces this concept through the example of the angular momentum bracket $\{F, G\} = \sum (\partial F/\partial M_i)(\partial G/\partial M_j)\{M_i, M_j\}$, where the symplectic leaves are concentric spheres (and the origin). Poisson structures are fundamental in modern mathematical physics, particularly in the theory of integrable systems, moment maps, and deformation quantization.
