The proof of this theorem, announced by Poincaré not long before his death, was given only later by G. D. Birkhoff. The argument proceeds by considering a curve of radially shifting points that separates the interior and exterior circles of the annulus. The image of this curve under the mapping must intersect the curve, since the regions into which the curve divides the annulus are carried to regions of equal area. If this curve and its image each intersect each radius once, then the points of intersection of the curve with its image are obviously fixed points of the mapping.

A refinement using generating functions shows that the fixed points correspond to critical points of a suitable function $F(x, y) = S(X(x, y), y)$, where $S$ is the generating function of the symplectic diffeomorphism defined by $Xy + S(X, y)$. The gradient of $F$ is directed either inside the annulus or outside on both boundary circles at once, by the condition on rotation in different directions. Every smooth function on the annulus whose gradient on both boundary circles is directed inward (or outward) has at least two critical points.
