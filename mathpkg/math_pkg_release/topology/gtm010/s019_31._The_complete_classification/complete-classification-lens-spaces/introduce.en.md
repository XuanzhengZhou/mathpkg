The final chapter of the book provides the complete classification of lens spaces up to the three fundamental equivalence relations: homotopy equivalence, simple-homotopy equivalence, and (for $n \geq 3$) homeomorphism. The simple-homotopy classification is the finest: it requires exact matching of the ordered parameters up to permutation, sign, and a common factor. This result demonstrates the power of Whitehead torsion as a geometric invariant — it captures information (the individual $q_i$ parameters) that is invisible to classical homotopy invariants (which only see the product). This makes lens spaces the quintessential example where simple-homotopy theory provides strictly more information than homotopy theory.
