The transfinite line is constructed by replacing each point of a well-ordered set $A$ with a copy of the half-open interval $[0,1)$, ordered lexicographically. When $A = \omega$ (the non-negative integers), this yields a space homeomorphic to $[0,\infty)$. When $A = \Omega_0$ (the set of countable ordinals), the result is the "long line," a classic example of a connected, locally Euclidean, countably compact space that is not metrizable. The transfinite line is an important source of counterexamples in general topology, particularly regarding compactness and metrizability properties.
