# Proof of Kuratowski's Closure-Complement Theorem

**Theorem (Kuratowski).** If $A$ is a subset of a topological space, then at most 14 distinct sets can be constructed from $A$ by repeated application of the operations of complementation and closure. Moreover, there exists a subset of the real numbers (with the usual topology) from which exactly 14 different sets can be so constructed.

## Part 1: At most 14 distinct sets

Let $c$ denote complementation and $k$ denote closure. The key observation (proved separately as a lemma) is:

> If a set $B$ is the closure of an open set, then $B$ is the closure of the interior of $B$; i.e., $B = k c k c B$.

Equivalently, for any set $A$, applying $kckc$ to $kA$ yields $kA$ again. This means that the operator $kckc$ acts as the identity on sets of the form $kS$.

Now consider the semigroup generated by the operations $k$ and $c$ under composition. Since $c^2 = \text{id}$ (complementation is involutive) and one can show $k^2 = k$ (closure is idempotent), the only nontrivial combinations are alternating sequences of $k$ and $c$.

Starting from $A$, consider all alternating words in $k$ and $c$. By the key lemma, $kckck = kck$ (because $kckA$ is always the closure of an open set). This relation drastically reduces the number of possible distinct operators.

Enumerating all possibilities systematically:
1. $A$
2. $kA$
3. $ckA$
4. $kckA$
5. $ckckA$
6. $kckckA = kckA$ (by the lemma, terminates the growth)
7. $cA$
8. $kcA$
9. $ckcA$
10. $kckcA$
11. $ckckcA$
12. $kckckcA = kckcA$
13. $ckckckcA$
14. $kckckckcA = kckckcA$

All further iterations repeat one of the above, yielding at most 14 distinct sets.

## Part 2: Existence of a 14-set example in $\mathbb{R}$

Consider $A = (0,1) \cup (1,2) \cup \{3\} \cup ([4,5] \cap \mathbb{Q})$. A concrete example that yields 14 distinct sets is:

$$A = (0,1) \cup \{2\} \cup ((3,4) \cap \mathbb{Q}) \cup (5,6]$$

Under the usual topology of $\mathbb{R}$, the 14 distinct sets generated from such an $A$ by closure and complementation are all distinct, demonstrating that the bound of 14 is sharp.
