# Proof that Uniform Topologies are Completely Regular (Corollary 6.17)

**Corollary 6.17.** A topology $\mathcal{J}$ for a set $X$ is the uniform topology for some uniformity if and only if $(X, \mathcal{J})$ is completely regular.

**Proof.** ($\Rightarrow$) If $\mathcal{J}$ is the uniform topology for a uniformity $\mathcal{U}$, then by Theorem 6.16, $(X, \mathcal{U})$ is uniformly isomorphic to a subspace of a product of pseudo-metric spaces, hence homeomorphic to a subspace of a product of pseudo-metrizable spaces (equivalently, metric spaces by identification of points at distance zero). By a standard characterization (Section 4.L), a topological space is completely regular if and only if it is homeomorphic to a subspace of a product of pseudo-metrizable spaces. Therefore $(X, \mathcal{J})$ is completely regular.

($\Leftarrow$) If $(X, \mathcal{J})$ is completely regular, then there exists a family $\mathcal{F}$ of continuous real-valued functions on $X$ which separates points from closed sets. Each $f \in \mathcal{F}$ induces a pseudo-metric $d_f(x,y) = |f(x) - f(y)|$. Let $\mathcal{U}$ be the uniformity generated by $\{d_f : f \in \mathcal{F}\}$. The uniform topology of $\mathcal{U}$ coincides with $\mathcal{J}$ because each $f$ is continuous and the family separates points from closed sets.
