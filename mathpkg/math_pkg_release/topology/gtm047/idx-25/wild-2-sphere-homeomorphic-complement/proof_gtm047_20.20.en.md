Consider $A_2$ and $V$ as in the proof of Theorem 4, and let $C^3$ be a $3$-cell neighborhood of $A_2 - \{A\}$, as in the proof of Theorem 2, so that $C^3 \cap \operatorname{Fr} V$ is the union of three $2$-cells whose interiors lie in $\operatorname{Int} C^3$. Since $C^3$ is a "thin, locally straight" neighborhood of $A_2$, the complement of the $2$-sphere constructed from this configuration has the required homeomorphism property. The point $Q$ is the unique point where the $2$-sphere fails to be locally polyhedral, corresponding to the wild point of $A_2$.
