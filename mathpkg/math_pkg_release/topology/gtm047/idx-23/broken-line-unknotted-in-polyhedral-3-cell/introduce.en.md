Theorem 1 of Chapter 19 establishes that every broken line in $\mathbf{R}^3$ is tame: there exists a polyhedral 3-cell containing the broken line in a standard unknotted position. More precisely, for any broken line $B$ with endpoints $P$ and $Q$, one can find a polyhedral 3-cell $C$ such that the interior of $B$ lies in the interior of $C$, the endpoints lie on the boundary, and there is a piecewise-linear homeomorphism sending the pair $(C, B)$ to $(\sigma^2 \times [0,1], R \times [0,1])$. The proof proceeds by induction on the number of edges of $B$. This theorem implies that all broken lines are imbedded in $\mathbf{R}^3$ in essentially the same way -- the concept of knottedness does not apply to broken lines.
