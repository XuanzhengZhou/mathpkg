Since $I$ is connected, there is a broken line $SR'$, lying in $I$ except for its end-points $S \in \text{Int } B$ and $R' \in \text{Int } A$. (See Figure 9.1.) We take $SR'$ in general position relative to $M$, in the sense that no vertex of either set lies in the other. If $SR' \cap M = \emptyset$, this contradicts the hypothesis that $M$ separates $R$ from $S$ in $\bar{I}$. We shall show that if no set $M_i$ is as in the conclusion of the theorem, then the number of points in $SR' \cap M$ can be reduced.

Suppose that the end-points $T, U$ of $M_i$ lie in $PS \subset B$ or in $SQ \subset B$; and suppose that $M_i \cap SR' \neq \emptyset$. Let $TU$ be the broken line in $B$ between $T$ and $U$, and let $J' = (J - TU) \cup M_i$. Since $\text{Int } M_i \subset I$, the interior of $J$, it follows that the interior $I'$ of $J'$ lies in $I$. Let $V$ be the first point of $SR'$ that lies in $M_i$, and let $W$ be the last (in the order from $S$ to $R'$). Consider the broken lines $SV \subset SR'$, $VW \subset M_i$, $WR' \subset SR'$. These form a broken line from $S$ to $R'$. Now $V$ and $W$ lie in $\text{Fr } I'$. Therefore there is a broken line $(SR')'$, lying close to $SV \cup VW \cup WR'$, with its interior in $I' \subset I$. When we pass from $SR'$ to $(SR')'$, the number of points in $SR' \cap M$ is reduced by at least two.

By repeating this process finitely many times, we eventually obtain a broken line from $S$ to $R'$ that does not intersect $M$, contradicting the hypothesis that $M$ separates $R$ from $S$. Therefore, at some stage, we must encounter an $M_i$ whose end-points separate $R$ from $S$ in $J$ (i.e., one endpoint lies in the arc of $J$ from $P$ to $S$ and the other in the arc from $S$ to $Q$, or similar configurations that force separation).
