The notion of tameness, introduced by E. Artin and R. H. Fox, distinguishes between triangulable sets in $\mathbf{R}^n$ that can be straightened to polyhedra by a global homeomorphism of the ambient space (tame) and those that cannot (wild). The definition generalizes to sets $M$ in any complex $K$ via barycentric coordinates. Tameness is relative to the ambient complex structure. In $\mathbf{R}^2$ the theory is trivial: every triangulable set is tame; in $\mathbf{R}^3$, arcs, $1$-spheres, and $2$-spheres may be wild.
