let $W_1$ be the open neighborhood of $y_0 \in Y$ and $W_2$ of $z_0 \in F$ such that $q_1(W_1 \times W_2) \subset W$. There exists a symmetric open neighborhood $N$ of $1 \in G$ and a neighborhood $V$ of $z_0$ in $F$ such that $NV \subset W_2$. Let $U$ be an open neighborhood of $x_0$ in $X$ such that $p(U) \subset q(W_1)$ and $r((U \times U) \cap X^*) \subset N$. We replace $W_1$ by $W_1 \cap q^{-1}(p(U)) \cap f^{-1}(U)$ and $U$ by $p^{-1}(q(W)) \cap U$. Then we have $p(U) = q(W_1)$ and $f(W_1) \subset U$. For each $(x,z) \in U \times V$, there exists $y \in W_1$ such that $f(y) \in U$ and $f(y) = xs$. This means that $s \in N$, and $g((y,s^{-1}z)H) = (f(y),s^{-1}z)G = (x,z)G$. Therefore, we have $g(W) \supset g(q_1(W_1 \times W_2)) \supset p_1(U \times V)$, and $g(W)$ is an open set. This proves the theorem.
