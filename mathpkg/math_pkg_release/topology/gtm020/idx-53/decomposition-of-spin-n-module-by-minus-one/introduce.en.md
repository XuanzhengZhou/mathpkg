For a $\text{Spin}(n)$-module $M$, the element $-1 \in \text{Spin}(n)$ satisfies $(-1)^2 = +1$, so it acts as an involution on $M$. This proposition decomposes $M$ into the $+1$-eigenspace $M_1$ and the $(-1)$-eigenspace $M_2$. The $+1$-eigenspace $M_1$ factorizes through the natural homomorphism $\phi: \text{Spin}(n) \to SO(n)$, making it an $SO(n)$-module, while the $(-1)$-eigenspace $M_2$ carries a $C_{n-1}$-module structure via the inclusion $\text{Spin}(n) \subset C_n^0$ and the isomorphism $C_n^0 \cong C_{n-1}$. This classification separates $\text{Spin}(n)$-modules into those coming from $SO(n)$ and those coming from Clifford modules.
