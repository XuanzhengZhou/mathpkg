Clearly, $\alpha(x_1)\alpha(x_2) = \beta(x_1)\beta(x_2)$. Moreover, if $y$ is orthogonal to $x_1$ and $x_2$, we have $R(x_1, x_2)^2y = \alpha(x_1)\alpha(x_2)y = \beta(x_1)\beta(x_2)y = y$. It suffices to show that $R(x_1, x_2)^2x_2 = \beta(x_1)\beta(x_2)x_2 = \beta(x_1)x_2$ equal the image of $x_2$ by a rotation of $180^\circ$ around $x_1$. But this is the image of $x_2$ under a rotation through $x_1$ by an angle twice the angle between $x_1$ and $x_2$.
