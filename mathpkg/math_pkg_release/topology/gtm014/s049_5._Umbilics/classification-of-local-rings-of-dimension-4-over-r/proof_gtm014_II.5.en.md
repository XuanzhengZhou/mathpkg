Let $\mathcal{M}$ be the maximal ideal of $\mathcal{R}$.

Suppose first that $\dim_{\mathbf{R}} \mathcal{M}/\mathcal{M}^2 = 1$. Let $t$ be an element of $\mathcal{M} \setminus \mathcal{M}^2$. Then $\mathcal{M}^i = \{c t^i\} + \mathcal{M}^{i+1}$, so the mapping of $\mathbf{R}[t]$ into $\mathcal{R}$ is onto. The kernel is generated by a polynomial $p(t)$, which we can write as $t^k q(t)$, with $q(t)$ having a nonzero constant term. The image of $q(t)$ in $\mathcal{R}$ is invertible, so the kernel is also generated by $t^k$. Hence $\mathcal{R}$ is isomorphic to $\mathbf{R}[t]/(t^k)$.

Now suppose $\dim_{\mathbf{R}} \mathcal{M}/\mathcal{M}^2 = 2$. Let $x$ and $y$ project to a basis of $\mathcal{M}/\mathcal{M}^2$. Consider the quadratic form (5.8) on $\mathcal{M}/\mathcal{M}^2$. By an appropriate linear change of coordinates, one can arrange that the relations are $x^2 = y^2$ and $x y = 0$. Therefore, $\mathcal{R}$ is isomorphic to the polynomial ring in two variables divided by the ideal of relations $(x^2 - y^2, xy)$. A similar argument shows that if the quadratic form (5.8) is nondegenerate and indefinite then $\mathcal{R}$ is the ring (5.3), and if (5.8) is degenerate, $\mathcal{R}$ is the ring (5.4).

Finally, if $\dim_{\mathbf{R}} \mathcal{M}/\mathcal{M}^2 = 3$, then $\mathcal{M}^2 = 0$ by (5.7) and $\mathcal{R}$ is the ring (5.5).
