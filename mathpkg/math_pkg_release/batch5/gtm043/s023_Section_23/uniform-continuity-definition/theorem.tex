If $\varphi$ is a mapping from $[X; \mathcal{D}]$ to $[Y; \mathcal{E}]$, then for any $e \in \mathcal{E}$, the function $(x, x') \mapsto e(\varphi x, \varphi x')$ is a pseudometric on $X$. If for every $e \in \mathcal{E}$, this pseudometric belongs to $\mathcal{D}$, then $\varphi$ is said to be \textit{uniformly continuous}.

In terms of bases: $\varphi$ is uniformly continuous if and only if, for each $e \in \mathcal{E}'$ and $\epsilon > 0$, there exist $d \in \mathcal{D}'$ and $\delta > 0$ such that $d(x, x') \leq \delta$ implies $e(\varphi x, \varphi x') \leq \epsilon$.

A one-one, uniformly continuous mapping whose inverse is also uniformly continuous is called a \textit{uniform isomorphism}.
