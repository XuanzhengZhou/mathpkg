Let $t$ be a homomorphism of $C(Y)$ into $C(X)$ with the property
that $t1 = 1$. If $Y$ is realcompact, then there exists a unique
continuous mapping $\tau$ of $X$ into $Y$ such that $t = \tau'$.

For each point $x$ of $X$, the mapping $g \rightarrow (tg)(x)$
is a homomorphism from $C(Y)$ into $\mathbf{R}$;
and, since $(t1)(x) = 1(x) \neq 0$, it is not the zero homomorphism.
Since $Y$ is realcompact, by 10.5(c) there is a point
$\tau x$ of $Y$ such that $(tg)(x) = g(\tau x)$
for all $g$ in $C(Y)$.
The mapping $\tau$ from $X$ into $Y$, thus defined,
evidently satisfies $tg = g \circ \tau$, for each $g$.
Since $tg$ is a continuous function,
Theorem 3.8 shows that $\tau$ is continuous.
As observed in 10.2, $\tau$ is the unique continuous mapping
for which $\tau' = t$.
