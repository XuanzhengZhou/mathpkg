**Proof of Theorem 10.6.**

For each point $x$ of $X$, the mapping $g \rightarrow (tg)(x)$ is a homomorphism from $C(Y)$ into $\mathbf{R}$; and, since $(t1)(x) = 1(x) \neq 0$, it is not the zero homomorphism. By 10.5(c), since $Y$ is realcompact, there is a point $\tau x$ of $Y$ such that $(tg)(x) = g(\tau x)$ for all $g$ in $C(Y)$. The mapping $\tau$ from $X$ into $Y$, thus defined, evidently satisfies $tg = g \circ \tau$, for each $g$. Since $tg$ is a continuous function, Theorem 3.8 shows that $\tau$ is continuous. As observed in 10.2, $\tau$ is the unique continuous mapping for which $\tau' = t$.
