Any nonempty zero-set in $\beta X$ that does not intersect $X$ must contain a topological copy of $\beta N$, the Stone-Čech compactification of the natural numbers. Consequently, its cardinality is at least the cardinality of $\beta N$, namely $2^{\mathfrak{c}}$. This result, Theorem 9.5, sharpens the general evaluation of the cardinality of $\beta X$ for an infinite discrete space by showing that the "growth" $\beta X \setminus X$ is always large, containing at least the entire structure of $\beta N$.
