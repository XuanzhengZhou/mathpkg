Every realcompact space $X$ is complete in $\mathcal{C}(X)$. Hence, if $X$ is realcompact, then it is complete in every admissible structure that contains $\mathcal{C}$. Shirota theorem states a sort of converse: barring measurable cardinals, if there exists any admissible structure in which $X$ is complete, then $X$ is also complete in $\mathcal{C}$.

Let us apply Theorem 15.21 to the structure $\mathcal{C}$. Since condition (1) is satisfied, so is (3): for $d \in \mathcal{C}$, $X$ is a nonmeasurable union of zero-sets of $d$-diameter less than any prescribed gauge.

The force of this result shows up particularly clearly in the case of discrete spaces. By definition, the discrete structure $U$ is that generated by the metric $u$ for which $u(x, y) = 1$ whenever $x \neq y$. It is manifest that $U$ contains every pseudometric on the space, and therefore is the largest structure; in particular, $U \supset \mathcal{C}$. Since the sets of $u$-diameter $< 1$ are single points, this shows that if the space $X$ is not countable, $\mathcal{C}$ must fall vastly short of $U$: for discrete $X$, $\mathcal{C} = U$ if and only if $|X| \leq \aleph_0$. Yet, Shirota theorem tells us that if the cardinal of $X$ is arbitrary, so long as it is not measurable, then, because $X$ is complete in $U$, it is also complete in $\mathcal{C}$.
