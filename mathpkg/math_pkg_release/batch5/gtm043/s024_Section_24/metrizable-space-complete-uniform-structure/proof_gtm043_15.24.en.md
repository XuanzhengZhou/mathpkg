It is clear, first of all, that the completion of a metric space $[X; D]$ is metric: if $d$ generates $D$, then $d^\gamma$ generates the uniform structure on $\gamma X$.

Now consider any metric space $X$ with metric structure $D$. By Shirota theorem (15.23), it suffices to show that $X$ is complete in some admissible structure. The metric structure $D$ itself need not be complete, but its completion $\gamma X$ is a complete metric space containing $X$ as a dense subspace. The restriction of the uniform structure of $\gamma X$ to $X$ gives an admissible structure on $X$ in which $X$ is complete. Hence, if the cardinal of $X$ is nonmeasurable, Shirota theorem implies that $X$ is complete in $\mathcal{C}(X)$, i.e., $X$ is realcompact.
