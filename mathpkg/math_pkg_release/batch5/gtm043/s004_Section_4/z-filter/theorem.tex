A nonempty subfamily $\mathcal{F}$ of $Z(X)$ is called a \text{$z$-filter} on $X$ provided that

(i) $\emptyset \notin \mathcal{F}$;
(ii) if $Z_1, Z_2 \in \mathcal{F}$, then $Z_1 \cap Z_2 \in \mathcal{F}$;
(iii) if $Z \in \mathcal{F}$, $Z' \in Z(X)$, and $Z' \supset Z$, then $Z' \in \mathcal{F}$.

By (iii), $X$ belongs to every $z$-filter. Because of (iii), (ii) may be replaced by: if $Z_1, Z_2 \in \mathcal{F}$, then $Z_1 \cap Z_2$ contains a member of $\mathcal{F}$.

Every family $\mathcal{B}$ of zero-sets that has the finite intersection property is contained in a $z$-filter: the smallest such is the family of all zero-sets containing finite intersections of members of $\mathcal{B}$. $\mathcal{B}$ generates the $z$-filter. When $\mathcal{B}$ itself is closed under finite intersection, it is called a base for the $z$-filter.

In any space $X$, the intersection with $Z(X)$ of any filter is a $z$-filter. Conversely, if $\mathcal{F}'$ is the smallest filter containing a given $z$-filter $\mathcal{F}$, then $\mathcal{F}' \cap Z(X) = \mathcal{F}$.
