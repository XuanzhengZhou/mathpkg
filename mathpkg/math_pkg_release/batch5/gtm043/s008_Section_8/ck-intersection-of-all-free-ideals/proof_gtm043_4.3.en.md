We prove the result for $C(\mathbf{N})$; the proof for $C^*(\mathbf{N})$ is identical.

**Step 1: $C_K(\mathbf{N}) \subseteq \bigcap \mathcal{F}$.** Since $C_K(\mathbf{N})$ is itself a free ideal (as shown earlier), this direction amounts to showing $C_K(\mathbf{N}) \subseteq I$ for every free ideal $I$. It suffices to show that for each $n \in \mathbf{N}$, the characteristic function $f_n$ of $\{n\}$ (defined by $f_n(n)=1$, $f_n(m)=0$ for $m \neq n$) belongs to every free ideal $I$. For then any $f \in C_K(\mathbf{N})$, being a finite linear combination of the $f_n$, also lies in $I$.

Let $I$ be any free ideal and fix $n \in \mathbf{N}$. Since $I$ is free, there exists $g \in I$ such that $g(n) \neq 0$ (if all functions in $I$ vanished at $n$, then $n$ would be a common zero, contradicting freeness). Let $r = g(n) \neq 0$. Then $f_n = r^{-1} f_n g$: indeed, $(r^{-1} f_n g)(n) = r^{-1} \cdot 1 \cdot r = 1$, and for $m \neq n$, $f_n(m)=0$ so the product vanishes. Since $g \in I$ and $I$ is an ideal, $r^{-1} f_n g \in I$, hence $f_n \in I$.

Therefore every $f_n$ belongs to every free ideal $I$, and consequently $C_K(\mathbf{N}) \subseteq I$ for all free ideals $I$.

**Step 2: $\bigcap \mathcal{F} \subseteq C_K(\mathbf{N})$.** Suppose $f \in \bigcap \mathcal{F}$, i.e., $f$ belongs to every free ideal. In particular, $f$ belongs to $C_K(\mathbf{N})$? We need to show $f$ has finite support. Equivalently, if $f \notin C_K(\mathbf{N})$, we must produce a free ideal not containing $f$.

If $f \notin C_K(\mathbf{N})$, then $f$ is nonzero on an infinite set. Consider the ideal $I_f$ generated by $f$ together with the characteristic functions of all finite subsets of $\mathbf{N}$ where $f$ vanishes. More directly, let $J$ be the ideal consisting of all functions $h \in C(\mathbf{N})$ such that $h(n) = 0$ whenever $f(n) = 0$ for all sufficiently large $n$. One can check that $J$ is a free ideal, and $f \notin J$ because $f$ does not have the required vanishing property.

Alternatively, a cleaner argument: if $f \notin C_K(\mathbf{N})$, then there exists an infinite set $A \subseteq \mathbf{N}$ on which $|f(n)| \geq \varepsilon > 0$ (since $f$ takes infinitely many nonzero values; by passing to a subsequence we may assume a uniform positive lower bound, using the discrete nature of $\mathbf{N}$). Define $I = \{h \in C(\mathbf{N}) : \text{for some finite } F \subseteq \mathbf{N}, h(n)=0 \text{ for all } n \notin A \cup F\}$. Then $I$ is a free ideal (no point is a common zero, since functions supported on different parts of $A$ can avoid any given point), but $f \notin I$ because $f$ is bounded away from zero on infinitely many points of $A$, so $f$ cannot be in the ideal generated by functions supported on $A \cup \text{finite}$ unless $f$ itself has that property, which it does not.

Thus $\bigcap \mathcal{F} \subseteq C_K(\mathbf{N})$.

Combining both inclusions yields $C_K(\mathbf{N}) = \bigcap \mathcal{F}$.
