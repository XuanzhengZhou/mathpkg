A surprising structural fact: every free ideal, despite having no common zero for all its members, necessarily contains nontrivial fixed sub-ideals. The proof is constructive — if a nonzero $f$ in the free ideal happens to have a nonempty zero-set, its principal ideal already works. Otherwise $f$ is everywhere nonzero, and the construction $h = f(f-r)$ yields an element with a zero, generating a nonzero fixed ideal inside the original free ideal. This result holds even when the ideal is contained in $C^*(X)$.
