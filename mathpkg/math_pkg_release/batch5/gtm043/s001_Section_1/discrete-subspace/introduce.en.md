A discrete subspace is a subset of a topological space that, when equipped with the relative topology, becomes a discrete space — every singleton is open in the relative topology. Importantly, a discrete subspace need not be closed in the ambient space. The standard example is the set $\{1/n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$ in $\mathbb{R}$: it is discrete because each point can be isolated by a small interval, but its closure includes the limit point $0$, so it is not closed.
