One proves by induction that $W(\alpha)$ is compact for every $\alpha$. Having established this, it follows that for any $\sigma < \alpha$, the subspace $W(\sigma + 1)$ (which is homeomorphic to a well-ordered space of a successor ordinal) is compact, thus providing a compact neighborhood for $\sigma$. Hence $W(\alpha)$ is locally compact. If $W(\alpha)$ is not compact, then $\alpha$ must be a limit ordinal, and $W(\alpha + 1)$ adds exactly one point, making the space compact. Thus $W(\alpha + 1)$ is the one-point compactification of $W(\alpha)$.
