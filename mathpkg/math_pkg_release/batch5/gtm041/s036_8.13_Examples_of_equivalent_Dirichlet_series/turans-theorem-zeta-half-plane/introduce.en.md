Turán's theorem provides a conditional proof that the Riemann hypothesis holds (i.e., $\zeta(s) \neq 0$ for $\sigma > 1/2$) under the assumption that the summatory function $C(x) = \sum_{n \leq x} \lambda(n)/n$ of Liouville's function is eventually nonnegative. The proof uses Bohr's equivalence theorem to relate the partial zeta sums $\zeta_n(s)$ to the Liouville function, Landau's theorem on Dirichlet series with nonnegative coefficients, and the integral representation from Theorem 8.19. Although Haselgrove later showed that $C(x)$ does change sign infinitely often, a modified argument (Theorem 8.21) with a small positive perturbation still yields the same conclusion.
