Morris Newman discovered in 1958 a congruence of a different type for the prime $p = 13$, since no congruence of the Lehner type (i.e., $c(13^\alpha n) \equiv 0$ mod some power of $13$) can exist — it is known that $c(13)$ is not divisible by $13$. Newman's congruence involves a quadratic relation among coefficients $c(13np)$, $c(13n)$, $c(13p)$, and $c(13n/p)$, with the modular inverse $p^{-1}$ modulo $13$ appearing as a coefficient. This type of congruence was later generalized by Atkin and O'Brien in 1967.
