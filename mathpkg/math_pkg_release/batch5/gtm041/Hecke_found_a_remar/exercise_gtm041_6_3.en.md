Let $f_1, \ldots, f_k$ be $k$ distinct nonzero $\alpha$-multiplicative functions. If a linear combination
$$f = \sum_{i=1}^{k} c_i f_i$$
is also $\alpha$-multiplicative, prove that:

(a) The functions $f_1, \ldots, f_k$ are linearly independent.

(b) Either all the $c_i$ are $0$ or else exactly one of the $c_i$ is $1$ and the others are $0$. Hence either $f = 0$ or $f = f_i$ for some $i$. In other words, linear combinations of $\alpha$-multiplicative functions are never $\alpha$-multiplicative except for trivial cases.
