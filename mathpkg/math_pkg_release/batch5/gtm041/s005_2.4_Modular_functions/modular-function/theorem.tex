A function $f$ is said to be \textbf{modular} if it satisfies the following three conditions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item[(a)] $f$ is meromorphic in the upper half-plane $H$.
\item[(b)] $f(A\tau) = f(\tau)$ for every $A$ in the modular group $\Gamma$.
\item[(c)] The Fourier expansion of $f$ has the form
$$f(\tau) = \sum_{n=-m}^{\infty} a(n)e^{2\pi i n \tau}.$$
\end{enumerate}

Property (a) states that $f$ is analytic in $H$ except possibly for poles. Property (b) states that $f$ is invariant under all transformations of $\Gamma$. Property (c) is a condition on the behavior of $f$ at the point $\tau = i\infty$. If $x = e^{2\pi i \tau}$ the Fourier series in (c) is a Laurent expansion in powers of $x$. The behavior of $f$ at $i\infty$ is described by the nature of this Laurent expansion near $0$. If $m > 0$ and $a(-m) \neq 0$ we say that $f$ has a pole of order $m$ at $i\infty$. If $m \leq 0$ we say $f$ is analytic at $i\infty$. Condition (c) states that $f$ has at worst a pole of order $m$ at $i\infty$.
