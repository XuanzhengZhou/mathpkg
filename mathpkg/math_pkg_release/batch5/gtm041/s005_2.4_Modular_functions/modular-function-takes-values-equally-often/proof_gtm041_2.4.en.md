Apply Theorem 2.4 to the function $f - c$. Since $f$ is modular (meromorphic in $H$, invariant under $\Gamma$, and possessing a Fourier expansion with at worst a pole at $i\infty$), the function $f - c$ satisfies the same three conditions: it is meromorphic in $H$, invariant under $\Gamma$, and its Fourier expansion differs from that of $f$ only in the constant term, so it also has at worst a pole at $i\infty$. Thus $f - c$ is modular. By Theorem 2.4, the number of zeros of $f - c$ equals the number of its poles in the closure of $R_{\Gamma}$. The zeros of $f - c$ are precisely the points where $f$ takes the value $c$, and the poles of $f - c$ are precisely the poles of $f$. Therefore $f$ takes the value $c$ as often as it takes the value $\infty$ (has poles) in the closure of $R_{\Gamma}$. Since this holds for every complex $c$, $f$ takes on every value equally often.
