Let $\Lambda = \{\lambda(n)\}$ be an infinite sequence of distinct real numbers. A \textit{basis} of the set $\Lambda$ is a finite or countably infinite sequence $B = \{\beta(n)\}$ of real numbers satisfying the following three conditions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item[(a)] The sequence $B$ is linearly independent over the rationals. That is, for all $m \geq 1$, if
$$\sum_{k=1}^{m} r_k \beta(k) = 0$$
with rational multipliers $r_k$, then each $r_k = 0$.

\item[(b)] Each $\lambda(n)$ is expressible as a finite linear combination of terms of $B$, say
$$\lambda(n) = \sum_{k=1}^{q(n)} r_{n,k} \beta(k)$$
where the $r_{n,k}$ are rational and the number of summands $q(n)$ depends on $n$. (By condition (a), if $\lambda(n) \neq 0$ this representation is unique.)

\item[(c)] Each $\beta(n)$ is expressible as a finite linear combination of terms of $\Lambda$, say
$$\beta(n) = \sum_{k=1}^{m(n)} t_{n,k} \lambda(k)$$
where the $t_{n,k}$ are rational and $m(n)$ depends on $n$.
\end{enumerate}
