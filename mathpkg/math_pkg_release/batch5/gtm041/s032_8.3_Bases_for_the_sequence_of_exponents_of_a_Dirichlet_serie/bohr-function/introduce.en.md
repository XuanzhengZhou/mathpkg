The Bohr function $F(Z)$ lifts a Dirichlet series $f(s) = \sum a(n)e^{-s\lambda(n)}$ to a function of countably many complex variables. By choosing a basis $B = \{\beta(n)\}$ for the exponents and writing $\Lambda = RB$ with a Bohr matrix $R$, each exponent $\lambda(n)$ becomes a rational linear combination of the variables $z_k$, so that $F(z_1, z_2, \ldots) = \sum a(n) \exp(-\sum_k r_{n,k} z_k)$. The original Dirichlet series is recovered along the diagonal $z_k = s \beta(k)$. This construction is the central tool in Harald Bohr's theory of equivalence of general Dirichlet series.
