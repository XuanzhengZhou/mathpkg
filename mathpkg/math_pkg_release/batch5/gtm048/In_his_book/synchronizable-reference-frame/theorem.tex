Let $Q$ be a reference frame on a spacetime $(M, g, D)$, and let $\omega$ be the 1-form physically equivalent to $Q$ (i.e., $\omega(X) = g(Q, X)$ for all vector fields $X$). Then $Q$ is called:

(a) **locally synchronizable** iff $\omega \wedge d\omega = 0$;

(b) **locally proper time synchronizable** iff $d\omega = 0$;

(c) **synchronizable** iff there exist $C^\infty$ functions $h > 0$ and $t$ on $M$ such that $\omega = -h \, dt$; any such $t$ is called a **time function** for $Q$;

(d) **proper time synchronizable** iff there exists a $C^\infty$ function $t$ on $M$ such that $\omega = -dt$; any such $t$ is called a **proper time function** for $Q$.

The implications are: proper time synchronizable $\Rightarrow$ synchronizable $\Rightarrow$ locally synchronizable; proper time synchronizable $\Rightarrow$ locally proper time synchronizable. Conversely, if $Q$ is locally synchronizable (resp. locally proper time synchronizable), then the restriction of $Q$ to every sufficiently small open set is synchronizable (resp. proper time synchronizable).
