Let $V$ be the vector field physically equivalent to $-dv$. Then $g(V, V) = 0$ and $g(U, V) < 0$, where $U$ is the future-pointing timelike vector field used to time-orient $K$. Thus $V$ is future-pointing, lightlike, and nowhere parallel to $U$.

We compute the derivative of $r$ along $\gamma$. On $M_{II}$, the differential of $r$ is given by
$$dr = 2\mu\left(1 - \frac{2\mu}{r}\right)\left(\frac{dv}{v} + \frac{du}{u}\right).$$

In the black hole region $M_{II}$, we have $r < 2\mu$, hence $1 - 2\mu/r < 0$. The vector field $V = -dv$ evaluates as $dv(V) = -1$. Combined with the fact that $\gamma$ is future-directed, this implies that $(d/ds)(r \circ \gamma) < 0$ for all $s \in \hat{\mathcal{E}}$, provided $\gamma$ remains in $M_{II}$.

Since $r$ is strictly decreasing along $\gamma$ once inside $M_{II}$, the trajectory cannot cross back to the surface $r = 2\mu$ (the event horizon), which forms the boundary of $M_{II}$. Therefore $\gamma(s) \in M_{II}$ for all $s \geq s_0$, i.e., $\gamma(\hat{\mathcal{E}}) \subset M_{II}$.

Physically, this means that even a photon going directly outward cannot escape the black hole region to reach infinity. The gravity in $M_{II}$ is so strong that all future-directed causal curves are trapped.
