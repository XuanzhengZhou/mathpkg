The luminosity distance $d_L$ is an operational distance measure in cosmology, abstracted from the elementary result that the apparent brightness of a small distant light source is inversely proportional to the square of the distance. Given an observer $(z, Z)$ measuring apparent luminosity $f$, and assuming the absolute luminosity $L$ is approximately known, $d_L$ is defined by the relation $4\pi f = L/d_L^2$. It is one of several distance concepts employed in general relativistic cosmology, alongside the angular diameter distance $d_A$ and the Newtonian distance $d_N$; under appropriate local conditions these three coincide.
