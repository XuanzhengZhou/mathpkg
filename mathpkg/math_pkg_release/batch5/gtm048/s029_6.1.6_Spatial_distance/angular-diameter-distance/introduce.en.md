The angular diameter distance $d_A$ is a cosmological distance measure based on the apparent angular size of a distant object. Given a galaxy whose intrinsic cross-sectional area $A$ is approximately known, and measuring its apparent angular size $\Omega$ subtended on the observer's sky, the angular diameter distance is defined by the relation $\Omega = A/d_A^2$. Together with the luminosity distance $d_L$, it is one of the fundamental distance measures in observational cosmology; in general relativistic spacetimes $d_A$ and $d_L$ generally differ, but they coincide with the Newtonian distance $d_N$ in sufficiently small neighborhoods where spacetime curvature is negligible.
