The frequency ratio is a coordinate-invariant way to compare the frequencies measured by two instantaneous observers at the emission and reception events of a light signal. Given a freely falling photon $\lambda$ and instantaneous observers at its endpoints, each observer measures a frequency $f_A = -g(\lambda_* A, Z_A)/h$. The ratio $r = f_a/f_b$ turns out to be independent of which representative photon is chosen within the light signal $[\lambda]$. This quantity is central to understanding Doppler effects, cosmological redshifts, and gravitational redshifts in general relativity without relying on global parallelism or relative velocity concepts that may not be available in curved spacetimes.
