In Section 8.1.4 show exactly one of the following holds. (Hint: one proof uses Jordan normal forms for the endomorphisms of a vector space over $\mathbb{C}$.)

(a) There is an orthonormal basis $(X_1, X_2, X_3, X_4)$ for $M_z$ such that $\hat{T} = \sum_{\alpha=1}^{3} p_\alpha X_\alpha \otimes X_\alpha + \rho X_4 \otimes X_4$, where $p_1, p_2, p_3, \rho \in \mathbb{R}$.

(b) There is a basis as in (a) such that
$$\hat{T} = \sum_{i=1}^{4} \lambda_i X_i \otimes X_i + q(X_3 \otimes X_4 + X_4 \otimes X_3),$$
where $\lambda_i, q \in \mathbb{R}$ and $(\lambda_4 - \lambda_3)^2 < 4q^2$.

(c) There is an ordered basis $(X_1, X_2, Y_1, Y_2)$ such that: $\forall A \in \{1, 2\}$, $X_A$ is unit spacelike and $Y_A$ is lightlike; $X_1$, $X_2$, and $\operatorname{span}\{Y_1, Y_2\}$ are pairwise orthogonal; $g(Y_1, Y_2) = \pm 1$; and the matrix representing $\hat{T}$ in this basis is
$$\hat{T} \sim \begin{bmatrix}
\lambda_1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & \lambda_2 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 0 & \lambda_3 & 1 \\
0 & 0 & 0 & \lambda_3
\end{bmatrix}, \quad \lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3 \in \mathbb{R}.$$

(d) There is a basis as in (c) such that
$$\hat{T} \sim \begin{bmatrix}
\lambda_1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & \lambda_2 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 0 & \lambda_3 & 0 \\
0 & 0 & 0 & \lambda_4
\end{bmatrix}, \quad \lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3, \lambda_4 \in \mathbb{R}.$$
