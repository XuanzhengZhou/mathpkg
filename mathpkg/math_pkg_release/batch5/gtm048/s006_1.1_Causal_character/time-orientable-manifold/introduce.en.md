A Lorentzian manifold $(M, g)$ is time orientable if the bundle $\mathcal{T}$ of timelike tangent vectors splits into two connected components. Intuitively, this means one can consistently distinguish "future-pointing" from "past-pointing" timelike vectors across the entire manifold. Time orientability depends on the Lorentzian metric $g$, not just the smooth structure: a manifold may admit both time-orientable and non-time-orientable Lorentzian metrics. However, if $M$ is simply connected, every Lorentzian metric on $M$ is time orientable.
