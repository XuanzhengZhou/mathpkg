In an $N$-dimensional Lorentzian vector space $(V, g)$, the metric $g$ is non-degenerate but indefinite, having signature $(-,+,\ldots,+)$ or $(+,-,\ldots,-)$. Unlike the Euclidean case, subspaces can have three qualitatively different behaviors depending on the sign of $g$ restricted to them. A subspace is called spacelike if $g$ is positive definite on it, lightlike if $g$ is positive semi-definite but degenerate, and timelike otherwise (i.e., if $g$ attains negative values on the subspace). For a single vector, the causal character is that of its span, and a vector is called causal if it is not spacelike. This fundamental trichotomy underlies all of Lorentzian geometry and general relativity, governing the structure of lightcones, the classification of curves, and the causal ordering of spacetime events.
