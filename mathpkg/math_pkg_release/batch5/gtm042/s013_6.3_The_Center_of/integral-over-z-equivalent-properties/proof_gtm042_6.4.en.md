$(ii) \Rightarrow (iii)$: Trivial, since $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ itself is a finitely generated submodule containing $\mathbb{Z}[x]$.

$(iii) \Rightarrow (ii)$: Since $\mathbb{Z}$ is Noetherian, any submodule of a finitely generated $\mathbb{Z}$-module is finitely generated. As $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ is a submodule of the finitely generated module given by (iii), $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ is finitely generated over $\mathbb{Z}$.

$(i) \Rightarrow (ii)$: If $x$ satisfies $x^n + a_1 x^{n-1} + \cdots + a_n = 0$ with $a_i \in \mathbb{Z}$, then the sub-$\mathbb{Z}$-module generated by $\{1, x, \ldots, x^{n-1}\}$ is stable under multiplication by $x$ (since $x^n$ is a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combination of lower powers). By induction, all powers of $x$ lie in this submodule, so it coincides with $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, which is thus generated by $n$ elements.

$(ii) \Rightarrow (i)$: Let $R_n$ be the sub-$\mathbb{Z}$-module generated by $1, x, \ldots, x^{n-1}$. The $R_n$ form an increasing sequence whose union is $\mathbb{Z}[x]$. Since $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ is finitely generated, the sequence stabilizes: $\mathbb{Z}[x] = R_n$ for some $n$. Then $x^n \in R_n$, giving a relation $x^n = -\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} a_i x^i$ with $a_i \in \mathbb{Z}$, so $x$ is integral over $\mathbb{Z}$.
