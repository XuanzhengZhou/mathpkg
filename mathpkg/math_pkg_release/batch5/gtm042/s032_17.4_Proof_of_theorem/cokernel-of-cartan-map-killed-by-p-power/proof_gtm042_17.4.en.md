Let $p^n$ be the largest power of $p$ dividing the order of $G$. We distinguish two cases.

**(a) $K$ is sufficiently large**

By the corollary to Theorem 39, $R_k(G)$ is generated by the $\operatorname{Ind}_H^G(R_k(H))$ with $H$ elementary. We are thus reduced to the case where $G$ is elementary, hence decomposes as a product $S \times P$, where $S$ has order prime to $p$ and $P$ is a $p$-group. We have seen in Section 15.7 that the Cartan matrix of such a group is the scalar matrix $p^n$. The theorem follows in this case.

**(b) General case**

Let $K'$ be a finite sufficiently large extension of $K$, with residue field $k'$. Scalar extension from $k$ to $k'$ gives us a commutative diagram:

$$
\begin{CD}
0 @>>> P_k(G) @>>> P_{k'}(G) @>>> P @>>> 0 \\
@VVV @VV{c}V @VV{c'}V @VV{\gamma}V \\
0 @>>> R_k(G) @>>> R_{k'}(G) @>>> R @>>> 0,
\end{CD}
$$

where $P = P_{k'}(G)/P_k(G)$ and $R = R_{k'}(G)/R_k(G)$. Whence the exact sequence:

$$
0 \to \operatorname{Ker}(c) \to \operatorname{Ker}(c') \to \operatorname{Ker}(\gamma) \to \operatorname{Coker}(c) \to \operatorname{Coker}(c') \to \cdots
$$

Since $K'$ is sufficiently large, case (a) shows that $p^n \cdot \operatorname{Coker}(c') = 0$. The exact sequence then implies that $p^n \cdot \operatorname{Coker}(c) = 0$, completing the proof.
