The argument used in the proof of Theorem 39 applies to many other situations (cf. Swan [21], Sections 3-4). In particular, it gives the analogue of Artin's theorem.

Starting from Artin's theorem (Theorem 26) in the ordinary case, which states that

$$Q \otimes \text{Ind} : \bigoplus_{H \in T} Q \otimes R_K(H) \rightarrow Q \otimes R_K(G)$$

is surjective, one writes the identity element in $Q \otimes R_K(G)$ as a rational linear combination of characters induced from cyclic subgroups. Applying the map $d \otimes Q$ (which commutes with induction) yields an analogous decomposition of the identity in $Q \otimes R_k(G)$. Multiplying by an arbitrary element $y \in Q \otimes R_k(G)$ (resp. $Q \otimes P_k(G)$) and using the induction-restriction formula $(*)$ as in the proof of Theorem 39 gives the surjectivity.
