If $a + M = b + M$, then $b = b + 0 \in a + M$. In other words, $-a + b = m$ for some $m$ in $M$. Thus (1) implies (2) and (2) implies (3).

If now $-a + b = m \in M$, then $b + M = (a + m) + M = a + (m + M)$. But any vector in $m + M$ is of the form $m + m'$ where $m, m' \in M$ and so $m + M \subset M$. Similarly $-m + M \subset M$, which implies that $M \subset m + M$. Hence $M = m + M$ and $b+M = a+M$.
