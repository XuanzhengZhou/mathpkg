A projective conic is defined by a homogeneous quadratic equation in three variables. Over any field, five points in general position determine a unique conic. Over $\mathbf{F}_3$, one can check directly that the six coefficients of a general conic $a_{00}X_0^2 + a_{01}X_0X_1 + a_{02}X_0X_2 + a_{11}X_1^2 + a_{12}X_1X_2 + a_{22}X_2^2 = 0$ are determined up to scalar by four points in general position because additional constraints from the small field restrict possible degeneracies. The explicit equation $X_0X_1 + X_1X_2 + X_2X_0 = 0$ verifies the existence for the four given points. Its non-degeneracy follows because the associated symmetric matrix has non-zero determinant. For the second claim: if a completion of $Q'$ were degenerate, it would be a product of two linear factors, hence a line pair; the intersection with the affine part would then contain at most 3 points (the intersections of the two lines with the affine plane). The line at infinity in $\mathbf{F}_3$ contains 4 points; the complement of $\{AB\} \cap$ (line at infinity) has 3 points; choosing any 2 of these defines a degenerate completion with $A$ and $B$.
