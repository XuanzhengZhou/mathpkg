Let $A = (f; (v_i), (v_j'))$ and $B = (f; (a_r), (a_s'))$. Put $P = (1_V; (a_r), (v_i))$ and $Q = (1_{V'}; (v_j'), (a_s'))$. Then by Proposition 2,
$$
PAQ = (1_V; (a_r), (v_i)) \, (f; (v_i), (v_j')) \, (1_{V'}; (v_j'), (a_s')) = (f; (a_r), (a_s')) = B.
$$
Both $P$ and $Q$ are invertible since they are matrices of the identity mapping with respect to different bases.
