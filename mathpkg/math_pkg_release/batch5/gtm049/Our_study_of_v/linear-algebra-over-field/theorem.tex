In the special case where $V = V'$, $\mathcal{L}(V, V)$ may be given more structure than just that of a vector space. For here the product $fg$ of any two elements is always defined and is an element of $\mathcal{L}(V, V)$. The following rules are satisfied:
\begin{align}
(fg)h &= f(gh), \\
f(g+h) &= fg + fh, \\
(f+g)h &= fh + gh, \\
x(fg) &= (xf)g = f(xg),
\end{align}
for all $f, g, h \in \mathcal{L}(V, V)$ and $x \in F$. A vector space equipped with a multiplication satisfying these axioms is called a \text{linear algebra} over $F$.
