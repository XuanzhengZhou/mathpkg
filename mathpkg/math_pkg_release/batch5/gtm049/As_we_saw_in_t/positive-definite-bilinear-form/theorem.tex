A real-valued symmetric bilinear form $\sigma$ on a real vector space $V$ is called:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{positive semi-definite} if $q(a) = \sigma(a, a) \geq 0$ for all $a \in V$;
\item \textbf{positive definite} if it is positive semi-definite and $q(a) = 0$ implies $a = 0$;
\item \textbf{negative semi-definite} if $q(a) = \sigma(a, a) \leq 0$ for all $a \in V$;
\item \textbf{negative definite} if it is negative semi-definite and $q(a) = 0$ implies $a = 0$.
\end{itemize}

The quadratic form $q(a) = \sigma(a, a)$ is correspondingly called positive/negative (semi-)definite.
