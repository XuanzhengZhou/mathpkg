A Euclidean geometry is an affine coset equipped with a distance function inherited from a distance on the underlying direction space. The points of a Euclidean geometry are not vectors but elements of an affine space; the distance between two points is the length of the vector connecting them. This definition unifies the synthetic approach (points, lines, distances) with the algebraic approach (vector spaces with bilinear forms).
