Let $V$ be a real vector space and $d$ a mapping of ordered pairs of vectors of $V$ into $\mathbf{R}$. Then $d$ is called a \textbf{distance} on $V$ if it satisfies:

\begin{itemize}
\item[D.1.] $d(a, b) = d(b, a)$;
\item[D.2.] $d(a, b) \geq 0$ and $d(a, b) = 0$ if and only if $a = b$;
\item[D.3.] $d(a, c) \leq d(a, b) + d(b, c)$ (the triangle inequality);
\item[D.4.] $d(a + c, b + c) = d(a, b)$;
\item[D.5.] for any real $x$, $d(xa, xb) = |x| d(a, b)$;
\item[D.6.] $d(a + b, 0)^2 + d(a - b, 0)^2 = 2(d(a, 0)^2 + d(b, 0)^2)$ (the parallelogram law).
\end{itemize}

A distance on a real vector space is essentially equivalent to a positive definite bilinear form.
