A set $R$ with rules of addition and multiplication that associate to any $x, y \in R$ a sum $x+y$ and a product $xy$, both in $R$, is called a \emph{ring} if:
\begin{itemize}
\item[R.1.] $R$ is a commutative group with respect to addition;
\item[R.2.] $(xy)z = x(yz)$ for all $x, y, z \in R$;
\item[R.3.] $x(y+z) = xy + xz$ and $(x+y)z = xz + yz$, for all $x, y, z \in R$.
\end{itemize}
The ring $R$ is \emph{commutative} if additionally
\begin{itemize}
\item[R.4.] $xy = yx$ for all $x, y \in R$.
\end{itemize}
