Let $R$ be a commutative ring with identity and $M$ a set with rules of addition and multiplication which associate to any $a, b \in M$ a sum $a+b \in M$, and to any $x \in R$, $a \in M$ a product $xa \in M$. Then $M$ is called a \emph{module over the ring $R$} (or an \emph{$R$-module}) if:
\begin{itemize}
\item[M.1.] $M$ is a commutative group with respect to addition;
\item[M.2.] $x(a+b) = xa + xb$;
\item[M.3.] $(x+y)a = xa + ya$;
\item[M.4.] $(xy)a = x(ya)$;
\item[M.5.] $1a = a$ where $1$ is the identity element of $R$,
\end{itemize}
for all $x, y \in R$ and all $a, b \in M$.
