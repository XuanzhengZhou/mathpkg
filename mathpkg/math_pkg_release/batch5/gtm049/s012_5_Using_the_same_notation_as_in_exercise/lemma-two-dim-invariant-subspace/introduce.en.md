Lemma 5 shows that each eigenvector $c$ of $f$ with non-real eigenvalue $e^{i\theta}$ gives rise to a two-dimensional $f$-invariant real subspace $T$ of $V$. Taking the real and imaginary parts of $c$, one obtains a cartesian basis $(u, v)$ of $T$ with respect to which the restriction of $f$ has the standard rotation matrix. This is the geometric realization of the complex eigenvalue: the automorphism $f$ acts as a rotation by angle $\theta$ on the plane $T$.
