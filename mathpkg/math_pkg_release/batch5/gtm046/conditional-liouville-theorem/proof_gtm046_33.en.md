Liouville's theorem in classical mechanics asserts that the Lebesgue measure $\lambda$ of Lebesgue sets $A$ in phase space remains invariant under the Hamiltonian flow. More precisely, the phase space undergoes a one-to-one transformation $\omega \leftrightarrow T_1 \omega$ such that $\lambda T_1 A = \lambda A$.

For conservative systems with constant energy $E$, the possible trajectories lie on the surface $H = E$ (hence the term "ergodic" from ergos meaning energy). The invariant measure $\mu$ is then defined by $d\mu = d\sigma / \text{grad } H$, where $d\sigma$ is the differential area of an element of the surface and grad $H$ is evaluated at a point of the element.

This invariance of measure under the flow is the phenomenological origin of ergodic theory: observables (phase functions) evolve along trajectories, and time averages are compared with phase averages.
