The fundamental group $\pi(U)$ of an open set $U$ is said to be locally commutative at a point $P$ in the closure of $U$ if, for every neighborhood $V$ of $P$, there exists a smaller neighborhood $W$ of $P$ such that any two closed paths in $U \cap W$ commute in the fundamental group $\pi(U \cap V)$. This definition, adapted from Artin and Fox, provides a topological invariant used to distinguish wild embeddings from tame ones: for a tame arc in $\mathbb{R}^3$, the fundamental group of its complement is locally commutative at each point, while for certain wild arcs it fails to be.
