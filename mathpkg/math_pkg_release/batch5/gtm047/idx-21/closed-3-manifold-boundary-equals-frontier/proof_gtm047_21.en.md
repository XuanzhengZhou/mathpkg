**Proof.** Let $U = M - \operatorname{Fr} M$. Thus $U$ is the topological interior of $M$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$, that is, the union of all open sets in $\mathbb{R}^3$ that lie in $M$. Obviously $M$ is locally Euclidean at every point of $U$. Therefore we have

$$U \subset \operatorname{Int} M, \quad \operatorname{Bd} M \subset \operatorname{Fr} M.$$

We need to show, conversely, that $\operatorname{Fr} M \subset \operatorname{Bd} M$. Let $P \in \operatorname{Fr} M \subset M$, and suppose that $P$ has an open neighborhood $V$ in $M$, homeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^3$. Since $P \in V$, $V$ cannot be open in $\mathbb{R}^3$; and so this contradicts the Invariance of Domain (Theorem 0.4).

Thus, for 3-manifolds $M$ with boundary, closed in $\mathbb{R}^3$, we need not distinguish between $\operatorname{Fr} M$ and $\operatorname{Bd} M$, and we can use the notation $\operatorname{Int} M$ for the interior $M - \operatorname{Fr} M$ of $M$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$.
