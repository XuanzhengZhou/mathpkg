The Dehn Lemma was originally formulated by Max Dehn in 1910 [D], who published what purported to be a proof. After over fifteen years, it was found that Dehn's proof was erroneous, and for many years the Dehn Lemma remained a classical open problem in topology.

The lemma was finally proved by Papakyriakopoulos $[P_1]$ using his method of "tower" covering spaces. Subsequently, Papakyriakopoulos $[P_2]$ proved the Loop Theorem. The Dehn Lemma is then a fairly easy consequence of the Loop Theorem (see Theorem 27.5 of this text):

**The Loop Theorem.** Let $M$ be a polyhedral $3$-manifold with boundary, and let $B$ be its boundary. Let $L$ be a loop in $B$, and suppose that $L$ is contractible in $M$ but not in $B$. Then there is a polyhedral $2$-cell $D$ in $M$, with its boundary in $B$, such that the boundary of $D$ is not contractible in $B$.

The proof of the Loop Theorem (Theorem 1) occupies Sections 27--30 of this text, using the method of normal systems, regular neighborhoods, and complexity arguments on triangulated 3-manifolds. The transition from the Dehn Lemma to the Loop Theorem is nontrivial: the known proofs of the Loop Theorem do not use the Dehn Lemma at all.

In 1958, J. H. C. Whitehead and Arnold Shapiro [WS] proved a generalization of the Dehn Lemma applying to singular annuli with no singularities on their boundaries. Their argument also constituted a much simpler proof of the Dehn Lemma. Starting with the methods developed by Whitehead and Shapiro, John Stallings $[S_2]$ further developed these techniques.
