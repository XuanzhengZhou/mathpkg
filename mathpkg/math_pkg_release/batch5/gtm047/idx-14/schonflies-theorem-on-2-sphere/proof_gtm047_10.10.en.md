Remove any point $P_0 \in S^2 - J$. Then $S^2 - P_0$ is homeomorphic to $\mathbf{R}^2$, and $J$ becomes a $1$-sphere in $\mathbf{R}^2$. By the classical Schönflies theorem, $J$ bounds a $2$-cell $C_1$ in $\mathbf{R}^2$, whose complement in $\mathbf{R}^2$ together with the point at infinity (i.e., the reinstated $P_0$) forms the second $2$-cell $C_2$. Restoring $P_0$ yields $S^2 = C_1 \cup C_2$ with $J = \operatorname{Fr} C_1 = \operatorname{Fr} C_2$.
