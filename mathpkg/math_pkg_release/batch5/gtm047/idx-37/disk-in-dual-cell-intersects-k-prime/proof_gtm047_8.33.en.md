Since, by hypothesis, $K$ has no end-points, this lemma is intuitively clear: $\Delta$ separates $C_{v_1}''$ into two components, and if $\Delta$ did not intersect $K'$, then one could construct a path in $\operatorname{Int} N' - K'$ connecting points on opposite sides of $\Delta$ without crossing $\Delta$, contradicting the separation property.

More formally, suppose $\Delta \cap K' = \emptyset$. Then one can find a path from a point $x_1$ on one side of $\Delta$ to a point $x_2$ on the other side, staying within $\operatorname{Int} N' - K'$. The path goes: from $x_1$ to a point $y_1$ near $C_{v_1}'' \cap \operatorname{Bd} N'$; then along $\operatorname{Bd} N'$ to a point $y_2$ near another pseudocell $E_2$; then from $y_2$ to a point $y_3$ near $\operatorname{Bd} E_2$, by a path close to $C_{v_1}'' \cap \operatorname{Bd} N'$; and finally from $y_3$ to $x_2$ by a path close to $E_2$. This contradiction completes the proof.
