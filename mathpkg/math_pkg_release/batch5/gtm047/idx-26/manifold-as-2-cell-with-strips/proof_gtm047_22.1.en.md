Consider the open cell-decomposition $\mathcal{C}$ of $M$ from Theorem 1, which has exactly one $2$-face $C^2$ homeomorphic to an open $2$-cell. The closure of $C^2$ is a polyhedral $2$-cell $D = \operatorname{Cl} C^2$. The complementary region $M - C^2$ is also a union of polyhedral $2$-cells (from the original triangulation edges not used as edges of $\mathcal{C}$). By applying Theorem 2 repeatedly to the $1$-skeleton of the complementary region, one obtains the decomposition into a second $2$-cell $D'$ and strips $S_i$, with the stated intersection properties.

More precisely, the boundary arcs of $D$ coming from the $n-1$ interior edges $e_i$ used in constructing $C^2$ (Theorem 1) form $2n$ arcs on $\operatorname{Bd} D$, which pair up to give the $2n$ arcs of $D \cap D'$. Each such pair, together with the intervening part of the complementary region, forms a strip $S_i$. $\square$
