When $p^2 + 3q^2$ is a cube and $p, q$ are coprime, Euler uses the identity $(a^2 + 3b^2)^3$ to deduce an explicit factorization of $p$ and $q$ in terms of new integers $a, b$. This yields $p = a(a - 3b)(a + 3b)$ and $q = 3b(a - b)(a + b)$, where $a$ and $b$ are relatively prime and of opposite parity. This step is the most delicate part of Euler's proof and relies on unique factorization properties in the ring $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-3}]$ -- precisely the point where Euler's original argument contained a gap.
