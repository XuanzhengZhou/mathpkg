It was observed in Section 1.8 that the prime divisors of $2^{32} + 1$ must all be 1 mod 64, so that 641 is only the fifth prime that need be tried. Show that in fact the prime factors of $2^{32} + 1$ must be 1 mod 128 and that 641 is only the second prime that need be tried. [Let $p$ be a prime divisor of $2^{32} + 1$. Since $p \equiv 1 \pmod{64}$, $p$ divides $x^2 - 2$ for some $x$. Since $x^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$, the previous argument shows that 64 divides $(p-1)/2$.]
