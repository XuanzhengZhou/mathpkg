Fermat's theorem on primes as sums of two squares is one of the foundational results in number theory. It states that every prime number congruent to 1 modulo 4 can be written as a sum of two integer squares. Euler proved this theorem in 1747 at age forty, in a letter to Goldbach. The proof uses the method of infinite descent, a technique pioneered by Fermat himself, combined with the Brahmagupta-Fibonacci identity for the product of sums of two squares. This theorem is the starting point for the study of representations of integers by quadratic forms and was a crucial step toward Euler's later work on Fermat's Last Theorem.
