This lemma, Statement (2') in Edwards' exposition, handles the special role of the prime 2 in the representation theory of the quadratic form $a^2+3b^2$. Unlike the case of sums of two squares $a^2+b^2$, where statement (4) applies to all factors, the form $a^2+3b^2$ exhibits anomalous behavior with respect to 2: for instance, $1^2+3\cdot1^2=4$ is divisible by 2 even though 2 itself is not of the form $a^2+3b^2$. This lemma states that any factor of 2 in such a number must actually appear as a factor of 4, and after dividing out the 4, the quotient retains the same quadratic form.
