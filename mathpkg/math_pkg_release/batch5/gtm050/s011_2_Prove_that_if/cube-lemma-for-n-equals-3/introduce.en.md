This lemma is the decisive step in Euler's proof of Fermat's Last Theorem for exponent $n=3$. It states that if two coprime integers $a$ and $b$ satisfy that $a^2+3b^2$ is a perfect cube, then the algebraic number $a+b\sqrt{-3}$ in the quadratic ring $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-3}]$ must itself be a perfect cube. The proof uses the factorization theorem (Statement 3) to write $a+b\sqrt{-3}$ as a product of linear factors, each corresponding to a prime divisor of the norm. Since $a^2+3b^2$ is a cube, its prime factorization has each prime appearing with multiplicity a multiple of 3. The uniqueness (up to sign) of the linear factors then forces the factors to appear in groups of three identical factors, from which the conclusion $a+b\sqrt{-3} = \pm(p+q\sqrt{-3})^3$ follows (and the sign can be absorbed by negating $p$ and $q$).
