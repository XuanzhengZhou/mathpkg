This theorem is Statement (5') in Edwards' exposition and is the analog for the quadratic form $a^2+3b^2$ of Statement (4) for sums of two squares $a^2+b^2$. It states that every odd divisor of $a^2+3b^2$, when $a$ and $b$ are coprime, can itself be represented in the same quadratic form. The proof uses infinite descent, but with an important modification: the inequality $zx \leq (x/2)^2 + 3(x/2)^2 = x^2$ yields $z \leq x$ rather than the strict $z < x$ obtained in the $a^2+b^2$ case. The strict inequality $z < x$ is recovered by using the fact that $x$ is odd, which makes the inequalities $|c| < x/2$, $|d| < x/2$ strict. This theorem is essential for Euler's work on the $n=3$ case of Fermat's Last Theorem.
