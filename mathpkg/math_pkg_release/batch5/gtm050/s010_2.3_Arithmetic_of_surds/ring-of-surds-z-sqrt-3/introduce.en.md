Numbers of the form $a + b\sqrt{-3}$ with integer $a$ and $b$ are called surds. Addition and subtraction are defined componentwise, and multiplication follows the rule $(a + b\sqrt{-3})(c + d\sqrt{-3}) = (ac - 3bd) + (ad + bc)\sqrt{-3}$. Under these operations, the surds satisfy the usual laws of commutativity, associativity, and distributivity, and $1 = 1 + 0\sqrt{-3}$ acts as the multiplicative identity. In modern algebraic language, this structure is the commutative ring $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-3}]$ with unit.
