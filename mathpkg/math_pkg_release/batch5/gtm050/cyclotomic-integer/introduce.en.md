Cyclotomic integers are the central algebraic objects in Kummer's theory of ideal factorization. They generalize ordinary integers by adjoining a primitive $\lambda$-th root of unity $\alpha$. The name "cyclotomic" (circle-cutting) comes from Gauss's theory of the division of the circle. The equation $x^\lambda + y^\lambda = z^\lambda$ factors as $(x+y)(x+\alpha y)(x+\alpha^2 y)\cdots(x+\alpha^{\lambda-1}y) = z^\lambda$ in cyclotomic integers, which is why their factorization properties are crucial for Fermat's Last Theorem. Kummer developed his theory to handle the fact that unique factorization fails for cyclotomic integers when $\lambda \geq 23$.
