This theorem states that the only triangular numbers that are also perfect fourth powers are the trivial ones $0$ and $1$. Equivalently, the Diophantine equation $\frac{1}{2}x(x-1) = y^4$ has no integer solutions for $x > 2$. The statement is simple to formulate but far from simple to prove. The first published proof appeared in Legendre's *Théorie des Nombres*, roughly a hundred and fifty years after Fermat's time. This theorem illustrates the depth of classical Diophantine analysis, where problems that are elementary to state can require highly sophisticated methods.
