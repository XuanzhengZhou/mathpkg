This is a direct reformulation of Fermat's theorem on the area of a right triangle. Given a rational right triangle, multiply all side lengths by their least common denominator to obtain a Pythagorean triple $x^2 + y^2 = z^2$ with integer sides. The area of the original triangle is $\frac{1}{2}xy$ divided by the square of the common denominator. For this area to be a rational square, $\frac{1}{2}xy$ multiplied by an appropriate square factor must be a square. By the lemma that if $Ad^2$ is a square then $A$ is a square, it follows that $\frac{1}{2}xy$ itself must be a square. Since $x$ and $y$ cannot both be odd in a primitive Pythagorean triple, $\frac{1}{2}xy$ is an integer. Thus the geometric statement is equivalent to the arithmetic statement that no Pythagorean triple has square area.
