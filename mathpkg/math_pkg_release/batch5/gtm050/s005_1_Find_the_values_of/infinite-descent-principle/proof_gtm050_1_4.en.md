The principle follows immediately from the well-ordering of the positive integers. Suppose, for contradiction, that some positive integer $n$ has a given set of properties $P$, and that whenever a positive integer has $P$, there exists a strictly smaller positive integer also having $P$. Then starting from $n$, we obtain an infinite strictly decreasing sequence $n > n_1 > n_2 > \cdots$ of positive integers, each possessing property $P$. But the set of positive integers is well-ordered: every nonempty subset has a least element. In particular, the set $\{n, n_1, n_2, \ldots\}$ must have a least element, contradicting the fact that it is strictly decreasing and infinite. Hence no positive integer can have property $P$.
