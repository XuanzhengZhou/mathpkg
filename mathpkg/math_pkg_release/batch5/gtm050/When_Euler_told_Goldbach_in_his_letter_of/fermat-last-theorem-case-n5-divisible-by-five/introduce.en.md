This theorem is a special case of Fermat's Last Theorem for exponent $n = 5$, stating that if three integers satisfy the Diophantine equation $x^5 + y^5 = z^5$, then at least one of them must be divisible by $5$. It is the first step in proving that $x^5 + y^5 = z^5$ has no nontrivial integer solutions, since if one variable is divisible by $5$, further descent arguments can be applied to reach a contradiction. This result was proved by Sophie Germain and later generalized by Legendre and Dirichlet in their full proof of the case $n = 5$.
