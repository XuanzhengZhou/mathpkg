If $x^5 + y^5 = z^5$ is a solution to Fermat's equation with exponent $5$ in integers, then at least one of $x$, $y$, or $z$ must be divisible by $5$. Equivalently, reformulating as $x^5 + y^5 + z^5 = 0$ with $x, y, z$ pairwise relatively prime, the theorem asserts that $5$ divides the product $xyz$. This result serves as a motivating example for Sophie Germain's more general theorem on Case I of Fermat's Last Theorem.
