The law of quadratic reciprocity, discovered by Euler and Legendre but first proved by Gauss in his *Disquisitiones Arithmeticae* (1801), is one of the most celebrated theorems in number theory. Gauss called it the "fundamental theorem" and gave eight different proofs over his lifetime. The book presents Gauss's second proof (from Section 5 of the *Disquisitiones*), which uses the theory of binary quadratic forms and the count of ambiguous classes. This proof illustrates the deep connection between quadratic reciprocity and the divisor class group. The law determines when a prime $p$ is a quadratic residue modulo another prime $q$ in terms of $q$ being a quadratic residue modulo $p$.
