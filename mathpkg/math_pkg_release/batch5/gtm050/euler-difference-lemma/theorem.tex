If $p = 4n + 1$ is prime, then $p$ divides at least one of the numbers
$$1^{2n} + 2^{2n}, \quad 2^{2n} + 3^{2n}, \quad \ldots, \quad (4n-1)^{2n} + (4n)^{2n}.$$
The proof uses that the $2n$-th differences of the sequence $1^{2n}, 2^{2n}, 3^{2n}, \ldots$ are all equal to $(2n)!$, which is not divisible by $p$.
