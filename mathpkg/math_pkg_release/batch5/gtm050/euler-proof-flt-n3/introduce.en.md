Euler gave the first proof of Fermat's Last Theorem for the exponent $n=3$ around 1753, though his proof contained a gap regarding unique factorization in the ring $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-3}]$. The proof uses the method of infinite descent and relies on the factorization $x^3 + y^3 = (x+y)(x^2 - xy + y^2)$. Euler introduced the bold idea of working with "numbers" of the form $a + b\sqrt{-3}$, extending arithmetic beyond ordinary integers. The gap in Euler's proof can be filled using arguments that Euler himself developed for sums of two squares (see Section 2.5).
