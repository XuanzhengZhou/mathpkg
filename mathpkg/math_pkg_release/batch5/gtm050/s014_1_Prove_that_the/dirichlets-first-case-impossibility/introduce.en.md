Dirichlet's First Case Impossibility is a major milestone in the history of Fermat's Last Theorem, presented before the Paris Academy in July 1825. For exponent $n = 5$, the equation $x^5 + y^5 = z^5$ splits into two subcases of Case II (where one of $x, y, z$ is divisible by $5$): the "first case" where the term divisible by $5$ is also even (hence divisible by $10$), and the "second case" where the even term and the term divisible by $5$ are distinct. Dirichlet proved the first case impossible by reducing the equation to $u^5 \pm v^5 = w^5$ with $w$ divisible by $10$, then using the substitution $u = p+q$, $v = p-q$ to transform the problem into one about the ring $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{5}]$. The proof culminates in showing that a certain element $P + Q\sqrt{5}$ must be a perfect fifth power, which follows from his key lemma.
