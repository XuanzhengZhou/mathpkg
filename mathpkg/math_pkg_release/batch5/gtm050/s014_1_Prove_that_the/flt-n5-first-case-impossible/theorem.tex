Let $x, y, z$ be pairwise relatively prime positive integers satisfying $x^5 + y^5 = z^5$. In Case II of Fermat's Last Theorem for $n = 5$, one of $x, y, z$ is divisible by 5. The equation further subdivides as:

\textbf{First case:} The number divisible by 5 is also divisible by 2 (so it is divisible by 10).

\textbf{Theorem.} The first case is impossible: there are no pairwise relatively prime positive integers $u, v, w$ such that $u^5 \pm v^5 = w^5$ with $w$ divisible by 10 and $u, v$ odd.
