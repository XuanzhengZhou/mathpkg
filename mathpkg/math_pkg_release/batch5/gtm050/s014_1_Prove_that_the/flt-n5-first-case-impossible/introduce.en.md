After Sophie Germain proved Case I of Fermat's Last Theorem for all primes less than 100, attention turned to Case II where one of $x, y, z$ is divisible by the exponent. For $n=5$, Dirichlet subdivided Case II further: the "first case" is when the term divisible by 5 is also even (hence divisible by 10), and the "second case" is when the even term and the term divisible by 5 are distinct. In July 1825, Dirichlet presented a proof to the Paris Academy that this first case is impossible, using an ingenious adaptation of Euler's method for $n=3$ combined with his new lemma on fifth-power factorization in $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{5}]$.
