In the proof of Fermat's Last Theorem for $n = 5$, the second case (Case II, where $5 \mid xyz$) is subdivided as follows:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{First subcase:} The term divisible by $5$ is also divisible by $2$, so the equation may be written as
$$u^5 \pm v^5 = w^5$$
where $u, v, w$ are pairwise relatively prime positive integers and $w$ is divisible by $10$.
\item \textbf{Second subcase:} The even number and the number divisible by $5$ are distinct.
\end{itemize}
This subdivision should not be confused with the division into Case I ($5 \nmid xyz$) and Case II ($5 \mid xyz$). Case I has already been eliminated for $n = 5$, and these "first" and "second" cases are subdivisions of Case II.
