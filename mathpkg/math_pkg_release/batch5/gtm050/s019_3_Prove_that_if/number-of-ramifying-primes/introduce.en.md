This proposition counts the ramifying primes in a quadratic order of discriminant $D$. The count is $m + \varepsilon$, where $m$ is the number of distinct odd prime factors of $D$, and $\varepsilon$ accounts for the prime $2$ ramifying exactly when $D \equiv 2$ or $3 \pmod{4}$. This leads to a bound of $2^{m+\varepsilon}$ on the number of divisors of the form $(p_1,*)(p_2,*)\cdots(p_k,*)$ when $D < 0$, providing an upper bound on the number of ambiguous classes.
