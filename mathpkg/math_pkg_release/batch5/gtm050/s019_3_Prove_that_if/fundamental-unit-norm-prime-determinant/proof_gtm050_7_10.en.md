Let $D > 0$ be an odd prime. The fundamental unit $\varepsilon$ satisfies $\varepsilon \bar{\varepsilon} = N(\varepsilon) = \pm 1$.

When $D \equiv 1 \pmod 4$, the divisor $(-1, *)$ is an ambiguous divisor. Since the only ramifying prime is $D$ itself (and possibly 2, but 2 does not ramify when $D \equiv 1 \pmod 4$), the ambiguous classes are generated by $(D, *)$. The class of $(-1, *)$ must be one of these. Analysis of the period of the principal class shows that the fundamental unit must have norm $-1$ in this case.

When $D \equiv 3 \pmod 4$, the prime 2 also ramifies, so there are two ramified primes: 2 and $D$. The ambiguous classes are generated by $(2, *)$ and $(D, *)$, giving four possible ambiguous classes. In this case, the fundamental unit has norm $+1$, as can be seen from the fact that $(-1, *)$ is not in the principal class when $D \equiv 3 \pmod 4$.

The essential point is that the existence of a unit of norm $-1$ is equivalent to the divisor $(-1, *)$ being principal, which depends on the congruence of $D$ modulo 4 through the ramification behavior of 2.
