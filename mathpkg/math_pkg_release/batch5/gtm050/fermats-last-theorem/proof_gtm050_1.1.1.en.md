The theorem is not proved in this book; it is the central unsolved problem that motivates the entire development. However, the book proves the theorem for many specific exponents:
- $n = 4$ (Fermat, Section 1.5)
- $n = 3$ (Euler, Chapter 2)
- $n = 5$ (Dirichlet and Legendre, Section 3.3)
- $n = 7$ (Lamé, Section 3.4)
- All regular primes (Kummer, Chapter 5)

The general reduction: if $n = mk$ and $x^n + y^n = z^n$, then $(x^k)^m + (y^k)^m = (z^k)^m$. Since every $n > 2$ is divisible by 4 or by an odd prime, it suffices to prove the theorem for $n = 4$ and for all odd primes.
