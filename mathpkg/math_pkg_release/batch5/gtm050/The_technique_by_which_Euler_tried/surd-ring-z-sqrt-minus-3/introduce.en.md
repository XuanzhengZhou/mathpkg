Euler, in his attempt to characterize cubes of the form $p^2 + 3q^2$, introduced the bold idea of working with "numbers" of the form $a + b\sqrt{-3}$ where $a$ and $b$ are integers. These expressions, called surds, form a commutative ring with unit under the usual operations of addition, subtraction, and multiplication, with the rule $(\sqrt{-3})^2 = -3$. In modern algebraic language, this is the ring $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-3}]$, a quadratic integer ring. Euler's technique of factoring $p^2 + 3q^2$ inside this ring was a precursor to the development of algebraic number theory.
