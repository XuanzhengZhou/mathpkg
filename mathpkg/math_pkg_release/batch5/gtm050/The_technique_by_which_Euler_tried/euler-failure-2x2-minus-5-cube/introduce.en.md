Euler himself recognized that his method of factoring in rings of surds was not universally applicable. In his discussion of the equation $2x^2 - 5 = \text{cube}$ (§195), Euler attempted to set $x\sqrt{2} + \sqrt{5} = (a\sqrt{2} + b\sqrt{5})^3$. Expanding the cube leads to the condition $1 = 6a^2b + 5b^3$, which forces $b = \pm 1$ and $6a^2 + 5 = \pm 1$, an impossibility. This self-observed failure demonstrates Euler's awareness that his analogical reasoning had limits, and it foreshadows the deeper understanding that unique factorization fails in some rings of algebraic integers. Dickson documented this case in his history of number theory.
