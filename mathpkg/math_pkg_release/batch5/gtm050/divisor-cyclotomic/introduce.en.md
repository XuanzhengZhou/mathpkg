Kummer introduced the concept of "ideal divisors" (what we now call ideals) to restore unique factorization when it fails for cyclotomic integers. Rather than working with actual cyclotomic integers as factors, Kummer worked with "prime divisors" which are characterized by the cyclotomic integers they divide. A divisor is determined by the set of all cyclotomic integers it divides. This idea was one of the most important innovations in 19th-century mathematics and directly led to Dedekind's theory of ideals. The notion of equivalence of divisors leads to the class group, whose size (the class number) measures the failure of unique factorization.
