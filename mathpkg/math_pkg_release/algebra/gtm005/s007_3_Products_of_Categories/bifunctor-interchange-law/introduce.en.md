The interchange law characterizes when a family of ordinary functors (one for each fixed object in the other variable) can be assembled into a single bifunctor $S: B \times C \to D$. The condition $S(b', g) \circ S(f, c) = S(f, c') \circ S(b, g)$ ensures that the two ways of going from $S(b, c)$ to $S(b', c')$ — first applying $f$ then $g$, or first $g$ then $f$ — coincide. This is the essential coherence condition that makes a "functor of two variables" well-defined.
