The coherence theorem yields unique "canonical" maps between words; hence, the plan of the proof is to embed the given category $M$ in a larger strict monoidal category $S$ consisting of iterated formal products (where all pairs of parenthesis start in front) of elements of $M$.

$S$ will be the free monoid generated by the elements of $M$. Specifically, take the objects $s$ of $S$ to be all finite strings $s = [b_1, \ldots, b_k]$ of objects of $M$, including the empty string $\varnothing$. A product $s \square t$ of strings $s$ and $t$ is then defined by concatenation of strings, as $s \cdot t$. This product is associative, so the associativity map $\alpha$ for $S$ can be the identity. Also, the empty string $\varnothing$ acts as a unit for this product, so the maps $\rho$ and $\lambda$ for $S$ can both be the identity. With these agreements, $S$ is an associative monoid with a unit, but not yet a monoidal category.

Now define a map $F : S \rightarrow M$ on strings in $S$ by setting

$$F(\varnothing) = e,$$

$$F(s) = F[b_1, \ldots, b_k] = (\ldots (b_1 \square b_2) \square b_3) \ldots) \square b_k),$$

where on the right all pairs of parenthesis begin in front. Now define the arrows $s \rightarrow t$ between strings $s$ and $t$ in $S$ to be exactly the arrows between the corresponding objects in $M$,

$$F(s) \rightarrow F(t),$$

with composition just as in $M$. This convention clearly makes $S$ into a category. Then the concatenation product $s \cdot u$ can be extended to a corresponding product $f \cdot g$ of arrows $f : s \rightarrow t$ and $g : u \rightarrow v$, where $u$ and $v$, like $s$ and $t$, are finite strings of objects of $M$. Specifically, this means that we define $f \cdot g$ as the following composite in $M$:

$$F(s \cdot u) \rightarrow F(s) \square F(u) \xrightarrow{f \square g} F(t) \square F(v) \rightarrow F(t \cdot v);$$

here the two outer arrows are the canonical isomorphisms given by coherence between the two ways of forming the product. This definition makes $S$ into a monoidal category (with $\alpha$, $\rho$, $\lambda$ all identities), so $S$ is strict monoidal.

The functor $F$ is made monoidal by the maps

$$F_2(s, t) : F(s \cdot t) \rightarrow F(s) \square F(t), \quad F_0 : F(\varnothing) = e \rightarrow e = 1_M,$$

both of which are the canonical coherence isomorphisms. The conditions (3) and (4) for a monoidal functor (as stated in Section 2) follow from the coherence theorem.

The functor $G : M \rightarrow S$ in the reverse direction is defined by

$$G(b) = [b], \quad G(f) = f,$$
$$G_0 = 1 : \varnothing \rightarrow [e],$$
$$G_2(b, c) = 1 : [b, c] \rightarrow [b \square c].$$

Here the last map is $1$ because by the definition above, a map of strings $[b, c] \rightarrow [b \square c]$ is just a map $b \square c \rightarrow b \square c$ in $M$. The conditions (3), (4), and (4') of Section 2 on $G$ then follow. In the case of (3), observe that the map $G_2 \square 1$ is

$$(G(a) \cdot G(b)) \cdot G(c) \xrightarrow{G_2 \square 1} G(a \square b) \cdot G(c),$$
$$[a, b, c] \longmapsto [a \square b, c],$$

therefore, by the definition of maps in $S$, must be the map $\alpha : a \square (b \square c) \rightarrow (a \square b) \square c$ in $M$ as a map in $S$; this matches the map $G(\alpha)$ at the base of (3) of Section 2, while $\alpha' = 1$ is at the top.

The composite functor $FG : M \rightarrow M$ is the identity, while the composite $GF$ is naturally isomorphic to the identity. Hence, the monoidal category $M$ is indeed categorically equivalent (by monoidal functors) to the strict monoidal category $S$, as claimed.
