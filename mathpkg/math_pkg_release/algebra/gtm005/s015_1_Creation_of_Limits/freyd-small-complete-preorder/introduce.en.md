Freyd's theorem shows that there is a fundamental size restriction on small-complete categories: if a category is both small (its objects and arrows form sets) and small-complete (all small diagrams have limits), then it must be a preorder — at most one arrow between any two objects. The proof is a cardinality argument: given two distinct parallel arrows, taking the product of many copies of the codomain yields exponentially many arrows to the product, contradicting the category being small. In a preorder, products become greatest lower bounds (meets) and coproducts become least upper bounds (joins).
