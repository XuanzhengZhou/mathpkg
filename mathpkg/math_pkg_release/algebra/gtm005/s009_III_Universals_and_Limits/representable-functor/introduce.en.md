A functor $K: D \to \mathbf{Set}$ is representable if it is naturally isomorphic to a hom-functor $D(r, -)$ for some object $r \in D$, called the representing object. The Yoneda Lemma shows that the representing object and the natural isomorphism are uniquely determined (up to isomorphism) by a universal element of $K$. Many important functors in mathematics are representable: for example, the forgetful functor from groups to sets is represented by the free group on one generator $\mathbb{Z}$, and the functor assigning to a set its power set is representable (by the two-element set). The concept of representability is central to modern algebraic geometry, where schemes are defined as representable functors.
