Proposition 2 states that a universal element $\langle r, e \rangle$ for a set-valued functor $K \colon D \to \mathbf{Set}$ (equivalently, a universal arrow from the one-point set $*$ to $K$) defines a representation $D(r, -) \cong K$ of $K$. This completes the triangle of equivalent notions: universal arrows, universal elements, and representable functors are three ways of expressing the same categorical phenomenon — the existence of a "best" or "most efficient" construction.
