The elementary theory of an abstract category (ETAC) consists of statements $\Sigma$ built from atomic statements involving objects $a, b, c, \ldots$ and arrows $f, g, h, \ldots$. The atomic statements are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item ``$a$ is the domain of $f$'' (written $a = \operatorname{dom} f$),
  \item ``$b$ is the codomain of $f$'' (written $b = \operatorname{cod} f$),
  \item ``$i$ is the identity arrow of $a$'' (written $i = 1_a$),
  \item ``$g$ can be composed with $f$ and $h$ is the composite'' (written $h = g \circ f$),
  \item equalities $a = b$ and $f = g$.
\end{itemize}
A statement $\Sigma$ is any well-formed formula built from these atomic statements using the ordinary propositional connectives ($\land, \lor, \lnot, \Rightarrow, \Leftrightarrow$) and the quantifiers ($\forall a$, $\forall f$, $\exists a$, $\exists f$). The notation $f: a \to b$ abbreviates the statement ``$a$ is the domain of $f$ and $b$ is the codomain of $f$''. A sentence is a statement in which all variables are bound (quantified).
