\textbf{(a) $\Leftrightarrow$ (b).} Immediate from (15.16), (10.15), and the fact (15.8) that $J(R/J(R)) = 0$.

\textbf{(a) $\Rightarrow$ (e).} By (15.5), $J(R)$ annihilates every simple left $R$-module. Thus $\operatorname{Soc} M \subseteq r_M(J(R))$ for every left $R$-module $M$. But $J(R) \cdot r_M(J(R)) = 0$, so $r_M(J(R))$ is an $R/J(R)$-module. Assuming (a), $r_M(J(R))$ is semisimple and contained in $\operatorname{Soc} M$. Hence equality holds.

\textbf{(e) $\Rightarrow$ (d).} Since $J(R)$ annihilates all semisimple modules, it annihilates all products of semisimple modules. Assuming (e), every product of semisimple modules equals its own socle and hence is semisimple.

\textbf{(d) $\Rightarrow$ (c).} Clear.

\textbf{(c) $\Rightarrow$ (a).} The ring $R/J(R)$ is cogenerated by simple $R$-modules. By (9.4) and (c), it follows that $R/J(R)$ embeds in a product of simple modules, which by (c) is semisimple. Hence $R/J(R)$ is semisimple.
