A morphism $f: A \to B$ is said to be a coequalizer if it can be realized as the coequalizer of some parallel pair $g_1, g_2: C \to A$. This is the second of three categorical generalizations of surjectivity studied in this section. Being a coequalizer is a structural property: it means $f$ is the universal quotient identifying the images of $g_1$ and $g_2$. By the Hierarchy Theorem (1.36), every split epimorphism is a coequalizer, and every coequalizer is an epimorphism.
