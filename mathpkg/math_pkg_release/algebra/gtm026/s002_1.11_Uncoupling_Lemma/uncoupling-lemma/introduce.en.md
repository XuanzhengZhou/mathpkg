The Uncoupling Lemma provides a unique factorization property for $\Omega$-words: every $\Omega$-word $p \in A\Omega$ of length greater than $1$ can be uniquely written as $p = p_1 \cdots p_n \omega$, where $\omega \in \Omega_n$ is an $n$-ary operator symbol and each $p_i$ is itself an $\Omega$-word. This lemma is foundational in universal algebra, as it guarantees that the syntactic construction of terms from an operator domain is unambiguous — each term has a unique "outermost" operator symbol and a unique sequence of immediate subterms. The result justifies recursive definitions on $\Omega$-words (such as the Principle of Finitary Algebraic General Recursion) and underpins the algebraic semantics of terms.
