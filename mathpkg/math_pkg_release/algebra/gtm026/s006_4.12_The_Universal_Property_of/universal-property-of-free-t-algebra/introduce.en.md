The Universal Property of $(AT, A\mu)$ characterizes the free $T$-algebra $(AT, A\mu)$ as the representing object for the functor that sends a $T$-algebra $(X, \xi)$ to the underlying $\mathcal{K}$-object $X$. Concretely, every $\mathcal{K}$-morphism $f: A \to X$ from the base object $A$ into the underlying object of a $T$-algebra $(X, \xi)$ extends uniquely to a $T$-homomorphism $f^{\#}: (AT, A\mu) \to (X, \xi)$. The extension is given explicitly by the composite $f^{\#} = fT \cdot \xi$, where $fT: AT \to XT$ is the image of $f$ under the functor $T$ and $\xi: XT \to X$ is the structure map of the algebra. This universal property is the categorical essence of the free-forgetful adjunction induced by an algebraic theory, and it plays the same pivotal role in universal algebra that the universal property of a free group plays in group theory.
