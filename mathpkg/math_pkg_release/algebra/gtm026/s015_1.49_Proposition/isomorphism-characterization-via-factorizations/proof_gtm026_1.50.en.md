$(1) \Rightarrow (2)$ and $(1) \Rightarrow (3)$ are clear since an isomorphism is both a coequalizer and an equalizer, and is both mono and epi.

For $(2) \Rightarrow (1)$: If $f$ is a coequalizer and $f$ is mono, then $f \cdot \operatorname{id} = \operatorname{id} \cdot f$ are two coequalizer-mono factorizations of $f$. By Proposition 1.49, these factorizations are unique up to isomorphism, which yields an inverse $f^{-1}$ for $f$.

$(3) \Rightarrow (1)$ follows by duality.
