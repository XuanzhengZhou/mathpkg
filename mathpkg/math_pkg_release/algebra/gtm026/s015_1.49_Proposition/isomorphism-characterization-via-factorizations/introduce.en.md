Corollary 1.50 provides three equivalent conditions characterizing when a morphism $f: A \to B$ is an isomorphism: $f$ is an isomorphism if and only if $f$ is simultaneously a coequalizer and a monomorphism, or equivalently, $f$ is simultaneously an equalizer and an epimorphism. This result follows directly from the uniqueness of coequalizer-mono factorizations (Proposition 1.49) and highlights a subtle point: while being both epi and mono is not sufficient for being an isomorphism in general categories, being a coequalizer and mono (or an equalizer and epi) is sufficient.
