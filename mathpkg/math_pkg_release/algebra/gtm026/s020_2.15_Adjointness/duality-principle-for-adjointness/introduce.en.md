The duality principle for adjointness states that reversing all arrows in an adjointness yields another adjointness. Specifically, if $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{K}, U, F, \eta, \varepsilon)$ is an adjointness with $U: \mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{K}$ and $F: \mathcal{K} \to \mathcal{A}$, then passing to the opposite categories swaps the roles of the functors and exchanges the unit and counit: $(\mathcal{K}^{\text{op}}, \mathcal{A}^{\text{op}}, F^{\text{op}}, U^{\text{op}}, \varepsilon, \eta)$ is also an adjointness. This principle is immediate from the definition because the triangular identities are symmetric under arrow reversal, and it is used later to dualize Theorem 2.18 to obtain Theorem 2.19 about cofree objects.
