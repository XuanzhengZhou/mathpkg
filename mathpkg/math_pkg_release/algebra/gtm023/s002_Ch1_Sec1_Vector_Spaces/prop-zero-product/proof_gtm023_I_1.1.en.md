Substituting $\mu = 0$ in the first distributive law II.2 yields
$$\lambda x = \lambda x + 0 \cdot x,$$
hence $0 \cdot x = 0$. Similarly, the second distributive law gives $\lambda \cdot 0 = 0$.

Conversely, suppose $\lambda x = 0$ and $\lambda \neq 0$. Then using II.1 and II.3,
$$1 \cdot x = (\lambda^{-1}\lambda)x = \lambda^{-1}(\lambda x) = \lambda^{-1} \cdot 0 = 0,$$
so $x = 0$ by II.3. Thus $\lambda x = 0$ implies $\lambda = 0$ or $x = 0$.
