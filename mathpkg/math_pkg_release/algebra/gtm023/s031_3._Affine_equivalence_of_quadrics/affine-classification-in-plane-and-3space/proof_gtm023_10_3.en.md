Apply Theorem 10.17 to $n = 2$ and $n = 3$.

For the plane ($n = 2$): The normal forms for quadrics with center are $\sum_{v=1}^{s} (\xi^v)^2 - \sum_{v=s+1}^{r} (\xi^v)^2 = 1$ with $1 \leq s \leq r$ and $r = 1, 2$. This yields three classes: $(r,s) = (2,2)$ (ellipse $\xi^2 + \eta^2 = 1$), $(2,1)$ (hyperbola $\xi^2 - \eta^2 = 1$), and $(1,1)$ (two parallel lines $\xi^2 = 1$). For quadrics without center, the condition $r \leq 2s$ and $1 \leq r \leq n-1 = 1$ give only $r = 1$, $s = 1$ (parabola $\xi^2 = 2\eta$).

For 3-space ($n = 3$): Centered quadrics have $r = 1, 2, 3$ with $1 \leq s \leq r$, giving six classes: $(3,3)$ (ellipsoid), $(3,2)$ (one-sheeted hyperboloid), $(3,1)$ (two-sheeted hyperboloid), $(2,2)$ (elliptic cylinder), $(2,1)$ (hyperbolic cylinder), $(1,1)$ (two parallel planes). Centerless quadrics satisfy $r \leq 2s$ and $1 \leq r \leq 2$, so $r = 1, 2$. For $r = 2$, $s = 1, 2$ (both satisfy $2 \leq 2s$), giving $(2,2)$ (elliptic paraboloid) and $(2,1)$ (hyperbolic paraboloid). For $r = 1$, $s = 1$ (parabolic cylinder). Total: three centerless classes in 3-space.
