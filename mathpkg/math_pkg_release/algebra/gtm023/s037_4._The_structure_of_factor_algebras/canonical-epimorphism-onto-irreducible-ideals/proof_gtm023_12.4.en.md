The map $\varphi_i$ is clearly a homomorphism since it is defined on the free algebra generators. To see it is surjective, note that $\varphi_i$ maps $\Gamma[t]$ onto the polynomial algebra generated by $\bar{t}_i$ and $\bar{e}_i$, which is precisely $I_i$ by the definition of $I_i$ as the ideal generated by $\bar{q}_i$ and the decomposition formulas of Theorem I.

To determine the kernel, observe that $\varphi_i(f) = f(\bar{t}_i)$. Since $f_i^{k_i} \mid f$ and $\bar{t}_i$ satisfies $f_i^{k_i}(\bar{t}_i) = 0$, we have $f(\bar{t}_i) = 0$, i.e., $\varphi_i(f) = 0$. Now consider the induced map

$$\bar{\varphi}_i : \Gamma[t]/I_f \to I_i.$$

Then

$$\bar{\varphi}_i(\bar{q}) = q(\bar{t}_i), \quad q \in \Gamma[t]$$

and, in view of formula (12.22),

$$\ker \bar{\varphi}_i = \sum_{j \neq i} I_j.$$

But $\sum_{j \neq i} I_j$ is the ideal generated by $\bar{f}_i^{k_i}$, and thus

$$\ker \varphi_i = I_{f_i^{k_i}}.$$

This completes the proof.
