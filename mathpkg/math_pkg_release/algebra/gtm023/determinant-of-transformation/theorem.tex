Let $\varphi$ be a linear transformation of the $n$-dimensional linear space $E$. Choose a non-trivial determinant function $\Delta$. Then $\Delta_\varphi$ defined by
$$\Delta_\varphi(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = \Delta(\varphi x_1, \ldots, \varphi x_n)$$
is again a determinant function. Hence $\Delta_\varphi = \alpha \Delta$ for a unique scalar $\alpha$ independent of $\Delta$. This scalar is the determinant of $\varphi$, denoted $\det \varphi$:
$$\Delta(\varphi x_1, \ldots, \varphi x_n) = \det \varphi \cdot \Delta(x_1, \ldots, x_n).$$

Properties:
(i) $\det(\lambda\iota) = \lambda^n$
(ii) $\det(\psi \circ \varphi) = \det \psi \cdot \det \varphi$
(iii) $\varphi$ is regular (invertible) iff $\det \varphi \neq 0$.
