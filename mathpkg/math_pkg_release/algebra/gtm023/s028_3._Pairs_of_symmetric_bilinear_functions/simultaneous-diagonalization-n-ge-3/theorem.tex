Let $E$ be a real vector space of dimension $n \geq 3$. Let $\Phi$ and $\Psi$ be symmetric bilinear functions on $E$ such that
$$\Phi(x)^2 + \Psi(x)^2 \neq 0 \quad \text{for all } x \neq 0,$$
where $\Phi(x) = \Phi(x,x)$ and $\Psi(x) = \Psi(x,x)$ denote the associated quadratic forms. Then there exists a basis $\{e_v\}_{v=1}^n$ of $E$ such that both $\Phi$ and $\Psi$ have diagonal form:
$$\Phi(e_v, e_\mu) = 0, \quad \Psi(e_v, e_\mu) = 0 \quad \text{for } v \neq \mu.$$
Moreover, by suitably normalizing the basis vectors, one can achieve
$$\Psi(e_v, e_\mu) = \varepsilon_v \delta_{v\mu}, \quad \varepsilon_v = \begin{cases} +1 & (v = 1, \ldots, s) \\ -1 & (v = s+1, \ldots, n) \end{cases}$$
and
$$\Phi(e_v, e_\mu) = \lambda_v \varepsilon_v \delta_{v\mu},$$
where $s$ is the signature of $\Psi$ and $\lambda_v \in \mathbb{R}$.
