Let $\Phi$ and $\Psi$ be symmetric bilinear functions on a finite-dimensional vector space $E$ over a field of characteristic $\neq 2$. Assume $\Psi$ is non-degenerate and let $\varphi: E \to E$ be the linear transformation satisfying
$$\Phi(x, y) = \Psi(\varphi x, y) \quad \text{for all } x, y \in E.$$
Then $\Phi$ and $\Psi$ can be simultaneously diagonalized if and only if $\varphi$ admits a basis of eigenvectors. More precisely:
\begin{enumerate}
\item If $\varphi$ is diagonalizable with eigenspaces $E_i$ corresponding to distinct eigenvalues $\lambda_i$, then in a basis adapted to the decomposition $E = \bigoplus_i E_i$, both $\Phi$ and $\Psi$ have diagonal form, with
$$\Phi(x, y) = \lambda_i \Psi(x, y) \quad \text{for } x, y \in E_i.$$
\item Conversely, if there exists a basis $\{e_i\}_{i=1}^n$ of $E$ such that
$$\Phi(e_i, e_j) = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \Psi(e_i, e_j) = 0 \quad \text{for } i \neq j,$$
then the $e_i$ are eigenvectors of $\varphi$.
\end{enumerate}
