Over a complex vector space, every linear transformation admits at least one eigenvalue. This follows directly from the fundamental theorem of algebra: the characteristic polynomial is a non-constant polynomial over $\mathbb{C}$ and therefore has at least one root. This starkly contrasts with the real case, where rotations in $\mathbb{R}^2$ provide examples of transformations without real eigenvalues.
