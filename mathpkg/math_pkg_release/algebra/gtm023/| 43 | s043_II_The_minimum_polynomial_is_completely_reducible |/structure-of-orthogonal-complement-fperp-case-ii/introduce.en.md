Since the invariant plane $F$ in Case II is pseudo-Euclidean of index $1$, its orthogonal complement $F^\perp$ naturally inherits a positive definite (Euclidean) inner product. The restriction of the Lorentz transformation $\varphi$ to $F^\perp$ is then a Euclidean rotation. The crucial observation is that the rotation angle is restricted to $0$ or $\pi$, since any other rotation angle would produce a non-real eigenvalue pair, giving the minimum polynomial an irreducible quadratic factor---contradicting the defining property of Case II that the minimum polynomial is completely reducible.
