We proceed by induction on $n$. For $n=1$, let $a$ be a basis vector of $E$. Then any two non-zero vectors $x, y$ satisfy $x = \lambda a$, $y = \mu a$ with $\lambda, \mu \neq 0$, so $\mu x - \lambda y = 0$, giving a non-trivial linear dependence.

Assume the statement holds for all spaces with a basis of fewer than $n$ vectors. Let $a_1, \ldots, a_n$ be a basis of $E$ and $x_1, \ldots, x_{n+1}$ be any $n+1$ vectors. We may assume $x_{n+1} \neq 0$ (otherwise the vectors are trivially dependent). Consider the factor space $E_1 = E / (x_{n+1})$ where $(x_{n+1})$ is the subspace generated by $x_{n+1}$, and the canonical projection $\pi: E \to E/(x_{n+1})$.

The system $\bar{a}_1, \ldots, \bar{a}_n$ generates $E_1$. Since $x_{n+1} = \sum_{v=1}^n \lambda^v a_v$, we have $\sum_{v=1}^n \lambda^v \bar{a}_v = 0$, so the vectors $\bar{a}_1, \ldots, \bar{a}_n$ are linearly dependent. Thus $E_1$ has a basis of fewer than $n$ vectors. By the induction hypothesis, $\bar{x}_1, \ldots, \bar{x}_n$ are linearly dependent. Hence there exists a non-trivial relation $\sum_{v=1}^n \xi^v \bar{x}_v = 0$, giving $\sum_{v=1}^n \xi^v x_v = \xi^{n+1} x_{n+1}$, which shows $x_1, \ldots, x_{n+1}$ are linearly dependent.
