Linear independence is the defining property that makes a set of vectors "minimal" for spanning: no vector can be written as a linear combination of the others. Equivalently, the only linear combination yielding the zero vector is the trivial one (all coefficients zero). Linear independence and spanning together characterize a basis, making it the central structural concept in linear algebra. Every subfamily of a linearly independent family remains independent, and distinct vectors in such a family are necessarily distinct elements of the space.
