**Proof.** 

**1)** Consider the surjective homomorphism $\Gamma[t] \to A$ given by $t \mapsto \bar{t}_S$. Since $g$ is the minimum polynomial of $\bar{t}_S$, this homomorphism induces an isomorphism

$$\Gamma[t] / I_g \xrightarrow{\cong} A.$$

According to Theorem III (applied to $g$), $\Gamma[t]/I_g$ is the unique direct sum of irreducible ideals $I_i$, where $I_i \cong \Gamma[t]/I_{f_i}$ for $i = 1, \dots, r$. Let $I_i$ also denote the corresponding ideal in $A$. Then

$$A = I_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus I_r$$

and the $I_i$ are irreducible ideals. Finally, since each $f_i$ is irreducible, Theorem I of sec. 12.13 implies that $\Gamma[t]/I_{f_i}$ is a field. This establishes part 1.

**2) and 3):** Let $I_N$ denote the ideal generated by $\bar{t}_N$ and let $I$ be the ideal of nilpotent elements in $\Gamma(\bar{t})$. Since $\bar{t}_N$ is nilpotent, we have

$$I_N \subset I \subset \operatorname{rad} \Gamma(\bar{t}). \tag{12.34}$$

Next, since $\bar{t} = \bar{t}_S + \bar{t}_N$, it follows that

$$\Gamma(\bar{t}) = A + I_N. \tag{12.35}$$

Moreover, since $A$ is the direct sum of the fields $I_i$, it contains no nonzero nilpotent elements. Thus $A \cap \operatorname{rad} \Gamma(\bar{t}) = 0$, which together with (12.34) and (12.35) yields

$$\Gamma(\bar{t}) = A \oplus \operatorname{rad} \Gamma(\bar{t}).$$

This proves part 2 (and implicitly part 3, since the radical is precisely the ideal of nilpotent elements).

**4)** We show first that every element in $A$ is semisimple. Indeed, let $\bar{x} \in A$. By Theorem IV (sec. 12.16), there exist elements $\bar{x}_S \in \Gamma(\bar{t})$ and $\bar{x}_N \in \Gamma(\bar{t})$ such that $\bar{x}_S$ is semisimple, $\bar{x}_N$ is nilpotent, and

$$\bar{x} = \bar{x}_S + \bar{x}_N.$$

In particular, $\bar{x}_N$ is nilpotent in $A$. Since $A$ is a direct sum of fields (by part 1), it contains no nonzero nilpotent elements. Thus $\bar{x}_N = 0$ and $\bar{x}$ is semisimple.

Conversely, let $\bar{x} \in \Gamma(\bar{t})$ be semisimple. In view of part 2 we can write

$$\bar{x} = \bar{x}_A + \bar{x}_R, \quad \bar{x}_A \in A, \quad \bar{x}_R \in \operatorname{rad} \Gamma(\bar{t}). \tag{12.38}$$

Then $\bar{x}_A$ is semisimple (by the first part) and $\bar{x}_R$ is nilpotent. Thus (12.38) must be the unique decomposition of $\bar{x}$ into its semisimple and nilpotent parts (Theorem IV). Since $\bar{x}$ is semisimple, it follows that $\bar{x}_R = 0$, whence $\bar{x} \in A$. This proves part 3.

**5)** Let $B$ be any subalgebra of $\Gamma(\bar{t})$ which complements the radical. Then dividing out by the radical yields an algebra isomorphism $A \xrightarrow{\cong} B$. Thus, by part 3, the elements of $B$ are semisimple. Hence, again by part 3, $B \subset A$. It follows that $B = A$, proving part 4.

This completes the proof of Theorem V.
