This prime ideal is called the center of $P$ in $o$. The center $p$ is always different from $o$ since $1 \notin M_p$; it is the zero ideal if and only if the restriction of $P$ to $o$ is an isomorphism (in particular, $p = (0)$ if $P$ is a place of $K$ over $o$). It is clear that the residue class ring $o/p$ is isomorphic to the subring $o/P$ of the residue field $\Delta$ of $P$.

Since any element of $o$ which is not in the center $p$ of $P$ in $o$ is a unit in the valuation ring $K_p$, it follows that $P$ is also finite on the local ring $o_p$ of the specialization induced by $P$ in $o$, and it is clear that the center of $P$ in $o_p$ is the maximal ideal $p_0_p$ in $o_p$. Conversely, if $p$ is a prime ideal in $o$, different from $o$, and if $P$ is a place of $K$ such that (1) $P$ is finite on $o_p$ and (2) the center of $P$ in $o_p$ is the maximal ideal $m$ in $o_p$, then $P$ is also finite on $o$ and has center $p$ in $o$ (since $m \cap o = p$). Note that condition (1) by itself is only equivalent to the following condition: $P$ is finite on $o$ and its center in $o$ is contained in $p$.

Isomorphic places have the same center in any ring $o$ on which they are finite. On the other hand, if we have two places $P$ and $Q$ such that $Q$ is a specialization of $P$, then if $Q$ is finite on $o$ also $P$ is finite on $o$ (since $K_p \supset K_2$) and the center of $P$ in $o$ is contained in the center of $Q$ in $o$ (for $M_p \subset M_2$).
