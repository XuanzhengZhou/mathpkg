When $\mathfrak{o} = \Phi$ is a field and the module is the whole vector space, the ring $\mathfrak{B}$ of $\mathfrak{o}$-endomorphisms coincides with the full ring $\mathfrak{L}$ of all linear transformations. By Theorem 20, the center of $\mathfrak{B}$ is $\mathfrak{o}_l$, which in this context is precisely $\Phi_1 = \{\alpha 1 : \alpha \in \Phi\}$. 

In matrix language, if an $n \times n$ matrix $M$ commutes with every $n \times n$ matrix, then in particular it commutes with the matrix units $E_{ij}$ (having 1 in position $(i,j)$ and 0 elsewhere). The condition $M E_{ij} = E_{ij} M$ for all $i,j$ forces all diagonal entries of $M$ to be equal and all off-diagonal entries to be zero, so $M = \alpha I$ for some $\alpha \in \Phi$.

A more direct proof can also be given later (as noted in the text, Chapter VIII, p. 229).
