Let $\Re$ be a Euclidean space with scalar product $(x, y)$. If $A$ is a linear transformation of $\Re$ over $\Phi$ into itself, then $g(x, y) = (xA, y)$ defines a scalar product on $\Re$.

Conversely, for any scalar product $g(x, y)$ on $\Re$, there exists a uniquely determined linear transformation $A$ such that $g(x, y) = (xA, y)$ for all $y$.

The \textbf{adjoint} (or transpose) $A'$ of a linear transformation $A$ is the uniquely determined linear transformation satisfying

$$(xA, y) = (x, yA')$$

for all $x, y \in \Re$. With respect to a Cartesian basis $(u_1, u_2, \cdots, u_n)$, the matrix of $A'$ is the transpose of the matrix of $A$.
