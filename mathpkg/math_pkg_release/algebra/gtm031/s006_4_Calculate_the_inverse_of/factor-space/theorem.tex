Let $\mathfrak{S}$ be any subspace of a vector space $\mathfrak{R}$ over a division ring $\Delta$. Then $\mathfrak{S}$ is a subgroup of the additive group of $\mathfrak{R}$. Since $\mathfrak{R}$ is commutative, we can define the factor group $\bar{\mathfrak{R}} = \mathfrak{R} / \mathfrak{S}$. The elements of this group are the cosets $\bar{x} = x + \mathfrak{S}$, and composition in $\bar{\mathfrak{R}}$ is given by
$$
\bar{x} + \bar{y} = \overline{x + y}.
$$
For any $\alpha \in \Delta$, if $x \equiv y \pmod{\mathfrak{S}}$, i.e., $x - y = z \in \mathfrak{S}$, then $\alpha z \in \mathfrak{S}$; hence $\alpha x \equiv \alpha y \pmod{\mathfrak{S}}$. Thus the coset $\overline{\alpha x}$ is uniquely determined by the coset $\bar{x}$ and by $\alpha \in \Delta$. Define $\alpha \bar{x} = \overline{\alpha x}$. Then $\bar{\mathfrak{R}}$, $\Delta$, and the scalar multiplication $(\alpha, \bar{x}) \mapsto \alpha \bar{x}$ constitute a vector space, called the \text{factor space} of $\mathfrak{R}$ relative to the subspace $\mathfrak{S}$.
