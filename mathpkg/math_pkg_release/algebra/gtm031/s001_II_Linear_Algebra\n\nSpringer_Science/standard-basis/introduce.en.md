The standard basis of \(\Delta^{(n)}\) consists of the vectors \(e_i\) that have 1 in the \(i\)-th coordinate and 0 elsewhere. Every vector \(x = (\xi_1, \dots, \xi_n)\) can be written uniquely as the linear combination \(\sum \xi_i e_i\). Jacobson uses this explicit basis to verify that \(\Delta^{(n)}\) satisfies property F, thereby confirming that \(\Delta^{(n)}\) is a finite dimensional vector space. The standard basis also serves as the prototype for the general concept of a basis introduced later in the chapter.
