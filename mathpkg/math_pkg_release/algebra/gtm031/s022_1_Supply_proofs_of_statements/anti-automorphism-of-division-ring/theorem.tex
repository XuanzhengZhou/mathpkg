Let $\Delta$ be a division ring. An **anti-automorphism** of $\Delta$ is a bijective mapping $\alpha \mapsto \bar{\alpha}$ from $\Delta$ onto itself satisfying the following conditions for all $\alpha, \beta \in \Delta$:

$$\overline{\alpha + \beta} = \bar{\alpha} + \bar{\beta},$$
$$\overline{\alpha \beta} = \bar{\beta}\, \bar{\alpha}.$$

If in addition $\bar{\bar{\alpha}} = \alpha$ for all $\alpha \in \Delta$, the anti-automorphism is called **involutorial**.

**Examples:**
1. $\Delta = \Phi$ a field: any automorphism of $\Phi$ is an anti-automorphism (since multiplication is commutative). In particular, the identity $\bar{\alpha} = \alpha$ is an involutorial anti-automorphism.
2. $\Delta = $ Hamilton's quaternions: the conjugation mapping a quaternion to its conjugate is an involutorial anti-automorphism.
