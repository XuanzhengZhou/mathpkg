When a Euclidean space is irreducible under a commutative set of linear transformations that are each either symmetric or skew, the space cannot have dimension greater than two. The proof hinges on analyzing the irreducible factors of characteristic polynomials: if any symmetric transformation in the set is nonzero, it must be a scalar multiplication (forcing dimension one); otherwise, all skew transformations are scalar multiples of a fixed invertible skew transformation $A$ with $A^2 = -\delta^2 1$, and the space can be at most two-dimensional. This structural result is fundamental for the simultaneous canonical form theorems that follow.
