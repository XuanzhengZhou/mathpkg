Suppose $V$ is a finite dimensional irreducible $L$-module. Then $V$ has at least one maximal vector, of uniquely determined weight $\lambda$, and the submodule it generates must be all of $V$ (by irreducibility). Therefore, $V$ is isomorphic to $V(\lambda)$ (Theorems A and B of (20.3)).

For each simple root $\alpha_i$, let $S_i = S_{\alpha_i}$ be the corresponding copy of $\mathfrak{sl}(2, F)$ in $L$. Then $V(\lambda)$ is also a finite dimensional module for $S_i$, and a maximal vector for $L$ is also a maximal vector for $S_i$. In particular, since there is a maximal vector of weight $\lambda$, the weight for the CSA $H_i \subset S_i$ is completely determined by the scalar $\lambda(h_i)$, where $h_i = h_{\alpha_i}$. By Theorem 7.2, a finite dimensional irreducible $\mathfrak{sl}(2, F)$-module has highest weight a nonnegative integer. Hence $\lambda(h_i)$ must be a nonnegative integer for each simple root $\alpha_i$. Thus $\lambda$ is dominant integral.
