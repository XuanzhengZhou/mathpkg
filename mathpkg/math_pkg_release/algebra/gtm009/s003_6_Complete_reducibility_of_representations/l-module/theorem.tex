Let $L$ be a Lie algebra. A vector space $V$, endowed with an operation $L \times V \rightarrow V$ (denoted $(x, v) \mapsto x.v$ or $xv$) is called an $L$-module if the following conditions are satisfied for all $x, y \in L$, $v, w \in V$, $a, b \in \mathbb{F}$:

\begin{itemize}
\item[(M1)] $(ax + by).v = a(x.v) + b(y.v)$,
\item[(M2)] $x.(av + bw) = a(x.v) + b(x.w)$,
\item[(M3)] $[xy].v = x.y.v - y.x.v$.
\end{itemize}

If $\phi: L \rightarrow \mathfrak{gl}(V)$ is a representation of $L$, then $V$ may be viewed as an $L$-module via $x.v = \phi(x)(v)$. Conversely, given an $L$-module $V$, this equation defines a representation $\phi: L \rightarrow \mathfrak{gl}(V)$.
