\textbf{(a) Existence.} This follows directly from Serre's Theorem (18.3). Given any root system $\Phi$ with base $\Delta$, let $(c_{ij})$ be the corresponding Cartan matrix. Construct the Lie algebra $L$ with generators $\{x_i, y_i, h_i\}$ and Serre relations as in Theorem 18.3. The theorem guarantees that $L$ is a finite-dimensional semisimple Lie algebra with Cartan subalgebra $H = \sum F h_i$ and root system $\Phi$, where $x_i \in L_{\alpha_i}$, $y_i \in L_{-\alpha_i}$.

\textbf{(b) Uniqueness.} It suffices to prove the statement when $L$ is the Lie algebra constructed in Serre's Theorem, with generators $x_{\alpha}, y_{\alpha}$ ($\alpha \in \Delta$) and $h_{\alpha} = [x_{\alpha}, y_{\alpha}]$.

Given the isomorphism $\Phi \cong \Phi'$, set $h_{\alpha'}' = \pi_0(h_{\alpha})$ for each $\alpha' \in \Delta'$ corresponding to $\alpha \in \Delta$. Since $[x_{\alpha'}', L_{-\alpha'}']$ is one-dimensional and contains $h_{\alpha'}'$ (as the image of $h_{\alpha}$ under the CSA isomorphism), there exists a unique $y_{\alpha'}' \in L_{-\alpha'}'$ such that $[x_{\alpha'}', y_{\alpha'}'] = h_{\alpha'}'$.

Now the elements $\{x_{\alpha'}', y_{\alpha'}', h_{\alpha'}'\}$ in $L'$ satisfy the Serre relations for the Cartan matrix of $\Phi'$, which is identical to that of $\Phi$ (since $\Phi \cong \Phi'$). By the universal property of the Lie algebra presented by these generators and relations (Serre's Theorem), there exists a unique surjective homomorphism $\pi: L \to L'$ sending $x_{\alpha} \mapsto x_{\alpha'}'$, $y_{\alpha} \mapsto y_{\alpha'}'$, $h_{\alpha} \mapsto h_{\alpha'}'$.

Since both $L$ and $L'$ are semisimple, $\ker \pi$ is an ideal of $L$. But any nonzero ideal of a semisimple Lie algebra contains a nonzero root space (by the structure theory), which would mean some nonzero $x_{\alpha}$ maps to zero. This would contradict the construction, so $\ker \pi = 0$. Thus $\pi$ is an isomorphism. Uniqueness follows because $L$ is generated by the $x_{\alpha}, y_{\alpha}, h_{\alpha}$, and the image of a generating set determines a homomorphism uniquely.
