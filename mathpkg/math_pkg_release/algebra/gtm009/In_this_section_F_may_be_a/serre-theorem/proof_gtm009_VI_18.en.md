The proof proceeds in several steps. Let $I$ (resp. $J$) be the ideal of $L_o = \hat{L}/K$ generated by the $x_{ij}$ (resp. $y_{ij}$), where $K$ is the ideal generated by relations (S1)-(S3) and $x_{ij} = (\operatorname{ad} x_i)^{-c_{ji}+1}(x_j)$, $y_{ij} = (\operatorname{ad} y_i)^{-c_{ji}+1}(y_j)$. Set $L = L_o/(I+J)$.

\textbf{Step (1):} $I$ is an ideal of $L_o$ contained in $X$ (the subalgebra generated by the $x_i$). This follows because the $x_{ij}$ are expressed as iterated brackets of the $x_i$, so they lie in $X$. The Serre Lemma (ad $x_k(y_{ij}) = 0$) shows that $[L_o, y_{ij}] \subset J$ for the $Y$-component, and the $S$-invariance forces $I$ to be an ideal.

\textbf{Step (2):} $I \cap \sum F h_i = 0$ and $J \cap \sum F h_i = 0$. This follows from the faithful action constructed in the hat-L-representation: the image of $\sum F\hat{h}_j$ in $L$ has the same dimension as in $L_o$, so no linear combination of $h_i$ lies in $I$ or $J$.

\textbf{Step (3):} $L = N^- \oplus H \oplus N$ (direct sum of subspaces), where $N^- = Y/J$, $N = X/I$, and $H = \sum F h_i$. Moreover, $\sum F x_i + \sum F h_i + \sum F y_i$ maps isomorphically into $L$, so $x_i, y_i, h_i$ may be identified with elements of $L$.

\textbf{Step (4):} The weight space decomposition holds. Define $L_\lambda = \{x \in L \mid [h x] = \lambda(h)x \text{ for all } h \in H\}$. Then $H = L_0$, $N = \sum_{\lambda > 0} L_\lambda$, $N^- = \sum_{\lambda < 0} L_\lambda$, and each $L_\lambda$ is finite-dimensional. This is clear from the direct sum decomposition and the identification of $x_i, y_i$ as root vectors.

\textbf{Step (5):} $\operatorname{ad} x_i$ and $\operatorname{ad} y_i$ are locally nilpotent. Let $M$ be the set of all elements of $L$ killed by some power of $\operatorname{ad} x_i$. Lemma 15.1 shows $M$ is a subalgebra. All $x_k$ lie in $M$ (by $(S_{ij}^+)$), all $y_k$ also lie in $M$ (by (S2) and the $\mathfrak{sl}(2)$ structure), and all $h_k = [x_k y_k] \in M$. Since these generate $L$, we have $M = L$, proving local nilpotence of $\operatorname{ad} x_i$. Similarly for $\operatorname{ad} y_i$.

\textbf{Step (6):} $L$ is finite-dimensional and semisimple. Since $\operatorname{ad} x_i$ and $\operatorname{ad} y_i$ are locally nilpotent for each $i$, and there are only finitely many generators, standard arguments show that the set of weights is finite and each weight space is finite-dimensional. Hence $\dim L < \infty$. The relations imply that the Killing form is non-degenerate (one checks that the radical vanishes using the weight space decomposition), so $L$ is semisimple.

\textbf{Step (7):} The root system of $L$ is $\Phi$. The $h_i$ span a Cartan subalgebra $H$. The weights of the adjoint action are identified with the roots, and the relations $(S_{ij}^\pm)$ guarantee exactly the root string lengths prescribed by the Cartan integers $c_{ij}$. Thus $\Phi$ is the root system of $L$ with base $\Delta$.
