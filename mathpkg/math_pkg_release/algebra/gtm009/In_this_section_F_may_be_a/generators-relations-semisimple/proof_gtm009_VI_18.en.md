Proposition 14.2 implies that $L$ is already generated by the $x_i$ and $y_i$ (along with the $h_i = [x_i y_i]$).

(S1) holds because $H$ is abelian.

(S2) $[x_i y_i] = h_i$ by the definition of standard generators. For $i \neq j$, $[x_i y_j] \in L_{\alpha_i - \alpha_j}$. Since $\alpha_i - \alpha_j$ is not a root (being the difference of distinct simple roots), $L_{\alpha_i - \alpha_j} = 0$, hence $[x_i y_j] = 0$.

(S3) Since $x_j \in L_{\alpha_j}$, we have $[h_i x_j] = \alpha_j(h_i)x_j = \langle \alpha_j, \alpha_i \rangle x_j$. Similarly, $y_j \in L_{-\alpha_j}$ gives $[h_i y_j] = -\alpha_j(h_i)y_j = -\langle \alpha_j, \alpha_i \rangle y_j$.

For $(S_{ij}^+)$: The element $(\operatorname{ad} x_i)^{-\langle \alpha_j, \alpha_i \rangle + 1}(x_j)$ lies in $L_{\alpha_j + (-\langle \alpha_j, \alpha_i \rangle + 1)\alpha_i}$. By the properties of root strings (the $\alpha_j$-string through $\alpha_i$ has length at most $-\langle \alpha_j, \alpha_i \rangle + 1$), this weight exceeds the maximal root in the $\alpha_i$-string through $\alpha_j$, so the corresponding root space is zero. The argument for $(S_{ij}^-)$ is symmetric.
