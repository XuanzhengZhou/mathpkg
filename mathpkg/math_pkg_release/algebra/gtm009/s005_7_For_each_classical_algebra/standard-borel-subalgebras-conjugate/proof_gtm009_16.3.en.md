The first statement (that $B(\Delta)$ is a Borel subalgebra) is proved in the text preceding the lemma:

Since $B(\Delta) = H + \sum_{\alpha > 0} L_{\alpha}$ is a subalgebra with derived algebra $N(\Delta) = \sum_{\alpha > 0} L_{\alpha}$, and $N(\Delta)$ is nilpotent (applying $\operatorname{ad} x$ for $x \in L_{\alpha}$ to root vectors of positive height increases height by at least one), it follows that $B(\Delta)$ is solvable.

To see that $B(\Delta)$ is maximal solvable: let $K$ be any subalgebra properly containing $B(\Delta)$. Since $K$ is stable under $\operatorname{ad} H$, it must include some $L_{\alpha}$ for $\alpha < 0$. But then $K$ contains the simple subalgebra $S_{\alpha} \cong \mathfrak{sl}(2, F)$ generated by $L_{\alpha}$ and $L_{-\alpha}$, which is not solvable. Hence no proper solvable extension of $B(\Delta)$ exists.

For the second statement (conjugacy): recall from (14.3) that the simple reflection $\sigma_{\alpha}$ acting on $H$ may be extended to an inner automorphism $\tau_{\alpha}$ of $L$, which by construction lies in $\mathcal{E}(L)$. This automorphism sends $B(\Delta)$ to $B(\sigma_{\alpha} \Delta)$.

Since the Weyl group $W$ acts transitively on the set of bases of $\Phi$, any base $\Delta'$ can be obtained from $\Delta$ by a sequence of simple reflections. Composing the corresponding automorphisms $\tau_{\alpha}$ yields an element of $\mathcal{E}(L)$ sending $B(\Delta)$ to $B(\Delta')$. Thus all standard Borel subalgebras relative to $H$ are conjugate under $\mathcal{E}(L)$.
