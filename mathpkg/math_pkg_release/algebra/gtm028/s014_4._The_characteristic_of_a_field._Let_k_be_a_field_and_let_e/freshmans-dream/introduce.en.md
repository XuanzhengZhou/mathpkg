In any field $k$ of positive characteristic $p$, the so-called "freshman's dream" identities hold: $p a = 0$ for all $a \in k$, and $(b \pm c)^p = b^p \pm c^p$. The first identity follows from $pa = p(ea) = (pe)a = 0$. The second follows from the binomial theorem: all intermediate binomial coefficients $\binom{p}{i}$ for $1 \le i \le p-1$ are divisible by $p$, hence vanish in $k$ by the first identity. For $p = 2$, the minus case becomes $(b-c)^2 = b^2 + c^2$, which is consistent because $2c^2 = 0$.
