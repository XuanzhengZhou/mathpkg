The **characteristic** of a field $k$ classifies fields into two fundamental types based on the behavior of the canonical homomorphism $\varphi: \mathbb{Z} \to k$ sending $n \mapsto ne$ (where $e$ is the multiplicative identity). If $\varphi$ is injective, $k$ has characteristic zero and its prime subfield is isomorphic to $\mathbb{Q}$. If $\varphi$ has a nontrivial kernel, then $\ker \varphi = p\mathbb{Z}$ where $p$ is a prime number, $k$ has characteristic $p$, and its prime subfield is the finite field $\mathbb{F}_p$ of $p$ elements. The characteristic governs arithmetic identities such as $pa = 0$ and the freshman's dream $(a + b)^p = a^p + b^p$ in characteristic $p$.
