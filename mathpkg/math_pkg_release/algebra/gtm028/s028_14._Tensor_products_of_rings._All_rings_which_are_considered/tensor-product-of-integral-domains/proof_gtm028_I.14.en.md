Let $z \in A \otimes_k B$ be a regular element. Suppose $x z = 0$ for some $x \in K \otimes_k K'$ with $x \neq 0$. We must show this is impossible.

Write $x$ and $z$ in terms of finite subsets. There exist finite-dimensional $k$-subspaces $A' \subseteq A$ and $B' \subseteq B$ such that $z \in A' \otimes_k B'$. Let $\{u_m\}$ be a $k$-basis of $A'$ and $\{v_n\}$ a $k$-basis of $B'$, so that

$$z = \sum_{m,n} c_{mn} u_m \otimes v_n, \quad c_{mn} \in k.$$

Similarly, there exist elements $\alpha' \in A'$ and $\beta' \in B'$, both non-zero, such that $\alpha'$ and $\beta'$ clear denominators so that

$$\alpha' z = \sum_m a_m \otimes v_m, \quad \beta' z = \sum_n u_n \otimes b_n$$

where $a_m \in A'$, $b_n \in B'$. Consequently, there exist $\alpha', \beta' \in A', B'$ with

$$\alpha' \beta' z = \sum_{m,n} \left( \sum_i a'_{im} b'_{in} \right) u_m \otimes v_n$$

where $a'_{im} \in A'$, $b'_{in} \in B'$.

Now suppose $x' \in [A', B']$ (the subring of $A \otimes_k B$ generated by $A'$ and $B'$) satisfies $x' \alpha' \beta' z = 0$. Then

$$0 = x' \alpha' \beta' z = \sum_{m,n} \left( x' \sum_i a'_{im} b'_{in} \right) u_m \otimes v_n.$$

Set $y'_{mn} = x' \sum_i a'_{im} b'_{in} \in [A', B']$. We can write

$$y'_{mn} = \sum_{p,q} c_{mnpq} a'_p b'_q$$

where $c_{mnpq} \in k$, the $a'_p$ are $k$-linearly independent elements of $A'$, and the $b'_q$ are $k$-linearly independent elements of $B'$. The linear independence of the $v_n$ over $B'$ and of the $b'_q$ over $k$ implies the products $b'_q v_n$ (in $B$) are $k$-linearly independent. Similarly $a'_p u_m$ are $k$-linearly independent. By the linear disjointness of $A$ and $B$ over $k$ in their quotient fields $K$ and $K'$, the products $u_m v_n a'_p b'_q$ are $k$-linearly independent.

Now the relations above yield

$$\sum_{m,n,p,q} c_{mnpq} u_m v_n a'_p b'_q = 0.$$

Linear independence forces all $c_{mnpq} = 0$, hence all $y'_{mn} = 0$, and thus $x' = 0$ (since $x'$ was in the ring generated by $A'$ and $B'$ and we can express it in terms of the basis). This shows that no non-zero element of $[A, B] = A \otimes_k B$ can annihilate a regular element $z$ after clearing denominators, hence $z$ remains regular in $K \otimes_k K'$.

Finally, the total quotient ring of $A \otimes_k B$ is $K \otimes_k K'$ because every element of $K \otimes_k K'$ can be written with a common denominator from $A \otimes_k B$, and the regular elements of $A \otimes_k B$ become invertible in $K \otimes_k K'$.
