Let $K = k(\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \cdots, \alpha_n)$ be a finite separable extension of $k$, generated by the separable algebraic elements $\alpha_1, \cdots, \alpha_n$ over $k$. We prove the theorem by induction on $n$. It suffices to consider the case $n = 2$, since then $k(\alpha_1, \alpha_2) = k(\theta)$ for some $\theta$, and $k(\theta, \alpha_3) = k(\theta')$, etc.

Thus let $K = k(\alpha, \beta)$ with $\alpha, \beta$ separable over $k$. Let $f(X)$ and $g(X)$ be the minimal polynomials of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ over $k$, respectively. Let $\alpha = \alpha_1, \alpha_2, \cdots, \alpha_r$ be the roots of $f$ and $\beta = \beta_1, \beta_2, \cdots, \beta_s$ the roots of $g$ in some algebraic closure of $k$.

If $k$ is finite, then $K$ is a finite field, and we already know that the multiplicative group of $K$ is cyclic; a generator of this group is a primitive element of $K/k$.

Assume now that $k$ is infinite. Consider the elements
\[
\gamma_{ij} = \frac{\alpha - \alpha_i}{\beta_j - \beta}, \qquad i = 1, 2, \cdots, r, \quad j = 2, 3, \cdots, s.
\]
Since $k$ is infinite, we can choose an element $c \in k$ different from all the $\gamma_{ij}$ (there are only finitely many of them). Set $\theta = \alpha + c\beta$. We claim that $k(\theta) = k(\alpha, \beta)$.

To prove this, consider the polynomial $h(X) = f(\theta - cX) \in k(\theta)[X]$. Then $h(\beta) = f(\theta - c\beta) = f(\alpha) = 0$. On the other hand, for any $j \neq 1$, we have $\theta - c\beta_j = \alpha + c(\beta - \beta_j) \neq \alpha_i$ for any $i$, because otherwise $c = (\alpha - \alpha_i)/(\beta_j - \beta) = \gamma_{ij}$, contradicting our choice of $c$. Hence $h(\beta_j) = f(\theta - c\beta_j) \neq 0$ for $j = 2, 3, \cdots, s$.

Thus $\beta$ is the unique common root of $g(X)$ and $h(X)$ in the algebraic closure. Since $g$ and $h$ are polynomials over $k(\theta)$, their greatest common divisor over $k(\theta)$ is $X - \beta$. Therefore $\beta \in k(\theta)$, and consequently $\alpha = \theta - c\beta \in k(\theta)$. Hence $k(\alpha, \beta) = k(\theta)$, completing the proof.
