If $ax = ax'$, then both $x$ and $x'$ are solutions of the equation $ay = b$ where $b = ax = ax'$. Since the equation $ay = b$ has a unique solution by the preceding proposition, we must have $x = x'$. Equivalently, one can multiply on the left by $a^{-1}$: $a^{-1}(ax) = a^{-1}(ax')$ implies $(a^{-1}a)x = (a^{-1}a)x'$, so $ex = ex'$, hence $x = x'$. The right cancellation law $xa = x'a \implies x = x'$ follows by the symmetric argument using right multiplication by $a^{-1}$.
