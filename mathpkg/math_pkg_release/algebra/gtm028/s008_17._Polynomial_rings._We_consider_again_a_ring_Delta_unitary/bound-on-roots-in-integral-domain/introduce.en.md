Over an integral domain, a non-zero polynomial of degree $n$ can have at most $n$ distinct roots. More strongly, if $a_1, \dots, a_m$ are distinct roots, then the product of the corresponding linear factors divides the polynomial. The proof uses induction and the fact that an integral domain has no zero divisors, so $f(a_m) = 0$ forces $q(a_m) = 0$ after factoring out previous linear factors.
