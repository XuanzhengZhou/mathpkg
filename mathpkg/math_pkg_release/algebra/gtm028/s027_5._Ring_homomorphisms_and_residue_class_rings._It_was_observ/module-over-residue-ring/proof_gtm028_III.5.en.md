If $\bar{c}$ arises also from $c_1$ in $R$, then $c_1 - c \in \mathfrak{A}$, hence $c_1x - cx = (c_1 - c)x = 0$ since $\mathfrak{A}$ is contained in the order of $M$. Thus the definition is independent of the choice of representative. Since every $\bar{c}$ in $\bar{R}$ is a residue class $c + \mathfrak{A}$, the definition amounts to setting $\bar{c}x$ equal to $(c + \mathfrak{A})x$, the latter product being intended in the sense of §3; $(c + \mathfrak{A})x$ consists of a single element of $M$ since $\mathfrak{A}x = (0)$. That $M$ thus becomes an $\bar{R}$-module is trivial to verify. Any $R$-submodule of $M$ will be an $\bar{R}$-submodule, and conversely.
