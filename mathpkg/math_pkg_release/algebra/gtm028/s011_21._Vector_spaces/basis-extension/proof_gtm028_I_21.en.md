There exist subsets $S''$ of $S$ such that $L \cup S''$ is free and $L \cap S''$ is empty (for example, the empty set). Thus, among the subsets $S''$ of $S$ such that $L \cup S''$ is free and $L \cap S''$ is empty, we may choose a maximal one, $S'$ (for example, one with the greatest possible number of elements). 

We need only to show now that $V = s(L \cup S')$. By $(S_4)$ this is equivalent to showing that $S \subset s(L \cup S')$, or that, for every element $x$ of $S$ such that $x \notin S'$, we have $x \in s(L \cup S')$. This, however, follows from the fact that the relation $x \notin s(L \cup S')$ would imply that $L \cup S' \cup \{x\}$ is free, according to the remark made above, and this contradicts the maximality of $S'$.
