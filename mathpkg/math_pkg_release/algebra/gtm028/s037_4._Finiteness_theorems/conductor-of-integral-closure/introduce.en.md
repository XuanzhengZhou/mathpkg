The conductor $\mathfrak{f}$ of $A$ in its integral closure $A'$ measures how far $A$ is from being integrally closed. It is the set of elements $z \in A$ that "conduct" the entire integral closure back into $A$: $zA' \subset A$. The conductor is simultaneously an ideal of both $A$ and $A'$, and is in fact the largest such ideal. A ring is integrally closed precisely when its conductor is the unit ideal. The conductor plays a crucial role in localization arguments: if $A'$ is a finite $A$-module then $\mathfrak{f} \neq 0$, and localizing at primes not containing $\mathfrak{f}$ preserves integral closedness.
