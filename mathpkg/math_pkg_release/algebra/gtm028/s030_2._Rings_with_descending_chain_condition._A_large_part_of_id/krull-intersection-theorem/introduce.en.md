Krull's Intersection Theorem is a cornerstone of commutative algebra. For a Noetherian ring $R$ and an ideal $\mathfrak{m}$, the infinite intersection of all powers $\mathfrak{m}^n$ is zero precisely when no element of the form $1 - m$ (with $m \in \mathfrak{m}$) is a zero-divisor. The proof uses the Artin-Rees lemma (or determinant trick) to show that if the intersection is nonzero, one can construct a zero-divisor in $1 - \mathfrak{m}$. Using associated primes of $(0)$, the condition is equivalent to $\mathfrak{m} + \mathfrak{p}_i \neq R$ for all associated primes $\mathfrak{p}_i$ of $(0)$.
