Over a field, each distinct root $c$ of a polynomial $f(x)$ contributes a linear factor $(x-c)$ to its factorization. By induction, $m$ distinct roots yield divisibility by the product $\prod_{i=1}^m (x-c_i)$. This proves the fundamental fact that a non-zero polynomial of degree $n$ can have at most $n$ distinct roots in any field containing its coefficients.
