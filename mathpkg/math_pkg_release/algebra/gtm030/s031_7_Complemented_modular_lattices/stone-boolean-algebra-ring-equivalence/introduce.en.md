Stone's theorem establishes a fundamental equivalence between Boolean algebras and Boolean rings with identity. Starting from a Boolean algebra, one obtains a Boolean ring using symmetric difference as addition and meet as multiplication. Conversely, from a Boolean ring with identity, one constructs a Boolean algebra by defining join as $a+b-ab$, meet as $ab$, and complement as $1-a$. These two constructions are mutual inverses, demonstrating that the two algebraic structures are essentially the same mathematical objects viewed from different perspectives. This result is fundamental to the modern algebraic treatment of logic and set theory.
