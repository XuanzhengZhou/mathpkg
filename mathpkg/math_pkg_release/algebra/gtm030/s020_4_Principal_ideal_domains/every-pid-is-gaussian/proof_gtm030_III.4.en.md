Let $\mathfrak{A}$ be a principal ideal domain.

First, we verify condition $A'$ (the ascending chain condition on principal ideals). Suppose
$$(a_1) \subseteq (a_2) \subseteq (a_3) \subseteq \cdots$$
is an ascending chain of principal ideals. Let $\mathfrak{B} = \bigcup_i (a_i)$. Then $\mathfrak{B}$ is an ideal in $\mathfrak{A}$. Since $\mathfrak{A}$ is a principal ideal domain, $\mathfrak{B} = (b)$ for some $b \in \mathfrak{A}$. Then $b \in (a_n)$ for some $n$, which implies $(b) \subseteq (a_n) \subseteq (a_{n+1}) \subseteq \cdots \subseteq (b)$. Hence $(a_n) = (a_{n+1}) = \cdots$, so the chain stabilizes. Thus no infinite properly ascending chain exists.

Second, let $a, b$ be any two elements of $\mathfrak{A}$ and let $(a, b)$ denote the ideal $(a) + (b)$ generated by $a$ and $b$. This ideal consists of all elements $ax + by$ with $x, y \in \mathfrak{A}$. Since $\mathfrak{A}$ is a principal ideal domain, $(a, b) = (d)$ for some $d \in \mathfrak{A}$.

Because $(d) \supseteq (a)$ and $(d) \supseteq (b)$, we have $d \mid a$ and $d \mid b$, so $d$ is a common divisor of $a$ and $b$. On the other hand, if $e \mid a$ and $e \mid b$, then $(e) \supseteq (a)$ and $(e) \supseteq (b)$. Hence $(e) \supseteq (a) + (b) = (d)$, which implies $e \mid d$. Therefore $d$ is a greatest common divisor of $a$ and $b$.

Thus condition $C$ (existence of g.c.d.) holds, and together with condition $A'$ (the ascending chain condition) this implies that $\mathfrak{A}$ is Gaussian.
