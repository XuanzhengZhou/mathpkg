Let $e$ be a left identity, so $ea = a$ for all $a \in \mathfrak{S}$. Let $f$ be a right identity, so $af = a$ for all $a \in \mathfrak{S}$. Then:

$$ef = f \quad \text{(since $e$ is a left identity, $e \cdot f = f$)}$$
$$ef = e \quad \text{(since $f$ is a right identity, $e \cdot f = e$)}$$

Hence $e = f$. This shows that if both a left and right identity exist, they coincide. Moreover, there cannot be more than one of either type: if $e_1$ and $e_2$ were both left identities, then $e_1 = f = e_2$ where $f$ is the right identity. In particular, a two-sided identity is unique.
