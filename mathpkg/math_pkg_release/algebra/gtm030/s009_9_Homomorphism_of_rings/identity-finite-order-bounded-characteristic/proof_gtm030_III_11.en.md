*Proof.* Assume that $\mathfrak{A}$ has an identity element $1$ of finite order $m > 0$ in the additive group $(\mathfrak{A}, +)$, i.e., $m1 = 0$ and $k1 \neq 0$ for $0 < k < m$. For any $a \in \mathfrak{A}$, we compute

$$ma = m(1a) = (m1)a = 0a = 0.$$

Thus $ma = 0$ for all $a \in \mathfrak{A}$, which means every element has finite additive order, and moreover the order of each $a$ divides $m$. Hence $m$ is an upper bound for the additive orders of elements of $\mathfrak{A}$.
