The product of additive subgroups in a ring distributes over the sum of subgroups. Specifically, $A(B + C) = AB + AC$ and $(B + C)A = BA + CA$. The proof of the first distributive law shows that each side is contained in the other: $A(B+C) \subseteq AB+AC$ because $a(b+c) = ab + ac$, while the reverse inclusion follows from $ab = a(b+0) \in A(B+C)$. These distributive laws are essential for the ideal structure theory of rings.
