We prove the first distributive law $A(B + C) = AB + AC$; the second follows symmetrically.

$A(B + C)$ is the subgroup generated by all products $a(b + c)$ with $a \in A$, $b \in B$, $c \in C$. For any such generator,
$$a(b + c) = ab + ac \in AB + AC.$$
Since $AB + AC$ is a subgroup containing all generators of $A(B + C)$, we have
$$A(B + C) \subseteq AB + AC.$$

Conversely, consider $ab \in AB$. We can write $ab = a(b + 0)$, and since $0 \in C$, the product $a(b + 0)$ belongs to $A(B + C)$. Hence $AB \subseteq A(B + C)$. Similarly, $ac = a(0 + c) \in A(B + C)$ for any $c \in C$, so $AC \subseteq A(B + C)$. Since $A(B + C)$ is a subgroup containing both $AB$ and $AC$, it must contain their sum:
$$AB + AC \subseteq A(B + C).$$

Therefore $A(B + C) = AB + AC$. The proof of $(B + C)A = BA + CA$ is analogous, using right multiplication.
