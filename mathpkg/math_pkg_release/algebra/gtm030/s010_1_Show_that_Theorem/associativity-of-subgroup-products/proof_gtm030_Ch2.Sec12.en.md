Both $(AB)C$ and $A(BC)$ are subgroups of the additive group. By the characterization of subgroup products, each consists of all finite sums of the form $\sum_i (a_i b_i) c_i$ and $\sum_i a_i (b_i c_i)$ respectively, where $a_i \in A$, $b_i \in B$, $c_i \in C$.

More precisely, $(AB)C$ is the subgroup generated by all products $x c$ with $x \in AB$, $c \in C$. Since $AB$ itself is the set of finite sums $\sum_j a_j b_j$, any product $x c$ can be written as $(\sum_j a_j b_j) c = \sum_j (a_j b_j) c$, and finite sums of such terms produce all finite sums $\sum_i (a_i b_i) c_i$. Similarly, $A(BC)$ is generated by products $a y$ with $a \in A$, $y \in BC$, and $BC$ is the set of finite sums $\sum_k b_k c_k$, so $A(BC)$ is the set of finite sums $\sum_i a_i (b_i c_i)$.

Both sets therefore consist of all finite sums of triple products $a_i b_i c_i$. Hence $(AB)C = A(BC)$.
