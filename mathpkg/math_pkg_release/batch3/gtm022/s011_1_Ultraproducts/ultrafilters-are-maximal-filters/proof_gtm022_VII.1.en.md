If $\mathcal{F}$ is an ultrafilter on $I$, suppose $\mathcal{G}$ is a filter with $\mathcal{F} \subsetneq \mathcal{G}$. Then there exists $A \in \mathcal{G} \setminus \mathcal{F}$. Since $\mathcal{F}$ is an ultrafilter, by condition (iv), $I \setminus A \in \mathcal{F} \subseteq \mathcal{G}$. Thus both $A$ and $I \setminus A$ belong to the filter $\mathcal{G}$, so by condition (iii) their intersection $A \cap (I \setminus A) = \varnothing$ belongs to $\mathcal{G}$, contradicting condition (i) that $\varnothing \notin \mathcal{G}$. Hence no such $\mathcal{G}$ exists, and $\mathcal{F}$ is maximal.

Conversely, if $\mathcal{F}$ is a maximal filter and condition (iv) fails, then there exists $A \subseteq I$ such that neither $A$ nor $I \setminus A$ belongs to $\mathcal{F}$. Then one can adjoin $A$ to $\mathcal{F}$ and take the filter generated by $\mathcal{F} \cup \{A\}$, which properly contains $\mathcal{F}$ (since $A$ has nonempty intersection with every member of $\mathcal{F}$, otherwise some $F \in \mathcal{F}$ would satisfy $F \subseteq I \setminus A$, forcing $I \setminus A \in \mathcal{F}$ by condition (ii)), contradicting maximality. Thus every maximal filter satisfies (iv) and is an ultrafilter.
