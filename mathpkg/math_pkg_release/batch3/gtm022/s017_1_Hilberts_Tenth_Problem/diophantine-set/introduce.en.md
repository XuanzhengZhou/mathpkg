A subset of $\mathbb{N}^n$ is called diophantine if membership can be characterised by the existence of a solution to a polynomial equation with natural number coefficients. This generalises the idea that the solution set of a polynomial equation is trivially diophantine by projecting onto some of its coordinates. The definition extends naturally to relations and functions: a function is diophantine if its graph is a diophantine set. The concept of diophantine definability is central to Hilbert's Tenth Problem, as the unsolvability result ultimately rests on showing that every recursively enumerable set is diophantine.
