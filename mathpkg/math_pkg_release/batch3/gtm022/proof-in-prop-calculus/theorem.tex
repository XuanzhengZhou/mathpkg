In the propositional calculus on the set $X$, a \emph{proof} of $q$ from the assumptions $A$ is a finite sequence $p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_n$ of elements $p_i \in P(X)$ such that $p_n = q$ and for each $i$, either $p_i \in \mathcal{A} \cup A$ or for some $j, k < i$, we have $p_k = (p_j \Rightarrow p_i)$. Here $\mathcal{A} = \mathcal{A}_1 \cup \mathcal{A}_2 \cup \mathcal{A}_3$ where
$$\mathcal{A}_1 = \{p \Rightarrow (q \Rightarrow p) \mid p, q \in P(X)\},$$
$$\mathcal{A}_2 = \{(p \Rightarrow (q \Rightarrow r)) \Rightarrow ((p \Rightarrow q) \Rightarrow (p \Rightarrow r)) \mid p, q, r \in P(X)\},$$
$$\mathcal{A}_3 = \{\sim\sim p \Rightarrow p \mid p \in P(X)\}.$$
