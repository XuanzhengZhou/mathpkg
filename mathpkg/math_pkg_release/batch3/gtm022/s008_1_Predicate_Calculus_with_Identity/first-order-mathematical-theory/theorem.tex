\textbf{Definition 2.1.} A \textit{first-order mathematical theory} is a triple $\mathcal{T} = (\mathbb{R}, A, C)$ where $\mathcal{I} \in \mathbb{R}$, $A \subseteq P(V, \mathbb{R})$ for some $V \supset C$ such that $V - C$ is infinite, and $\text{var}(A) = C$. The set $A$ is called the set of \textit{(mathematical) axioms} of $\mathcal{T}$, the set $C$ is called the set of \textit{(individual) constants} of $\mathcal{T}$, while the \textit{language} of $\mathcal{T}$ is the subset
\[
\mathcal{L}(\mathcal{T}) = \{p \in P(V, \mathbb{R}) \mid \text{var}(p) \subseteq C\}
\]
of $P(V, \mathbb{R})$. A \textit{theorem} of $\mathcal{T}$ is an element $p \in \mathcal{L}(\mathcal{T})$ such that $A \vdash_g p$.
