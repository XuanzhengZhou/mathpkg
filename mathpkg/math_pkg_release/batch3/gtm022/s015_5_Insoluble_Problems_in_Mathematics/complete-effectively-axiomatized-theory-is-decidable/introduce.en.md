A complete and effectively axiomatised first-order theory is always decidable. Since the theory is effectively axiomatised, proofs can be enumerated; since it is complete, for any formula $p$, either $p$ or $\sim p$ is provable. A Turing machine can enumerate all proofs until it encounters either a proof of $p$ or a proof of $\sim p$, thereby deciding theoremhood and providing the proof as a certificate.
