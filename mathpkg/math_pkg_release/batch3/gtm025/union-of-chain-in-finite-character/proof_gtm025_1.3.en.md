It suffices to show that each finite subset of \(\bigcup \mathcal{B}\) is in \(\mathcal{F}\). Let \(F = \{x_1, \ldots, x_n\} \subset \bigcup \mathcal{B}\). Then there exist sets \(B_1, \ldots, B_n\) in \(\mathcal{B}\) such that \(x_j \in B_j\) for \(j = 1, \ldots, n\). Since \(\mathcal{B}\) is a chain, there exists \(j_0 \in \{1, \ldots, n\}\) such that \(B_j \subset B_{j_0}\) for each \(j\). Then \(F \subset B_{j_0} \in \mathcal{F}\). But \(\mathcal{F}\) is of finite character, so \(F \in \mathcal{F}\).
