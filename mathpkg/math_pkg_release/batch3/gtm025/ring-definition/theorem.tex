Consider a set \(A\) with two binary operations \(+\) and \(\cdot\), which is an Abelian group under \(+\) with identity \(0\), and in which for all \(a, b, c \in A\):
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] \(a \cdot (b + c) = (a \cdot b) + (a \cdot c)\) (left distributive law);
\item[(ii)] \((a + b) \cdot c = (a \cdot c) + (b \cdot c)\) (right distributive law);
\item[(iii)] \((a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c)\) (associative law for multiplication).
\end{enumerate}
Then \(A\) is called a \textbf{ring}. If \(a \cdot b = b \cdot a\) for all \(a, b\), the ring is \textbf{commutative}.
