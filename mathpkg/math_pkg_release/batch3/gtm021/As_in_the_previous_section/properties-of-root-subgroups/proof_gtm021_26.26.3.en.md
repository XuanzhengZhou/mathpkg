(a) $U_{\alpha}$ is constructed as the unipotent part of one of the two Borel subgroups of $Z_{\alpha}$ containing $T$ (part (d) of Corollary 26.2B). Uniqueness: if $V_{\alpha}$ also satisfies these conditions, its Lie algebra consists of nilpotent elements, so $V_{\alpha}$ must be unipotent rather than toral (cf. (20.5)). Moreover, $H = TV_{\alpha}$ is a connected solvable subgroup of $G$, so it lies in some $B \in \mathfrak{B}^T$. Since $T_{\alpha}$ centralizes $\mathfrak{h} = \mathfrak{t} + \mathfrak{g}_{\alpha}$, $T_{\alpha}$ must also centralize $H$ (Proposition 18.4A). Therefore, $V_{\alpha} \subset B \cap Z_{\alpha}$, forcing $V_{\alpha} = U_{\alpha}$.

(b) This follows from the fact that $\operatorname{Ad} n$ sends $\mathfrak{g}_{\alpha}$ to $\mathfrak{g}_{\sigma(\alpha)}$ (cf. (16.4)).

(c) Since $U_{\alpha}$ is 1-dimensional, connected, unipotent, and $T$-stable, it is isomorphic to $\mathbf{G}_a$. Choosing an isomorphism $\varepsilon_{\alpha}$, the conjugation action of $T$ yields the formula because the derivative of the conjugation action on the Lie algebra is given by the character $\alpha$.
