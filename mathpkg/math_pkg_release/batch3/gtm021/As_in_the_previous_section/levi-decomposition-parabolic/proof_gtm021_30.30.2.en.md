Begin with the torus $Z = (\bigcap_{\alpha \in I} \operatorname{Ker} \alpha)^\circ$ and define $L = C_G(Z)$. Then $L$ is automatically reductive, thanks to Corollary 26.2A. Those roots which are trivial on $Z$ are just the roots in $\Psi$ (since the characters of $T$ trivial on $Z$ are $\mathbb{R}$-linear combinations of $I$). In particular, $\mathfrak{l} = \mathfrak{t} \oplus \bigsqcup_{\alpha \in \Psi} \mathfrak{g}_{\alpha} \subset \mathcal{L}(P_I)$. Since global and infinitesimal centralizers of tori correspond, it follows that $L \subset P_I$ and then that $P_I = L V$ (since the Lie algebra of this subgroup is $\mathcal{L}(P_I)$). Since $\mathfrak{l} \cap \mathfrak{v} = 0$, $L \cap V$ is finite as well as $T$-stable, so $L \cap V = e$ (28.1). The uniqueness up to $R_u(P)$-conjugacy follows from the fact that any two maximal tori of the radical are conjugate by an element of $R_u(P)$ (Theorem 19.3).
