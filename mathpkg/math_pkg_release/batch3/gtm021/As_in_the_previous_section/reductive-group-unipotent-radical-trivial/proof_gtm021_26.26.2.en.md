(a) By definition, a reductive group has $R_u(G) = e$. (A group is reductive if its unipotent radical is trivial.)

(b) Let $S$ be a torus in $G$. If $S$ is regular, then $C = C_G(S)$ is solvable (24.2) as well as being reductive (as a centralizer of a torus in a reductive group). Being both solvable and reductive, $C$ must be a torus. More generally, for any torus $S$, $C_G(S)$ is reductive — this follows from the fact that the unipotent radical of $C_G(S)$ would be normalized by $S$ and contained in $R_u(G) = e$.
