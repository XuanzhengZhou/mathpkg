By definition, the discriminant $\mathfrak{d}_{R'/R}$ is determined by local data: if $M$ is the complement of a prime ideal $\mathfrak{p}$ in $R$, then the discriminant $\mathfrak{d}_M$ of $R'_M$ over $R_M$ is $\mathfrak{d} R_M$. In particular, the exponent of $\mathfrak{p}$ in $\mathfrak{d}$ equals $\sum_{\mathfrak{P} \mid \mathfrak{p}} f(\mathfrak{P}) m(\mathfrak{P})$.

Since $R_M$ is a PID, $R'_M$ admits a basis $\{u_1, \ldots, u_n\}$ over $R_M$ (Corollary 2 to Theorem 7, §4). Consider $n$ elements $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ of $K'$ defined by the equations $T(u_i v_j) = \delta_{ij}$ (Kronecker symbols). If we set $v_j = \sum_s a_{js} u_s$ with $a_{js} \in K$, then $\{v_j\}$ is another basis of $K'$ over $K$.

Let $d(u) = \det(T(u_i u_j))$. Two key facts about discriminants of bases are used:

(a) If $\{u'_i\}$ and $\{u''_i\}$ are two bases such that each $u''_i$ is in the $R$-module (or $R_M$-module) generated by the $u'_j$, then $d(u'') \in R \cdot d(u')$ (or $d(u'') \in R_M d(u')$).

(b) If the two bases generate the same $R$-module (or $R_M$-module), then $d(u')/d(u'')$ is a unit in $R$ (or in $R_M$).

The complementary module $\mathfrak{C}_M$ is a principal fractionary ideal since $R'_M$ is a PID. Write $\mathfrak{C}_M = R'_M y$. Then $\{y u_1, \ldots, y u_n\}$ is a basis of $\mathfrak{C}_M$ over $R_M$. Using the discriminant formula as the square of a determinant, the discriminant of this basis is $(N(y))^2 d(u)$. By (b), $(N(y))^2 d(u) / d(v)$ is a unit in $R_M$.

The formula $u_i = \sum_j T(u_i u_j) v_j$ shows that $d(u) = (\det(T(u_i u_j)))^2 d(v)$, i.e., $d(u) d(v) = 1$ since $\det(T(u_i u_j)) = d(u)$. Hence $(N(y) d(u))^2$ is a unit in $R_M$, so $N(y) d(u)$ is a unit in $R_M$. By Lemma 3 and the definition of the discriminant, $N(y)^{-1}$ generates $\mathfrak{d}_M$. Thus $\mathfrak{d}_M = R_M d(u)$.

Now for the global statement: if $\{u'_1, \ldots, u'_n\}$ is any basis of $K'/K$ contained in $R'$, its image in $R'_M$ generates an $R_M$-submodule. If $\{u_1, \ldots, u_n\}$ is an $R_M$-basis of $R'_M$, then (a) gives $d(u) \in R_M d(u')$. Varying over all $\mathfrak{p}$ yields $d(u) \in \mathfrak{d}$, proving part (1).

For any basis $\{u_1, \ldots, u_n\}$ of $R'_M$ over $R_M$, we have $\mathfrak{d}_M = R_M d(u)$. The ideal generated by all $d(u')$ for all bases $\{u'\}$ of $R'$ over $R$ is therefore $\mathfrak{d}$, proving part (2).

If $\{u'_1, \ldots, u'_n\}$ is a basis of $R'$ over $R$, then it is also a basis of $R'_M$ over $R_M$ for every $\mathfrak{p}$, hence $\mathfrak{d}_M = R_M d(u')$ for all $\mathfrak{p}$, so $\mathfrak{d} = R \cdot d(u')$. Conversely, if $\mathfrak{d} = R \cdot d(u')$, then $d(u')$ generates $\mathfrak{d}_M$ for every $\mathfrak{p}$, which implies $\{u'_i\}$ is an $R_M$-basis of $R'_M$ for every $\mathfrak{p}$; hence it is an $R$-basis of $R'$.

Moreover, if $\{u_1, \ldots, u_n\}$ is a basis of $R'$ over $R$, then the dual basis $\{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$ defined by $T(u_i v_j) = \delta_{ij}$ is a basis of the complementary module $\mathfrak{C}$ over $R$.
