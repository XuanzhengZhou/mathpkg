If $ab \in \mathfrak{b}$, then $abM \subset N$. If furthermore $a \notin \mathfrak{r}(N)$ (equivalently: no power of $a$ belongs to $\mathfrak{b}$), then from the primary property of $N$, for each $m \in M$, $a(bm) \in N$ and $a \notin \mathfrak{r}(N)$ imply $bm \in N$. Thus $bM \subset N$, i.e., $b \in \mathfrak{b}$. This proves $\mathfrak{b}$ is primary. (The converse is false in general.)
