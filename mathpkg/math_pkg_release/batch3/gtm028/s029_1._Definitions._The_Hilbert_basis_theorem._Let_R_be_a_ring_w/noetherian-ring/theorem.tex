A commutative ring $R$ is called \textbf{noetherian} if it satisfies any one (and hence all) of the following three equivalent finiteness conditions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item[(a)] \textbf{Ascending chain condition (a.c.c.).} Every strictly ascending chain
    $$
    \mathfrak{A}_1 \subsetneq \mathfrak{A}_2 \subsetneq \mathfrak{A}_3 \subsetneq \cdots
    $$
    of ideals of $R$ is finite. Equivalently, for any ascending chain
    $\mathfrak{A}_1 \subseteq \mathfrak{A}_2 \subseteq \mathfrak{A}_3 \subseteq \cdots$
    of ideals, there exists $n$ such that $\mathfrak{A}_n = \mathfrak{A}_{n+1} = \cdots$.

    \item[(b)] \textbf{Maximum condition.} Every non-empty family of ideals of $R$ contains a maximal element (with respect to inclusion).

    \item[(c)] \textbf{Finite basis condition.} Every ideal $\mathfrak{A}$ of $R$ has a finite basis $\{a_1, \dots, a_n\}$ such that
    $$
    \mathfrak{A} = Ra_1 + \cdots + Ra_n + Ja_1 + \cdots + Ja_n,
    $$
    where $J$ denotes the set of integers.
\end{enumerate}
