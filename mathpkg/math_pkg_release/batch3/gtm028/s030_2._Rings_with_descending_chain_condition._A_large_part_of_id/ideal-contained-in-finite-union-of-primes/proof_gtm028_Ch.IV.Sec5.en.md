We may assume that for $i \neq j$, $\mathfrak{p}_i \not\subset \mathfrak{p}_j$ (otherwise deleting the larger prime does not affect the hypothesis or conclusion). Suppose $\mathfrak{a}$ is contained in no $\mathfrak{p}_i$. For any $i$, the ideal $\mathfrak{a} \cap \bigcap_{j \neq i} \mathfrak{p}_j$ is not contained in $\mathfrak{p}_i$ (by Property A). Choose $a_i$ in this ideal but not in $\mathfrak{p}_i$. Then the element $\sum_i a_i$ belongs to $\mathfrak{a}$ but to no $\mathfrak{p}_i$, a contradiction. Hence $\mathfrak{a}$ is contained in some $\mathfrak{p}_i$.

Consequently, a finite union $\bigcup_i \mathfrak{p}_i$ of prime ideals is never an ideal unless all $\mathfrak{p}_i$ are contained in one of them.
