\begin{proof}
Since $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ are algebraically independent over $k$, the substitution homomorphism
\[
\varphi: k[X_1, \ldots, X_n, X_{n+1}] \to k[x_1, \ldots, x_n, x_{n+1}], \quad X_i \mapsto x_i
\]
has kernel $\ker\varphi = A$, the set of polynomials vanishing on $(x_1, \ldots, x_{n+1})$. The $n+1$ elements are algebraically dependent, so $A \neq \{0\}$.

Consider the field $k(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$. The polynomial ring $k[X_1, \ldots, X_n, X_{n+1}]$ is isomorphic to $k[X_1, \ldots, X_n][X_{n+1}]$, a polynomial ring in one variable over a UFD (in fact, over a polynomial ring over a field). Since $k[X_1, \ldots, X_n]$ is a UFD, its quotient field is $k(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$.

Let $f \in A$ be a non-zero polynomial of minimal positive degree in $X_{n+1}$ (such an $f$ exists because the $n+1$ elements are algebraically dependent while the first $n$ are independent, so any algebraic relation must involve $X_{n+1}$). We may take $f$ to be primitive when viewed as a polynomial in $X_{n+1}$ with coefficients in $k[X_1, \ldots, X_n]$. Then $f$ is irreducible: if $f = gh$ in $k[X_1, \ldots, X_n, X_{n+1}]$, then considering degrees in $X_{n+1}$, the minimality forces one of $g, h$ to have degree $0$ in $X_{n+1}$, hence to lie in $k[X_1, \ldots, X_n]$. Since $f$ is primitive, that factor must be a unit.

Now for any $g \in A$, by the division algorithm in $k(X_1, \ldots, X_n)[X_{n+1}]$, we can write $g = qf + r$ with $r = 0$ or $\partial_{X_{n+1}} r < \partial_{X_{n+1}} f$. Clearing denominators gives $a g = q' f + r'$ with $a \in k[X_1, \ldots, X_n]$ and $q', r' \in k[X_1, \ldots, X_n][X_{n+1}]$. Evaluating at $(x_1, \ldots, x_{n+1})$ yields $r'(x_1, \ldots, x_{n+1}) = 0$. By minimality of $\partial_{X_{n+1}} f$, we must have $r' = 0$, so $ag = q'f$. Since $f$ is irreducible and does not divide $a$ (as $a$ does not involve $X_{n+1}$), it follows that $f \mid g$ in $k[X_1, \ldots, X_n, X_{n+1}]$. Hence $A = (f)$ is a principal ideal generated by the irreducible polynomial $f$, and $f$ involves $X_{n+1}$ effectively.
\end{proof}
