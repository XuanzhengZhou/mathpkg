This is an immediate consequence of Theorem 1. If $f_1(X)$ and $f_2(X)$ are two irreducible polynomials in $k[X]$ such that $f_1(x) = f_2(x) = 0$, then by Theorem 1(b), $f_1(X)$ divides $f_2(X)$ and $f_2(X)$ divides $f_1(X)$. Hence $f_1(X) = c f_2(X)$ for some nonzero $c \in k$. Thus the irreducible polynomial is unique up to a unit factor. Requiring the polynomial to be monic determines it uniquely.
