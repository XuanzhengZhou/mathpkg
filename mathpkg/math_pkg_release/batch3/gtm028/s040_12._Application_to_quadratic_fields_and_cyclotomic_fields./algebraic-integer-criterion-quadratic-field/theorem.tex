Let $K = \mathbb{Q}(e)$ be a quadratic field with $e^2 = m$, where $m$ is a square-free integer. For $x = a + be \in K$ (with $a, b \in \mathbb{Q}$) to be an algebraic integer, it is necessary and sufficient that its trace $2a$ and its norm $a^2 - mb^2$ be ordinary integers.

This implies that $a = \frac{1}{2}a'$ ($a'$ an integer) and $2b$ is an integer, whence $b = \frac{1}{2}b'$ where $b'$ is an integer. The condition reduces to:
$$a'^2 - mb'^2 \equiv 0 \pmod{4}.$$

If $m \equiv 1 \pmod{4}$, the condition $a'^2 - mb'^2 \equiv 0 \pmod{4}$ is equivalent to $a' \equiv b' \pmod{2}$, and the algebraic integers are of the form $(a' + b'e)/2$ with $a' \equiv b' \pmod{2}$.
If $m \equiv 2, 3 \pmod{4}$, the condition $a'^2 - mb'^2 \equiv 0 \pmod{4}$ implies that $a'$ and $b'$ are even; thus the algebraic integers are of the form $a + be$ with integers $a, b$.
