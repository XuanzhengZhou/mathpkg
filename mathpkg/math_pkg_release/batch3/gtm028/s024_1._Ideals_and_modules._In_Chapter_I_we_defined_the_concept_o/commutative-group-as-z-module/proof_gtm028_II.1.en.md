For $n \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $x \in M$, define $nx$ as the $n$-fold sum of $x$ with itself (if $n > 0$), as $0$ (if $n = 0$), or as $(-n)(-x)$ (if $n < 0$). The module axioms follow from the properties of integer multiples in a commutative group: $n(x+y) = nx + ny$, $(m+n)x = mx + nx$, and $(mn)x = m(nx)$. Since $1x = x$, the module is unitary.
