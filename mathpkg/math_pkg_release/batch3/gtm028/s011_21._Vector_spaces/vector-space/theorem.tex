Let $F$ be a field. A set $V$ is called a vector space over $F$ if

(a) $V$ is a commutative group (the group operation will be written additively) and if

(b) with every ordered pair $(a, x)$ $(a \in F, x \in V)$ there is associated a unique element of $V$, to be denoted by $ax$, such that the following relations hold for any elements $a, b$ of $F$ and any elements $x, y$ of $V$:

(1) $a(x + y) = ax + ay$;

(2) $(a + b)x = ax + bx$;

(3) $(ab)x = a(bx)$;

(4) $1 \cdot x = x$.

The elements of a vector space $V$ are sometimes called vectors. The element $ax$ is sometimes called the product of $a$ and $x$. It is easily proved that $a0 = 0x = 0$ (we denote by the same symbol $0$ the element zero of $F$ and the element zero of $V$) and that $(-1)x = -x$. Notice also that the relation $ax = 0$ implies $a = 0$ or $x = 0$: in fact, if $a \neq 0$, $a$ admits an inverse $a^{-1}$, whence $x = 1x = (a^{-1}a)x = a^{-1}(ax) = 0$.
