**Proof.** Since $T$ is onto, for any $\bar{a} \in \bar{R}$ there exists $a \in R$ with $aT = \bar{a}$. Then $\bar{a} \cdot 1T = (aT)(1T) = (a \cdot 1)T = aT = \bar{a}$, and similarly $1T \cdot \bar{a} = \bar{a}$. Thus $1T$ acts as an identity in $\bar{R}$. The proviso that $\bar{R}$ is not a nullring ensures $1T \neq \bar{0}$, which would contradict the definition of an identity element.
