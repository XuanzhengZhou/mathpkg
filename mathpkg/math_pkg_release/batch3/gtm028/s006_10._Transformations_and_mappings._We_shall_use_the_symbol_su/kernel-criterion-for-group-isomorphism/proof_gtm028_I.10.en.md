**Proof.** In the first place it is obvious that if $T$ is an isomorphism — hence a univalent mapping — then $e$ is the only element of $G$ which is mapped into the identity element $\bar{e}$ of $\bar{G}$. Conversely, let us assume that the kernel $H$ of $T$ contains only the identity $e$ of $G$ and let $a$ and $b$ be elements of $G$ having the same $T$-image: $aT = bT$. Then

$$(ab^{-1})T = aT \cdot (bT)^{-1} = \bar{e},$$

hence $ab^{-1} \in H$, and therefore $ab^{-1} = e$, so $a = b$. This shows that $T$ is univalent, and since $T$ is a homomorphism by hypothesis, it is therefore an isomorphism.
