The cancellation law states that in a group, one can cancel a common factor from both sides of an equation. If $ax = ax'$, then multiplying on the left by $a^{-1}$ yields $x = x'$; similarly, $xa = x'a$ implies $x = x'$ by right multiplication by $a^{-1}$. This is an immediate consequence of the unique solvability of equations in a group and is one of the most frequently used properties in group-theoretic reasoning.
