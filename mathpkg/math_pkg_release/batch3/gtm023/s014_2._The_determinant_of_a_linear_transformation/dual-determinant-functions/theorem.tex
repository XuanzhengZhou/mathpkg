Let $E^*, E$ be a pair of dual vector spaces of dimension $n$. Let $\Delta^* \neq 0$ and $\Delta \neq 0$ be determinant functions in $E^*$ and $E$ respectively. There exists a non-zero scalar $\alpha$ such that

$$\Delta^*(x^{*1}, \ldots, x^{*n}) \, \Delta(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = \alpha \det\!\big(\langle x^{*i}, x_j \rangle\big),$$

where $\det(\langle x^{*i}, x_j \rangle)$ denotes the determinant of the $n \times n$ matrix with entries $\langle x^{*i}, x_j \rangle$.

The determinant functions $\Delta^*$ and $\Delta$ are called dual if $\alpha = 1$, i.e.,

$$\Delta^*(x^{*1}, \ldots, x^{*n}) \, \Delta(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = \det\!\big(\langle x^{*i}, x_j \rangle\big).$$

To every determinant function $\Delta \neq 0$ in $E$ there exists precisely one dual determinant function $\Delta^*$ in $E^*$.
