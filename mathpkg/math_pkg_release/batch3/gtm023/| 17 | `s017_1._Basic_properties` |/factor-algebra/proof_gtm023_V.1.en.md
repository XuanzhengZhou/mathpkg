It has to be shown that the product $\bar{x}\bar{y} = \pi(xy)$ does not depend on the choice of representatives $x$ and $y$. Let $x'$ and $y'$ be two other elements such that $\pi x' = \bar{x}$ and $\pi y' = \bar{y}$. Then

$$x' - x \in B \quad \text{and} \quad y' - y \in B.$$

Hence we can write

$$x' = x + b, \quad b \in B \quad \text{and} \quad y' = y + c, \quad c \in B.$$

It follows that

$$x' y' - xy = by + xc + bc \in B$$

since $B$ is an ideal. Therefore

$$\pi(x' y') = \pi(xy).$$

The multiplication in $A/B$ clearly satisfies $(M_1)$ and $(M_2)$ as follows from the linearity of $\pi$. Finally,

$$\pi(xy) = \pi x \cdot \pi y$$

shows that $\pi$ is a homomorphism and that the multiplication in $A/B$ is uniquely determined by the requirement that $\pi$ be a homomorphism.
