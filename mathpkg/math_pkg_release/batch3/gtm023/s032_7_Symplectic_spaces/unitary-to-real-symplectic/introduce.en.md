This theorem exhibits a fundamental relationship between unitary geometry and symplectic geometry: the underlying real vector space $E_{\mathbb{R}}$ of an $n$-dimensional complex unitary space $E$ (real dimension $2n$) carries both a positive definite symmetric inner product (the real part of the Hermitian inner product) and a symplectic inner product (the imaginary part). These two structures are intertwined by the complex structure $J: x \mapsto ix$, which satisfies $\langle x, y \rangle = -(ix, y)$ and $\langle ix, y \rangle = (x, y)$. Consequently, multiplication by $i$ is simultaneously a symplectic and a skew-symplectic transformation. This construction is the linear-algebraic foundation of Kahler geometry, where a complex manifold carries compatible Riemannian and symplectic structures.
