(i) Let $S = (x_1, \ldots, x_m)$ be a finite system of generators of $E$. Choose a system of generators with the smallest possible number $n$ of elements. Let these be $x_1, \ldots, x_n$. If these vectors were linearly dependent, there would be a non-trivial relation
$$\sum_{i=1}^{n} \lambda^{i} x_{i} = 0$$
with some $\lambda^{k} \neq 0$. Then
$$x_{k} = -\sum_{i \neq k} (\lambda^{k})^{-1} \lambda^{i} x_{i}$$
and so the $n-1$ vectors $x_i$ ($i \neq k$) would generate $E$, contradicting the minimality of $n$. Hence $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ are linearly independent and thus form a basis.

(ii) We proceed by induction on $n$ ($n \geq r$). If $n = r$, there is nothing to prove. Assume the assertion holds for $n-1$. Consider the vector space $F$ generated by $x_1, \ldots, x_r, x_{r+1}, \ldots, x_{n-1}$. By induction, $F$ has a basis of the form
$$x_1, \ldots, x_r, y_1, \ldots, y_s \quad \text{where } y_j \in S.$$

Now consider $x_n$. If $x_1, \ldots, x_r, y_1, \ldots, y_s, x_n$ are linearly independent, they form a basis of $E$ with the desired property. Otherwise there is a non-trivial relation
$$\sum_{\varrho=1}^{r} \alpha_{\varrho} x_{\varrho} + \sum_{\sigma=1}^{s} \beta_{\sigma} y_{\sigma} + \gamma x_n = 0.$$

Since $x_1, \ldots, x_r, y_1, \ldots, y_s$ are linearly independent, $\gamma \neq 0$. Thus
$$x_n = \sum_{\varrho=1}^{r} \lambda_{\varrho} x_{\varrho} + \sum_{\sigma=1}^{s} \mu_{\sigma} y_{\sigma}.$$

Hence $x_n$ is a linear combination of $x_1, \ldots, x_r, y_1, \ldots, y_s$, so these vectors generate $E$. Since they are linearly independent, they form a basis.
