Since $\Psi$ is non-degenerate, by Proposition III of section 2.33 there exists a linear transformation $\varphi: E \to E$ such that $\Phi(x,y) = \Psi(\varphi x, y)$ for all $x, y \in E$.

For the forward direction: Assume $\varphi$ is diagonalizable. Let $E_i$ be the eigenspaces corresponding to distinct eigenvalues $\lambda_i$. By the orthogonality lemma, eigenvectors with distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal with respect to both $\Phi$ and $\Psi$. For $x, y \in E_i$, we have $\varphi x = \lambda_i x$, hence
$$\Phi(x, y) = \Psi(\varphi x, y) = \Psi(\lambda_i x, y) = \lambda_i \Psi(x, y).$$
Thus $\Phi$ also has diagonal form when expressed in a basis adapted to the decomposition $E = \bigoplus_i E_i$. Combining bases of each $E_i$ yields a basis of $E$ in which both $\Phi$ and $\Psi$ have diagonal form.

For the converse: Let $\{e_i\}_{i=1}^n$ be a basis of $E$ such that $\Phi(e_i, e_j) = 0$ and $\Psi(e_i, e_j) = 0$ for $i \neq j$. Then
$$\Psi(\varphi e_i, e_j) = \Phi(e_i, e_j) = 0 \quad \text{for } i \neq j.$$
This shows that $\varphi e_i$ lies in the orthogonal complement (with respect to $\Psi$) of the subspace $F_i$ generated by $\{e_\nu : \nu \neq i\}$. Since $\Psi$ is non-degenerate, $F_i^\perp$ is the 1-dimensional subspace generated by $e_i$. Therefore $\varphi e_i = \lambda_i e_i$ for some $\lambda_i$, so the $e_i$ are eigenvectors of $\varphi$.
