**Proof of 1):** Consider the surjective homomorphism $\Gamma(t) \to A$ given by $t \mapsto \bar{t}_S$. Since $g$ is the minimum polynomial of $\bar{t}_S$, it induces an isomorphism
$$
\Gamma[t] / I_g \xrightarrow{\cong} A.
$$
According to Theorem III (applied to $g$), $\Gamma[t] / I_g$ is the unique direct sum of irreducible ideals $I_i$ where $I_i \cong \Gamma[t] / I_{f_i}$ ($i = 1, \ldots, r$). Let $I_i$ also denote the corresponding ideal in $A$. Then
$$
A = I_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus I_r
$$
and the $I_i$ are irreducible ideals.

Finally, since each $f_i$ is irreducible, Theorem I, sec. 12.13, implies that $\Gamma[t] / I_{f_i}$ is a field.

**Proof of 2) and 3):** Let $I_N$ denote the ideal generated by $\bar{t}_N$ and let $I$ be the ideal of nilpotent elements in $\Gamma(\bar{t})$. Since $\bar{t}_N$ is nilpotent we have
$$
I_N \subset I \subset \operatorname{rad} \Gamma(\bar{t}). \tag{12.34}
$$
Next, since $\bar{t} = \bar{t}_S + \bar{t}_N$, it follows that
$$
\Gamma(\bar{t}) = A + I_N. \tag{12.35}
$$
Moreover, since $A$ is the direct sum of the fields $I_i$, it follows that $A \cap \operatorname{rad} \Gamma(\bar{t}) = 0$. Together with (12.34) and (12.35) this yields the direct sum decomposition
$$
\Gamma(\bar{t}) = A \oplus \operatorname{rad} \Gamma(\bar{t}).
$$
