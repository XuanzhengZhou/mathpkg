(i) Let $n$ be the minimal number of elements in a generating set for $E$, and let $(x_1, \dots, x_n)$ be such a minimal generating set. If the $x_i$ were linearly dependent, then by Proposition II one $x_i$ would be a linear combination of the others, yielding a generating set of size $n-1$, contradicting minimality. Hence $(x_1, \dots, x_n)$ is a basis.

(ii) By induction on $n$ ($n \geq r$). If $n = r$, done. Assume true for $n-1$. Let $F$ be the subspace generated by $x_1, \dots, x_r, x_{r+1}, \dots, x_{n-1}$. By induction, $F$ has a basis $x_1, \dots, x_r, y_1, \dots, y_s$ with $y_j \in S$.

Now consider $x_n$. If $x_1, \dots, x_r, y_1, \dots, y_s, x_n$ are linearly independent, they form the desired basis. Otherwise, there is a non-trivial relation
$$\sum_{\rho=1}^r \alpha_\rho x_\rho + \sum_{\sigma=1}^s \beta_\sigma y_\sigma + \gamma x_n = 0.$$
Since $x_1, \dots, x_r, y_1, \dots, y_s$ are independent, $\gamma \neq 0$. Solving for $x_n$,
$$x_n = \sum_{\rho=1}^r \lambda_\rho x_\rho + \sum_{\sigma=1}^s \mu_\sigma y_\sigma.$$
Thus $x_1, \dots, x_r, y_1, \dots, y_s$ generate $E$, and since they are independent, they form a basis of the required form.
