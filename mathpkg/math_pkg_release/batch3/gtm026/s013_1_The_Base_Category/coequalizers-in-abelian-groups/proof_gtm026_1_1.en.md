Given homomorphisms $f, g: A \to B$, set $Q = B / \operatorname{Im}(f - g)$ and let $q: B \to Q$ be the canonical projection.

First, $q \circ f = q \circ g$: for any $a \in A$, $f(a) - g(a) = (f - g)(a) \in \operatorname{Im}(f - g)$, so $f(a) \equiv g(a) \pmod{\operatorname{Im}(f - g)}$, hence $q(f(a)) = q(g(a))$.

Now suppose $q': B \to Q'$ is a homomorphism with $q' \circ f = q' \circ g$. Then for any $a \in A$, $q'(f(a) - g(a)) = q'(f(a)) - q'(g(a)) = 0$, so $\operatorname{Im}(f - g) \subseteq \ker q'$. By the universal property of the quotient group, there exists a unique homomorphism $h: Q \to Q'$ such that $q \circ h = q'$.

For the example: $2\mathbb{Z} = \operatorname{Im}(0 - 2)$, so $Q = \mathbb{Z} / 2\mathbb{Z} = \mathbb{Z}_2$, a 2-element group. The coequalizer in $\mathbf{Set}$ would be $\mathbb{Z}/R$ where $R$ is generated by $\{(0, 2), (\pm 2, \pm 4), \dots\}$, yielding infinitely many equivalence classes.

For torsion-free abelian groups: let $Q = B/\operatorname{Im}(f-g)$ in $\mathbf{Ab}$ and let $T$ be the torsion subgroup of $Q$. The composite $B \to Q \to Q/T$ has image a torsion-free group. For the universal property: given a homomorphism $q': B \to Q'$ with $Q'$ torsion-free and $q' \circ f = q' \circ g$, factor $q'$ through $Q$ to get $h: Q \to Q'$. Since $Q'$ is torsion-free, $h$ must vanish on $T$ (elements of finite order map to torsion elements in $Q'$, which must be $0$). Thus $h$ factors through $Q/T$, yielding the coequalizer.

For the special case $\text{coeq}(0, 2)$ in torsion-free abelian groups: $Q = \mathbb{Z}_2$, and $T = \mathbb{Z}_2$ (all elements have finite order), so $Q/T = 0$.
