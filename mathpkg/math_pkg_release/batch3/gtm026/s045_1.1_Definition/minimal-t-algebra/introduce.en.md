A $T$-algebra is called minimal if it is nonempty and contains no proper, nonempty $T$-subalgebra. This concept generalizes the notion of minimality from topological dynamics, where a compact minimal orbit closure is a transformation group in which every orbit is dense. The behavior of minimal algebras depends crucially on whether the theory $T$ admits nullary operations: when $\emptyset T \neq \emptyset$, every $T$-algebra possesses a unique minimal subalgebra (the one generated by the empty set), making the theory uninteresting from a dynamical perspective. When $\emptyset T = \emptyset$, as in the theory of semigroups, the existence of minimal subalgebras is nontrivial and forms the central object of study.
