A literal category of $\mathcal{K}$-objects with structure axiomatizes the notion of "objects of $\mathcal{K}$ equipped with additional structure." It consists of a class of structures $\mathcal{C}(K)$ assigned to each $\mathcal{K}$-object $K$, together with an admissibility criterion for $\mathcal{K}$-morphisms to serve as $\mathcal{C}$-morphisms. The two axioms ensure that identities are admissible and that admissible morphisms compose, yielding a category $\mathcal{C}$ with a forgetful functor $U: \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{K}$. A typical example is topological groups, where $\mathcal{K}$ is the category of groups and $\mathcal{C}(K)$ is the set of topologies on $K$ compatible with the group structure.
