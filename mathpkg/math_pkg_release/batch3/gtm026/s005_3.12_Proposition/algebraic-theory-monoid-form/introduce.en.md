An algebraic theory in monoid form, also known as a monad or triple, consists of an endofunctor $T$ on a category $\mathcal{K}$ equipped with two natural transformations: the unit $\eta: \mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{K}} \rightarrow T$ and the multiplication $\mu: TT \rightarrow T$. These data must satisfy three coherence diagrams — associativity of $\mu$ and the left and right unit laws — which are direct analogues of the axioms defining an ordinary monoid. The terminology "monoid form" reflects this analogy: $\mu$ plays the role of binary multiplication and $\eta$ plays the role of the unit element. This definition, due to Eilenberg and Moore (who called them "triples"), is coextensive with Lawvere's algebraic theories and with the clone form $(T, \eta, \circ)$ via the bijection established in Theorem 3.18.
