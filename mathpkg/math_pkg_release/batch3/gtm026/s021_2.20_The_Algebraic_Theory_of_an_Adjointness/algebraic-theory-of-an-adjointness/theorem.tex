Let $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{K}, U, F, \eta, \varepsilon)$ be an adjointness. Then $T = FU\colon \mathcal{K} \longrightarrow \mathcal{K}$ is a functor and

$$\text{id}_{\mathcal{K}} \xrightarrow{\eta} T \qquad TT = F(UF)U \xrightarrow{\mu = F\varepsilon U} FU = T$$

are natural transformations. For each object $A$ of $\mathcal{A}$ the diagrams

\[
\begin{CD}
A @>{A\eta}>> AUF\\
@V{A\eta}VV @VV{A\eta T}V\\
AUF @>{A\eta F}>> AUFU
\end{CD}
\qquad
\begin{CD}
AUFU @>{A\varepsilon UF}>> AUF\\
@V{AUF\varepsilon}VV @VV{A\varepsilon}V\\
AUF @>{A\varepsilon}>> A
\end{CD}
\]

are immediate consequences of 2.15, which is reminiscent of 1.4.9 and 1.4.10. Setting $A = KF$ and observing that $\eta T \cdot \mu = (\eta F \cdot F\varepsilon)U = \text{id}_T$, we obtain all the diagrams of 1.3.16; that is $\mathbf{T} = (T, \eta, \mu)$ is an algebraic theory in $\mathcal{K}$. $\mathbf{T}$ is called the algebraic theory induced by the adjointness $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{K}, U, F, \eta, \varepsilon)$.
