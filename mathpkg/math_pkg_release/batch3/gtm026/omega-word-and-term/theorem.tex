Let $A$ be a set. A word in $A$ is an $n$-tuple of elements of $A$ with $n$ an integer $> 0$; $n$ is the length of the word. We write $a_1 a_2 \cdots a_n$ instead of $(a_1, \ldots, a_n)$.

Let $\Omega$ be an operator domain, and let $|\Omega|$ be the union of all $\Omega_n$. An $\Omega$-word in $A$ is a word in the disjoint union $A + |\Omega|$, i.e., a finite nonempty sequence of symbols each drawn from $A \cup |\Omega|$.

An $\Omega$-term in $A$ is an $\Omega$-word constructed inductively by the rules:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Every $a \in A$ is an $\Omega$-term.
\item If $p_1, \ldots, p_n$ are $\Omega$-terms and $\omega \in \Omega_n$, then $p_1 \cdots p_n \omega$ is an $\Omega$-term.
\end{enumerate}
