A natural transformation $\alpha: H \to H'$ provides a way to compare two parallel functors $H, H': \mathcal{K} \to \mathcal{L}$. It assigns to each object $A$ of $\mathcal{K}$ a morphism $A\alpha: AH \to AH'$ in $\mathcal{L}$ such that for every morphism $f: A \to B$ in $\mathcal{K}$, the naturality square commutes. This concept is introduced early in Manes' book because natural transformations arise already in Chapter 1: the unit $\eta$ of an algebraic theory is a natural transformation, and the structure map of a $T$-algebra satisfies naturality conditions. Natural transformations provide the appropriate notion of "morphism between functors" and complete the fundamental categorical trinity of categories, functors, and natural transformations.
