An adjointness formalizes the notion of adjoint functors as a 6-tuple $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{K}, U, F, \eta, \varepsilon)$ consisting of two categories, two functors going in opposite directions, and two natural transformations (the unit $\eta$ and counit $\varepsilon$) satisfying triangular identities. This is equivalent to the standard definition of an adjunction via natural bijections $\mathcal{A}(KF, A) \cong \mathcal{K}(K, AU)$, and the terminology "adjointness" is used throughout the book in place of the more common "adjunction." The triangular identities ensure that $U$ and $F$ determine each other up to natural isomorphism and that $F$ provides free objects over $U$ while $U$ provides cofree objects over $F$.
