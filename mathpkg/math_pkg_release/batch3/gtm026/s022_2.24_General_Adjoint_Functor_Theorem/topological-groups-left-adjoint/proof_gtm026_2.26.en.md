The proof illustrates the use of 2.24. Clearly $\mathcal{A}$ (the category of topological groups and continuous group homomorphisms) is locally small. The way one constructs products in $\mathcal{A}$ is to provide the product group with the product topology. With regard to equalizers, the subspace topology on a subgroup makes for a topological subgroup. Therefore all side conditions on $\mathcal{A}$ and conditions (1) and (2) in 2.24 are obvious at a glance.

To prove (3), fix a topological space $K$, set $\mathcal{S}$ to be the set of all cardinals less than or equal to the cardinality of the free group generated by $K$, and define $\mathcal{S}_K = \{(S, s)\}$ where $S$ ranges over all topological groups whose underlying set has cardinality in $\mathcal{S}$, and $s: K \longrightarrow SU$ is a continuous map. $\mathcal{S}_K$ is a small set because the class of all topological groups on a set of given cardinality is small.

Now consider an arbitrary pair $(A, f)$ where $A$ is a topological group and $f: K \longrightarrow AU$ is continuous. Let $I$ be the subgroup of $A$ generated by the image of $f$ made into a topological group by using the subspace topology. Then the inclusion map $i: I \longrightarrow A$ is a morphism in $\mathcal{A}$. Since the cardinal of $I$ is in $\mathcal{S}$ (cf. 1.4.31) there exists $S \in \mathcal{S}$ and a bijection $\Gamma: S \longrightarrow I$. It is clear that there exists a unique topological group structure on $S$ making $\Gamma$ a topological group isomorphism (specifically, a subset of $S$ is open just in case its image under $\Gamma$ was open and $s * s' = (s\Gamma \cdot s'\Gamma)\Gamma^{-1}$). Finally, define $s = f.(\Gamma U)$ so that $s: K \longrightarrow SU$ and $(S, s) \in \mathcal{S}_K$. Then $s.(\Gamma^{-1}U) = f.(\Gamma U).(\Gamma^{-1}U) = f$, so $\Gamma^{-1}: S \longrightarrow A$ is the required mediating morphism. Thus condition (3) is satisfied, and by Theorem 2.24, $U$ has a left adjoint.
