McShane's theorem (1934) states that the real line $\mathbb{R}$ with the usual metric is an injective object in the category of metric spaces with distance-decreasing (1-Lipschitz) maps. Concretely, this means any real-valued 1-Lipschitz function defined on a subset of a metric space can be extended to a 1-Lipschitz function on the whole space without increasing the Lipschitz constant. The explicit extension formula $g(x) = \sup\{f(a) - d(x,a) : a \in A\}$ is a precursor to the Kantorovich–Rubinstein duality in optimal transport and demonstrates that $\mathbb{R}$ enjoys a powerful extension property analogous to that of injective modules in algebra.
