An injective object in a category is the dual notion of a projective object: an object $K$ is injective if every morphism into $K$ can be extended along any monomorphism. Formally, for any $f: A \to K$ and any monomorphism $i: A \rightarrowtail B$, there exists $g: B \to K$ with $g \circ i = f$. This is the diagrammatic dual of the lifting property that defines projective objects. Injective objects play a fundamental role in homological algebra (as targets of resolutions) and appear naturally in categories of modules, sheaves, and — as McShane's theorem shows — metric spaces.
