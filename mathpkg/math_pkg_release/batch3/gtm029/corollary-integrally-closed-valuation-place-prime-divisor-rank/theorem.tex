The further development of the theory of complete ideals in $v$ depends on the repeated application of successive quadratic transformations. If $v'$ is a quadratic transform of $v$ then we may consider any (of the infinitely many) quadratic transforms $v''$ of $v'$, and this procedure can be continued indefinitely, leading to infinite, strictly ascending sequences $v < v' < v'' < \cdots < v^{(i)} < \cdots$ of regular rings $v^{(i)}$ of dimension 2, each $v^{(i)}$ being a quadratic transform of its immediate predecessor $v^{(i-1)}(v^{(0)} = v)$. For each ideal $A$ in $v$ we have defined its transform $A'$ in $v'$ [see (4)]. The property of $A$ of being a contracted ideal is not preserved under quadratic transformations, i.e., the ideal $A'$ in $v'$ is not necessarily a contracted ideal (in the sense of the definition given immediately after the proof of Proposition 4, with $v$ being replaced by $v'$; see p. 373). However, for complete ideals we have the possibility of using an inductive process, in view of the following property of these ideals:
