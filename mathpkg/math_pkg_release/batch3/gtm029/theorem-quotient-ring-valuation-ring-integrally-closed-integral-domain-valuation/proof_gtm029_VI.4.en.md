The first part of the lemma is self-evident. Assume now that $K_{\mathcal{G}}^{\star} \cap K = K_{\mathcal{G}}$, and let $\mathcal{P}_1$ be the restriction of $\mathcal{P}^{\star}$ to $K$. Then $K_{\mathcal{G}}_1 = K_{\mathcal{G}}$, and hence $\mathcal{P}$ and $\mathcal{P}_1$ are isomorphic places of $K$. Hence $\mathcal{P} = \mathcal{P}_1 f$, where $f$ is an isomorphism of the residue field $\Delta_1$ of $\mathcal{P}_1$ onto the residue field $\Delta$ of $\mathcal{P}$. Extend $f$ to an isomorphism $f^{\star}$ of the residue field of $\mathcal{P}^{\star}$ and set $\mathcal{P}_1^{\star} = \mathcal{P}^{\star} f^{\star}$. Then $\mathcal{P}^{\star}$ and $\mathcal{P}_1^{\star}$ are isomorphic places, and $\mathcal{P}_1^{\star}$ is an extension of $\mathcal{P}$, which proves the second part of the lemma. Furthermore, it is clear that $\mathfrak{M}_{\mathcal{G}}^{\star} \cap K = \mathfrak{M}_{\mathcal{G}}$, and this proves one half of the last part of the lemma. Assume now that we have $K_{\mathcal{G}}^{\star} \supset K_{\mathcal{G}}$ and $\mathfrak{M}_{\mathcal{G}}^{\star} \supset \mathfrak{M}_{\mathcal{G}}$ for two given places $\mathcal{P}$ and $\mathcal{P}^{\star}$ of $K$ and $K^{\star}$ respectively. If $x$ is any element of $K$, not in $K_{\mathcal{G}}$, then $1/x$ belongs to $\mathfrak{M}_{\mathcal{G}}$, hence $1/x \in \mathfrak{M}_{\mathcal{G}}^{\star}$, and therefore $x \notin K_{\mathcal{G}}^{\star}$. This completes the proof of the lemma.
