Let $y$ be any element of $F$. We show inductively the existence, for every $n \geq 0$, of elements $a_{ni}$ of $A$ such that

$$y \equiv \sum_{i=1}^{q} a_{ni} x_i \pmod{\mathfrak{m}^n E}.$$

This is obvious for $n=0$. Suppose it holds for a given integer $n$. If $y - \sum a_{ni} x_i \in \mathfrak{m}^{n+1}E$, we take $a_{n+1,i} = a_{ni}$. If not, the initial form $G(y - \sum a_{ni} x_i)$ is an element of degree $n$ in the leading submodule $F'$ of $F$. Since $F'$ is generated by the homogeneous elements $G(x_i)$, we can write

$$G\left(y - \sum_{i=1}^{q} a_{ni} x_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^{q} G(c_{ni}) G(x_i),$$

where $\partial(G(c_{ni})) = n - \partial(G(x_i))$. By the definition of initial forms,

$$y - \sum_{i=1}^{q} a_{ni} x_i \equiv \sum_{i=1}^{q} c_{ni} x_i \pmod{\mathfrak{m}^{n+1} E}.$$

Setting $a_{n+1,i} = a_{ni} + c_{ni}$ gives the induction step. Since $A$ is complete and Hausdorff, for each $i$, the sequence $\{a_{ni}\}_n$ is Cauchy and converges to a limit $a_i \in A$. Taking limits, $y = \sum a_i x_i$, so $F$ is generated by $\{x_1, \ldots, x_q\}$.
