Let $K$ be an arbitrary field. A place of $K$ is a homomorphic mapping $\mathcal{P}$ of a subring $K_\mathcal{P}$ of $K$ into a field $\Delta$, such that the following conditions are satisfied:

(1) if $x \in K$ and $x \notin K_\mathcal{P}$, then $1/x \in K_\mathcal{P}$ and $(1/x)\mathcal{P} = 0$;

(2) $x\mathcal{P} \neq 0$ for some $x$ in $K_\mathcal{P}$.

In many applications of ideal theory (and especially in algebraic geometry) a certain basic field $k$ is given in advance, called the ground field, and the above arbitrary field $K$ is restricted to be an extension of $k$: $k \subset K$. We shall show at once the following property of places: if $\mathcal{P}$ is a place of $K$, then $\mathcal{P}$ has no proper extensions in $K$. Or more precisely: if $\varphi$ is a homomorphic mapping of a subring $L$ of $K$ (into some field), such that $L \supset K_p$ and $\varphi = \mathcal{P}$ on $K_p$, then $L = K_p$. We note first that, by condition (1), the element 1 of $K$ belongs to $K_p$. It follows then from condition (2) that $1\mathcal{P}$ must be the element 1 of $\Delta$. Now, let $x$ be any element of $L$. We cannot have simultaneously $1/x \in K_p$ and $(1/x)\mathcal{P} = 0$, for then we would have $1 = 1\varphi = (x \cdot 1/x)\varphi = x\varphi \cdot (1/x)\varphi = x\varphi \cdot 0 = 0$, a contradiction. It follows therefore, by condition (1), that $x \in K_p$. Hence $L = K_p$, as asserted.
