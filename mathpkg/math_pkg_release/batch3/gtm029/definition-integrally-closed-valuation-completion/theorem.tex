If $M$ is an $0$-module (contained in $K$) the completion of $M$ is the $0$-module

$$M' = \bigcap_{v \in S} R_v M.$$

The completion of $M$ will be denoted by $M'$. The module $M$ will be said to be complete if $M = M'$.

COROLLARY. If $0$ denotes the integral closure of $0$ in $K$

For $S$ is also the set of all valuations of $K$ which are non-negative on $\bar{0}$, and hence

$$\bar{M}' = \bigcap_{v \in S} R_v \bar{M} = \bigcap_{v \in S} R_v M = M'.$$

It follows that the class of complete $\bar{0}$-modules coincides with the class of complete $\varnothing$-modules. In the study of complete $\varnothing$-modules it would be therefore permissible, without loss of generality, to restrict the treatment to integrally closed domains $\varnothing$.

We list at once a number of formal properties of the operation of "completion" consisting in passing from $M$ to $M'$. To what extent these properties characterize axiomatically the operation of completion will be briefly discussed later on in this appendix. We denote by $\bar{0}$ the integral closure of $\varnothing$ in $K$. In the following proposition, $M, N$ and $L$ denote $\varnothing$-modules contained in $K$.

**PROPOSITION 1.** The operation $M \rightarrow M'$ satisfies the following conditions:

(a) $\varnothing' = \bar{0}$.
(b) $M' \supset M$.
(c) If $M \supset N$ then $M' \supset N'$.
(d) $(M')' = M'$.
(e) $(MN)' = (M'N')'$, where by the product $MN$ of two $\varnothing$-modules $M, N$ we mean the $\varnothing$-module generated by the products $mn$ ($m \in M, n \in N$).
(f) $(xM)' = xM'$ ($x \in K$).
(g) If $(MN)' \subset (ML)'$ and if the $\varnothing$-module $M$ is either finite or is the completion of a finite $\varnothing$-module, then $N' \subset L'$.
