If the ideal $\mathfrak{A}$ of Theorem 1 is complete, then the factors $\mathfrak{B}_i$ in (18) and the ideals $\mathfrak{A}_i$ in (19) are also complete.

Proof. We consider an irredundant decomposition (28) of $\mathfrak{A}$ into valuation ideals. By Lemma 4, the set of directional forms of the valuations $v_1, v_2, \cdots, v_n$ coincides with the set $(\bar{g}_1, \bar{g}_2, \cdots, \bar{g}_m)$ of the irreducible factors of $c(\mathfrak{A})$. For each $i=1, 2, \cdots, m$, let $\mathfrak{A}_i$ be the intersection of $m^r$ and of those $q_j$ for which $v_j$ has directional form $\bar{g}_i$. Then

$$\mathfrak{A} = \bar{\mathfrak{A}}_1 \cap \bar{\mathfrak{A}}_2 \cap \cdots \cap \bar{\mathfrak{A}}_m,$$

where each $\mathfrak{A}_i$ is a complete ideal, and again by Lemma 4, $c(\mathfrak{A}_i)$ is a power of $\bar{g}_i$. Furthermore, we have obviously $r(\mathfrak{A}_i) = r$. From the uniqueness of the decomposition (22) (Theorem 1, Corollary 3) it follows that $\mathfrak{A}_i = \mathfrak{A}_i$, and thus $\mathfrak{A}_i$ is a complete ideal. The completeness of $\mathfrak{B}_i$ now follows directly from the relation (20) in Theorem 1.
