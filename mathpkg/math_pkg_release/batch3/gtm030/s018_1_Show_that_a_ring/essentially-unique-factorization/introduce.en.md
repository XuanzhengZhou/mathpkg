A factorization into irreducibles is essentially unique if any two such factorizations have the same number of factors and the factors can be paired into associates. The qualifier "essentially" accounts for the trivial ambiguity introduced by units: multiplying one irreducible factor by a unit and another by its inverse yields a formally different but substantively identical factorization. This notion captures the familiar idea of unique prime factorization in the integers, generalized to arbitrary commutative semigroups with cancellation.
