In a polynomial ring $\mathfrak{A}[x_1, x_2, \cdots, x_r]$ over an integral domain $\mathfrak{A}$, the only invertible elements are precisely the constants that are already units in the coefficient ring $\mathfrak{A}$. This means that non-constant polynomials can never be units, and the unit group of $\mathfrak{A}[x_1, \cdots, x_r]$ coincides with the unit group of $\mathfrak{A}$. The result relies on the fact that in an integral domain, the degree of a product is the sum of the degrees, so a non-constant polynomial cannot have a polynomial inverse.
