For any $\alpha \in S_n$, begin with any number $i_1 \in \{1, 2, \cdots, n\}$ and form $i_1\alpha = i_2, i_2\alpha = i_3, \cdots$. Since the set is finite, eventually a repetition occurs. The first repetition must be $i_{r+1} = i_1$ (if $i_k = i_l$ with $l > k \geq 1$, then $i_1\alpha^{k-1} = i_1\alpha^{l-1}$, implying $i_1\alpha^{l-k} = i_1$, so the cycle closes earlier). Thus we obtain the cycle $(i_1 i_2 \cdots i_r)$. Remove these $r$ elements and repeat the process with the remaining elements. This yields a decomposition of $\alpha$ into disjoint cycles. Uniqueness follows from the fact that the cycles are determined by the orbits of the action of the cyclic subgroup generated by $\alpha$ on $\{1, \cdots, n\}$.
