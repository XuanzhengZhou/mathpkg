Let $\mathfrak{R}$ denote the collection of finite products $a_1 a_2 \cdots a_n$ where each $a_i \in M$ or $a_i$ is the inverse of an element of $M$. It is immediate that $\mathfrak{R}$ is a subgroup of $\mathfrak{G}$ containing $M$: the product of two such finite products is again a finite product, the empty product (or the identity) is in $\mathfrak{R}$, and the inverse of a product $a_1 \cdots a_n$ is $a_n^{-1} \cdots a_1^{-1}$, which is also of the required form.

On the other hand, if $\mathfrak{H}$ is any subgroup of $\mathfrak{G}$ containing $M$, then $\mathfrak{H}$ contains every $a \in M$ and every $a^{-1}$ with $a \in M$. Since $\mathfrak{H}$ is closed, it contains all finite products of such elements, hence $\mathfrak{H} \supseteq \mathfrak{R}$. Thus $\mathfrak{R}$ is contained in every subgroup containing $M$, so $\mathfrak{R}$ is the intersection of all such subgroups, i.e., $\mathfrak{R} = [M]$.
