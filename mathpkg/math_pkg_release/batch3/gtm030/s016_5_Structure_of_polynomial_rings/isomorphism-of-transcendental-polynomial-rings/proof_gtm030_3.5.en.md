If $\mathfrak{A} = \mathfrak{A}_1 = \mathfrak{A}_2$ and $\sigma$ is the identity mapping, then Theorem 4 shows that $\mathfrak{A}[u]$ for arbitrary $u$ is a homomorphic image of $\mathfrak{A}[x]$, where $x$ is transcendental. Hence by the fundamental theorem of homomorphism, $\mathfrak{A}[u] \cong \mathfrak{A}[x]/\mathfrak{R}$, where $\mathfrak{R}$, the kernel of the homomorphism, is an ideal in $\mathfrak{A}[x]$. Since the homomorphism $\Sigma$ is the identity mapping on $\mathfrak{A}$, it is clear that $\mathfrak{A} \cap \mathfrak{R} = 0$.

If $u$ is also transcendental, then $f(x)^\Sigma = 0$ implies $f(u) = 0$, hence $f(x) = 0$. This shows that $\mathfrak{R} = 0$. Therefore $\Sigma$ is an isomorphism.
