The ring $[[1]]$ generated by $1$ is known (from Section 9, Chapter II) to be isomorphic to either $I$ or to $I/(m)$ for some $m > 0$. If the second alternative holds, then $m = p$ must be a prime; for otherwise $I/(m)$ would contain non-zero zero-divisors, implying $[[1]]$ would have non-zero zero-divisors, which is impossible in a field.

If Case I holds, $[[1]]$ is an integral domain but not a field. Hence to obtain the prime field, one must take the totality of elements of the form $(m1)(n1)^{-1}$ where $m, n \in I$ and $n \neq 0$. Thus $\mathfrak{P}$ is isomorphic to the field of rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$, and $\mathfrak{F}$ has characteristic $0$.

If Case II holds, $[[1]]$ is already a field since $I/(p)$ is a field for prime $p$. Therefore the prime field $\mathfrak{P}$ equals $[[1]]$, and $\mathfrak{F}$ has characteristic $p$.
