(1) For any $$a\eta \in \mathfrak{A}\eta$$, we have

$$(a\eta)(1\eta) = (a \cdot 1)\eta = a\eta, \quad (1\eta)(a\eta) = (1 \cdot a)\eta = a\eta.$$

Thus $$1\eta$$ acts as the identity in $$\mathfrak{A}\eta$$.

(2) If $$u$$ is a unit with inverse $$v$$ (so $$uv = vu = 1$$), then

$$(u\eta)(v\eta) = (uv)\eta = 1\eta, \quad (v\eta)(u\eta) = (vu)\eta = 1\eta.$$

Since $$1\eta$$ is the identity in $$\mathfrak{A}\eta$$, $$v\eta$$ is an inverse for $$u\eta$$ in $$\mathfrak{A}\eta$$.

(3) If $$1\eta = 0$$, then for any $$a \in \mathfrak{A}$$,

$$a\eta = (a \cdot 1)\eta = (a\eta)(1\eta) = (a\eta) \cdot 0 = 0.$$

Hence $$\mathfrak{A}\eta = 0$$.

(4) Suppose $$\mathfrak{A}$$ is a division ring and $$\mathfrak{A}\eta \neq 0$$. Then $$1\eta \neq 0$$ by (3). For any nonzero $$a\eta \in \mathfrak{A}\eta$$, we have $$a \neq 0$$ in $$\mathfrak{A}$$, so $$a$$ has an inverse $$a^{-1}$$ in $$\mathfrak{A}$$. By (2), $$(a^{-1})\eta$$ is an inverse for $$a\eta$$ in $$\mathfrak{A}\eta$$. Thus every nonzero element of $$\mathfrak{A}\eta$$ is a unit, so $$\mathfrak{A}\eta$$ is a division ring.
