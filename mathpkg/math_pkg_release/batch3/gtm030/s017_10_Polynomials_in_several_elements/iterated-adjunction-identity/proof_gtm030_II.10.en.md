The case $r = 1$ is clear by definition. Assume the statement holds for $s - 1$ elements and consider the ring $A[u_1, u_2, \dots, u_s]$. This ring contains $A[u_1, \dots, u_{s-1}]$ (since it is generated by a larger set) and also contains $u_s$. Hence it contains the subring generated by $A[u_1, \dots, u_{s-1}]$ and $u_s$, namely $A[u_1, \dots, u_{s-1}][u_s]$.

Conversely, $A[u_1, \dots, u_{s-1}][u_s]$ is a subring of $B$ that contains $u_1, u_2, \dots, u_s$ (since the first factor $A[u_1, \dots, u_{s-1}]$ contains $u_1, \dots, u_{s-1}$, and $u_s$ is adjoined). It also contains $A$. Hence it contains the subring generated by $A$ and $u_1, \dots, u_s$, namely $A[u_1, \dots, u_s]$.

Thus we have the equality

$$
A[u_1, \dots, u_s] = A[u_1, \dots, u_{s-1}][u_s].
$$

By the induction hypothesis,

$$
A[u_1, \dots, u_{s-1}] = A[u_1][u_2] \cdots [u_{s-1}].
$$

Combining these gives

$$
A[u_1, \dots, u_s] = A[u_1][u_2] \cdots [u_{s-1}][u_s],
$$

which completes the induction.
