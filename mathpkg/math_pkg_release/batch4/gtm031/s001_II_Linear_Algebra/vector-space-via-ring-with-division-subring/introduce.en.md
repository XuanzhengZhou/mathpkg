This construction reveals a deep connection between ring theory and linear algebra: any ring that contains a division subring $\Delta$ sharing the same multiplicative identity automatically carries the structure of a vector space over $\Delta$. The vector addition is the ring addition and the scalar multiplication is just the ring multiplication restricted to $\Delta \times \mathfrak{R}$. The vector space axioms S1--S4 follow immediately from the ring axioms (distributivity, associativity, and the identity property). Jacobson gives two examples: the field of complex numbers is a finite dimensional vector space over the real numbers (with basis $\{1, \sqrt{-1}\}$), while the polynomial ring $\Delta[\lambda]$ is an infinite dimensional vector space over $\Delta$.
