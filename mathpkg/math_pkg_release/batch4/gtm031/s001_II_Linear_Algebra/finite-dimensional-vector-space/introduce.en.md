A finite dimensional vector space is one that possesses a finite basis -- a finite set of vectors $\{e_1, \ldots, e_n\}$ such that every vector in the space admits a unique representation as a linear combination of these basis vectors with coefficients from the division ring $\Delta$. This condition, stated as axiom F, is the key assumption that distinguishes finite dimensional spaces from general (possibly infinite dimensional) vector spaces. The first eight chapters of Jacobson's Lectures focus exclusively on finite dimensional spaces, where the theory of linear transformations attains its richest structural results.
