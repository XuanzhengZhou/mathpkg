Suppose to the contrary that the minimum polynomial of some $A \in \Omega$ is not a prime power. Then we can obtain a proper decomposition of $\Re$ as

$$\Re = \Re_1 \oplus \Re_2 \oplus \cdots \oplus \Re_s$$

where the $\Re_i$ are the primary components relative to $A$. Now if $B$ is any linear transformation in $\Omega$ that commutes with $A$, then each primary component satisfies $\Re_i B \subseteq \Re_i$. Hence the $\Re_i$ are invariant relative to the whole set $\Omega$. This gives a proper decomposition of $\Re$ into $\Omega$-invariant subspaces, contradicting the indecomposability of $\Omega$. Therefore the minimum polynomial of every $A \in \Omega$ must be a prime power.
