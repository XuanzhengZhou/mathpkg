A vector space \(\mathfrak{R}\) over a division ring \(\Delta\) is called **finite dimensional** if it satisfies property F: there exists a finite set of vectors \(e_1, \dots, e_n\) such that every vector can be expressed uniquely as a linear combination \(\sum \xi_i e_i\) with coefficients \(\xi_i \in \Delta\). Jacobson uses this property to distinguish the spaces studied in the first eight chapters of the book from infinite dimensional spaces, which are treated separately in Chapter IX. The uniqueness of the representation is essential -- it means the \(e_i\) form a basis, and the number \(n\) is the dimension.
