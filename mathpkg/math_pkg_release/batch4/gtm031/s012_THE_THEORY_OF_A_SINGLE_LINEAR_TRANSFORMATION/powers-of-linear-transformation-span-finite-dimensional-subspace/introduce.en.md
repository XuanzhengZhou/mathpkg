Since the algebra $\mathfrak{L}(\mathfrak{R}, \mathfrak{R})$ of linear transformations on a finite-dimensional vector space is itself finite-dimensional (of dimension $n^2$), the infinite sequence $1, A, A^2, A^3, \dots$ cannot be linearly independent. By successively adding powers, one eventually reaches a minimal $m$ for which $A^m$ lies in the span of lower powers. This gives a polynomial relation satisfied by $A$ and shows that the subspace spanned by all powers of $A$ is at most $m$-dimensional.
