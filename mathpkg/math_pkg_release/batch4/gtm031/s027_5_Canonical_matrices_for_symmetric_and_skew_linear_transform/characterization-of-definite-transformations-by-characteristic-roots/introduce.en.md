A symmetric transformation on a Euclidean space is positive definite precisely when all its characteristic roots (eigenvalues) are strictly positive, and semi-definite when all are non-negative. The proof uses the orthogonal diagonalization theorem: in a diagonalizing Cartesian basis $(y_i)$, we have $(xA, x) = \sum \rho_i \xi_i^2$, whose sign is determined entirely by the signs of the $\rho_i$. As a corollary, a semi-definite transformation that is injective must be positive definite.
