Every semi-definite linear transformation on a Euclidean space admits a semi-definite square root. The construction diagonalizes $B$ orthogonally (by Theorem 4) and takes the non-negative square roots of its characteristic roots to define $P$ on each eigenspace. Since $P$ is defined on each characteristic subspace where it acts as scalar multiplication by $\sqrt{\rho_i}$, it commutes with $B$ and hence respects the orthogonal decomposition. The square root is unique in the positive definite case and fundamental to the polar decomposition theorem.
