The polar decomposition theorem states that every linear transformation on a Euclidean space factors uniquely as the product of a semi-definite transformation $P$ and an orthogonal transformation $O$, analogous to the polar decomposition $z = re^{i\theta}$ of a complex number. The semi-definite factor $P$ is the unique semi-definite square root of $AA'$, and when $A$ is invertible, $O = P^{-1}A$ is uniquely determined. In the general (possibly singular) case, $O$ is constructed by extending an isometry between the ranges of $P$ and $A$.
