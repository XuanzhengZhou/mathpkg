This corollary follows immediately from Theorem 1 and the fact that the degree of the order of any vector $u$ equals the dimension of its cyclic subspace $\{u\}$, which is at most $n = \dim \Re$. Since there exists a vector whose order equals the minimum polynomial, we have $\deg \mu(\lambda) = \dim \{u\} \leq n$. This gives a sharp upper bound that complements the Cayley-Hamilton theorem (which states $\deg \mu(\lambda) \leq n$ with $\mu(\lambda)$ dividing the characteristic polynomial).
