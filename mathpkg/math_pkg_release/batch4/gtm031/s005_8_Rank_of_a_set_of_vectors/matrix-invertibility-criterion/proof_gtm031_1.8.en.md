We have seen that the matrix $(\alpha) \in L(\Phi, n)$ if and only if the row vectors $(x_1, x_2, \cdots, x_n)$, where $x_i = \sum_j \alpha_{ij} e_j$, form a basis for $\Re$. The latter condition is equivalent to the statement that the row rank of $(\alpha)$ is $n$. By the equality of row rank and determinantal rank established above, this is equivalent to the determinantal rank being $n$, which means precisely that the $n$-rowed minor of $(\alpha)$—the determinant itself—is not zero in $\Phi$. Hence $(\alpha) \in L(\Phi, n)$ if and only if the determinant of this matrix is not zero in $\Phi$.
