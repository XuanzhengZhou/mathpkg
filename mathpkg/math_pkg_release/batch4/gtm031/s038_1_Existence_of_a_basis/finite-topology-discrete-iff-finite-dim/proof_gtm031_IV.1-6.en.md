First, let $\mathfrak{R}$ have the finite basis $e_1, e_2, \cdots, e_n$ and let $A \in \mathfrak{L}(\mathfrak{R}, \mathfrak{S})$. Then $O(e_i; e_i A) = \{A\}$ so that $\{A\}$ is an open set. It follows from the axiom that arbitrary unions of open sets are open that every subset of $\mathfrak{L}(\mathfrak{R}, \mathfrak{S})$ is open. Next let $\dim \mathfrak{R}$ be infinite. Then if $x_1, x_2, \cdots, x_r$ is a linearly independent set, $O(x_j; y_j)$ contains more than one element since there exist many linear transformations taking the given values on a finite linearly independent set and extending arbitrarily on the rest of a basis. Hence the topology is not discrete.
