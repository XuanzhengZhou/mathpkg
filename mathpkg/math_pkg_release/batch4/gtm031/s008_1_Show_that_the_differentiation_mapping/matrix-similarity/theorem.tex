Two $n \times n$ matrices $(\alpha)$ and $(\tilde{\alpha})$ over a division ring $\Delta$ are called \text{similar} if there exists an invertible matrix $(\mu) \in \mathrm{GL}(n, \Delta)$ such that
$$(\tilde{\alpha}) = (\mu)(\alpha)(\mu)^{-1}.$$
Two square matrices are similar if and only if they represent the same linear transformation $A \in \mathfrak{L}(\mathfrak{R}, \mathfrak{R})$ relative to (possibly different) bases of the $n$-dimensional space $\mathfrak{R}$. The problem of determining similarity conditions requires a fairly elaborate analysis (to be undertaken in the next chapter on the theory of a single linear transformation).
