When $\Delta = \Phi$ is commutative, the matrix correspondence $A \mapsto (\alpha)$ relative to fixed bases $(e_1, \dots, e_{n_1})$ in $\mathfrak{R}_1$ and $(f_1, \dots, f_{n_2})$ in $\mathfrak{R}_2$ satisfies $\gamma A \mapsto \gamma(\alpha)$, i.e., it preserves scalar multiplication. This is because $\Phi$ being commutative means $\gamma_l$ is indeed a linear transformation and the scalar multiplication $\gamma A = \gamma_l A$ corresponds to scalar multiplication of the matrix entries.

Since $A \mapsto (\alpha)$ is a group isomorphism preserving scalar multiplication, it is a vector space isomorphism between $\mathfrak{L}(\mathfrak{R}_1, \mathfrak{R}_2)$ and the space of $n_1 \times n_2$ matrices over $\Phi$.

The matrix space has a natural basis: matrices $E_{pq}$ with $1$ in position $(p,q)$ and $0$ elsewhere. There are $n_1 n_2$ such matrices, so the matrix space has dimension $n_1 n_2$. By the isomorphism, $\mathfrak{L}(\mathfrak{R}_1, \mathfrak{R}_2)$ also has dimension $n_1 n_2$.
