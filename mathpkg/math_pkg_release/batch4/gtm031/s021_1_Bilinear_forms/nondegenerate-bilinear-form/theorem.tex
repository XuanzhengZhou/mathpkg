A bilinear form $g(x, y')$ on $\mathfrak{R} \times \mathfrak{R}'$ is called \textbf{non-degenerate} if its left radical and right radical are both zero:

$$\mathfrak{N}_L = \{0\}, \quad \mathfrak{N}_R = \{0\}.$$

Equivalently, $g$ is non-degenerate if the only vector $z \in \mathfrak{R}$ such that $g(z, y') = 0$ for all $y' \in \mathfrak{R}'$ is $z = 0$, and the only vector $z' \in \mathfrak{R}'$ such that $g(x, z') = 0$ for all $x \in \mathfrak{R}$ is $z' = 0$.

If $g$ is non-degenerate, then the induced linear transformations $L: \mathfrak{R} \to (\mathfrak{R}')^*$ and $R: \mathfrak{R}' \to \mathfrak{R}^*$ are injective (1-1). Moreover, by the radical dimension formula, $\dim \mathfrak{R} = r = \dim \mathfrak{R}'$, so $\mathfrak{R}$ and $\mathfrak{R}'$ have the same dimension, and $L$, $R$ are isomorphisms.
