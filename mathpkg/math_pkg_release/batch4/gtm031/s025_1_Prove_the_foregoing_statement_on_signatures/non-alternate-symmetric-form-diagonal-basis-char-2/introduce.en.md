In characteristic two, skew-symmetric and symmetric bilinear forms coincide algebraically. A non-alternate symmetric (equivalently, non-alternate skew-symmetric) scalar product is one where $g(x,x) \neq 0$ for some vector $x$. Jacobon's Theorem 10 asserts that unlike the alternate case which leads to hyperbolic pairs, a non-alternate symmetric scalar product over a field of characteristic two always admits a basis in which the Gram matrix is diagonal with non-zero diagonal entries. The proof constructs such a basis by an inductive orthogonalization process that handles the characteristic-two subtleties where $g(x,x)$ may be non-zero.
