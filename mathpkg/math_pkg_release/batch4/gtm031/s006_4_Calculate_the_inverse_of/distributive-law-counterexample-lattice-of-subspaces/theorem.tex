The full distributive law
$$
\mathfrak{S}_1 \cap (\mathfrak{S}_2 + \mathfrak{S}_3) = \mathfrak{S}_1 \cap \mathfrak{S}_2 + \mathfrak{S}_1 \cap \mathfrak{S}_3
$$
does not hold without restriction in the lattice $L$ of subspaces. Counterexample: let $x_1$ and $x_2$ be linearly independent vectors in a vector space and set
$$
\mathfrak{S}_1 = [x_1], \quad \mathfrak{S}_2 = [x_2], \quad \mathfrak{S}_3 = [x_1 + x_2].
$$
Then $\mathfrak{S}_2 + \mathfrak{S}_3 = [x_1, x_2]$, so $\mathfrak{S}_1 \cap (\mathfrak{S}_2 + \mathfrak{S}_3) = \mathfrak{S}_1$. On the other hand, $\mathfrak{S}_1 \cap \mathfrak{S}_2 = 0$ and $\mathfrak{S}_1 \cap \mathfrak{S}_3 = 0$, so $\mathfrak{S}_1 \cap \mathfrak{S}_2 + \mathfrak{S}_1 \cap \mathfrak{S}_3 = 0$. Thus $\mathfrak{S}_1 \neq 0$, and the distributive law fails.
