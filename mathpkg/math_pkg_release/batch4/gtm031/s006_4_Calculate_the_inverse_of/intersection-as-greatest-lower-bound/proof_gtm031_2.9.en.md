First, $\mathfrak{S}_1 \cap \mathfrak{S}_2$ is a subspace (the intersection of two subspaces is always a subspace). It is clearly contained in both $\mathfrak{S}_1$ and $\mathfrak{S}_2$, i.e., $\mathfrak{S}_1 \cap \mathfrak{S}_2 \supseteq \mathfrak{S}_1$ and $\mathfrak{S}_1 \cap \mathfrak{S}_2 \supseteq \mathfrak{S}_2$ under the inclusion ordering (where $\supseteq$ means "is a subset of").

Now suppose $\mathfrak{S}'$ is any subspace contained in (subset of) both $\mathfrak{S}_1$ and $\mathfrak{S}_2$. Then for every $x \in \mathfrak{S}'$, we have $x \in \mathfrak{S}_1$ and $x \in \mathfrak{S}_2$, hence $x \in \mathfrak{S}_1 \cap \mathfrak{S}_2$. Therefore $\mathfrak{S}' \subseteq \mathfrak{S}_1 \cap \mathfrak{S}_2$, or equivalently $\mathfrak{S}' \supseteq \mathfrak{S}_1 \cap \mathfrak{S}_2$ in the inclusion ordering. This proves that $\mathfrak{S}_1 \cap \mathfrak{S}_2$ is the greatest lower bound.
