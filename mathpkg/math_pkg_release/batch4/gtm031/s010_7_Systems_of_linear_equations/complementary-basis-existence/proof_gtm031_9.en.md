We know that in $\mathfrak{R}^*$ we can find a basis $(e_1^*, e_2^*, \dots, e_n^*)$ that is complementary to a given basis. The natural mapping $x \mapsto x(f)$ of $\mathfrak{R}$ into $\mathfrak{R}^{**}$ is a $1$-$1$ linear transformation. Since $\mathfrak{R}$ is $n$-dimensional, this mapping is onto $\mathfrak{R}^{**}$. Hence for the given dual basis of $\mathfrak{R}^{**}$ corresponding to $(e_1^*, \dots, e_n^*)$, there exist vectors $e_1, e_2, \dots, e_n$ in $\mathfrak{R}$ such that $e_j^*(e_i) = e_i^*(e_j^*) = \delta_{ij}$ for all $i, j = 1, 2, \dots, n$. These vectors satisfy the required condition and form a basis of $\mathfrak{R}$.
