Given two ordered bases $(e_i)$ and $(f_j)$ of an $n$-dimensional vector space, the change-of-basis matrix $(\alpha)$ records how each $f_j$ expands in the $e_i$ basis: its $(i,j)$-entry $\alpha_{ij}$ is the coefficient of $e_i$ when expressing $f_j$. The matrix of a third basis $(g_k)$ relative to $(e_i)$ is then the product of the matrix of $(g_k)$ relative to $(f_j)$ and the matrix of $(f_j)$ relative to $(e_i)$.
