Since $\mathfrak{B}$ contains the scalar multiplications $\Phi$, it can be viewed as a vector space over the field $\Phi$. Via the bijection with normalized matrices $\mathfrak{U}$, the dimension of $\mathfrak{B}$ can be computed explicitly in terms of the degrees $n_j = \deg \delta_j$ of the invariant factors. When $t > 1$ (i.e., $A$ is not cyclic), the dimension strictly exceeds $\sum n_j = n$, the dimension of $\Phi[A]$, proving that there exist transformations commuting with $A$ that are not polynomials in $A$.
