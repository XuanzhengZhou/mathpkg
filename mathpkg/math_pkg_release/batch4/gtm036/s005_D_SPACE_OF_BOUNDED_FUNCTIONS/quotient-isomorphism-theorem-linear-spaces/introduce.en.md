Given a linear map $\mathcal{J} : G \to F$ and subspaces $N \subseteq G$, $M \subseteq F$ with $\mathcal{J}(N) \subseteq M$, there is an induced map $I : G/N \to F/M$ defined by $I(x + N) = \mathcal{J}(x) + M$. When $\mathcal{J}$ is suitably non-degenerate (i.e., $\mathcal{J}^{-1}(M) = N$ and $\mathcal{J}$ is surjective onto a complement), $I$ is an isomorphism. This is the linear-space analogue of the general isomorphism theorems familiar from group and ring theory, providing a canonical way to relate quotients of domain and codomain.
