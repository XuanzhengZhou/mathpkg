A set $A$ in a linear space is radial at a point $x \in A$ if for every direction $y$, the line segment from $x$ in direction $y$ remains in $A$ for sufficiently small positive scalars. While convex sets containing $0$ are typically radial at all their points, this is not true in general. The proposition asserts that in the plane $\mathbb{R}^2$, one can construct a set that is radial at exactly one point, serving as a counterexample to potential conjectures about radiality being a global property.
