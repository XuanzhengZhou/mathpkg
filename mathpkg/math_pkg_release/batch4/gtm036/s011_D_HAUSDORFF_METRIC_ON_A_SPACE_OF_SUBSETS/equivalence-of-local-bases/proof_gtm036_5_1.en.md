If each member of $\mathcal{U}$ contains a member of $\mathcal{V}$, then for any set $W$ that is open in the topology determined by $\mathcal{U}$, each point $x \in W$ has $U \in \mathcal{U}$ with $x + U \subset W$. Choosing $V \in \mathcal{V}$ with $V \subset U$, we have $x + V \subset x + U \subset W$, so $W$ is also open in the $\mathcal{V}$-topology. Thus the $\mathcal{V}$-topology is stronger. The converse follows by symmetry. For (b), the mutual inclusion implies both topologies are simultaneously stronger than each other, hence equal.
