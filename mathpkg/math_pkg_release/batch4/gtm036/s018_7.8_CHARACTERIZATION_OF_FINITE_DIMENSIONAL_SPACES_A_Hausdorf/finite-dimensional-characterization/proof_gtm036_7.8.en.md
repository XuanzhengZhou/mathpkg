One half of the theorem has been established (every finite dimensional Hausdorff linear topological space has a totally bounded neighborhood of $0$). The converse is proved as follows.

Assume that $U$ is a totally bounded neighborhood of $0$, and let $V$ be a circled neighborhood of $0$ such that $V + V \subset U$. Choose a non-zero member $x_1$ of $U$, and let $Y_1$ be the subspace generated by $x_1$. Since $E$ is Hausdorff, $Y_1$ is closed. By the lemma, an element $x_2$ can be chosen from $U$ such that $(x_2 + V) \cap Y_1$ is empty. In general, choose $x_{n+1}$ from $U$ such that $(x_{n+1} + V) \cap Y_n$ is empty, where $Y_n$ is the subspace generated by $\{x_1, \dots, x_n\}$. If some $Y_n$ equals $E$, the theorem is proved.

Otherwise, the sequence $\{x_n\}$ is infinite. Since $U$ is totally bounded, there exists a finite set $F$ such that $U \subset F + V$. Then infinitely many members of the sequence $\{x_n\}$ lie in some set $y + V$ with $y \in F$. But if $x_m$ and $x_n$ with $m < n$ are both in $y + V$, then $x_n \in x_m + V - V \subset x_m + U \subset Y_m + V$, so $(x_n + V) \cap Y_m$ is non-empty. For $n > m$, $Y_m \subset Y_{n-1}$, and this contradicts the construction which ensures $(x_n + V) \cap Y_{n-1}$ is empty. Hence the process must terminate, and $E$ is finite dimensional.
