In a Hilbert space, the algebraic sum $F + G$ of two closed subspaces need not be closed, even when the subspaces intersect trivially. This theorem gives a precise geometric criterion: $F + G$ is closed exactly when the cosine of the Friedrichs angle between $F$ and $G$ is strictly less than one. Equivalently, the unit spheres of $F$ and $G$ are separated by a positive angular gap, preventing the two subspaces from approaching each other asymptotically. The condition $\sup|(x,y)| < 1$ for unit vectors $x \in F$ and $y \in G$ is both necessary and sufficient; when the supremum equals $1$, the sum fails to be closed. Note that the source text refers to this as "the angle between $F$ and $G$ is zero," although under standard conventions the inequality $\sup < 1$ corresponds to a strictly positive angle.
