Both (i) and (ii) result from straightforward computations with polars, using the results of this section and of Section 16. 

For (i): Since $\mathcal{A}$ is an admissible family, by definition it covers $F$, each member is $w(F,E)$-bounded, and $\bigcup\{B : B \in \mathcal{B}\} = F$ for the generated family. The family of polars $\{A^\circ : A \in \mathcal{A}\}$ forms a local base for the topology $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}}$ of uniform convergence on members of $\mathcal{A}$ (this follows from the definition of the polar topology). That $\mathcal{A}$ is a co-base for the family of $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}}$-equicontinuous sets follows from the bipolar theorem and the definition of equicontinuity: a subset of $E$ is $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}}$-equicontinuous precisely when its polar contains a member of the admissible family, which means the set is contained in the polar of some $A \in \mathcal{A}$, i.e., is absorbed by members of the co-base.

For (ii): Since $\mathcal{B}$ is generated by $\mathcal{A}$ (by taking finite unions, then $w(F,E)$-closed circled convex extensions, then scalar multiples), every member of $\mathcal{A}$ is contained in a member of $\mathcal{B}$ and conversely every member of $\mathcal{B}$ is absorbed by members of $\mathcal{A}$ in the sense of the polar topology. Specifically, $\mathcal{A} \subset \mathcal{B}$ up to scalar multiples, and $\mathcal{B}$ is precisely the saturation of $\mathcal{A}$ under the operations of taking finite unions, $w(F,E)$-closed circled convex hulls, and scalar multiplication. Since polars are unchanged by taking $w(F,E)$-closed circled convex hulls (by the bipolar theorem 16.3) and by scalar multiplication (up to reciprocal scaling of the polar), the polar topologies $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}}$ and $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}$ coincide.

For (iii): The topology $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}}$ is pseudo-metrizable if and only if it has a countable local base. Since the polars of members of a co-base for $\mathcal{A}$ form a local base, $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}}$ has a countable local base precisely when $\mathcal{A}$ has a countable co-base. Similarly, $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}}$ is pseudo-normable if and only if it has a local base consisting of a single set and its scalar multiples, which means the polars of scalar multiples of a single member $A \in \mathcal{A}$ form a local base. This happens exactly when the family of scalar multiples of $A$ is a co-base for $\mathcal{A}$.
