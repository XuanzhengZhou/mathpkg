Let $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$ be families of bounded subsets of locally convex spaces $E$ and $F$ such that $E = \cup \mathcal{A}$ and $F = \cup \mathcal{B}$, and let $H$ be a linear space of bilinear mappings of $E \times F$ into a locally convex space $G$.

(a) If for each $f \in H$, $f[A \times B]$ is bounded for all $A \in \mathcal{A}$ and $B \in \mathcal{B}$, then $H$ may be given the topology $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A},\mathcal{B}}$ of uniform convergence on the sets $A \times B$, making it a locally convex linear topological space.

(b) If the sets of either $\mathcal{A}$ or $\mathcal{B}$ are convex, circled, and complete, the set of all separately continuous bilinear mappings of $E \times F$ into $G$ can be topologized in this way (see 12G).

(c) Under condition (b), the space of all separately continuous bilinear functionals on $E \times F$ is $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A},\mathcal{B}}$-complete if and only if the adjoint of $E$ is $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}}$-complete, and the adjoint of $F$ is $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{B}}$-complete. (If $\{f_\alpha\}$ is Cauchy, then $f_\alpha(x,y) \rightarrow f(x,y)$ for each $x \in E$ and $y \in F$, and clearly $f$ is bilinear. If $y$ is fixed, the linear functional $x \rightarrow f(x,y)$ is the $\mathcal{T}_{\mathcal{A}}$-limit of continuous linear functionals $x \rightarrow f_\alpha(x,y)$.)

(d) More generally, if also $G$ is complete, then (c) holds with ``functionals on $E \times F$'' replaced by ``mappings of $E \times F$ into $G$'', provided that $E$ and $F$ are Mackey spaces. (Consider the convergence of $\phi(f_\alpha(x,y))$ for each continuous linear functional $\phi$ on $G$.)
