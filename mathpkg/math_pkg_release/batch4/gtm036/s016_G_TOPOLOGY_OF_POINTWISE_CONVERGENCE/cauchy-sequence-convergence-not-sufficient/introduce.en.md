In general topological spaces, completeness is defined in terms of Cauchy nets, not sequences. This example shows why: the subspace $F$ of $\prod_{t\in(0,1)}\mathbb{R}$ consisting of functions vanishing outside a countable set has the property that every Cauchy sequence converges in $F$, yet $F$ is not complete. This is because $F$ is a proper dense subspace of the complete product space $E$, and Cauchy nets in $F$ can converge to limits in $E \setminus F$.
