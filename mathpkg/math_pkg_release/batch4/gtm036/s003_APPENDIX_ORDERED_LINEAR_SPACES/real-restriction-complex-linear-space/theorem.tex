The real restriction of a complex linear space is obtained by restricting the domain of scalar multiplication to the real numbers. The real restriction has the same set of elements and the same operation of addition as the original complex linear space, and scalar multiplication coincides when both are defined. The crucial difference is that the domain of scalar multiplication is a proper subset. For example, the real restriction of the complex field is the two-dimensional Euclidean space. Not every real linear space is the real restriction of a complex linear space (e.g., one-dimensional real Euclidean space).
