For one-dimensional random walk with $\mu = 0, 0 < \sigma^2 < \infty$

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \mathbf{P}\left[ \max_{1 \leq k \leq n} |\mathbf{S}_k| \leq \sigma x \sqrt{n} \right] = 1 - F\left(\frac{1}{x^2}\right), \quad x > 0,$$

with $F(x)$ as defined in T2.

Proof: Let us call $G_n(x)$ the distribution of $(\sigma^2 n)^{-1/2} \max_{1 \leq k \leq n} |\mathbf{S}_k|$. Then T3 amounts to saying that the sequence

$$G_n(x) = \sum_{y=0}^{2[\sigma x \sqrt{n}]} Q_{2[x \sigma \sqrt{n}]}^n([\sigma x \sqrt{n}], y), \quad x > 0,$$

converges to the limit

$$G(x) = 1 - F(x^{-2}), \quad x > 0,$$

as $n \to \infty$. It seems impossible to obtain sufficiently powerful estimates for the iterates of $Q$ to verify this result directly. However, we shall see that most of the work was already done in the course of proving T2. The crucial observation concerns the two events

(i) $\max_{1 \leq k \leq n} |\mathbf{S}_k| \leq \sigma x \sqrt{n},$

(ii) $\mathbf{T}_{[\sigma x \sqrt{n}]} > n$.

They are obviously the same event! In other words

$$G_n(x) = \mathbf{P}\left[\mathbf{T}_{[\sigma x \sqrt{n}]} > n\right].$$

If we fix $x > 0$, and let $m = m(n) = [\sigma x \sqrt{n}]$, then

$$\frac{m^2}{\sigma^2 x^2} \leq n < \frac{(m+1)^2}{\sigma^2 x

by T2, and also

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} G_n(x) \geq \lim_{m \to \infty} P\left[T_m > \frac{m^2}{\sigma^2} \frac{1}{x^2}\left(1 + \frac{1}{m}\right)^2\right]$$

$$\geq \lim_{m \to \infty} P\left[T_m > \frac{m^2}{\sigma^2} \frac{c}{x^2}\right] = 1 - F\left(\frac{c}{x^2}\right)$$

for every real $c > 1$. But $F(x)$ as well as $F(x^{-2})$ are continuous functions of $x$, and therefore the fact that $c$ may be taken arbitrarily close to one yields

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} G_n(x) = 1 - F(x^{-2}).$$

Remark: The first rigorous proof of T2 and T3 is due to Erdős and Kac [29], 1946. Their proof is not only shorter, but in a way more interesting than ours, as it reduces the problem to the proof of T3 for the Brownian motion process, which is the limiting case of a sequence of random walks, if the time and space scales are changed appropriately. In the context of Brownian motion T3 had indeed been known already to Bachelier, in the beginning of the century, so that the crux of the proof in [29] was the justification of the limiting process, by means of a so-called invariance principle [22].
