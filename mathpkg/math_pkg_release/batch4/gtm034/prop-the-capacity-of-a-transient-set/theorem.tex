The capacity of a transient set may also be defined as

$$C(A) = \sup_{\psi} \sum_{x \in A} \psi(x),$$

the supremum being taken over all $\psi$ such that

$$\psi(x) \geq 0 \text{ on } A, \quad \sum_{y \in A} G(x,y)\psi(y) \leq 1 \text{ on } A.$$

Proof: If $C(A) < \infty$, in the sense of D3, then the total equilibrium charge is finite, and its potential

$$H_A(x) = \sum_{y \in A} G(x,y)E_A(y) = 1 \text{ for } x \in A.$$

By part (c) of P9

Thus not only is $C(A)$ the supremum in P9, but the supremum is actually attained by the charge $\psi(x) = E_A(x)$.

If, on the other hand, $C(A) = +\infty$ in the sense of D3, then $E_A$ is a charge $\psi$, satisfying the conditions in P10, such that $\sum_{x \in A} \psi(x) = +\infty$. That completes the proof.
