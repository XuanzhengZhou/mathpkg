The technique of diagrams, introduced by Henkin and A. Robinson, provides a logical method for handling substructures. Given an $\mathcal{L}$-structure $\mathfrak{A}$, one expands the language $\mathcal{L}$ by adding a new constant symbol for each element of $A$, forming the $A$-expansion $(\mathcal{L}, \mathbf{c}_a)_{a \in A}$. The (atomic) diagram of $\mathfrak{A}$ is the set of all atomic and negated atomic sentences in this expanded language that hold in $(\mathfrak{A}, a)_{a \in A}$. Diagrams characterize substructures: $\mathfrak{A} \subseteq \mathfrak{B}$ iff $\mathfrak{B}$ can be expanded to a model of the diagram of $\mathfrak{A}$.
