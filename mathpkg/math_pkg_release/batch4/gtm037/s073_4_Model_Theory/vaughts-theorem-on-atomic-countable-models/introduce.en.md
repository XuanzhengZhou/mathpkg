For any consistent theory $\Gamma$ in a countable language, Vaught's theorem guarantees the existence of a countable model with a remarkable property: every finite tuple of elements satisfies either an $n$-atomic formula over $\Gamma$ or an $n$-atomless formula over $\Gamma$. This means that the Boolean algebra of definable sets modulo $\Gamma$-equivalence is either discrete (having atoms) or continuous (having atomless elements) at every tuple. The proof uses the omitting types theorem: one constructs a set $\Delta_n$ of negations of $n$-atomic and $n$-atomless formulas and shows $\Gamma$ locally omits each $\Delta_n$, yielding the desired countable model.
