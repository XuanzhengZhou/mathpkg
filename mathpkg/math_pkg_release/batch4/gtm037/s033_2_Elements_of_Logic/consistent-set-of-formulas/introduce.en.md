A set $\Gamma$ of formulas is defined to be consistent if it does not prove every formula — that is, there exists at least one formula $\varphi$ that is not deductively provable from $\Gamma$. Equivalently (by Proposition 10.90), $\Gamma$ is inconsistent precisely when it proves both a formula and its negation, or when it proves every formula. The notion of consistency is central to elementary logic: it separates theories that are free of contradiction from those that are not, and is the key concept in the statement and proof of Godel's completeness theorem, where a consistent set of sentences is shown to have a model.
