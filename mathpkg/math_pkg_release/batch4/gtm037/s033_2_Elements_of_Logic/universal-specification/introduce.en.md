The universal specification theorem (also known as universal instantiation) permits the inference from a universally quantified formula $\forall \alpha \varphi$ to the formula $\text{Subf}_{\sigma}^{\alpha} \varphi$ obtained by substituting the term $\sigma$ for the variable $\alpha$, provided that no free occurrence of $\alpha$ in $\varphi$ falls within the scope of a quantifier on a variable occurring in $\sigma$. This condition prevents unintended variable capture, ensuring that the substitution is logically sound. The theorem is fundamental to formal reasoning in first-order predicate logic, bridging the gap between universal statements and their specific instances.
