Definition 17.1 introduces the concept of a strong theory, which is central to the study of the unprovability of consistency (Chapter 17). A theory $\Gamma$ in a language $\mathcal{L}$ expanding $\mathcal{L}_{\text{nos}}$ (the language of number theory) is strong if every elementary function $f$ on the natural numbers can be represented by some operation symbol $\mathbf{O}$ in $\mathcal{L}$. Specifically, $\Gamma$ must prove the correct values $\mathbf{O}(\mathbf{x}_0, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_{m-1}) = \mathbf{y}$ whenever $f(\vec{x}) = y$, and the negation whenever $f(\vec{x}) \neq y$. Examples of strong theories include definitional expansions of Robinson arithmetic $R$ and the set theory $S$. This definition provides the minimal environment needed to formalize syntax and provability predicates for Godel's Second Incompleteness Theorem.
