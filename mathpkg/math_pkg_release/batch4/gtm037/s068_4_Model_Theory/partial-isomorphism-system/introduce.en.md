Definition 26.9 introduces the standard formulation of back-and-forth relations via partial isomorphisms. A partial isomorphism is a finite partial function that preserves and reflects all atomic formulas, capturing local indistinguishability. An $\alpha$-system is a nested family $\langle I_m : m \in \alpha \rangle$ of nonempty sets of partial isomorphisms with the crucial extension property (also called the "forth" and "back" properties): any partial isomorphism at level $m+1$ can be extended to include any single element from either structure, yielding a partial isomorphism at level $m$. This definition is the modern formulation used in Fraisse's theorem, replacing the earlier $m$-sequence definition with the more flexible notion of systems of partial isomorphisms.
