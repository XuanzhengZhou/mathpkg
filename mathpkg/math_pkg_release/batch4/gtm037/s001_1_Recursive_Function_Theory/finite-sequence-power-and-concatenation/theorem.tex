If $a$ is any set and $m \in \omega$, let $a^{(m)}$ be the unique element of $^{m}\{a\}$. Thus $a^{(m)}$ is an $m$-termed sequence of $a$'s,
$$a^{(m)} = \langle a, a, \ldots, a \rangle \quad (m \text{ times}).$$
If $x$ and $y$ are finite sequences, say $x = \langle x_0, \ldots, x_{m-1} \rangle$ and $y = \langle y_0, \ldots, y_{n-1} \rangle$, we let
$$xy = \langle x_0, \ldots, x_{m-1}, y_0, \ldots, y_{n-1} \rangle.$$
Frequently we write $a$ for $\langle a \rangle$.
