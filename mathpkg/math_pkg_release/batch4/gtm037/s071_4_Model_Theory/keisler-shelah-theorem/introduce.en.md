The Keisler-Shelah theorem is one of the deepest results in model theory. It states that two first-order structures $\mathfrak{A}$ and $\mathfrak{B}$ are elementarily equivalent ($\mathfrak{A} \equiv_{\mathrm{ee}} \mathfrak{B}$) if and only if they have isomorphic ultrapowers. The direction (ii) $\Rightarrow$ (i) is an immediate consequence of Łoś's theorem. The converse direction (i) $\Rightarrow$ (ii) is the hard part and involves a sophisticated transfinite construction building an ultrafilter on a suitably large cardinal $n$ such that the resulting ultrapowers become isomorphic via a back-and-forth argument.
