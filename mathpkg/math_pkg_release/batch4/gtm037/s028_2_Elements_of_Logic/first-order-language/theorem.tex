A first-order language is a quadruple $\mathcal{L} = (L, v, \mathcal{O}, \mathcal{R})$ with the following properties:

(i) $L$, $v$, $\mathcal{O}$, and $\mathcal{R}$ are functions such that $\text{Rng}(L)$, $\text{Rng}(v)$, $\text{Dmn}(\mathcal{O})$, and $\text{Dmn}(\mathcal{R})$ are pairwise disjoint.

(ii) $\text{Dmn}(L) = 5$, and $L$ is one-one. $L_0$ is the negation symbol, $L_1$ the disjunction symbol, $L_2$ the conjunction symbol, $L_3$ the universal quantifier, and $L_4$ the equality symbol of $\mathcal{L}$.

(iii) $\text{Dmn}(v) = \omega$, and $v$ is one-one. $v_i$ is called the $i$th individual variable.

(iv) $\text{Rng}(\mathcal{O}) \subseteq \omega$. For $\mathbf{O} \in \text{Dmn}(\mathcal{O})$, $\mathbf{O}$ is called an operation symbol of rank $\mathcal{O}(\mathbf{O})$.

(v) $\text{Rng}(\mathcal{R}) \subseteq \omega$. For $\mathbf{R} \in \text{Dmn}(\mathcal{R})$, $\mathbf{R}$ is called a relation symbol of rank $\mathcal{R}(\mathbf{R})$.
