The \text{mean} of a random variable $f$, denoted $M[f]$, is defined by
$$M[f] = \int_{\Omega} f \, d\mu,$$
where $M[f]$ exists if and only if $\int_{\Omega} f \, d\mu$ exists and where $\mu$ is the measure associated with the probability space. Since means are Lebesgue integrals, they satisfy all the properties of Lebesgue integrals. In particular, if $\{f_n\}$ is a sequence of non-negative random variables, then by Corollary 1-46,
$$M\left[\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} f_n\right] = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} M[f_n].$$
In addition, if $g_n$ is a sequence of random variables with the properties that $|g_n| \leq c$ for every $n$ and that $g_n$ converges to $g$, then
$$M[g] = \lim_{n \to \infty} M[g_n]$$
by Corollary 1-50.
