**Proof.** A point $\mathfrak{z} \in M$ is singular if the rank of the Jacobian matrix of the local defining functions for $M$ is less than the maximum possible rank. The singular locus $S(M)$ can be characterized locally by the vanishing of all $(n-k) \times (n-k)$ minors of the Jacobian matrix of the defining holomorphic functions $f_1, \ldots, f_{n-k}$. Since these minors are holomorphic functions, their common zero set is analytic. Moreover, the set of regular points is open in $M$, and by analytic continuation arguments, it is also dense. Hence $S(M)$ is a nowhere dense analytic subset of $G$.
