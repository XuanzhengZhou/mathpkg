A random variable is the fundamental bridge between measure theory and probability theory. Given a probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{B}, \mu)$, a random variable is simply a measurable function from the sample space $\Omega$ into the extended real numbers $\overline{\mathbb{R}}$. This definition allows us to apply all the standard machinery of measurable functions---limits, integrals, and convergence theorems---to probabilistic quantities. In the context of stochastic processes on sequence space, a function that is $\mathcal{B}_n$-measurable depends only on a bounded number of outcomes and is automatically a random variable.
