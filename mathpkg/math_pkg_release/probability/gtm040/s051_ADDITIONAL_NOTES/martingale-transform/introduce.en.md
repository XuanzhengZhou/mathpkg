A martingale transform generalizes the idea of optional sampling and the upcrossing argument. Given a martingale $(f_n, \mathcal{F}_n)$ with differences $d_n = f_n - f_{n-1}$, one multiplies each difference by a predictable weight $v_n$ measurable with respect to $\mathcal{F}_{n-1}$ and sums to obtain $g_n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} v_k d_k$. The resulting process $g_n$ remains a martingale provided integrability holds. Burkholder and Gundy used transforms to study martingale convergence, leading to deep integral inequalities analogous to the Lusin area function and Littlewood-Paley $g$-function in Fourier analysis.
