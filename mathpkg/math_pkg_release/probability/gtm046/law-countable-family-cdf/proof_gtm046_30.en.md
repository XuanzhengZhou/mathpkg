The law of the countable family $(X_1, X_2, \cdots)$ is defined by the distribution of this family, which determines and is determined by a consistent family of finite-dimensional distributions $P S_1, P S_{12}, \cdots$. Because of the consistency requirement, this family is superabundant.

Conditioning permits determination of the law by a non-superabundant family. By repeated application of the decomposition $P(S_1 \times S_2) = \int_{S_1} P(dx_1) P(x_1; S_2)$, we find that the law determines and is determined by the family
$$P(S_1), \; P(x_1; S_2), \; P(x_1, x_2; S_3), \; \cdots$$
of conditional distribution functions. These c.d.f.'s have no mutual consistency requirements — each $P(x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1}; S_n)$ is merely a measurable probability on the $n$-th coordinate space for each fixed $(x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1})$. The c.d.f.'s can be replaced by conditional distribution functions or conditional characteristic functions as needed.
