Liouville's theorem in Hamiltonian dynamics states that the flow generated by Hamilton's equations preserves the Lebesgue measure (Liouville measure) in phase space. Consider a Hamiltonian system with generalized coordinates \(q = (q_1, \ldots, q_N)\) and conjugate momenta \(p = (p_1, \ldots, p_N)\). The equations of motion are

$$
\dot{q}_i = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}, \quad \dot{p}_i = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q_i}, \quad i = 1, \ldots, N.
$$

Let \(T_t\) denote the phase flow at time \(t\). The divergence of the Hamiltonian vector field is

$$
\sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( \frac{\partial \dot{q}_i}{\partial q_i} + \frac{\partial \dot{p}_i}{\partial p_i} \right) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( \frac{\partial^2 H}{\partial q_i \partial p_i} - \frac{\partial^2 H}{\partial p_i \partial q_i} \right) = 0.
$$

By the divergence theorem (or Liouville's formula for the Jacobian determinant), the vanishing divergence implies that the volume element \(dq_1 \cdots dq_N dp_1 \cdots dp_N\) is invariant under the flow. More precisely, if \(J(t) = \det(\partial T_t / \partial (q,p))\) denotes the Jacobian, then \(\dot{J}(0) = \operatorname{div}(\dot{q}, \dot{p}) = 0\), so \(J(t) \equiv 1\). Hence \(\lambda(T_t A) = \lambda(A)\) for every Lebesgue-measurable set \(A\), establishing that the transformation \(\omega \leftrightarrow T_1 \omega\) preserves Lebesgue measure. This invariance is the foundation of classical ergodic theory, as it provides a probability measure (normalized Lebesgue measure on a compact energy surface) that is invariant under the dynamics.
