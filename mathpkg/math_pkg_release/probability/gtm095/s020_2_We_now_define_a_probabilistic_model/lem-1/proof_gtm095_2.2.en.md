# Proof of Lemma 1 (Existence of Smallest Algebra and $\sigma$-Algebra)

**Lemma 1.** Let $\mathcal{E}$ be a collection of subsets of $\Omega$. Then there exist the smallest algebra $\alpha(\mathcal{E})$ and the smallest $\sigma$-algebra $\sigma(\mathcal{E})$ containing all the sets that are in $\mathcal{E}$.

*Proof.* The class $\mathcal{F}^*$ of all subsets of $\Omega$ (the power set) is a $\sigma$-algebra, and hence also an algebra. Therefore there is at least one algebra and at least one $\sigma$-algebra containing $\mathcal{E}$.

We now define $\alpha(\mathcal{E})$ (respectively $\sigma(\mathcal{E})$) to consist of all sets that belong to every algebra (respectively every $\sigma$-algebra) containing $\mathcal{E}$. In other words,

$$\alpha(\mathcal{E}) = \bigcap \{\mathcal{A} : \mathcal{A} \text{ is an algebra}, \mathcal{E} \subseteq \mathcal{A}\},$$

$$\sigma(\mathcal{E}) = \bigcap \{\mathcal{F} : \mathcal{F} \text{ is a } \sigma\text{-algebra}, \mathcal{E} \subseteq \mathcal{F}\}.$$

It is easy to verify that an arbitrary intersection of algebras is again an algebra, and an arbitrary intersection of $\sigma$-algebras is again a $\sigma$-algebra. Consequently, $\alpha(\mathcal{E})$ is an algebra containing $\mathcal{E}$, $\sigma(\mathcal{E})$ is a $\sigma$-algebra containing $\mathcal{E}$, and by construction each is minimal with respect to these properties. $\square$

**Remark 1.** The algebra $\alpha(\mathcal{E})$ (or $\sigma(\mathcal{E})$, respectively) is often referred to as the smallest algebra (or $\sigma$-algebra) generated by $\mathcal{E}$.
