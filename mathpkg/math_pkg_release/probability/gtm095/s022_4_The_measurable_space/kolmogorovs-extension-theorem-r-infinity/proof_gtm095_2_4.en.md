# Proof of Kolmogorov's Theorem on the Extension of Measures on $(R^\infty, \mathcal{B}(R^\infty))$

Let $B^n \in \mathcal{B}(R^n)$ and let $\mathcal{I}_n(B^n)$ be the cylinder with base $B^n$. We assign the measure $P(\mathcal{I}_n(B^n))$ to this cylinder by taking $P(\mathcal{I}_n(B^n)) = P_n(B^n)$.

Let us show that, in virtue of the consistency condition, this definition is consistent, i.e., the value of $P(\mathcal{I}_n(B^n))$ is independent of the representation of the set $\mathcal{I}_n(B^n)$. In fact, let the same cylinder be represented in two ways:

$$\mathcal{I}_n(B^n) = \mathcal{I}_{n+k}(B^{n+k}).$$

It follows that, if $(x_1, \ldots, x_{n+k}) \in R^{n+k}$, we have

$$(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \in B^n \iff (x_1, \ldots, x_{n+k}) \in B^{n+k}.$$

By the consistency condition (16),

$$P_{n+k}(B^{n+k}) = P_{n+k}(B^n \times R^k) = P_n(B^n),$$

which shows that the definition is indeed independent of the representation.

It is easily verified that the cylinder sets form an algebra $\mathcal{A}$, and that $P$ is finitely additive on this algebra. To apply Carathéodory's theorem, we need to verify that $P$ is countably additive on $\mathcal{A}$.

Suppose the sets $\hat{B}_n$ are cylinders in $\mathcal{A}$, $\hat{B}_n \supseteq \hat{B}_{n+1}$, and $\bigcap \hat{B}_n = \varnothing$. We need to show $\lim_{n \to \infty} P(\hat{B}_n) = 0$.

Suppose the contrary, i.e., let $\lim P(\hat{B}_n) = \delta > 0$ (the limit exists due to monotonicity). We may suppose without loss of generality that $\{\hat{B}_n\}$ has the form

$$\hat{B}_n = \{x: (x_1, \ldots, x_n) \in B_n\}, \quad B_n \in \mathcal{B}(R^n).$$

We use the following property of probability measures $P_n$ on $(R^n, \mathcal{B}(R^n))$ (see Problem 9): if $B_n \in \mathcal{B}(R^n)$, for a given $\delta > 0$ we can find a compact set $A_n \in \mathcal{B}(R^n)$ such that $A_n \subseteq B_n$ and

$$P_n(B_n \setminus A_n) \leq \delta/2^{n+1}.$$

Therefore if

$$\hat{A}_n = \{x: (x_1, \ldots, x_n) \in A_n\},$$

we have

$$P(\hat{B}_n \setminus \hat{A}_n) = P_n(B_n \setminus A_n) \leq \delta/2^{n+1}.$$

Form the set $\hat{C}_n = \bigcap_{k=1}^{n} \hat{A}_k$ and let $C_n$ be such that

$$\hat{C}_n = \{x: (x_1, \ldots, x_n) \in C_n\}.$$

Then, since the sets $\hat{B}_n$ decrease, we obtain

$$P(\hat{B}_n \setminus \hat{C}_n) \leq \sum_{k=1}^{n} P(\hat{B}_n \setminus \hat{A}_k) \leq \sum_{k=1}^{n} P(\hat{B}_k \setminus \hat{A}_k) \leq \delta/2.$$

But by assumption $\lim_n P(\hat{B}_n) = \delta > 0$, and therefore $\lim_n P(\hat{C}_n) \geq \delta/2 > 0$, which means there exists for each $n$ a point $x^{(n)} \in \hat{C}_n$. From the compactness of the $A_n$, a diagonal process yields a point $x^* \in \bigcap \hat{A}_n \subseteq \bigcap \hat{B}_n$, contradicting the assumption $\bigcap \hat{B}_n = \varnothing$.

Hence $P$ is countably additive on $\mathcal{A}$, and by Carathéodory's theorem it can be extended uniquely to a probability measure on $\sigma(\mathcal{A}) = \mathcal{B}(R^\infty)$. This completes the proof.

It is known that this is a property not only of spaces $(R^n, \mathcal{B}(R^n))$, but also of arbitrary complete separable metric spaces with $\sigma$-algebras generated by the open sets. Consequently Theorem 3 remains valid if we suppose that $P_1, P_2, \ldots$ is a sequence of consistent probability measures on

$$(\Omega_1, \mathcal{F}_1), (\Omega_1 \times \Omega_2, \mathcal{F}_1 \otimes \mathcal{F}_2), \ldots,$$

where $(\Omega_i, \mathcal{F}_i)$ are complete separable metric spaces with $\sigma$-algebras $\mathcal{F}_i$ generated by open sets.
