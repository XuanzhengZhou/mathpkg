The Continuity Theorem (also known as Lévy's continuity theorem) establishes the fundamental link between weak convergence of distribution functions and pointwise convergence of their characteristic functions on the real line. Part (1) states that weak convergence of distributions implies pointwise convergence of the corresponding characteristic functions. Part (2) provides the converse: if the characteristic functions converge pointwise to a function continuous at zero, that limit is itself a characteristic function and the distributions converge weakly. This theorem serves as the primary tool for proving limit theorems in probability theory, as it reduces the problem of establishing weak convergence to checking convergence of characteristic functions.
