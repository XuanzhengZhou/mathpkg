# Proof of the Equality of the $\sigma$-Algebras $\mathcal{B}(R^T)$, $\mathcal{B}_1(R^T)$, and $\mathcal{B}_2(R^T)$

Let $T$ be an arbitrary set and let $R^T$ be the space of all real functions $x = (x_t)_{t \in T}$. Recall the three types of cylinder sets:

$$\mathcal{I}_{t_1, \ldots, t_n}(I_1 \times \cdots \times I_n) = \{x : x_{t_1} \in I_1, \ldots, x_{t_n} \in I_n\},$$

$$\mathcal{I}_{t_1, \ldots, t_n}(B_1 \times \cdots \times B_n) = \{x : x_{t_1} \in B_1, \ldots, x_{t_n} \in B_n\},$$

$$\mathcal{I}_{t_1, \ldots, t_n}(B^n) = \{x : (x_{t_1}, \ldots, x_{t_n}) \in B^n\},$$

where $I_k = (a_k, b_k]$, $B_k \in \mathcal{B}(R)$, and $B^n \in \mathcal{B}(R^n)$.

Let $\mathcal{B}(R^T)$, $\mathcal{B}_1(R^T)$, and $\mathcal{B}_2(R^T)$ be the smallest $\sigma$-algebras generated respectively by the cylinder sets of the first, second, and third types. It is clear from the inclusions of the generating classes that

$$\mathcal{B}(R^T) \subseteq \mathcal{B}_1(R^T) \subseteq \mathcal{B}_2(R^T). \tag{14}$$

**Theorem.** $\mathcal{B}(R^T) = \mathcal{B}_1(R^T) = \mathcal{B}_2(R^T)$.

*Proof.* Let $\mathcal{E}$ denote the collection of sets of the form

$$\{x : (x_{t_1}, x_{t_2}, \ldots) \in B\} \tag{15}$$

for various countable aggregates $(t_1, t_2, \ldots) \subseteq T$ and Borel sets $B \in \mathcal{B}(R^\infty)$.

We first show that $\mathcal{E}$ is a $\sigma$-algebra. Let $A_1, A_2, \ldots \in \mathcal{E}$ with corresponding countable aggregates $T^{(1)} = (t_1^{(1)}, t_2^{(1)}, \ldots)$, $T^{(2)} = (t_1^{(2)}, t_2^{(2)}, \ldots)$, $\ldots$. Set

$$T^{(\infty)} = \bigcup_{k=1}^{\infty} T^{(k)},$$

which is a countable set. By enumerating $T^{(\infty)} = \{\tau_1, \tau_2, \ldots\}$, every $A_i$ can be represented as

$$A_i = \{x : (x_{\tau_1}, x_{\tau_2}, \ldots) \in B_i\},$$

where each $B_i$ is a Borel set in the same $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{B}(R^\infty)$. Since $\mathcal{B}(R^\infty)$ is a $\sigma$-algebra, the collection of such $B_i$ is closed under countable unions, countable intersections, and complements. It follows immediately that $\mathcal{E}$ is a $\sigma$-algebra.

Clearly $\mathcal{E}$ contains all cylinder sets of the third type $\mathcal{I}_{t_1, \ldots, t_n}(B^n)$ (take the aggregate to consist of $t_1, \ldots, t_n$ followed by arbitrarily chosen indices, and take $B = B^n \times R^\infty$). Since $\mathcal{B}_2(R^T)$ is the smallest $\sigma$-algebra containing these cylinder sets, we have

$$\mathcal{B}_2(R^T) \subseteq \mathcal{E}.$$

Combining with (14), we obtain

$$\mathcal{B}(R^T) \subseteq \mathcal{B}_1(R^T) \subseteq \mathcal{B}_2(R^T) \subseteq \mathcal{E}. \tag{16}$$

Now consider an arbitrary set $A \in \mathcal{E}$, represented as in (15) with a fixed countable aggregate $(t_1, t_2, \ldots)$. The map $\varphi : R^T \to R^\infty$ defined by

$$\varphi(x) = (x_{t_1}, x_{t_2}, \ldots)$$

is measurable with respect to the $\sigma$-algebra generated by sets of the form $\{x : x_{t_k} \in I\}$ with $I = (a, b]$. But these are precisely cylinder sets of the first type. Hence $A = \varphi^{-1}(B)$ belongs to the $\sigma$-algebra generated by the cylinder sets $\mathcal{I}_{t_1, \ldots, t_n}(I_1 \times \cdots \times I_n)$, which is exactly $\mathcal{B}(R^T)$.

Thus $\mathcal{E} \subseteq \mathcal{B}(R^T)$. Together with (16), this yields

$$\mathcal{B}(R^T) = \mathcal{B}_1(R^T) = \mathcal{B}_2(R^T) = \mathcal{E},$$

which completes the proof. $\square$

**Remark.** This result shows that on the function space $R^T$, all three natural ways of generating a $\sigma$-algebra from finite-dimensional cylinder sets lead to the same $\sigma$-algebra. Moreover, this $\sigma$-algebra coincides with the collection $\mathcal{E}$ of sets that depend on at most countably many coordinates.
