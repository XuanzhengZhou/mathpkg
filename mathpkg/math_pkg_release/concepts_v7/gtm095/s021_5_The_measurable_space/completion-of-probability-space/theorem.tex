Let $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ be a probability space. Let $\overline{\mathcal{F}}^P$ be the collection of all subsets $A$ of $\Omega$ for which there exist sets $B_1, B_2 \in \mathcal{F}$ such that

$$B_1 \subseteq A \subseteq B_2, \quad P(B_2 \setminus B_1) = 0.$$

The probability measure is extended to $\overline{\mathcal{F}}^P$ by $P(A) = P(B_1)$ for such sets $A$. The resulting space $(\Omega, \overline{\mathcal{F}}^P, P)$ is called the \textit{completion} of $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ with respect to $P$.

A probability measure such that $\overline{\mathcal{F}}^P = \mathcal{F}$ is called \textit{complete}, and the corresponding space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ is a \textit{complete probability space}.
