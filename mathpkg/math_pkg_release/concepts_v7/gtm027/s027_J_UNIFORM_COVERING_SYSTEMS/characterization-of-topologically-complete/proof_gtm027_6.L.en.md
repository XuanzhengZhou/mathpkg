# Proof of Characterization of Topologically Complete Completely Regular Spaces

From part (a) of this section, if $\mathfrak{U}$ and $\mathfrak{V}$ are uniformities for $X$ with $\mathfrak{V} \subset \mathfrak{U}$, $(X, \mathfrak{U})$ complete, and the topologies coincide, then $(X, \mathfrak{V})$ is also complete.

Let $\mathfrak{J}$ be a completely regular topology on $X$. Let $\mathfrak{U}_{\max}$ be the largest uniformity whose topology is $\mathfrak{J}$ (this is the universal uniformity, generated by all continuous pseudo-metrics on $X$ with respect to $\mathfrak{J}$).

If $(X, \mathfrak{J})$ is topologically complete, then by definition there exists some uniformity $\mathfrak{U}$ such that $(X, \mathfrak{U})$ is complete and the topology of $\mathfrak{U}$ is $\mathfrak{J}$. Since $\mathfrak{U} \subset \mathfrak{U}_{\max}$ (as $\mathfrak{U}_{\max}$ is the finest uniformity compatible with $\mathfrak{J}$), by part (a) we need to verify that the topology of $\mathfrak{U}_{\max}$ equals $\mathfrak{J}$, which holds by construction.

Conversely, if $(X, \mathfrak{U}_{\max})$ is complete, then $X$ is topologically complete (taking $\mathfrak{U} = \mathfrak{U}_{\max}$).

Thus, a completely regular space is topologically complete iff it is complete relative to the largest uniformity whose topology is $\mathfrak{J}$.
