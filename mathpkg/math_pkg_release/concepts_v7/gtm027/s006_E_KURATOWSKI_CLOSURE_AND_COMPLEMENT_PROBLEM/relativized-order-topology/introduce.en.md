A subtlety in the theory of order topologies: when $Y$ is a subset of a linearly ordered set $X$, there are two natural topologies one can place on $Y$. One is the subspace topology inherited from the order topology on $X$, and the other is the order topology coming from the restricted linear order on $Y$ itself. These two topologies are in general distinct. A classic counterexample is the subset $\{0\} \cup (1, 2)$ of the real line, where the two topologies differ at the point $0$.
