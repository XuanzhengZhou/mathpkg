Taking the orthogonal complement twice returns the original closed subspace: $N^{\perp\perp} = N$. The inclusion $N \subseteq N^{\perp\perp}$ always holds; the reverse inclusion uses the orthogonal decomposition theorem to write any $x \in N^{\perp\perp}$ as a sum of an element of $N$ and an element of $N^\perp$, forcing the latter to vanish. This makes the orthogonal complement an involution on the lattice of closed subspaces.
