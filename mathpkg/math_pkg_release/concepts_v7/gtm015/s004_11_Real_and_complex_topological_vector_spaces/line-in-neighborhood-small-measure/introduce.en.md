When the underlying measure $\mu$ takes arbitrarily small positive values (e.g., Lebesgue measure on $[0,1]$), the space $[\mathcal{M}]$ exhibits striking pathological behavior: every neighborhood of the origin contains an entire one-dimensional subspace (a line through the origin). This means the space fails dramatically to be locally convex — in a locally convex space, neighborhoods of zero can be chosen to contain no nontrivial subspaces. This property is the root cause of many counterexample behaviors of $[\mathcal{M}]$.
