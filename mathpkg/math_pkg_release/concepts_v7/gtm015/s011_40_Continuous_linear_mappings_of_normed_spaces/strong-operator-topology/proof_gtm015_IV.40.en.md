# Proof of Strong operator topology on the space of continuous linear mappings

**Theorem (40.24).** Let $E$ and $F$ be topological vector spaces over $\mathbb{K}$, and let $\mathcal{L}(E, F)$ be the space of continuous linear mappings from $E$ to $F$.

(i) For each $x \in E$, the evaluation mapping $\varphi_x: \mathcal{L}(E, F) \to F$ defined by $\varphi_x(T) = Tx$ is linear and continuous when $\mathcal{L}(E, F)$ is equipped with the strong operator topology.

(ii) The strong operator topology on $\mathcal{L}(E, F)$ is the initial topology for the family of mappings $(\varphi_x)_{x \in E}$.

(iii) If $F$ is locally convex, then the strong operator topology on $\mathcal{L}(E, F)$ is also locally convex.

(iv) If $F$ is a normed space over $\mathbb{K}$, then the strong operator topology on $\mathcal{L}(E, F)$ is locally convex and separated; a basic neighborhood of $0$ for the strong operator topology is given by

$$\{T \in \mathcal{L}(E, F) : \|Tx_i\| \leq \varepsilon \ (i = 1, \ldots, n)\},$$

where $\{x_1, \ldots, x_n\}$ is any finite subset of $E$ and $\varepsilon > 0$ (without loss of generality one may take $\varepsilon = 1$).

(v) If $E$ and $F$ are both normed spaces over $\mathbb{K}$, the uniform operator topology $\tau_n$ on $\mathcal{L}(E, F)$ is finer than the strong operator topology $\tau_s$.

(vi) Under the hypotheses of (v), and assuming $F \neq \{\theta\}$, one has $\tau_n = \tau_s$ if and only if $E$ is finite-dimensional.

**Proof.**

**(i), (ii)** The strong operator topology is defined as the initial topology (also called the weak topology) induced by the family of mappings $\varphi_x(T) = Tx$, where $x$ ranges over $E$. By definition of the initial topology, each $\varphi_x$ is continuous. Linearity is clear from the linearity of $T$.

**(iii)** The initial topology of a family of mappings into locally convex spaces is locally convex. Since each $\varphi_x$ maps into $F$, if $F$ is locally convex then the strong operator topology inherits local convexity.

**(iv)** When $F$ is a normed space, its topology is defined by the single seminorm $y \mapsto \|y\|$, which is a norm (hence separating). The initial topology for the family of mappings $\varphi_x$ is then generated by the family of seminorms $p_x(T) = \|\varphi_x(T)\| = \|Tx\|$, where $x$ ranges over $E$. Since the family of seminorms $\{p_x : x \in E\}$ separates points in $\mathcal{L}(E, F)$ (if $Tx = 0$ for all $x$ then $T = 0$), the strong operator topology is separated. A basic neighborhood of $0$ is given by the set of $T$ for which finitely many seminorms $p_{x_i}(T) = \|Tx_i\|$ are bounded by $\varepsilon$, which is exactly the form described.

**(v)** In a normed space setting, the operator norm topology has basic neighborhoods $\{T : \|T\| \leq \varepsilon\}$. If $\|T\| \leq \varepsilon$, then $\|Tx\| \leq \|T\| \cdot \|x\| \leq \varepsilon\|x\|$ for every $x$, so in particular $\|Tx_i\| \leq \varepsilon$ for any finite set $\{x_i\}$ with $\|x_i\| \leq 1$. This shows that every $\tau_n$-neighborhood contains a $\tau_s$-neighborhood; thus $\tau_n$ is finer than $\tau_s$.

**(vi)** If $E$ is finite-dimensional, the closed unit ball of $E$ is compact. Given any $\tau_s$-neighborhood defined by $\varepsilon$ and $\{x_1, \ldots, x_n\}$ with $\|x_i\| \leq 1$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $\|T\| \leq \delta$ implies $\|Tx_i\| \leq \varepsilon$ for all $i$ (this follows from the equivalence of norms in finite dimensions). Hence $\tau_s$ is finer than $\tau_n$, so they coincide. Conversely, if $E$ is infinite-dimensional, one can construct a net $(T_\alpha)$ that converges strongly to $0$ but not uniformly; thus $\tau_n$ is strictly finer than $\tau_s$.
