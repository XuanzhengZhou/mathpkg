Suppose $(S, d)$ is a metric space. A subset $A \subset S$ is \textbf{compact} if, whenever $A$ is contained in the union of a collection of open sets, it is contained in the union of some finite subcollection. That is, if each $x \in A$ has a neighborhood $N(x)$, then there are finitely many points $x_1, \ldots, x_n \in A$ such that $A \subset \bigcup_{j=1}^{n} N(x_j)$.

A subset $A \subset S$ is \textbf{sequentially compact} if every sequence in $A$ has a subsequence converging to a point of $A$.
