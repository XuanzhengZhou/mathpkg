If $w, z \in \mathbb{C}$ and $w = \exp z$, then $z$ is said to be a logarithm of $w$, written
$$
z = \log w.
$$
By Theorem 6.4, every nonzero $w \in \mathbb{C}$ has a logarithm; in fact it has infinitely many, whose imaginary parts differ by integer multiples of $2\pi$. Thus $\log w$ is not a single-valued function of $w$ in the usual sense. It can be rendered single-valued by restricting the imaginary part: for any $a \in \mathbb{R}$, the $z$ such that $\exp z = w$ and $\Im z \in [a, a + 2\pi)$ is unique.
