Theorem 6.2 reveals the deep connection between harmonic functions and complex analysis: every harmonic function is locally the real part of a holomorphic function. The proof reduces to the case of the unit disc centered at the origin, where the Dirichlet problem solution provides a Fourier series representation. By using the fact that the boundary values $g(\theta) = u(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$ have Fourier coefficients satisfying $b_{-n} = \overline{b_n}$ (since $u$ is real-valued), the series $\sum r^{|n|} b_n e^{in\theta}$ can be rewritten as the real part of a convergent power series $\sum a_n (re^{i\theta})^n$. This theorem is the converse of the fact that the real part of any holomorphic function is harmonic (which follows from the Cauchy-Riemann equations), and together they establish that harmonic functions are precisely the real parts of holomorphic functions.
