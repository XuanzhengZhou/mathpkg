# Proof of Theorem 41: Integers under Addition as Cyclic Group

We verify the group axioms for the set of integers $\mathbb{Z}$ under addition.

**Axioms (i)–(ii).** For $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$, $a + b \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $a + b = b + a$ (commutativity). Also $a + (b + c) = (a + b) + c$ (associativity). These hold by the elementary properties of integer addition.

**Axiom (iii).** If $a + b = a' + b$, then subtracting $b$ from both sides yields $a = a'$. Thus the cancellation law holds.

**Axiom (iv).** For any $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$, there exists an integer $x$ such that $a + x = b$, namely $x = b - a \in \mathbb{Z}$.

The unit element is $0$, since $a + 0 = a$ for all $a \in \mathbb{Z}$.

The group is generated by the element $1$: every positive integer $n$ is obtained by adding $1$ to itself $n$ times; $0$ is the empty sum; and negative integers are obtained by adding the inverse $(-1)$ repeatedly. Hence $\mathbb{Z}$ is an infinite cyclic group generated by $1$.

By Theorem 2 (proved in Chapter I), every subgroup of $\mathbb{Z}$ is of the form $k\mathbb{Z} = \{kn \mid n \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ for some $k \geq 0$, and is thus again cyclic. In the language of modules, these subgroups are precisely the modules of integers.

The group is torsion-free: if $n \cdot a = 0$ for some integer $n \neq 0$ and $a \in \mathbb{Z}$, then $na = 0$ implies $a = 0$ (since $\mathbb{Z}$ has no zero divisors). Equivalently, no nonzero element has finite order.

Thus $\mathbb{Z}$ is a torsion-free cyclic group with one basis element, i.e., an infinite cyclic group. $\square$
