# Proof of Theorem 102

If $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_n$ are basis elements of the ideal $\mathfrak{a}$, then the $n$ sequences of numbers $\beta_1^{(p)}, \ldots, \beta_n^{(p)}$ ($p = 1, 2, \ldots, n$) are basis elements of the complementary ideal $\mathfrak{m} = \mathfrak{a}^{-1}\mathfrak{d}^{-1}$.

Moreover, $\mathfrak{m}\mathfrak{a}$ is independent of the choice of $\mathfrak{a}$ and equals $\mathfrak{d}^{-1}$, the reciprocal of the different $\mathfrak{d}$ of the field. The ideal $\mathfrak{d}$ itself is integral, and a prime ideal $\mathfrak{p}$ divides $\mathfrak{d}$ if and only if $\mathfrak{p}$ divides the discriminant $d$ of the field.

This follows from Theorem 101: the complementary module $\mathfrak{m}$ defined by the trace condition $S(\lambda\alpha) = \text{integer}$ for all $\alpha \in \mathfrak{a}$ is precisely the ideal $\mathfrak{a}^{-1}\mathfrak{d}^{-1}$, and the product $\mathfrak{m}\mathfrak{a} = \mathfrak{d}^{-1}$ is independent of $\mathfrak{a}$. The discriminant enters through the determinant relation $N(\mathfrak{a})\sqrt{d}$ appearing in the solution for $\lambda^{(i)}$.
