This is Lemma None from Lectures on the Theory of Algebraic Numbers by Erich Hecke.

Suppose that $(m, n) = 1$. Then if for each $a$ there exist two integers $\alpha$ and $\beta$ in $k(\sqrt{d})$ such that

$$a \equiv N(\alpha) \pmod{m} \quad \text{and} \quad a \equiv N(\beta) \pmod{n},$$

there is also an integer $\gamma$ in $k(\sqrt{d})$ for which

$$a \equiv N(\gamma) \pmod{mn}.$
