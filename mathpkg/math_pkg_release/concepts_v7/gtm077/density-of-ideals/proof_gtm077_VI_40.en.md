# Proof of Theorem 121: Density of Ideals in a Class

**Theorem 121.** Let $A$ be an arbitrary class of ideals of $K$, and let $Z(t; A)$ denote the number of integral ideals in the class $A$ whose norm is $\leq t$. Then the limit

$$\lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{Z(t; A)}{t} = \varkappa$$

exists, and is given by the formula

$$\varkappa = \frac{2^{r_1 + r_2} \pi^{r_2} R}{w |\sqrt{d}|}$$

which is independent of $A$ and is determined by the field alone. (The notation is that of §§34, 35: $r_1$ real conjugates, $r_2$ complex conjugate pairs, $R$ the regulator, $w$ the number of roots of unity in $K$, $d$ the discriminant.)

*Proof.* Let $\mathfrak{a}$ be an integral ideal of the class $A^{-1}$, reciprocal to $A$, so that each ideal of $A$ becomes a principal ideal by multiplication by $\mathfrak{a}$. Accordingly, for each integral ideal $\mathfrak{b}$ in $A$ there exists a single principal ideal $(\omega)$ which is divisible by $\mathfrak{b}$ such that

$$\mathfrak{a}\mathfrak{b} = (\omega).$$

Consequently $Z(t; A)$ is equal to the number of nonassociated (i.e., modulo units) integral numbers $\omega$ of the field which are divisible by $\mathfrak{a}$ and whose norm satisfies $|N(\omega)| \leq t \cdot N(\mathfrak{a})$.

We now attempt to extract a single element from each system of associated numbers by means of inequalities. For this purpose let $\varepsilon_1, \varepsilon_2, \ldots, \varepsilon_r$ be a system of $r$ basic units as in §35 (so $r = r_1 + r_2 - 1$). To each number $\omega$ of the field different from $0$, there is a uniquely determined system of real numbers $c_1, c_2, \ldots, c_r$ such that for the first $r$ conjugates we have:

$$\log \frac{|\omega^{(p)}|}{\sqrt[n]{|N(\omega)|}} = c_1 \log \left| \varepsilon_1^{(p)} \right| + \cdots + c_r \log \left| \varepsilon_r^{(p)} \right| \quad (p = 1, 2, \ldots, r).$$

Let us call the $c_i$ the *exponents* of $\omega$. Again $e_p = 1$ if $K^{(p)}$ is real, $e_p = 2$ otherwise. Then since

$$\sum_{p=1}^{r+1} e_p \log \frac{|\omega^{(p)}|}{\sqrt[n]{|N(\omega)|}} = 0$$

(the sum over all conjugates of the logarithmic expression vanishes because the product $\prod |\omega^{(p)}|^{e_p} = |N(\omega)|$), the $(r+1)$-st conjugate is determined by the first $r$ conjugates.

Now the numbers $\omega$ we are to count satisfy the following conditions:

$$|N(\omega)| = |\omega^{(1)} \cdot \omega^{(2)} \cdots \omega^{(n)}| \leq N(\mathfrak{a})\,t \tag{85}$$

$$\log \frac{|\omega^{(p)}|}{\sqrt[n]{|N(\omega)|}} = \sum_{q=1}^{r} c_q \log |\varepsilon_q^{(p)}| \qquad (p = 1, \ldots, r) \tag{86}$$

$$0 \leq c_q < 1 \qquad (q = 1, \ldots, r). \tag{87}$$

Condition (87) selects exactly one representative from each set of associated numbers (the fundamental domain for the action of the unit group).

However, in order that $\omega$ be divisible by $\mathfrak{a}$ it is necessary and sufficient that

$$\omega^{(p)} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} x_k \alpha_k^{(p)} \quad (p = 1, 2, \ldots, n) \tag{88}$$

with rational integers $x_1, \ldots, x_n$, where $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_n$ denotes a definite basis of the ideal $\mathfrak{a}$. Consequently, $w \cdot Z(t; A)$ is the number of rational integers $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ which satisfy the three conditions (85), (86), (87), where not all $x_i = 0$ (the factor $w$ accounts for the roots of unity, each principal ideal $(\omega)$ is generated by $w$ different numbers).

If we now choose arbitrary real values for the $x_i$, then to the corresponding $\omega^{(p)}$ there is associated a uniquely determined real number $c_q$ by Equation (86), provided all $\omega^{(p)} \neq 0$. Now let $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ be the Cartesian rectangular coordinates of a point in $n$-dimensional space and, to begin with, consider only those points which do not lie on one of the manifolds $\omega^{(p)} = 0$ of lower dimension.

Then Inequalities (85) and (86) obviously define, in the complementary space, a domain $B_t$ lying entirely in finite space; for we have

$$|\omega^{(p)}| = \sqrt[n]{|N(\omega)|} \left| \exp\left( \sum_{q=1}^{r} c_q \log |\varepsilon_q^{(p)}| \right) \right| \leq \sqrt[n]{t \cdot N(\mathfrak{a})} \, e^{rM}$$

where $M = \max_{p,q} |\log|\varepsilon_q^{(p)}||$. The domain $B_t$ is obtained from the domain $B_1^*$ (corresponding to $t = 1$) by the similarity transformation

$$\mathbf{x} \mapsto t^{1/n} \mathbf{x}.$$

Therefore the volume of $B_t$ is:

$$V(B_t) = t \cdot V(B_1^*).$$

Now $Z(t; A) \cdot w$ is the number of integer lattice points in $B_t$ (not all zero). By the geometry of numbers (comparing the number of lattice points with the volume),

$$\lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{w \cdot Z(t; A)}{t} = V(B_1^*)$$

so that

$$\varkappa = \frac{V(B_1^*)}{w}.$$

It remains to evaluate the $n$-dimensional volume $J = V(B_1^*)$.

---

**Evaluation of the integral $J$.** The domain $B_1^*$ is described by the following inequalities. Setting

$$\omega^{(p)} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} y_k \alpha_k^{(p)} \quad (p = 1, 2, \ldots, n)$$

with real variables $y_k$, we have:

$$0 < |\omega^{(1)} \cdot \omega^{(2)} \cdots \omega^{(n)}| \leq N(\mathfrak{a})$$

$$\log \frac{|\omega^{(p)}|}{\sqrt[n]{|N(\omega)|}} = \sum_{q=1}^{r} c_q \log \left| \varepsilon_q^{(p)} \right|$$

with $0 \leq c_q < 1$ $(p, q = 1, 2, \ldots, r)$. Additionally, $|\omega^{(p)}| \leq e^{rM} \sqrt[n]{N(\mathfrak{a})}$. The conditions $\omega^{(p)} = 0$ define only manifolds of lower dimension, so they make no contribution to the $n$-fold integral and can be omitted.

To evaluate the integral $J = \int \cdots \int dy_1 \cdots dy_n$, we introduce the real and imaginary parts of the $\omega^{(p)}$ as new variables in place of the $y$'s:

$$z_p = \omega^{(p)} \quad \text{for } p = 1, 2, \ldots, r_1,$$

$$z_p + iz_{p+r_2} = \omega^{(p)} \quad \text{for } p = r_1 + 1, \ldots, r_1 + r_2,$$

so that the functional determinant (as in Theorem 95) is

$$\left| \frac{\partial (z_1, \ldots, z_n)}{\partial (y_1, \ldots, y_n)} \right| = 2^{-r_2} N(\mathfrak{a}) \left| \sqrt{d} \right|.$$

If we then introduce polar coordinates for the complex variables:

$$z_p = \rho_p \quad (\rho_p > 0) \quad \text{for real places } p = 1, \ldots, r_1,$$

$$z_{p} + iz_{p+r_2} = \rho_p e^{i\varphi_{p-r_1}} \quad (\rho_p > 0, 0 \leq \varphi_{p-r_1} < 2\pi) \quad \text{for } p = r_1+1, \ldots, r_1+r_2.$$

The integration with respect to the $\varphi_i$ yields a factor $2\pi$ for each complex place. Since we need only integrate over the portion of the domain with $\rho_1 > 0, \ldots, \rho_{r_1+r_2} > 0$, and accounting for the sign of real variables, we obtain:

$$\begin{aligned}
J &= \frac{2^{r_1+r_2} \pi^{r_2}}{N(\mathfrak{a}) \left| \sqrt{d} \right|} \int \cdots \int \rho_{r_1+1} \cdot \rho_{r_1+2} \cdots \rho_{r_1+r_2} \; d\rho_1 d\rho_2 \cdots d\rho_{r+1} \\
&= \frac{2^{r_1+r_2} \pi^{r_2}}{N(\mathfrak{a}) \left| \sqrt{d} \right|} \int \cdots \int dv_1 dv_2 \cdots dv_{r+1}
\end{aligned}$$

where we introduce $v_k = \rho_k^{e_k}$ (recall $e_k = 1$ for real, $e_k = 2$ for complex places; then $dv_k = e_k \rho_k^{e_k-1} d\rho_k$, and the additional factor cancels exactly with the Jacobian of the transformation). Here $r+1 = r_1 + r_2$ is the number of Archimedean places.

The conditions for the $v_i$ read:

$$0 < v_1 \cdot v_2 \cdots v_{r+1} \leq N(\mathfrak{a}), \quad v_p > 0$$

$$\log v_p = \frac{e_p}{n} \log(v_1 \cdots v_{r+1}) + e_p \sum_{q=1}^{r} c_q \log |\varepsilon_q^{(p)}|, \quad 0 \leq c_q < 1.$$

Finally we introduce the $c_1, \ldots, c_r$ as new variables in place of (say) $v_1, \ldots, v_r$ and set

$$u = v_1 \cdot v_2 \cdots v_{r+1},$$

and we thus obtain

$$\log v_p = \frac{e_p}{n} \log u + e_p \sum_{q=1}^{r} c_q \log |\varepsilon_q^{(p)}| \quad (p = 1, \ldots, r+1).$$

The functional determinant is:

$$\frac{\partial(v_1, \ldots, v_{r+1})}{\partial(u, c_1, \ldots, c_r)} = \frac{v_1 \cdot v_2 \cdots v_{r+1}}{u} \cdot \det \left( e_p \log |\varepsilon_q^{(p)}| \right)_{p,q=1,\ldots,r}.$$

The determinant on the right is exactly the regulator $R$ (up to sign, since the $(r+1)$-st row is determined by the first $r$ rows via the relation $\sum e_p \log |\varepsilon_q^{(p)}| = 0$). Moreover, the domain of integration for $u$ is $0 < u \leq N(\mathfrak{a})$, and for the $c_q$ it is the unit cube $0 \leq c_q < 1$ ($q = 1, \ldots, r$).

Carrying out the integration:

$$\begin{aligned}
J &= \frac{2^{r_1+r_2} \pi^{r_2}}{N(\mathfrak{a}) \left| \sqrt{d} \right|} \int_{0}^{N(\mathfrak{a})} du \int_{0}^{1} \cdots \int_{0}^{1} R \; dc_1 \cdots dc_r \\
&= \frac{2^{r_1+r_2} \pi^{r_2} R}{N(\mathfrak{a}) \left| \sqrt{d} \right|} \cdot N(\mathfrak{a}) \cdot 1 \\
&= \frac{2^{r_1+r_2} \pi^{r_2} R}{\left| \sqrt{d} \right|}.
\end{aligned}$$

Therefore:

$$\varkappa = \frac{J}{w} = \frac{2^{r_1+r_2} \pi^{r_2} R}{w \left| \sqrt{d} \right|}.$$

Since this result does not depend on the choice of the class $A$, the density is the same for all ideal classes of $K$. $\square$
