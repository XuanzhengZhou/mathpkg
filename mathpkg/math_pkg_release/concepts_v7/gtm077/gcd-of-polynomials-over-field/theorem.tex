Two arbitrary nonzero polynomials $f_1(x)$ and $f_2(x)$ over $k$ have a uniquely determined greatest common divisor $d(x)$, that is, there is a polynomial $d(x)$ with leading coefficient 1, such that

$$d(x) \mid f_1(x), \quad d(x) \mid f_2(x)$$

and every polynomial which divides $f_1(x)$ and $f_2(x)$, also divides $d(x)$.

Moreover, $d(x)$ can be represented in the form

$$d(x) = g_1(x)f_1(x) + g_2(x)f_2(x),$$

(33)

where $g_1(x)$ and $g_2(x)$ are polynomials over $k$, and thus $d(x)$ is also a polynomial over $k$.
