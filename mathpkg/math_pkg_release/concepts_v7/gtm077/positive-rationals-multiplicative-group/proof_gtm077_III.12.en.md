# Proof: Positive Rationals as Multiplicative Group

We verify that the positive rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}_{>0}$ form an infinite Abelian group under multiplication, and that the primes constitute a basis.

**Group axioms.** Let $a, b, c \in \mathbb{Q}_{>0}$.

(i) **Closure:** $a \cdot b \in \mathbb{Q}_{>0}$ — the product of two positive rationals is a positive rational.

(ii) **Associativity:** $a \cdot (b \cdot c) = (a \cdot b) \cdot c$ — multiplication of rational numbers is associative.

(iii) **Commutativity:** $a \cdot b = b \cdot a$ — multiplication of rationals is commutative.

(iv) **Identity:** The number $1$ satisfies $a \cdot 1 = a$ for all $a$.

(v) **Inverses:** For each $a = p/q \in \mathbb{Q}_{>0}$ (with $p, q \in \mathbb{N}$), the reciprocal $a^{-1} = q/p \in \mathbb{Q}_{>0}$ satisfies $a \cdot a^{-1} = 1$.

Thus $\mathfrak{M} = \mathbb{Q}_{>0}$ is an infinite Abelian group under multiplication.

**Torsion-free.** Suppose $a \in \mathfrak{M}$ and $a^n = 1$ for some positive integer $n$. Expressing $a = p/q$ with $(p, q) = 1$, the equation $(p/q)^n = 1$ implies $p^n = q^n$, hence $p = q$. Thus $a = 1$. Therefore $\mathfrak{M}$ is torsion-free: no element other than the identity has finite order.

**The primes as a basis.** By the fundamental theorem of arithmetic, every positive rational $a \in \mathfrak{M}$ can be uniquely expressed as a finite product
$$a = \prod_{i=1}^{k} p_i^{e_i},$$
where each $p_i$ is a (positive) prime, the exponents $e_i$ are nonzero integers, and the representation is unique up to ordering of factors. This means exactly that the set of all positive prime numbers forms an infinite basis for $\mathfrak{M}$ in the sense of group theory (cf. §11): each element is a unique finite linear combination (with integer exponents) of the basis elements.

The simplest subgroups of $\mathfrak{M}$ are obtained by restricting to rational numbers whose prime factorizations involve only a specified subset of primes (finite or infinite). For example, the set $\{2^a 3^b \mid a, b \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ is a subgroup of rank 2 freely generated by the primes 2 and 3. $\square$
