# Proof of Theorem 107: Relative Norm of an Ideal

**Theorem 107.** The ideal $N_k(\mathfrak{A})$ is an ideal in $k$. If $k$ is the field of rational numbers, then $N_k(\mathfrak{A}) = (N(\mathfrak{A}))$.

**Proof.** To begin let $\mathfrak{A} = (A_1, \ldots, A_s)$ be an integral ideal, where the $A_i$ are numbers in $K$. Then, by §28, for any variables $u_1, \ldots, u_s$, the content of the conjugate polynomials

$$F^{(i)}(u) = A_1^{(i)}u_1 + \cdots + A_s^{(i)}u_s$$

for $i = 1, \ldots, m$ are the corresponding relative conjugate ideals $\mathfrak{A}^{(i)}$. By the Gauss lemma on contents, the content of the product of these polynomials is the product of their contents. But the coefficients of the product

$$\prod_{i=1}^{m} F^{(i)}(u)$$

are symmetric functions of the conjugates, hence they lie in $k$. The content of this product is therefore an ideal in $k$. On the other hand, the content is precisely

$$\mathfrak{A}^{(1)} \cdot \mathfrak{A}^{(2)} \cdots \mathfrak{A}^{(m)} = N_k(\mathfrak{A}),$$

which proves that $N_k(\mathfrak{A})$ is an ideal in $k$.

For the second assertion: if $k = k(1)$ is the field of rational numbers, then by definition the relative norm coincides with the absolute norm of the ideal up to the principal ideal generated by the norm $N(\mathfrak{A})$. Indeed, $N_k(\mathfrak{A})$ as an ideal in $k(1)$ is a principal ideal $(a)$ for some rational integer $a$, and comparing the indices shows $a = N(\mathfrak{A})$.

Thus Theorem 88 of §29, which was only proved in the case of Galois fields, is now seen as valid for every number field, and at the same time the terminology "norm of $\mathfrak{A}$" for the number of residue classes mod $\mathfrak{A}$ has received its justification.
