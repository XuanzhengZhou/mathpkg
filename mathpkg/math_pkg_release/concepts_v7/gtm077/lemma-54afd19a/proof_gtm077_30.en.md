# Proof of If the $r$ relations (58) hold for the $k$ units $\varepsilon_1, \varepsilon_2,

Obviously it is enough to prove this for those units which are not roots of unity. Suppose we choose a number $q$ such that the $r$ equations among the units $\varepsilon_1, \varepsilon_2, \ldots, \varepsilon_{q-1}$

$$\sum_{m=1}^{q-1} \alpha_m \log|\varepsilon_m^{(i)}| = 0 \quad (i=1, \ldots, r)$$

hold only for $\alpha_1 = \cdots = \alpha_{q-1} = 0$ and on the other hand such that between the $q$ units such a system

$$\sum_{m=1}^{q} \beta_m \log|\varepsilon_m^{(i)}| = 0 \quad (i=1, 2, \ldots, r)$$

holds with real $\beta_1, \ldots, \beta_q$ not all vanishing. Thus $2 \leq q \leq k$, and by the assumption about $q$ we necessarily have $\beta_q \neq 0$ and the $q-1$ quotients $\beta_1/\beta_q, \ldots, \beta_{q-1}/\beta_q$ are uniquely determined.

The lemma will be proved once we show that these $q-1$ quotients $\beta_m/\beta_q$ are rational. This follows from a density argument: if any $\beta_m/\beta_q$ were irrational, infinitely many distinct systems $(\rho_1, \ldots, \rho_{q-1})$ with $|\rho_i| \leq 1/2$ would arise via $N\rho_i - n_i$ for varying integers $N, n_i$, contradicting the finiteness established by Lemma (a) of §34. Hence all quotients are rational, and the lemma is proved.
