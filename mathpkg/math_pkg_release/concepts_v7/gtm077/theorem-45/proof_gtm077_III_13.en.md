# Proof of Theorem 45: Structure of $\Re(2^\alpha)$

**Theorem 45.** The groups $\Re(2)$ and $\Re(4)$ are cyclic. If $\alpha \geq 3$ then the group $\Re(2^\alpha)$ of order $h = \varphi(2^\alpha) = 2^{\alpha-1}$ has exactly two basis classes. One is of order $2$, the other of order $h/2 = 2^{\alpha-2}$.

*Proof.* The statements are trivial for the moduli $2$ and $4$:
- $\Re(2) = \{1\}$ is trivially cyclic (order $1$).
- $\Re(4) = \{1, 3\}$ is cyclic of order $2$.

Now suppose $\alpha \geq 3$. The residue class $-1$ modulo $2^\alpha$ has order $2$, since $(-1)^2 = 1$ and $-1 \not\equiv 1 \pmod{2^\alpha}$ for $\alpha \geq 2$. This gives one basis class of order $2$.

For the second basis class, we consider the residue class of $5$ and prove by induction on $\alpha$ that

$$5^{2^{\alpha-2}} \equiv 1 \pmod{2^\alpha},$$

while $5^{2^{\alpha-3}} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{2^\alpha}$. Equivalently, we show

$$5^{2^{\alpha-2}} = 1 + 2^\alpha u, \quad \text{where $u$ is odd}, \quad (\alpha \geq 3).$$

**Base case $\alpha = 3$:** $5^{2^{3-2}} = 5^2 = 25 = 1 + 8 \cdot 3 = 1 + 2^3 \cdot 3$, and $3$ is odd. ✓

**Inductive step:** Assume the statement holds for some $\alpha \geq 3$:

$$5^{2^{\alpha-2}} = 1 + 2^\alpha u, \quad \text{$u$ odd}.$$

Squaring both sides:

$$5^{2^{\alpha-1}} = (1 + 2^\alpha u)^2 = 1 + 2^{\alpha+1} u + 2^{2\alpha} u^2 = 1 + 2^{\alpha+1} u (1 + 2^{\alpha-1} u).$$

Since $u$ is odd and $2^{\alpha-1} u$ is even (for $\alpha \geq 2$), the factor $1 + 2^{\alpha-1} u$ is odd. Hence $u(1 + 2^{\alpha-1} u)$ is odd, and we have

$$5^{2^{(\alpha+1)-2}} = 1 + 2^{\alpha+1} \cdot (\text{odd integer}).$$

Thus the statement holds for $\alpha+1$. By induction, the statement is valid for all $\alpha \geq 3$.

**Consequences:** The residue class of $5$ has order exactly $2^{\alpha-2}$ modulo $2^\alpha$:
- $5^{2^{\alpha-2}} \equiv 1 \pmod{2^\alpha}$ (proved above).
- $5^{2^{\alpha-3}} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{2^\alpha}$ (since otherwise $5^{2^{\alpha-3}} = 1 + 2^\alpha v$ would imply $5^{2^{\alpha-2}} = 1 + 2^{\alpha+1} v + 2^{2\alpha} v^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{2^{\alpha+1}}$, contradicting the induction hypothesis that the unit is odd).

The intersection of the subgroup generated by $-1$ (order $2$) and the subgroup generated by $5$ (order $2^{\alpha-2}$) is trivial, because $-1 \equiv 2^\alpha - 1 \pmod{2^\alpha}$ is not a power of $5$ modulo $2^\alpha$ for $\alpha \geq 3$.

Therefore $\Re(2^\alpha)$ is the direct product of a cyclic group of order $2$ and a cyclic group of order $2^{\alpha-2}$, giving total order $2 \cdot 2^{\alpha-2} = 2^{\alpha-1} = \varphi(2^\alpha)$. This completes the proof. $\square$

**Remark on non-cyclicity of composite moduli:** For composite $n$, the group $\Re(n)$ is not cyclic in general. If $p$ is a divisor of $\varphi(n)$, then by Theorem 42 the basis number $e(p)$ of $\Re(n)$ is the sum of the basis numbers $e_i(p)$ in each $\Re(p_i^{\alpha_i})$. For odd $p_i$, $2 \mid \varphi(p_i^{\alpha_i})$, so $e_i(2) = 1$. Thus if two distinct odd primes divide $n$, then $e(2) \geq 2$ for $\Re(n)$, so the group is not cyclic.
