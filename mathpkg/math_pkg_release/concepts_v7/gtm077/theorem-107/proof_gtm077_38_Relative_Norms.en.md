# Proof of Theorem 107

**Statement.** The ideal $N_k(\mathfrak{A}) = \mathfrak{A}^{(1)} \cdot \mathfrak{A}^{(2)} \cdots \mathfrak{A}^{(m)}$ is an ideal in $k$. If $k$ is the field of rational numbers, then $N_k(\mathfrak{A}) = (N(\mathfrak{A}))$.

*Proof.* Let $\mathfrak{A} = (A_1, \ldots, A_s)$ be an integral ideal, where the $A_i$ are numbers in $K$. Form, for any variables $u_1, \ldots, u_s$, the conjugate polynomials

$$F^{(i)}(u) = A_1^{(i)}u_1 + \cdots + A_s^{(i)}u_s \quad (i = 1, \ldots, m).$$

By §28, the content (in the sense of Kronecker) of the product polynomial

$$F(u) = \prod_{i=1}^{m} F^{(i)}(u)$$

is the ideal $\mathfrak{A}^{(1)} \cdots \mathfrak{A}^{(m)} = N_k(\mathfrak{A})$. Since the coefficients of $F(u)$ are symmetric expressions in the conjugates, they belong to $k$. Therefore, by the theory of polynomial contents, $N_k(\mathfrak{A})$ is an ideal in $k$.

If $k$ is the field of rational numbers $k(1)$, then the relative conjugates coincide with the ordinary conjugates, and the relative norm ideal $N_k(\mathfrak{A})$ equals the principal ideal generated by the absolute norm $N(\mathfrak{A})$ of the ideal $\mathfrak{A}$.

Thus Theorem 88 of §29, which was originally proved only for Galois fields, is now seen to be valid for every number field, and the terminology "norm of $\mathfrak{A}$" for the number of residue classes mod $\mathfrak{A}$ receives its justification.
