# Proof of Lemma 1.2: Structure of the Augmentation Ideal

**(i)** The elements of $\mathbb{Z}G$ are finite sums $\sum_{x \in G} m(x) x$ with $m(x) \in \mathbb{Z}$. Under the augmentation $\varepsilon: \mathbb{Z}G \to \mathbb{Z}$, we have $\varepsilon\big(\sum m(x)x\big) = \sum m(x)$. Hence $\sum m(x)x \in IG = \ker \varepsilon$ if and only if $\sum m(x) = 0$. We may therefore rewrite

$$\sum_{x \in G} m(x) x = \sum_{x \neq 1} m(x) x + m(1) \cdot 1 = \sum_{x \neq 1} m(x) x - \Big(\sum_{x \neq 1} m(x)\Big) \cdot 1 = \sum_{x \neq 1} m(x)(x - 1).$$

Thus every element of $IG$ is a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combination of the elements $x-1$, $x \neq 1$. To see that $W = \{x-1 \mid 1 \neq x \in G\}$ is a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis, suppose $\sum_{x \neq 1} n_x (x-1) = 0$ in $\mathbb{Z}G$. Then in the free abelian group on $G$,

$$\sum_{x \neq 1} n_x x - \Big(\sum_{x \neq 1} n_x\Big) \cdot 1 = 0.$$

Since the elements of $G$ form a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $\mathbb{Z}G$, the coefficient of each $x \neq 1$ must be zero, i.e. $n_x = 0$ for all $x \neq 1$. Hence $W$ is linearly independent, and therefore a basis of $IG$ as an abelian group.

**(ii)** Let $S$ be a generating set for $G$. Every $x \in G$ can be written as a word in the elements of $S$. We prove by induction on the length of such a word that $x-1$ lies in the $G$-submodule generated by $\{s-1 \mid s \in S\}$. For the identity, $1-1 = 0$ is trivially in the submodule. For $s \in S$, $s-1$ is a generator. Now observe the identity

$$xy - 1 = x(y - 1) + (x - 1),$$

valid for all $x, y \in G$. By induction, if $y-1$ is expressed in terms of $\{s-1\}$, then $x(y-1)$ is also in the submodule (since the submodule is $G$-invariant), and together with $x-1$ we obtain $xy-1$. A similar formula holds for inverses:

$$x^{-1} - 1 = -x^{-1}(x - 1).$$

Thus by induction on word length, $x-1$ belongs to the $G$-submodule generated by $\{s-1 : s \in S\}$ for every $x \in G$. Since $IG$ is additively generated by $\{x-1 : x \neq 1\}$ by part (i), the $G$-module $IG$ is generated by $S-1 = \{s-1 \mid s \in S\}$.
