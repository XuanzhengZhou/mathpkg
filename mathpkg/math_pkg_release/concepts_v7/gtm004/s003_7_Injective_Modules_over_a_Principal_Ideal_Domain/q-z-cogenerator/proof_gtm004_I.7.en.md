# Proof of Injective Cogenerator $\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$ for Abelian Groups

**Proposition.** The abelian group $\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$ is injective and has the property that for any abelian group $G \neq 0$ there exists a nonzero homomorphism $\psi: G \to \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$. Consequently, every abelian group $A$ admits a monomorphism

$$\beta: A \to \prod_{a \in A} (\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z})_a$$

into a product of copies of $\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$.

---

## Proof

### Step 1: $\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$ is injective

By Theorem 7.1, over the PID $\mathbb{Z}$, a module is injective iff it is divisible. The group $\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$ is divisible: for any coset $x + \mathbb{Z} \in \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$ and any non-zero integer $n$, the rational $x/n$ yields $n \cdot (x/n + \mathbb{Z}) = x + \mathbb{Z}$. Hence $\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$ is an injective $\mathbb{Z}$-module.

### Step 2: Existence of non-zero maps into $\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$

Let $A \neq 0$ be an abelian group. For each non-zero element $a \in A$, we construct a homomorphism $\beta_a: A \to \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$ such that $\beta_a(a) \neq 0$.

Let $(a)$ denote the cyclic subgroup generated by $a$. Define a map $\alpha_a: (a) \to \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$ as follows:

- If $a$ has **infinite order**, choose any non-zero element of $\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$, say $\alpha_a(a) = \frac{1}{2} + \mathbb{Z}$, and extend by $\alpha_a(na) = n \cdot \alpha_a(a)$.

- If $a$ has **finite order** $n$, choose $\alpha_a(a) = \frac{1}{n} + \mathbb{Z}$ (which has order $n$ in $\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$), and extend by $\alpha_a(ka) = k \cdot \alpha_a(a)$. This is well-defined since $k \equiv \ell \pmod{n}$ implies $k \cdot \frac{1}{n} - \ell \cdot \frac{1}{n} \in \mathbb{Z}$.

In both cases $\alpha_a$ is a well-defined homomorphism on the subgroup $(a)$ and $\alpha_a(a) \neq 0$.

Since $\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$ is injective, the homomorphism $\alpha_a: (a) \to \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$ extends to a homomorphism $\beta_a: A \to \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$ on the whole group:

\[
\begin{CD}
(a) @>{\iota}>> A \\
@V{\alpha_a}VV @VV{\beta_a}V \\
\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z} @= \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}
\end{CD}
\]

By construction, $\beta_a(a) = \alpha_a(a) \neq 0$ whenever $a \neq 0$.

### Step 3: Universally embedding $A$ into a product

By the universal property of the direct product, the family of homomorphisms $\{\beta_a: A \to \mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}\}_{a \in A}$ induces a unique homomorphism

$$\beta: A \to \prod_{a \in A} (\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z})_a, \qquad \beta(x) = (\beta_a(x))_{a \in A}.$$

**$\beta$ is a monomorphism.** If $\beta(x) = 0$, then $\beta_a(x) = 0$ for all $a \in A$. Taking $a = x$, we have $\beta_x(x) \neq 0$ whenever $x \neq 0$, a contradiction. Hence $\ker\beta = \{0\}$ and $\beta$ is injective.

Thus every abelian group embeds into a product of copies of the injective cogenerator $\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$. $\square$
