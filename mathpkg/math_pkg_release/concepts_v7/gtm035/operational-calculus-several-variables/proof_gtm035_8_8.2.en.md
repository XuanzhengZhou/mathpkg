# Proof of Operational Calculus in Several Variables

**Theorem 8.2.** Let $\mathfrak{A}$ be a commutative Banach algebra and $x_1, \ldots, x_n \in \mathfrak{A}$. Let $\sigma = \sigma(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ be the joint spectrum. Let $\Omega \subset \mathbb{C}^n$ be an open set containing $\sigma$, and let $H(\Omega)$ be the algebra of functions holomorphic on $\Omega$. Then there exists a continuous homomorphism $\Phi : H(\Omega) \to \mathfrak{A}$ such that for each $F \in H(\Omega)$ and each $M \in \mathcal{M}$,

$$\widehat{\Phi(F)}(M) = F(\hat{x}_1(M), \ldots, \hat{x}_n(M)).$$

In other words, we can define $F(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \in \mathfrak{A}$ for every $F \in H(\Omega)$ such that the Gelfand transform of $F(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ is $F(\hat{x}_1, \ldots, \hat{x}_n)$.

**Proof.** We proceed in two steps. First we prove the theorem under the additional assumption that $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ generate $\mathfrak{A}$; then we reduce the general case to this one.

---

**Step 1. The case where $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ generate $\mathfrak{A}$.**

Assume that the smallest closed subalgebra of $\mathfrak{A}$ containing $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ coincides with $\mathfrak{A}$. Without loss of generality we may also assume $\|x_j\| \leq 1$ for all $j$ (otherwise replace $x_j$ by $x_j / \max_k \|x_k\|$ and rescale the domain $\Omega$ accordingly).

By Lemma 8.3, $\sigma = \sigma(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ is a polynomially convex subset of $\mathbb{C}^n$, and since $\|\hat{x}_j\|_\infty \leq \|x_j\| \leq 1$, we have $\sigma \subset \overline{\Delta}^n$ (the closed unit polydisk).

Since $\sigma$ is polynomially convex and contained in $\Delta^n$, and $\Omega$ is an open neighborhood of $\sigma$, Lemma 7.4 provides a $p$-polyhedron $\Pi = P^n(p_1, \ldots, p_r)$ with $\sigma \subset \Pi \subset \Omega$, where

$$P^n(p_1, \ldots, p_r) = \{z \in \Delta^n \mid |p_k(z)| \leq 1,\; k = 1, \ldots, r\}$$

and each $p_k$ is a polynomial in $z_1, \ldots, z_n$.

Fix $\phi \in H(\Omega)$. By the Oka extension theorem (Theorem 7.5), there exists a function $\Phi$ holomorphic in a neighborhood of $\Delta^{n+r}$ such that

$$\Phi(z_1, \ldots, z_n, p_1(z), \ldots, p_r(z)) = \phi(z) \quad \text{for all } z \in \Pi.$$

Expand $\Phi$ in a Taylor series around the origin in $\mathbb{C}^{n+r}$:

$$\Phi(\zeta_1, \ldots, \zeta_{n+r}) = \sum_{\nu} C_\nu \zeta_1^{\nu_1} \cdots \zeta_{n+r}^{\nu_{n+r}},$$

which converges uniformly on $\Delta^{n+r}$.

Now set

$$y_1 = x_1, \ldots, y_n = x_n, \quad y_{n+1} = p_1(x_1, \ldots, x_n), \ldots, y_{n+r} = p_r(x_1, \ldots, x_n).$$

One verifies that $\max_{\mathcal{M}} |\hat{y}_j| \leq 1$ for $j = 1, \ldots, n+r$ (this follows from $\sigma \subset \Pi \subset \Delta^n$ and $|p_k| \leq 1$ on $\Pi$). By Exercise 8.1 (which asserts that if a power series converges on $\Delta^k$, then substituting elements of norm at most $1$ yields a convergent series in the Banach algebra), the series

$$y = \sum_{\nu} C_\nu x_1^{\nu_1} \cdots x_n^{\nu_n} (p_1(x))^{\nu_{n+1}} \cdots (p_r(x))^{\nu_{n+r}}$$

converges in $\mathfrak{A}$. Taking the Gelfand transform and using the continuity of the homomorphism $\hat{\cdot} : \mathfrak{A} \to C(\mathcal{M})$, we obtain for every $M \in \mathcal{M}$:

$$\hat{y}(M) = \Phi(\hat{x}_1(M), \ldots, \hat{x}_n(M), p_1(\hat{x}(M)), \ldots, p_r(\hat{x}(M))).$$

Since $(\hat{x}_1(M), \ldots, \hat{x}_n(M)) \in \sigma \subset \Pi$, the defining property of $\Phi$ yields

$$\hat{y}(M) = \phi(\hat{x}_1(M), \ldots, \hat{x}_n(M)).$$

Thus we may set $\phi(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = y \in \mathfrak{A}$, and the map $\phi \mapsto \phi(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ is the desired continuous homomorphism (continuity follows because the Taylor coefficients are determined by the topology of $H(\Omega)$, an $F$-space, and by the closed graph theorem).

---

**Step 2. Reduction of the general case to the finitely generated case.**

Let $\mathfrak{A}_0$ denote the closed subalgebra generated by $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ and set $\sigma_0 = \sigma_{\mathfrak{A}_0}(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$. If $\sigma_0 \subset \Omega$, then we are done by Step 1 applied to $\mathfrak{A}_0$. Otherwise, consider $\zeta \in \sigma_0 \setminus \Omega$.

Since $\zeta \notin \sigma = \sigma_{\mathfrak{A}}(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$, Lemma 8.1 implies the existence of $y_1^{(\zeta)}, \ldots, y_n^{(\zeta)} \in \mathfrak{A}$ such that

$$\sum_{j=1}^{n} y_j^{(\zeta)}(x_j - \zeta_j) = 1.$$

Let $\mathfrak{A}(\zeta)$ be the closed subalgebra generated by $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ together with all the $y_j^{(\zeta)}$. Because

$$\sum_{j=1}^{n} y_j^{(\zeta)}(x_j - \alpha_j)$$

is a continuous function of $\alpha$, there exists a neighborhood $\mathcal{N}_\zeta$ of $\zeta$ in $\mathbb{C}^n$ such that for all $\alpha \in \mathcal{N}_\zeta$, the element $\sum_j y_j^{(\zeta)}(x_j - \alpha_j)$ remains invertible in $\mathfrak{A}(\zeta)$. Consequently, $\alpha \notin \sigma_{\mathfrak{A}(\zeta)}(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ for all $\alpha \in \mathcal{N}_\zeta$.

The set $\sigma_0 \setminus \Omega$ is compact (closed subset of the compact set $\sigma_0$), so there exist finitely many points $\zeta_1, \ldots, \zeta_m$ whose neighborhoods $\mathcal{N}_{\zeta_k}$ cover $\sigma_0 \setminus \Omega$. Collect all the corresponding $y_j^{(\zeta_k)}$ and call them $C_1, \ldots, C_M$. Let $B$ be the closed subalgebra generated by $x_1, \ldots, x_n, C_1, \ldots, C_M$.

By construction, $\sigma_B(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \subset \sigma_0$ (enlarging the algebra can only shrink the spectrum), and every point of $\sigma_0 \setminus \Omega$ is excluded from $\sigma_B(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$. Hence $\sigma_B(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \subset \Omega$.

Now $B$ is generated (as a closed subalgebra) by finitely many elements (namely $x_1, \ldots, x_n, C_1, \ldots, C_M$), so it is finitely generated. Applying Step 1 to the algebra $B$ with $\sigma_B \subset \Omega$ yields the required element $\phi(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \in B \subset \mathfrak{A}$.

Finally, the map $\phi \mapsto \phi(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ is continuous from $H(\Omega)$ to $\mathfrak{A}$: $H(\Omega)$ is an $F$-space, and the closed graph theorem (see Dunford–Schwartz, Chap. II) applied to the evaluation map yields the continuity. $\square$

**Corollary.** If $\mathfrak{A}$ is semisimple, then $F_j \to F$ in $H(\Omega)$ implies $F_j(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \to F(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ in $\mathfrak{A}$.
