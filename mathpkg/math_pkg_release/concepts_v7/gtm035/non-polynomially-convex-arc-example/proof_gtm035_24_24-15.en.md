# Proof of Example of Non-Polynomially Convex Arc in $\mathbb{C}^3$

**Theorem.** There exists an arc $J_0$ in $\mathbb{C}^3$ that is not polynomially convex. Hence $P(J_0) \neq C(J_0)$.

**Construction.** Let $\gamma$ be an arc in the complex plane having positive planar (2-dimensional Lebesgue) measure. Such an arc exists: take a compact totally disconnected set $E$ on the real line having positive linear measure (e.g., a fat Cantor set); then $E \times E \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ is a compact totally disconnected set of positive planar measure. By a theorem of F. Riesz, every compact totally disconnected planar set can be contained in an arc; let $\gamma$ be such an arc.

Let $\mathfrak{A}_\gamma$ be the algebra of continuous functions on the Riemann sphere $S^2$ that are analytic on $S^2 \setminus \gamma$. Since $\gamma$ has positive area, Lemma 24.14 provides three functions $F, F_2, F_3 \in \mathfrak{A}_\gamma$ that separate the points of $S^2$. Explicitly, one constructs $F$ via the Cauchy integral of an $L^1$ function supported on $\gamma$:

$$F(\zeta) = \frac{1}{\pi} \int_\gamma \frac{g(w)}{w - \zeta} \, dA(w),$$

where $g \in L^1(\gamma)$ is chosen so that $F$ is not constant. Then define

$$F_2(z) = \frac{F(z) - F(a)}{z - a}, \quad F_3(z) = \frac{F(z) - F(b)}{z - b}$$

for fixed $a, b \in S^2 \setminus \gamma$ with $F(a) \neq F(b)$. One checks that for any distinct $z_1, z_2 \in S^2$, if $F(z_1) = F(z_2)$, then either $F_2$ or $F_3$ separates them. Hence $\{F, F_2, F_3\}$ separates points.

Define the arc $J_0 \subseteq \mathbb{C}^3$ as the image of $\gamma$ under the map

$$\zeta \mapsto (F(\zeta), F_2(\zeta), F_3(\zeta)), \quad \zeta \in \gamma.$$

Since each of $F, F_2, F_3$ is continuous on $S^2$, the image $J_0$ is indeed an arc.

**Proof that $J_0$ is not polynomially convex.** Fix $\zeta_0 \in S^2 \setminus \gamma$ and set

$$x^0 = (F(\zeta_0), F_2(\zeta_0), F_3(\zeta_0)) \in \mathbb{C}^3.$$

Clearly $x^0 \notin J_0$ since $\zeta_0 \notin \gamma$. We claim that $x^0 \in \widehat{J_0}$, the polynomial hull of $J_0$.

Let $P(z_1, z_2, z_3)$ be any polynomial in three variables. Consider the function

$$f(\zeta) = P(F(\zeta), F_2(\zeta), F_3(\zeta)), \quad \zeta \in S^2.$$

Since $F, F_2, F_3 \in \mathfrak{A}_\gamma$ and $\mathfrak{A}_\gamma$ is an algebra, $f \in \mathfrak{A}_\gamma$. In particular, $f$ is continuous on $S^2$ and analytic on $S^2 \setminus \gamma$. By the maximum modulus principle applied to $S^2$ (which is compact without boundary in the sense of Riemann surfaces), $f$ attains its maximum modulus on the boundary of its domain of analyticity, namely on $\gamma$:

$$|f(\zeta_0)| \leq \max_{\zeta \in \gamma} |f(\zeta)|.$$

But this translates directly to

$$|P(x^0)| \leq \max_{x \in J_0} |P(x)|.$$

Since this holds for every polynomial $P$, it follows that $x^0$ belongs to the polynomial hull $\widehat{J_0}$. As $x^0 \notin J_0$, the set $J_0$ is not polynomially convex.

Consequently, there exist continuous functions on $J_0$ that cannot be uniformly approximated by polynomials; i.e., $P(J_0) \neq C(J_0)$. $\square$

**Remark.** This example is striking because it contrasts with Theorem 12.4, which states that every *smooth* arc in $\mathbb{C}^n$ is polynomially convex. The arc $\gamma$ constructed above is highly non-smooth (it has positive area), which allows the existence of non-constant analytic functions on its complement on the Riemann sphere. The three functions $F, F_2, F_3$ serve as "coordinates" that embed the arc in $\mathbb{C}^3$ in such a way that the maximum principle forces additional points into the polynomial hull.
