# Proof of Characterization of Joint Spectrum via Invertibility

**Lemma 8.1.** Let $\mathfrak{A}$ be a commutative Banach algebra with maximal ideal space $\mathcal{M}$, and let $x_1, \ldots, x_n \in \mathfrak{A}$. Then $(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n) \in \mathbb{C}^n$ lies in $\sigma(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ if and only if the equation

$$\sum_{j=1}^{n} y_j(x_j - \lambda_j) = 1$$

has no solution $y_1, \ldots, y_n \in \mathfrak{A}$.

**Proof.** Recall that by definition, $\sigma(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = \{(\hat{x}_1(M), \ldots, \hat{x}_n(M)) \mid M \in \mathcal{M}\}$.

$(\Rightarrow)$ Suppose $(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n) \in \sigma(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$. Then there exists a maximal ideal $M \in \mathcal{M}$ such that $\hat{x}_j(M) = \lambda_j$ for all $j = 1, \ldots, n$. If there existed $y_1, \ldots, y_n \in \mathfrak{A}$ satisfying $\sum_{j=1}^{n} y_j(x_j - \lambda_j) = 1$, then applying the Gelfand transform (which is a homomorphism) and evaluating at $M$ would give

$$\sum_{j=1}^{n} \hat{y}_j(M)(\hat{x}_j(M) - \lambda_j) = \hat{1}(M) = 1.$$

But $\hat{x}_j(M) - \lambda_j = 0$ for each $j$, so the left-hand side is $0$, contradicting $0 = 1$. Hence no such $y_1, \ldots, y_n$ exist.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Conversely, suppose $(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n) \notin \sigma(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$. Then for every maximal ideal $M \in \mathcal{M}$, the $n$-tuple $(\hat{x}_1(M), \ldots, \hat{x}_n(M))$ differs from $(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)$. This means that the ideal $I$ generated by $\{x_1 - \lambda_1, \ldots, x_n - \lambda_n\}$ is not contained in any maximal ideal of $\mathfrak{A}$. Indeed, if $I \subseteq M$ for some $M \in \mathcal{M}$, then $x_j - \lambda_j \in M$, implying $\hat{x}_j(M) = \lambda_j$ for all $j$, contradicting the assumption.

Since $I$ is not contained in any maximal ideal, and every proper ideal of a commutative Banach algebra with identity is contained in some maximal ideal, $I$ must be the whole algebra $\mathfrak{A}$. Hence $1 \in I$, which means there exist $y_1, \ldots, y_n \in \mathfrak{A}$ such that

$$\sum_{j=1}^{n} y_j(x_j - \lambda_j) = 1.$$

This completes the proof. $\square$

**Remark.** When $n = 1$, this recovers the well-known characterization: $\lambda \in \sigma(x)$ if and only if $x - \lambda 1$ is not invertible, since invertibility of $x - \lambda 1$ is equivalent to the existence of $y$ with $y(x - \lambda 1) = 1$.
