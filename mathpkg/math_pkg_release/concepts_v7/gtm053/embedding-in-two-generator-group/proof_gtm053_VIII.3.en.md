# Proof: Embedding in Groups with Two Generators

**Theorem 3.1.** Any countable or finite group $G$ can be embedded in a group with two generators.

*Proof.* The proof uses the free product construction and the fact that free groups of rank $\geq 2$ contain free subgroups of countably infinite rank.

**Step 1: Reduction to the free group $F_2$.** Let $F_2 = \langle a, b \rangle$ be the free group on two generators. It is a classical fact that $F_2$ contains a free subgroup of countably infinite rank. For instance, the elements
$$g_n = a^n b a^{-n} \quad (n \in \mathbb{Z})$$
freely generate a free subgroup $F_\infty \leq F_2$ of countably infinite rank.

**Step 2: Embedding an arbitrary countable group.** Let $G$ be a countable group. Then $G$ can be presented as a quotient of a free group: there exists a normal subgroup $N \trianglelefteq F_\infty$ (where $F_\infty$ is the free group on countably many generators) such that $G \cong F_\infty / N$.

Since $F_\infty$ embeds in $F_2$ (via the identification $g_n \mapsto a^n b a^{-n}$), we have $F_\infty \leq F_2$. Let $\bar{N}$ be the normal closure of $N$ in $F_2$ (i.e., the smallest normal subgroup of $F_2$ containing $N$). Then
$$G \cong F_\infty / N = F_\infty / (F_\infty \cap \bar{N}) \hookrightarrow F_2 / \bar{N}.$$

Thus $G$ embeds in the group $H = F_2 / \bar{N}$, which is generated by the two elements $a \bar{N}$ and $b \bar{N}$ (the images of the generators of $F_2$). Hence $G$ embeds in a two-generator group.

**Step 3: Finite groups.** A finite group is a special case of a countable group, so the same construction applies. Alternatively, one can give a direct embedding: if $|G| = n$, embed $G$ into the symmetric group $S_n$, and note that $S_n$ can be generated by two elements (e.g., the cyclic permutation $(1\ 2\ \cdots\ n)$ and the transposition $(1\ 2)$). Thus $G$ embeds in a two-generator group.

**Remark.** This theorem is a key ingredient in the proof of Higman's Embedding Theorem (VIII.1.5). The fact that any countable group embeds in a two-generator group allows one to focus on subgroups of $F_2$ when studying recursively presented groups, since $F_2$ itself is finitely presented and plays a central role in the embedding construction. $\square$
