Theorem 16.8 establishes a fundamental bridge between "real" mathematical objects and their internal Boolean-valued counterparts. Given definite sets $u, v \in V^{(\mathbf{B})}$ and an extensional function $\varphi: \mathcal{D}(u) \to \mathcal{D}(v)$, one can construct an internal function $f \in V^{(\mathbf{B})}$ such that $\llbracket f: u \to v \rrbracket = 1$ and $\llbracket f(x) = \varphi(x) \rrbracket = 1$ for every $x \in \mathcal{D}(u)$. The construction defines $f$ internally as the set of ordered pairs $\langle x, \varphi(x) \rangle$, and the extensionality of $\varphi$ guarantees that $f$ is single-valued in the Boolean-valued sense. Theorem 16.28 provides the converse.
