This theorem establishes that the kernel of any Boolean homomorphism is always a proper ideal (it contains $0$, is closed under addition, and is downward closed, but does not contain $1$). Moreover, if the kernel is trivial (only $\{0\}$), then the homomorphism is injective and thus an isomorphism onto its image. This is the Boolean-algebraic analog of the first isomorphism theorem.
