When the underlying partial order comes from a Boolean algebra (with $0$ removed), the associated ultrafilter space is Hausdorff. This is because distinct ultrafilters $F_1$ and $F_2$ on a Boolean algebra can be separated: there exist $p_1 \in F_1$ and $p_2 \in F_2$ with $p_1 p_2 = 0$, yielding disjoint basic open neighborhoods. In the general case of an arbitrary partial order, the space is only guaranteed to be $T_1$. Together with compactness properties, this yields the Stone representation theorem for Boolean algebras.
