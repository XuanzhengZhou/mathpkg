We use induction on the index $(G:H)$ of $H$ in $G$. If $(G:H) = 1$, then $H = G$ and there is nothing to prove.

Otherwise, let $x$ be an element of $G$ not contained in $H$, and let $n$ be the smallest integer $> 1$ such that $x^n \in H$. Let $\chi$ be a character of $H$. We wish to extend $\chi$ to the subgroup $H'$ generated by $H$ and $x$.

Since $x^n \in H$, the value $\chi(x^n)$ is already determined. Choose an $n$th root of $\chi(x^n)$ in $\mathbb{C}^*$ (which is always possible since $\mathbb{C}^*$ is divisible) and define $\chi'(x)$ to be this root. For any element $h x^a \in H'$ with $h \in H$ and $0 \leq a < n$, define $\chi'(h x^a) = \chi(h) \chi'(x)^a$.

One checks that $\chi'$ is well-defined and is a homomorphism $H' \to \mathbb{C}^*$, hence a character of $H'$ extending $\chi$. Since $(G:H') < (G:H)$, the induction hypothesis gives an extension of $\chi'$ (and hence $\chi$) to all of $G$.

The remark about the kernel and exact sequence follows from the isomorphism theorem: $\widehat{G}/\ker(\rho) \cong \operatorname{im}(\rho) = \widehat{H}$, and $\ker(\rho) = \{\chi \in \widehat{G} : \chi|_H = 1\} \cong \widehat{G/H}$.
