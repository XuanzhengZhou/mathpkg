For any finite field $\mathbf{F}_q$ with $q = p^f$ elements, its multiplicative group $\mathbf{F}_q^*$ (the set of nonzero elements under multiplication) is always a cyclic group of order $q-1$. This fundamental fact implies the existence of primitive elements in every finite field and underpins many results in number theory, including power sum evaluations (Section 2.1) and the theory of quadratic residues. The proof combines the Euler totient divisor sum identity with the fact that a polynomial equation $x^d = 1$ has at most $d$ roots in a field.
