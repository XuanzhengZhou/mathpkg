The Hasse–Minkowski theorem is the fundamental local-global principle for quadratic forms. It states that a rational quadratic form has a nontrivial zero over $\mathbf{Q}$ precisely when it has a nontrivial zero over every completion $\mathbf{Q}_v$. The proof proceeds by induction on the rank $n$, with the cases $n=1$ (trivial), $n=2$ (norm form), $n=3$ (Legendre's theorem), $n=4$, and $n \geq 5$ treated separately. This result does not extend to homogeneous polynomials of degree $\geq 3$, as shown by Selmer's counterexample $3X^3+4Y^3+5Z^3=0$.
