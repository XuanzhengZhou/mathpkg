It is clear that (ii) $\Rightarrow$ (i). Conversely, if $g$ represents $a$, the quadratic module $V$ corresponding to $g$ contains an element $x$ such that $x \cdot x = a$; if $H$ denotes the orthogonal complement of $x$, we have $V = H \hat{\oplus} kx$, hence $g \sim h + aZ^2$ where $h$ denotes the quadratic form attached to a basis of $H$. This proves (i) $\Rightarrow$ (ii).

The equivalence (ii) $\Leftrightarrow$ (iii) follows from the fact that $f = g - aZ^2$ represents $0$ precisely when there is a nonzero vector $(y, z)$ with $g(y) - az^2 = 0$, i.e., $g(y/z) = a$ (when $z \neq 0$), which is equivalent to (i).
