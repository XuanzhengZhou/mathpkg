This is a translation of Proposition 3 and its corollary (from the module-theoretic treatment in §1.3). If $f$ represents $0$, the corresponding quadratic module $V$ contains a nonzero isotropic element $x$. Since $f$ is nondegenerate, there exists $y \in V$ such that $x \cdot y \neq 0$. The subspace spanned by $x$ and $y$ is then a hyperbolic plane, and $V$ decomposes as the orthogonal direct sum of this hyperbolic plane and its orthogonal complement. Translating back to forms yields $f \sim f_2 + g$ where $f_2$ is hyperbolic.
