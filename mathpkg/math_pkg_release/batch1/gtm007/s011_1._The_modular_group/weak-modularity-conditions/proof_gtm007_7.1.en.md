For $g = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \in G$, the derivative is
$$\frac{d(gz)}{dz} = (cz + d)^{-2}.$$
The weak modularity condition $f(gz) = (cz + d)^{2k} f(z)$ can be written as
$$\frac{f(gz)}{f(z)} = \left( \frac{d(gz)}{dz} \right)^{-k},$$
or equivalently $f(gz) d(gz)^k = f(z) dz^k$, meaning the differential form of weight $k$ is $G$-invariant.

Since $G$ is generated by $S$ and $T$ (Theorem 2), it suffices to verify invariance under these two transformations. For $T(z) = z + 1$, we have $d(Tz)/dz = 1$, giving $f(z+1) = f(z)$. For $S(z) = -1/z$, we have $d(Sz)/dz = z^{-2}$, giving $f(-1/z) = z^{2k} f(z)$. Conversely, if both relations hold, then $f$ is invariant under the subgroup generated by $S$ and $T$, which is all of $G$.
