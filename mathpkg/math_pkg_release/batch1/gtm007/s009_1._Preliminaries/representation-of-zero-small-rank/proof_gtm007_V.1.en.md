\textbf{(i) $n=2$:} The signature is $(1,1)$, so $d(E) = -1$. In $\mathbf{Q}$, $-d(E) = 1$ is a square, so $f$ is isotropic.

\textbf{(ii) $n=3$:} For $p \neq 2$, the reduced form mod $p$ has a nontrivial zero (Chapter I, §2), which lifts to a $p$-adic zero (Chapter II, §2.2). For $p = 2$, the same holds. At $\infty$, the form is indefinite. By Corollary 3 to Theorem 8 (Chapter IV), $f$ represents $0$ in $\mathbf{Q}$.

\textbf{(iii) $n=4$:} The same local argument gives representation in all $\mathbf{Q}_p$ for $p \neq 2$ and in $\mathbf{R}$. If $d(E) = 1$, the corollary to Theorem 8 gives representation in $\mathbf{Q}$. If $d(E) = -1$, then $-d(E) = 1$ is a square in $\mathbf{Q}_2^*/\mathbf{Q}_2^{*2}$, so by Theorem 6 of Chapter IV, §2.2, $f$ represents $0$ in $\mathbf{Q}_2$ as well. Hasse-Minkowski then gives representation in $\mathbf{Q}$.

\textbf{(iv) $n \geq 5$:} This follows from the Hasse-Minkowski theorem and the fact that every quadratic form in at least 5 variables over $\mathbf{Q}_p$ represents $0$ (Chapter IV, Theorem 6).
