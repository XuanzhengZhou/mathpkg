If $\mathcal{S}$ is a set then a \textbf{permutation} of $\mathcal{S}$ is a one-to-one mapping of $\mathcal{S}$ onto itself. The set of all these mappings forms a group under the usual composition of mappings: if $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are permutations of $\mathcal{S}$ then $\alpha\beta$ is the permutation defined by $x^{\alpha\beta} = (x^\alpha)^\beta$. This group is the \textbf{symmetric group} on $\mathcal{S}$ and clearly depends only on the cardinality of $\mathcal{S}$.
