We first make the trivial observation that, since $\alpha$ preserves incidence, for any pair of points $A$ and $B$, $A$ is on $B^\alpha$ if and only if $B$ is on $A^\alpha$.

Since the two claims of the lemma are self-dual we need only prove one of them. If $m$ is any absolute line of $\alpha$ then, by definition, $m^\alpha$ is on $m$ and so $m$ contains at least one absolute point. Put $A = m^\alpha$ and suppose there is a second absolute point $B$ on $m$. Since $B$ is on $A^\alpha$, $A$ must be on $B^\alpha$ and thus, since $B$ is also on $B^\alpha$ (by the assumption that $B$ is absolute), $B^\alpha = AB = m$. But this implies $m^\alpha = B$, contradicting $m^\alpha = A$ and $A \neq B$. Hence $m$ contains exactly one absolute point, namely $A = m^\alpha$.
