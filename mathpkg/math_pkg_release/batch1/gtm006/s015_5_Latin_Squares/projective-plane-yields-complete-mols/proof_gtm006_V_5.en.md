**Proof.**

If $\mathcal{P}$ is a finite projective plane, then $\mathcal{P}$ may be coordinatized by a planar ternary ring $(R, T)$ (by Theorem 5.4). Let $R = \{0, 1, \dots, n-1\}$. For each $x \in R$ with $x \neq 0$, define the $n \times n$ matrix $\{x\}$ by setting the $(i, j)$-th entry to $T(x, i, j)$. By Lemma 5.7, each $\{x\}$ is a Latin square and the squares $\{x\}$ and $\{y\}$ are orthogonal whenever $x \neq y$. Since there are $n-1$ nonzero elements in $R$, this construction yields $n-1$ pairwise orthogonal $n \times n$ Latin squares, which is precisely a complete set of mutually orthogonal Latin squares.
