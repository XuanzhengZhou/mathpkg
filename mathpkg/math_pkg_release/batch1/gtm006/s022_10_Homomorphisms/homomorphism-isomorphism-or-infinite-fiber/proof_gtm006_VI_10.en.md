Let $X_2, Y_2, O_2, I_2$ be a coordinatizing quadrangle for $\mathcal{P}_2$ (see Chapter V), and $(R_2, T_2)$ a PTR which corresponds. Choose $X_1, Y_1, O_1, I_1$ in $\mathcal{P}_1$ so that $X_1^\phi = X_2$, $Y_1^\phi = Y_2$, $O_1^\phi = O_2$, $I_1^\phi = I_2$, and let $(R_1, T_1)$ be a PTR for $\mathcal{P}_1$ corresponding to this quadrangle choice. We shall use subscripts to distinguish coordinates in the two planes; e.g., $(x, y)_1$ is a point of $\mathcal{P}_1$, while $(u, v)_2$ is a point of $\mathcal{P}_2$.

Let $Z$ be the set of all $x$ in $R_1$ such that $(x, 0)_1^\phi = (0, 0)_2$. It is clear that $(1)_1^\phi = (1)_2$, and it follows from Theorem 5.5 that $Z$ is an ideal in $R_1$. Moreover, $(x, y)_1^\phi = (u, v)_2$ and $(x', y')_1^\phi = (u, v)_2$ imply that $x - x'$ and $y - y'$ are in $Z$, and conversely. Thus the pre-image of any point of $\mathcal{P}_2$ has cardinality $|Z|^2$. Similarly, the pre-image of any line of $\mathcal{P}_2$ has cardinality $|Z|$. If $Z = \{0\}$, then $\phi$ is an isomorphism. If $Z$ contains a nonzero element $w$, then it contains all elements of the form $nw$ for integers $n$, so $Z$ is infinite. Furthermore, $J$ (the set of elements of $R_1$ having an inverse) contains every element $w$ where $wz = 1$ for some $z$ in $Z$. So if the pre-image of one point (that is, $(0, 0)_2$) is infinite, then the pre-image of every point is infinite.
