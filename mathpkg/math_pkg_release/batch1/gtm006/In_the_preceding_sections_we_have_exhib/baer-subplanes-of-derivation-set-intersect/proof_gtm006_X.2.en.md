Suppose $X$ and $Y$ are two distinct affine points belonging to both $\mathcal{A}_i$ and $\mathcal{A}_j$, with $i \neq j$. Then $X$ and $Y$ lie in both Baer subplanes $\mathcal{B}_i$ and $\mathcal{B}_j$ of $\mathcal{P}$. Moreover, the line $XY$ in $\mathcal{P}$ meets $\ell_{\infty}$ at a point in $\mathcal{S}$, since both $\mathcal{B}_i$ and $\mathcal{B}_j$ contain $\mathcal{S}$. But two distinct Baer subplanes of a projective plane can share at most a line and its points, and since $\mathcal{B}_i$ and $\mathcal{B}_j$ both contain $\mathcal{S}$ (which has $n+1$ points on $\ell_{\infty}$), they cannot share two additional affine points without being identical. This contradicts $i \neq j$. Hence $\mathcal{A}_i \cap \mathcal{A}_j$ contains at most one point.
