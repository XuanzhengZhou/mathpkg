A configuration is confined when each of its points and lines is incident with at least three other elements. This is a finiteness-type condition: every element has minimum degree three. Confined configurations necessarily contain at least seven points and seven lines, making them the smallest non-trivial configurations from the perspective of the free-closure theory. The importance of confinedness lies in its role as a key hypothesis under which free closures can be characterized and compared.
