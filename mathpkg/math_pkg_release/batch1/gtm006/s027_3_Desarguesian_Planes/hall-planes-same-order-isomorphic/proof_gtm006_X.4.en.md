From Theorem 10.16, the multiplication in the derived quasifield satisfies $a * (c + \lambda d) = ac + \lambda ad$ for appropriate choice of the defining quadratic. For a finite field $GF(q)$, every irreducible monic quadratic over $GF(q)$ has its roots in the unique quadratic extension $GF(q^2)$. By Exercise 10.7, any irreducible monic quadratic $g(x)$ over $GF(q)$ can be written in the form $g(x) = x^2 + (v/u)x - 1/u$ where $x^2 - vx - u$ is also irreducible. Since all irreducible quadratics over a finite field are equivalent (they all split in the same quadratic extension), the resulting Hall quasifields are isomorphic, and hence the corresponding Hall planes are isomorphic.

For infinite Hall planes, the statement follows from the fact that the isomorphism type of the Hall plane depends on the equivalence class of the defining irreducible quadratic; two quadratics are equivalent if and only if they have zeros in the same quadratic extension field. The converse (that inequivalent quadratics yield non-isomorphic Hall planes) is stated as likely true but unresolved. $\square$
