A planar ternary ring $(R, T)$ is called linear if the ternary operation satisfies $T(a, b, c) = ab + c$ for some binary operations $+$ and $\cdot$ on $R$. The linearity of $(R, T)$ corresponds to the projective plane being Desarguesian (or more generally, to strong algebraic properties of the coordinate ring). Theorem 6.1 provides a purely configurational characterization: $(R, T)$ is linear precisely when a certain geometric configuration involving two triangles and the points $(0)$ and $[0]$, $[\infty]$ holds. This connects the algebraic notion of linearity with a visual, incidence-geometric condition in the coordinatized plane.
