If $\mathcal{B}$ is a closed Baer subset and is a subplane, then it is a Baer subplane by definition. Suppose $\mathcal{B}$ is a closed Baer subset which is not a subplane. Then $\mathcal{B}$ cannot contain a quadrangle.

\textbf{Case (a).} $\mathcal{B}$ contains a triangle $ABC$. Since $\mathcal{B}$ does not contain a quadrangle, every other point of $\mathcal{B}$ must lie on the same side, $BC$ say, of the triangle $ABC$. Let $D$ be any other point of $\mathcal{B}$ on $BC$. Then the lines through $A$ and points of $\mathcal{B}$ on $BC$ generate the configuration described.

The argument proceeds by showing that if $\mathcal{B}$ contains a triangle but no quadrangle, it must be of the degenerate form (line + point). If $\mathcal{B}$ contains no triangle, a similar case analysis yields the same conclusion.
