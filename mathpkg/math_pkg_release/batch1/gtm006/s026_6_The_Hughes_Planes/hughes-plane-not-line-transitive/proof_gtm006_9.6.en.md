By Exercise 4.28, if a projective plane is $(V,\ell)$-transitive for some flag $(V,\ell)$ (i.e., $V \in \ell$) and also for some anti-flag $(V',\ell')$ (i.e., $V' \notin \ell'$), then the plane is desarguesian. Since Corollary 1 established that the Hughes plane $\mathcal{H}$ is non-desarguesian, it cannot be $(V,\ell)$-transitive for all possible choices of $V$ and $\ell$.

Moreover, a translation plane is by definition a projective plane that is $(V,\ell)$-transitive for some point $V$ on $\ell$ (where $\ell$ is the line at infinity in the affine restriction). Similarly, a dual translation plane is $(\ell,V)$-transitive. Since $\mathcal{H}$ admits no such transitivity for any choice of point or line, it is neither a translation plane nor a dual translation plane. This provides another structural distinction between the Hughes planes and the non-desarguesian planes constructed via quasifields earlier in the chapter, many of which are translation planes.
