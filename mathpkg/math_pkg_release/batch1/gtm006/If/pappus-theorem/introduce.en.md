Pappus's Theorem is one of the most celebrated results of classical projective geometry. Given two lines $L_1$ and $L_2$ with three distinct points on each (none being the intersection $L_1 \cap L_2$), the three intersection points $A_3 = (B_1 + C_2) \cap (B_2 + C_1)$, $B_3 = (A_1 + C_2) \cap (A_2 + C_1)$, and $C_3 = (A_1 + B_2) \cap (A_2 + B_1)$ are collinear if and only if the underlying skewfield $K$ is commutative (i.e., is a field). This makes Pappus' Theorem a geometric characterization of commutativity. The nine points and nine lines form Pappus' Configuration.
