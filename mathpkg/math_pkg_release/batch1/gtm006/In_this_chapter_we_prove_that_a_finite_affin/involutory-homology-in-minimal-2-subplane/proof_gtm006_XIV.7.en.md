Let $2^u \mid n+1$, $2^v \mid k$ (where $|\Pi_{C,l}| = k$ for any affine flag $C,l$), and $|\Gamma| = 2^w$.

Since $\Gamma$ fixes a subplane pointwise, $\Gamma$ is not a Sylow $2$-subgroup of $\Pi_{AB,m}$ for appropriate $A, B, m$. Let $\Gamma^*$ be a $2$-subgroup of $\Pi_{AB,m}$ containing $\Gamma$ as a normal subgroup of index $2$. Such a $\Gamma^*$ exists because $\Gamma$ is maximal among $2$-subgroups fixing a subplane pointwise, and the action on flags forces the existence of an additional involution.

(i) For any affine flag in $\mathcal{B}$, the subgroup $\Gamma^*$ constructed above contains an element which acts on $\mathcal{B}$ as an involutory homology fixing that flag. This follows from the structure of $\Gamma^*$ as an extension of $\Gamma$ by an involution: the non-trivial coset elements act as involutory homologies on the fixed subplane $\mathcal{Q}$.

(ii) If there exists an involutory homology $\alpha \in \Sigma$ fixing $A, B, l$, then for any other affine line $m$ through $B$, conjugating $\alpha$ by an appropriate element of the subgroup $\Sigma_{AB}$ (which acts transitively on the lines through $B$ by the Frobenius group properties) yields an involutory homology fixing $A, B, m$.

The key to both parts is that $\Gamma$ is not a Sylow $2$-subgroup of certain stabilizers, forcing the existence of $2$-subgroups of twice the order that produce the required involutory homologies via their non-central involutions. $\square$
