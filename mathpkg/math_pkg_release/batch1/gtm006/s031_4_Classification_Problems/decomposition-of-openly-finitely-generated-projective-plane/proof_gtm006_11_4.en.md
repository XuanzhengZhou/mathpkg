Suppose $\mathcal{P} = \langle \mathcal{A} \rangle_{\mathcal{G}}$ where $\mathcal{A}$ is openly finite. Let $\mathcal{K} = \mathcal{K}(\mathcal{P})$ be the core of $\mathcal{P}$, and set $\mathcal{B} = \mathcal{A} \cup \mathcal{K}$. Since $\mathcal{A}$ is openly finite, only finitely many of its elements lie outside $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{A})$, hence only finitely many lie outside $\mathcal{K} \supseteq \mathcal{K}(\mathcal{A})$. Thus $\mathcal{B}$ is openly finite and $\mathcal{P} = \langle \mathcal{B} \rangle_{\mathcal{G}}$.

If $\mathcal{P} \neq \mathcal{K}$, then there exists an element of $\mathcal{P}$ not contained in any finite confined subconfiguration. Choose a generating configuration $\mathcal{C}$ for $\mathcal{P}$ with minimal open rank among all openly finite configurations generating $\mathcal{P}$. Decompose $\mathcal{C}$ into free extension steps; the minimality of the open rank forces each step to be a genuine free extension, showing that $\mathcal{P} = \mathcal{F}(\mathcal{C})$ with $\mathcal{C} \neq \mathcal{P}$. Otherwise, if no such element exists, then $\mathcal{P} = \mathcal{K}$.
