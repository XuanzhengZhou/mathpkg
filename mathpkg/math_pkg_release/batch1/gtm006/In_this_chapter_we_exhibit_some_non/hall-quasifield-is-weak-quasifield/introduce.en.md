This theorem establishes that the Hall construction indeed yields a quasifield. The proof proceeds by solving the system of equations derived from the multiplication definition, showing that for any given $p, q$ and non-zero coefficient pair $(x + \lambda y)$ or $(z + \lambda t)$, the equation $(x + \lambda y)(z + \lambda t) = p + \lambda q$ has a unique solution for the other factor. The irreducibility of $f(s)$ over $F$ is crucial to guarantee the non-vanishing of the right-hand side at a key step. Since $H$ has finite dimension over its kernel $F$, being a weak quasifield suffices to conclude it is a quasifield (by Theorem 7.3).
