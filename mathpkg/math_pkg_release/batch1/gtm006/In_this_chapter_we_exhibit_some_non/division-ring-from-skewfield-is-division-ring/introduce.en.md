Theorem 9.7 verifies that the construction indeed produces a division ring. Since $D$ is finite-dimensional over its centre, it suffices to prove the absence of non-zero divisors. The proof involves a careful case analysis using the anti-automorphism $\theta$ and the property that $x^{1+\theta}$ lies in the centre. The irreducibility-like condition (2) plays a role analogous to the irreducibility of $f(s)$ in the Hall quasifield construction, ensuring the uniqueness of solutions to the linear equations that arise.
