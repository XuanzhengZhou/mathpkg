This is clear if we interpret the additive maps $P, Q, R$ as permutations of the points of the lines $[\infty], [0, 0], [0]$ respectively.

Specifically, an autotopism $lpha$ of $\mathcal{P}(D)$ is a collineation fixing the triangle $(\infty), (0), (0, 0)$. Such a collineation acts on the coordinate lines: it sends points $(m)$ on $[\infty]$ to $(mP)$, points $(0, y)$ on $[0, 0]$ to $(0, yR)$, and points $(x)$ on $[0]$ to $(xQ)$, where $P, Q, R$ are non-singular additive maps of $D$ onto itself satisfying $(xP)(yQ) = (xy)R$. Conversely, any such triple defines an autotopism. The correspondence $lpha \leftrightarrow (P, Q, R)$ is a bijection that respects composition in both groups, giving the required isomorphism. $\square$
