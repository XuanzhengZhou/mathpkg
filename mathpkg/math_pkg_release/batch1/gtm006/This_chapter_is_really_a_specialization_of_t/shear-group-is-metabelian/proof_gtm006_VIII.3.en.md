If $H, K$ are subgroups of a given group $G$ then the complex $HK$ is a subgroup if either $H$ or $K$ is normal in $G$. Thus if we show that $\Pi_{([\infty], [0])} 	rianglelefteq \Pi$ we will have proved (i) and the first part of (ii).

By Corollary 2 of Lemma 4.11, for any $lpha$ in $\Pi$ we have $lpha^{-1} \Pi_{([\infty], [0])} lpha = \Pi_{([\infty]^lpha, [0]^lpha)}$. Now $[\infty]^lpha = [\infty]$ since $[\infty]$ is the unique translation line of a proper division ring plane (or is fixed by definition in the desarguesian case). Moreover $[0]^lpha$ is a line through $(\infty)$ since $(\infty)$ is the dual translation point and is therefore also fixed. By the $([\infty], [\infty])$-transitivity, there exists an elation mapping $[0]^lpha$ back to $[0]$, which conjugates $\Pi_{([\infty], [0]^lpha)}$ to $\Pi_{([\infty], [0])}$. Hence $\Pi_{([\infty], [0])} 	rianglelefteq \Pi$, and consequently $\Sigma = \Pi_{([\infty], [0])} \Pi_{([\infty], [\infty])}$ is a subgroup.

For the second part of (ii): any element in the intersection $\Pi_{([\infty], [0])} \cap \Pi_{([\infty], [\infty])}$ is both a shear with axis $[0]$ and an elation with axis $[\infty]$. An elation that fixes two distinct lines through its centre must be the identity, so the intersection is trivial. Since $\Pi_{([\infty], [0])} 	rianglelefteq \Sigma$ and the quotient $\Sigma / \Pi_{([\infty], [0])} \cong \Pi_{([\infty], [\infty])}$ is isomorphic to the additive group of $D$ (which is abelian), $\Sigma$ is metabelian. $\square$
