This follows from Theorem 10.14. A division ring $D$ of dimension two over its left nucleus $N_l$ (or middle nucleus $N_m$) satisfies the conditions of the theorem. Since $N_l \subseteq D$ (respectively $N_m \subseteq D$) is a field contained in the kernel of $D$, and $D$ has dimension $2$ over this nucleus, the sets $\mathcal{B}(a,b,c)$ defined in Theorem 10.14 form Baer subplanes of the affine plane $\mathcal{A}$ coordinatized by $D$.

The derived plane obtained in this case will be a translation plane, in contrast to the general right quasifield case of Corollary 1 where the derived plane is not a translation plane. This distinction arises because division rings yield translation planes (being special cases of quasifields), and the derivation process preserves the translation plane property.
