Consider $M_f = (K_1 \times I) \cup_f K_2$ and $M_g = (K_2 \times I) \cup_g K_3$. Sew them along the common copy of $K_2$ to obtain $M_f \cup M_g$. Now $M_{gf}$ can be viewed as $(K_1 \times I) \cup_{gf} K_3$. There is a natural map from $M_{gf}$ to $M_f \cup M_g$, and formal collapses can be performed to establish the equivalence. Specifically, $M_{gf}$ can be expanded to include a copy of $K_2 \times I$, and then collapsed appropriately to match the union structure.
