The homotopy classification follows from (29.5). The simple-homotopy classification follows from (30.1): if $f: L \to L'$ is a simple-homotopy equivalence with $f_\#(g) = g'^a$, then $(q_1, \dots, q_n)$ is a permutation of $(\varepsilon_1 a q'_1, \dots, \varepsilon_n a q'_n)$ with $\varepsilon_i = \pm 1$. Conversely, if this condition holds, one can construct an explicit simple-homotopy equivalence.

The fact that the torsion invariant determines the simple-homotopy type follows from the natural equivalence (21.1): $Wh(L) \cong Wh(\mathbb{Z}_p)$, and the torsion $\tau(L)$ is a complete invariant of simple-homotopy type within a given homotopy type.

For the relationship with homeomorphism: in dimensions $\geq 5$, the $s$-cobordism theorem implies that simple-homotopy equivalent lens spaces are homeomorphic. Thus the classification up to simple-homotopy equivalence coincides with the classification up to homeomorphism (for $2n-1 \geq 5$, i.e., $n \geq 3$). In dimension 3, the situation is more subtle and involves the Poincaré conjecture (now theorem).
