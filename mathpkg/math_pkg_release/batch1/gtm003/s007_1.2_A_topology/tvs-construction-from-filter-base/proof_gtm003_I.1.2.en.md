This corollary follows directly from Theorem 1.2. Given a filter base $\mathfrak{V}$ satisfying (a), (b), and (c), one defines a translation-invariant topology $\mathfrak{T}$ on $L$ by declaring $\mathfrak{V}$ to be a $0$-neighborhood base (i.e., $V \subset L$ is a neighborhood of $x$ if and only if $x + U \subset V$ for some $U \in \mathfrak{V}$). By Theorem 1.2, this topology satisfies $(LT)_1$ and $(LT)_2$, hence $(L, \mathfrak{T})$ is a t.v.s. The uniqueness follows because a translation-invariant topology is completely determined by the $0$-neighborhood filter.

As an example, let $K[t]$ be the vector space of polynomials over $K$ and let $r$ be a fixed real number with $0 < r \leq 1$. Denote by $V_{\varepsilon}$ the set of polynomials $\sum_n \alpha_n t^n$ for which $\sum_n |\alpha_n|^r \leq \varepsilon$. The family $\{V_{\varepsilon} : \varepsilon > 0\}$ satisfies conditions (a)-(c) and hence defines a unique t.v.s. topology on $K[t]$.
