The proof proceeds by induction on $n = \dim L$. The case $n = 1$ is given by Theorem (3.1). Assume the statement holds for all Hausdorff t.v.s. of dimension $n-1$ over $K$. Let $\{x_1, \ldots, x_n\}$ be a basis of $L$ and define $M$ to be the subspace generated by $\{x_1, \ldots, x_{n-1}\}$ and $N$ the subspace generated by $\{x_n\}$.

By the induction hypothesis, $M$ is isomorphic with $K_0^{n-1}$. Since $K_0$ is complete (as $K$ is complete), $M$ is complete, and since $L$ is Hausdorff, $M$ is closed in $L$. By (2.3), $L/M$ is Hausdorff and clearly of dimension 1; hence the map $v$, assigning to each equivalence class mod $M$ its unique representative in $N$, is an isomorphism by (3.1). It follows from (2.4) that $L = M \oplus N$, which means that

$$(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n) \rightarrow \lambda_1 x_1 + \cdots + \lambda_n x_n$$

is an isomorphism of $K_0^{n-1} \times K_0 = K_0^n$ onto $L$. The uniqueness statement follows from the fact that every isomorphism of $K_0^n$ onto $L$ is of this form.
