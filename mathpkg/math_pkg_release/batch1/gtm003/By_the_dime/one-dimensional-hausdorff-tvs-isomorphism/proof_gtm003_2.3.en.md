It follows from $(LT)_2$ that $\lambda \rightarrow \lambda x_0$ is continuous; moreover, this mapping is an algebraic isomorphism of $K$ onto $L$. Since $L$ is Hausdorff and one-dimensional, the inverse mapping $x \mapsto \lambda$ (where $x = \lambda x_0$) is continuous. Indeed, every linear functional on a finite-dimensional Hausdorff t.v.s. is continuous. Hence $\lambda \mapsto \lambda x_0$ is a topological isomorphism. The uniqueness statement follows from the fact that any isomorphism of $K_0$ onto $L$ must send the basis element $1 \in K_0$ to some non-zero $x_0 \in L$, and by linearity is then given by $\lambda \mapsto \lambda x_0$.
