As before, suppose $A$ is unital without loss of generality.

**(i)** It is clear that $A_+$ is invariant under multiplication by (real) scalars $\geq 0$; thus it is a cone of vertex $0$. To show $A_+$ is convex, it must be proved that $x_1, x_2 \in A_+$ implies $x_1 + x_2 \in A_+$.

Let $x_1, x_2$ and $t_1, t_2 \in \mathbf{R}_+$ satisfy $t_j \geq \|x_j\|$ and $\|t_j e - x_j\| \leq t_j$ ($j = 1, 2$) in accordance with the Lemma above. Then we have

$$\|(t_1 + t_2)e - (x_1 + x_2)\| \leq \|t_1e - x_1\| + \|t_2e - x_2\| \leq t_1 + t_2,$$

which implies $x_1 + x_2 \in A_+$ by part (b) of the Lemma. This part also implies $A_+$ is closed. Finally, part (b) of the Lemma implies $\|x_1 + x_2\| e - (x_1 + x_2) \in A_+$, hence

$$\|x_1 + x_2\| e - x_1 = (\|x_1 + x_2\| e - (x_1 + x_2)) + ((x_1 + x_2) - x_1) \in A_+.$$

Another application of part (b) of the Lemma shows that $\|x_1 + x_2\| \geq \|x_1\|$. By the characterization of normal cones (Chapter V, Section 1), $A_+$ is normal.

**(ii)** Let $x \in A_{sa}$. Using the Gelfand representation (Theorem 2.2, Corollary 1), the $C^*$-algebra generated by $x$ and $e$ is isomorphic to $C(\sigma(x))$. Let $\hat{x}$ be the Gelfand transform of $x$, a real-valued continuous function on $\sigma(x)$. Define $\hat{x}^+(t) = \max(\hat{x}(t), 0)$ and $\hat{x}^-(t) = \max(-\hat{x}(t), 0)$; then $\hat{x}^+, \hat{x}^- \in C(\sigma(x))$, $\hat{x}^+ \hat{x}^- = 0$, and $\hat{x} = \hat{x}^+ - \hat{x}^-$. Pulling back via the Gelfand isomorphism yields $x = x^+ - x^-$ with $x^+, x^- \in A_+$ and $x^+ x^- = 0$.

In the non-commutative case, $A_+$ is not invariant under multiplication: $a, b \in A_+$ does not imply $ab \in A_+$ unless $a$ and $b$ commute. Any $x \in A$ can be written as $x = a + ib$ with $a, b \in A_{sa}$, and each of $a, b$ decomposes as $a = a^+ - a^-$, $b = b^+ - b^-$, so $x$ is a linear combination (over $\mathbf{C}$) of at most four positive elements.
