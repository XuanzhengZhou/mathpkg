As before, suppose $A$ is unital without loss of generality (otherwise work in the unitization $\tilde{A}$). By the Gelfand representation, the $C^*$-algebra generated by $x$ and the unit $e$ is isometrically $*$-isomorphic to $C(\sigma(x))$. Since $\sigma(x) \subset \mathbf{R}_+$, the continuous function $f(t) = \sqrt{t}$ is well-defined on $\sigma(x)$. Let $y$ be the element corresponding to $f$ under the Gelfand isomorphism. Then $\sigma(y) = f(\sigma(x)) \subset \mathbf{R}_+$, $y \in A_{sa}$, and $y^2 = x$.

For uniqueness, suppose $y_1, y_2 \in A_{sa}$ both satisfy $\sigma(y_j) \subset \mathbf{R}_+$ and $y_j^2 = x$ ($j = 1, 2$). Then $y_1$ commutes with $x = y_1^2$, so $y_1$ commutes with every element of the $C^*$-algebra generated by $x$. In particular $y_1$ commutes with $y_2$ (since $y_2$ belongs to this algebra, being approximable by polynomials in $x$). Thus $y_1, y_2$ generate a commutative $C^*$-algebra. In the Gelfand representation, both $y_1$ and $y_2$ correspond to nonnegative continuous functions whose squares equal the same function, so they must coincide. Hence $y_1 = y_2$.
