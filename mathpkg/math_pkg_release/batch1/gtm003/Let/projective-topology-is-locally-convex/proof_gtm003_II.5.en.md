Since the $f_\alpha$ are linear maps and the $\mathfrak{T}_\alpha$ are locally convex topologies on the respective spaces $E_\alpha$, the inverse images $f_\alpha^{-1}(U_\alpha)$ of convex neighborhoods $U_\alpha$ are convex. Finite intersections of such sets remain convex. Thus $\mathfrak{T}$ has a neighborhood base of $0$ consisting of convex sets.

Moreover, $\mathfrak{T}$ is translation-invariant because each $f_\alpha^{-1}(\mathfrak{T}_\alpha)$ is a vector space topology (being the initial topology for a linear map). The upper bound of translation-invariant topologies is translation-invariant. The convex $0$-neighborhood base satisfies conditions (a) and (b) of (I, 1.2) — namely that the topology is Hausdorff if and only if the intersection of all $0$-neighborhoods is $\{0\}$, and that scalar multiplication and addition are continuous — because these properties are preserved under projective limits. Hence $\mathfrak{T}$ is a locally convex topology on $E$.
