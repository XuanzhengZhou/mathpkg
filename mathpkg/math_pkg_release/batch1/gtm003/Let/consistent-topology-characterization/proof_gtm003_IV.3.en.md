By the Mackey-Arens theorem (IV, 3.2), a locally convex topology $\mathfrak{T}$ on $F$ is consistent with the duality $\langle F, G \rangle$ if and only if $\mathfrak{T}$ is an $\mathfrak{S}$-topology for a saturated class $\mathfrak{S}$, covering $G$, of $\sigma(G, F)$-relatively compact subsets of $G$. The topology $\sigma(F, G)$ corresponds to the family of finite subsets of $G$, while the Mackey topology $\tau(F, G)$ corresponds to the family of all convex, circled, $\sigma(G, F)$-compact subsets of $G$. The Mackey topology is the finest locally convex topology on $F$ consistent with the duality (by Corollary 1 of (IV, 3.2)), and $\sigma(F, G)$ is the coarsest, since every consistent topology must be an $\mathfrak{S}$-topology containing the topology of simple convergence $\sigma(F, G)$.

If $\mathfrak{T}$ yields the dual $G$, then $\mathfrak{T}$ is an $\mathfrak{S}$-topology for some saturated family $\mathfrak{S}$ covering $G$, hence $\mathfrak{T}$ is finer than $\sigma(F, G)$. By Corollary 1 of (IV, 3.2), every consistent topology is coarser than $\tau(F, G)$. Conversely, if $\sigma(F, G) \leq \mathfrak{T} \leq \tau(F, G)$, then by (IV, 3.2) $\mathfrak{T}$ is consistent with $\langle F, G \rangle$ since $\sigma(F, G)$ and $\tau(F, G)$ are. The statement about bounded sets follows from Corollary 2 of (IV, 3.2): every $\sigma(F, G)$-bounded subset is bounded for $\tau(F, G)$, hence for any intermediate topology $\mathfrak{T}$. Similarly, the bipolar theorem (IV, 1.5) implies that a convex subset is $\mathfrak{T}$-closed if and only if it is $\tau(F, G)$-closed.
