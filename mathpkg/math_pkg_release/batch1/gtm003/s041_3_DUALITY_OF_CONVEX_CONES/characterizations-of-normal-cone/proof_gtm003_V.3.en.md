Let $\mathfrak{U}$ denote the neighborhood filter of $0$ in $L$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(a) $\Leftrightarrow$ (b):} By definition, $C$ is normal if $\mathfrak{U} = [\mathfrak{U}]$. Since $[\mathfrak{U}]$ is the filter generated by the filter base $\{[U] : U \in \mathfrak{U}\}$, this is precisely the statement that $\mathfrak{B} = \{[U] : U \in \mathfrak{U}\}$ is a neighborhood base of $0$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(a) $\Rightarrow$ (c):} If (a) holds, then $\mathfrak{B} = \{[U] : U \in \mathfrak{U}\}$ is a neighborhood base of $0$. For any $V = [U] \in \mathfrak{B}$, we have $V \cap C \subset V$, so $[V \cap C] \subset [V] = V$ (since $V$ is $C$-saturated). Thus $\mathfrak{B}$ satisfies the condition in (c).

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(c) $\Rightarrow$ (a):} Given $U \in \mathfrak{U}$, we must show there exists $W \in \mathfrak{U}$ such that $[W] \subset U$. Let $\mathfrak{B}$ be a $0$-neighborhood base as described in (c); select $V \in \mathfrak{B}$ such that $V + V \subset U$ and a circled $W \in \mathfrak{U}$ such that $W + W \subset V$. Then:
$$[W] = \bigcup_{x, y \in W} [x, y] = \bigcup_{x, y \in W} (x + [0, y - x]) \subset W + [(W + W) \cap C] \subset V + [V \cap C] \subset V + V \subset U.$$
The inclusion $[W] \subset W + [(W + W) \cap C]$ follows because $[0, y - x] = (0 + C) \cap ((y - x) - C) \subset C \cap ((y-x) - C)$, and since $y - x \in W - W \subset W + W$, we get $[x, y] \subset x + (C \cap (W + W)) = x + [(W + W) \cap C]$. Taking the union over $x, y \in W$ yields $[W] \subset W + [(W + W) \cap C]$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Locally convex case ($K = \mathbb{R}$).}

\noindent\textbf{(a) $\Rightarrow$ (d):} Assume the topology is locally convex and (a) holds. Let $\mathfrak{U}_1$ be the family of all convex, circled $0$-neighborhoods in $L$. Then $\mathfrak{B} = \{[U] : U \in \mathfrak{U}_1\}$ is a base at $0$ consisting of convex, circled, and $C$-saturated sets. Each $[U]$ is convex because $U$ is convex and the $C$-saturated hull preserves convexity; $[U]$ is circled because $U$ is circled and the $C$-saturated hull preserves circledness for real scalar multiplication; and $[U]$ is $C$-saturated by construction.

\noindent\textbf{(d) $\Rightarrow$ (e):} Let $\mathfrak{B}$ be a $0$-neighborhood base as in (d). For each $W \in \mathfrak{B}$, let $p_W$ be the gauge (Minkowski functional) of $W$. The family $\{p_W : W \in \mathfrak{B}\}$ defines the topology of $L$. If $0 \leq x \leq y$ (i.e., $x \in C$ and $y - x \in C$), then for any $\lambda > 0$ with $y \in \lambda W$, since $W$ is $C$-saturated we have $[0, y] \subset \lambda W$, and $x \in [0, y]$, so $x \in \lambda W$. Hence $p_W(x) \leq p_W(y)$, establishing the monotonicity property.

\noindent\textbf{(e) $\Rightarrow$ (c):} Let $\mathcal{P}$ be a family of seminorms as in (e). The family of all finite intersections of sets of the form $V_{p, \varepsilon} = \{x \in L : p(x) < \varepsilon\}$ ($p \in \mathcal{P}, \varepsilon > 0$) is a $0$-neighborhood base. For any such $V = \bigcap_{i=1}^n V_{p_i, \varepsilon_i}$, if $z \in [V \cap C]$, then $z \in [x, y]$ for some $x, y \in V \cap C$, so $x \leq z \leq y$. Since each $p_i$ is monotone, $p_i(z) \leq p_i(y) < \varepsilon_i$, proving $z \in V$. Thus $[V \cap C] \subset V$.
