Take $\mathfrak{S}$ to be the saturated hull of the family of all finite subsets of $E'$. Then the $\mathfrak{S}$-topology on $E$ coincides with the weak topology $\sigma(E, E')$. By definition, $C$ is weakly normal exactly when $C$ is normal for this $\mathfrak{S}$-topology. By Theorem 3.3, since $C'$ is automatically an $\mathfrak{S}$-cone for this choice of $\mathfrak{S}$ (the family of all finite sets covers $E'$ and consists of $\sigma(E', E)$-relatively compact sets), the condition $E' = C' - C'$ is equivalent to condition (b) of Theorem 3.3, which in turn is equivalent to $C$ being normal for the $\mathfrak{S}$-topology — i.e., $C$ being weakly normal. The final statement follows because if $C$ is normal for the original topology of $E$, then $C$ is also normal for the coarser weak topology $\sigma(E, E')$.
