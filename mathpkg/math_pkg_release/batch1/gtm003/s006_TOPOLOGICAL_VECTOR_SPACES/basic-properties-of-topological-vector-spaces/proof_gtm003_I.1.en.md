*Proof.* Each part follows directly from the joint continuity of addition and scalar multiplication in a t.v.s.

**(i)** The map $T(x) = \lambda_0 x + x_0$ is the composition of the dilation $x \mapsto \lambda_0 x$ (continuous by $(LT)_2$ with fixed $\lambda_0$) and the translation $y \mapsto y + x_0$ (continuous by $(LT)_1$ with fixed $x_0$). Hence $T$ is continuous. The inverse $T^{-1}(y) = \lambda_0^{-1}(y - x_0)$ is of the same form (since $\lambda_0 \neq 0$), hence also continuous. Thus $T$ is a homeomorphism.

**(ii)** A point $x \in L$ belongs to $\bar{A}$ iff every neighborhood of $x$ meets $A$. By translation invariance of the topology (from (i)), this is equivalent to: every neighborhood $U$ of $0$ satisfies $(x + U) \cap A \neq \varnothing$, i.e., $x \in A - U$ for every $U \in \mathfrak{U}$. Since $\mathfrak{U}$ is a base of neighborhoods of $0$ and $U$ is symmetric up to a base change (using continuity of $x \mapsto -x$), we may also write $x \in A + U$ for all $U \in \mathfrak{U}$, giving $\bar{A} = \bigcap \{A + U : U \in \mathfrak{U}\}$.

**(iii)** For each $b \in B$, the translation $x \mapsto x + b$ is a homeomorphism by (i). Thus $A + b$ is open for each $b \in B$. Then $A + B = \bigcup_{b \in B} (A + b)$ is a union of open sets, hence open.

**(iv)** Let $z \in \overline{A + B}$. There exists a filter $\mathfrak{F}$ on $A + B$ converging to $z$. Consider the filter $\mathfrak{F}_A$ on $A$ generated by the projections of elements of $\mathfrak{F}$ onto $A$ under the subtraction map. Since every filter on $A$ has an adherent point by hypothesis, $\mathfrak{F}_A$ has an adherent point $a \in A$. The corresponding $b = z - a$ belongs to $B$ by a standard argument using joint continuity of subtraction. Hence $z = a + b \in A + B$, so $A + B$ is closed.

**(v)** Let $A$ be circled, i.e., $\lambda A \subseteq A$ for all $|\lambda| \leq 1$. For any such $\lambda$, continuity of scalar multiplication gives $\lambda \bar{A} \subseteq \overline{\lambda A} \subseteq \bar{A}$. Hence $\bar{A}$ is circled. $\square$
