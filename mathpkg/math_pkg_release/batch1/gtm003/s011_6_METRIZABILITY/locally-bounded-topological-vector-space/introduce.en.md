A topological vector space is locally bounded when the origin admits a bounded neighborhood---equivalently, when it has a neighborhood base at zero consisting entirely of bounded sets. This property occupies a middle ground between normability (which requires a convex bounded neighborhood) and general metrizability. Canonical examples are the $L^p$ spaces for $0 < p \leq 1$, which are locally bounded but, for $p < 1$, not locally convex and admit no non-trivial continuous linear functionals. Every locally bounded Hausdorff t.v.s. is automatically metrizable (Theorem 6.2), but the converse is false.
