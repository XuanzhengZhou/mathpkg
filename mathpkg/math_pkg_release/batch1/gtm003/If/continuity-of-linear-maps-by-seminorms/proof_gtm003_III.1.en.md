A linear map $u: L \to M$ is continuous if and only if it is continuous at $0 \in L$. For if $V$ is a given $0$-neighborhood in $M$ and $U$ is a $0$-neighborhood in $L$ such that $u(U) \subset V$, then $x - y \in U$ implies $u(x - y) = u(x) - u(y) \in V$. Hence if $u$ is continuous at $0$, it is even uniformly continuous.

Now suppose the topologies of $L$ and $M$ are generated by families of seminorms $\mathcal{P}$ and $\mathcal{Q}$. A $0$-neighborhood $V$ in $M$ contains a set $\{y: \sup_i q_i(y) \leq \varepsilon\}$ for some $q_1, \ldots, q_m \in \mathcal{Q}$ and $\varepsilon > 0$. If $u$ is continuous at $0$, there exists a $0$-neighborhood $U$ in $L$ with $u(U) \subset V$. Taking $U$ of the form $\{x: \sup_j p_j(x) \leq \delta\}$ yields the seminorm estimate. Conversely, the seminorm estimate implies that each seminorm $q \in \mathcal{Q}$ is continuous when pulled back via $u$, so $u$ is continuous.
