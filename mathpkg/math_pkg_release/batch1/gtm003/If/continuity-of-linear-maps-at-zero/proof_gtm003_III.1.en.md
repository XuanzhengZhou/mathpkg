If $u$ is continuous on $L$, then trivially $u$ is continuous at $0$. Conversely, suppose $u$ is continuous at $0$. Let $V$ be a given $0$-neighborhood in $M$. By continuity at $0$, there exists a $0$-neighborhood $U$ in $L$ such that $u(U) \subset V$. For any $x, y \in L$ with $x - y \in U$, linearity gives $u(x) - u(y) = u(x - y) \in V$. Hence $u$ is uniformly continuous on $L$ for the respective additive uniformities.
