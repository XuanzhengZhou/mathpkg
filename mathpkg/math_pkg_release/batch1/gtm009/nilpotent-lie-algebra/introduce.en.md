A Lie algebra is nilpotent if its descending central series terminates at zero. Nilpotency is a stronger condition than solvability: every nilpotent algebra is solvable, but not conversely (the standard example is the algebra of upper triangular matrices). Nilpotent Lie algebras are the natural setting for Engel's theorem, which characterizes them in terms of the nilpotence of adjoint operators.
