Fix standard generators $\{x_i, y_i\}$ of $L$.

**Lemma (Commutator identities).** The following identities hold in $\mathfrak{U}(L)$, for $k \geq 0$, $1 \leq i, j \leq \ell$:

(a) $[x_j, y_i^{k+1}] = 0$ when $i \neq j$;

(b) $[h_j, y_i^{k+1}] = -(k+1)\alpha_i(h_j)y_i^{k+1}$;

(c) $[x_i, y_i^{k+1}] = -(k+1)y_i^k(k \cdot 1 - h_i)$.

*Proof of Lemma.* (a) follows from the fact (Lemma 10.1) that $\alpha_j - \alpha_i$ is not a root when $i \neq j$. For (b), use induction on $k$, the case $k = 0$ being $[h_j, y_i] = -\alpha_i(h_j)y_i$ (cf. (18.1)). For (c), also use induction on $k$.

Now the proof of the theorem proceeds in eight steps.

**(1)** Set $m_i = \langle \lambda, \alpha_i \rangle$, which is a nonnegative integer since $\lambda$ is dominant integral. From the commutator identities, $y_i^{m_i+1}$ annihilates a maximal vector $v^+ \in V(\lambda)$. Indeed, $x_i y_i^{m_i+1} v^+ = y_i^{m_i+1} x_i v^+ + [x_i, y_i^{m_i+1}]v^+ = 0 - (m_i+1)y_i^{m_i}(\lambda(h_i) - m_i)v^+ = 0$, and for $j \neq i$, $x_j y_i^{m_i+1} v^+ = y_i^{m_i+1} x_j v^+ = 0$. Similarly $h_j$ acts by the correct scalar. So $y_i^{m_i+1} v^+$ is a maximal vector of weight $\lambda - (m_i+1)\alpha_i$, which is not linked to $\lambda$ unless it is zero. Since $V(\lambda)$ has a unique maximal vector (up to scalar), $y_i^{m_i+1} v^+ = 0$.

**(2)** The subspace spanned by all vectors of the form $y_{i_1} \cdots y_{i_k} v^+$ (for all $k$ and all $i_j$) is an $L$-submodule. Since $V(\lambda)$ is irreducible, this subspace equals all of $V(\lambda)$.

**(3)** The sum of all finite dimensional $S_i$-submodules of $V(\lambda)$ is nonzero by step (2). Let $V'$ be this sum. If $W$ is any finite dimensional $S_i$-submodule, the span of all subspaces $x_\alpha W$ ($\alpha \in \Phi$) is finite dimensional and $S_i$-stable. Therefore $V'$ is stable under $L$, and $V' = V(\lambda)$ because $V(\lambda)$ is irreducible.

**(4)** For $1 \leq i \leq \ell$, $\phi(x_i)$ and $\phi(y_i)$ are locally nilpotent endomorphisms of $V(\lambda)$ (cf. (18.3)). Indeed, if $v \in V(\lambda)$, then $v$ lies in a finite sum of finite dimensional $S_i$-submodules (hence in a finite dimensional $S_i$-submodule), by (3). On such a module $\phi(x_i)$ and $\phi(y_i)$ are nilpotent (cf. (6.4)).

**(5)** $s_i = \exp \phi(x_i) \exp \phi(-y_i) \exp \phi(x_i)$ is a well-defined automorphism of $V(\lambda)$. This follows at once from (4) (cf. (18.3)).

**(6)** If $\mu$ is any weight of $V(\lambda)$, then $s_i(V_\mu) = V_{\sigma_i\mu}$, where $\sigma_i$ is the reflection relative to $\alpha_i$. $V_\mu$ lies in a finite dimensional $S_i$-submodule $V'$ (cf. step (3)), and $s_i|_{V'}$ is the same as the automorphism $\tau$ constructed in (7.2); the claim now follows from the discussion in (7.2).

**(7)** The set of weights $\Pi(\lambda)$ is stable under $W$, and $\dim V_\mu = \dim V_{\sigma\mu}$ for all $\mu \in \Pi(\lambda)$, $\sigma \in W$. Since $W$ is generated by $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_\ell$ (Theorem 10.3(d)), this follows from (6).

**(8)** $\Pi(\lambda)$ is finite. From the definition of $V(\lambda)$ as a quotient of $Z(\lambda)$, it is clear that every weight is of the form $\lambda - \sum k_i \alpha_i$. Combined with (7), each weight must also satisfy dominant integral type inequalities, which force the $k_i$ to be bounded. Hence the number of weights is finite, and since each weight space is finite dimensional (by the PBW theorem and the structure of $Z(\lambda)$), $\dim V(\lambda)$ is finite.
