\textbf{Uniqueness.} Suppose $L$ and $L'$ are both free Lie algebras on the set $X$, with inclusion maps $i: X \to L$ and $i': X \to L'$. Applying the universal property of $L$ to $i': X \to L'$ yields a unique homomorphism $\phi: L \to L'$ with $\phi \circ i = i'$. Applying the universal property of $L'$ to $i: X \to L$ yields a unique homomorphism $\psi: L' \to L$ with $\psi \circ i' = i$. Then $\psi \circ \phi: L \to L$ satisfies $(\psi \circ \phi) \circ i = i$, and since the identity $1_L$ also satisfies this, uniqueness forces $\psi \circ \phi = 1_L$. Similarly $\phi \circ \psi = 1_{L'}$.

\textbf{Existence.} Let $V$ be a vector space over $F$ with basis $X$, and let $\mathfrak{T}(V)$ be the tensor algebra on $V$. View $\mathfrak{T}(V)$ as a Lie algebra with bracket $[a, b] = a \otimes b - b \otimes a$. Let $L$ be the Lie subalgebra of $\mathfrak{T}(V)$ generated by $X \subset V = T^1 V$.

Now let $\phi: X \to M$ be any map into a Lie algebra $M$. Extend $\phi$ first to a linear map $\tilde{\phi}: V \to M$. Next, compose with the canonical injection $M \hookrightarrow \mathfrak{U}(M)$ to obtain a linear map $V \to \mathfrak{U}(M)$. By the universal property of the tensor algebra, this extends to a unique associative algebra homomorphism $\Phi: \mathfrak{T}(V) \to \mathfrak{U}(M)$ sending $1$ to $1$.

Since $\Phi$ is an algebra homomorphism, it satisfies $\Phi([a, b]) = \Phi(a \otimes b - b \otimes a) = \Phi(a)\Phi(b) - \Phi(b)\Phi(a) = [\Phi(a), \Phi(b)]$, so $\Phi$ is also a Lie algebra homomorphism. The restriction $\psi = \Phi|_L: L \to \mathfrak{U}(M)$ is a Lie algebra homomorphism. Since $\psi(X) = \phi(X) \subset M$ and $L$ is generated by $X$ as a Lie algebra, the image $\psi(L)$ lies in the Lie subalgebra of $\mathfrak{U}(M)$ generated by $\phi(X)$, which is contained in $M$ (via the PBW Corollary B identifying $M$ with a Lie subalgebra of $\mathfrak{U}(M)$). Thus $\psi: L \to M$ is the required extension. Uniqueness follows because $L$ is generated by $X$ as a Lie algebra, so any two homomorphisms agreeing on $X$ must agree everywhere.
