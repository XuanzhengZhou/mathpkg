A Lie algebra $L$ over a field $F$ is called \textbf{free} on a set $X$ if, given any mapping $\phi$ of $X$ into a Lie algebra $M$, there exists a unique Lie algebra homomorphism $\psi: L \to M$ extending $\phi$.

\textbf{Construction.} Begin with a vector space $V$ having $X$ as basis, form the tensor algebra $\mathfrak{T}(V)$ (viewed as a Lie algebra via the commutator bracket $[a, b] = a \otimes b - b \otimes a$), and let $L$ be the Lie subalgebra of $\mathfrak{T}(V)$ generated by $X$. Then $L$ is a free Lie algebra on $X$.

If $L$ is free on $X$, and $R$ is the ideal of $L$ generated by elements $f_j$ ($j$ running over some index set), then the quotient $L/R$ is called the \textbf{Lie algebra with generators $x_i$ and relations $f_j = 0$}, where $x_i$ are the images in $L/R$ of the elements of $X$.
