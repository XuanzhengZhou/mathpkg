If $(\alpha, \beta) \leq 0$ for all $\beta \in \Delta$, then $\Delta \cup \{\alpha\}$ would be linearly independent (by a remark from the proof of Theorem 10.1"), which is absurd since $\Delta$ is already a basis of $E$. So $(\alpha, \beta) > 0$ for some $\beta \in \Delta$, and then $\alpha - \beta \in \Phi$ by Lemma 9.4 (which applies since $\beta$ cannot be proportional to $\alpha$). Writing $\alpha = \sum_{\gamma \in \Delta} k_\gamma \gamma$ (all $k_\gamma \geq 0$, some $k_\gamma > 0$ for $\gamma \neq \beta$) and subtracting $\beta$ yields a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combination of simple roots with at least one positive coefficient. This forces all coefficients to be nonnegative, thanks to the uniqueness of expression in (B2). Hence $\alpha - \beta$ is positive.
