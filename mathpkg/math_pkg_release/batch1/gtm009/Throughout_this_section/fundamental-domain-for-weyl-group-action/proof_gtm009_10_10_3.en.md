Use induction on $\ell(\sigma)$, the case $\ell(\sigma) = 0$ being clear (then $\sigma = 1$, $\lambda = \mu$). Let $\ell(\sigma) > 0$. By Lemma A, $\sigma$ must send some positive root to a negative root; so $\sigma$ cannot send all simple roots to positive roots. Thus there exists $\alpha \in \Delta$ such that $\sigma(\alpha) < 0$. From $\lambda \in \overline{\mathfrak{C}(\Delta)}$, we have $(\lambda, \alpha) \geq 0$. On the other hand, $(\mu, \sigma(\alpha)) = (\sigma^{-1}\mu, \alpha) = (\lambda, \alpha) \geq 0$, but $\sigma(\alpha) < 0$ and $\mu \in \overline{\mathfrak{C}(\Delta)}$ forces $(\mu, \sigma(\alpha)) \leq 0$. Hence $(\lambda, \alpha) = 0$, so $\sigma_\alpha(\lambda) = \lambda$. Then $\sigma \sigma_\alpha(\lambda) = \mu$ with $\ell(\sigma \sigma_\alpha) = \ell(\sigma) - 1$, and induction applies.
