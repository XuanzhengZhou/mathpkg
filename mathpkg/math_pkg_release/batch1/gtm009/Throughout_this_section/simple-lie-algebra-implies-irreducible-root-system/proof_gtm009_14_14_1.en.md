Suppose $\Phi$ were reducible. Then $\Phi = \Phi_1 \cup \Phi_2$ with the $\Phi_i$ orthogonal. If $\alpha \in \Phi_1$, $\beta \in \Phi_2$, then $(\alpha+\beta, \alpha) \neq 0$ and $(\alpha+\beta, \beta) \neq 0$, so $\alpha+\beta$ cannot be a root (otherwise it would be in both $\Phi_1$ and $\Phi_2$, impossible by orthogonality). Hence $[L_\alpha L_\beta] = 0$. The subalgebra $K$ of $L$ generated by all $L_\alpha$ ($\alpha \in \Phi_1$) is centralized by all $L_\beta$ ($\beta \in \Phi_2$). This forces $K$ to be a proper nonzero ideal of $L$, contradicting simplicity.
