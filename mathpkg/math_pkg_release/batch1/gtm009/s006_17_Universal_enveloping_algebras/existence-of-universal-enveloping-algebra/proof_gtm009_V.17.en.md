First, note that $J \subset \bigoplus_{i > 0} T^iL$, so $\pi$ maps $T^0L = F$ isomorphically into $\mathfrak{U}(L)$; therefore $\mathfrak{U}(L)$ contains at least the scalars. The map $i: L \to \mathfrak{U}(L)$ is linear by construction, and for $x, y \in L$,
$$
i(x)i(y) - i(y)i(x) = \pi(x \otimes y - y \otimes x) = \pi([xy]) = i([xy]),
$$
since $x \otimes y - y \otimes x - [xy] \in J$. Thus $i$ is a Lie algebra homomorphism.

Now let $j: L \to \mathfrak{A}$ be any Lie algebra homomorphism into an associative $F$-algebra $\mathfrak{A}$ with $1$. By the universal property of $\mathfrak{T}(L)$, the linear map $j: L \to \mathfrak{A}$ extends uniquely to an algebra homomorphism $\phi': \mathfrak{T}(L) \to \mathfrak{A}$ with $\phi'(1) = 1$. For any generators $x \otimes y - y \otimes x - [xy]$ of $J$,
$$
\phi'(x \otimes y - y \otimes x - [xy]) = j(x)j(y) - j(y)j(x) - j([xy]) = 0,
$$
since $j$ is a Lie algebra homomorphism. Hence $J \subseteq \ker \phi'$, and $\phi'$ induces an algebra homomorphism $\phi: \mathfrak{U}(L) = \mathfrak{T}(L)/J \to \mathfrak{A}$ satisfying $\phi \circ i = j$. Uniqueness follows because $\mathfrak{U}(L)$ is generated by $i(L)$ and $1$.
