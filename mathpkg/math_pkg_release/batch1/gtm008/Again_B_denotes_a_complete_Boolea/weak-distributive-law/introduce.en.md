The weak distributive laws are generalizations of the complete distributive law $\prod_i \sum_j b_{ij} = \sum_{f} \prod_i b_{i,f(i)}$ that hold in completely distributive Boolean algebras. For an arbitrary complete Boolean algebra, the equation typically fails because the right-hand side sum over all choice functions may be strictly smaller. The $(\omega, \omega)$-weak distributive law asserts that the equality holds when both products and sums are countable, with the right-hand side indexed by functions $f \in \omega^\omega$. The $(\omega, \omega_\alpha)$ variant generalizes this to a mixed product over $\omega$ and sum over $\omega_\alpha$. These laws are fundamental in Boolean-valued model theory, where they characterize whether certain cardinal arithmetic properties (such as cofinalities) are preserved in generic extensions.
