**Proof.** Since $M \subseteq M[G]$ and $\mathrm{On}^M = \mathrm{On}^{M[G]}$, every cardinal in $M[G]$ is a cardinal in $M$ because the statement "$a$ is not a cardinal" is equivalent to $(\exists f)\,\varphi(f, a)$ for some $\varphi$ that is absolute with respect to transitive models.

Conversely, suppose there is a cardinal in $M$ that is not a cardinal in $M[G]$. Then there exists a cardinal $\lambda$ in $M[G]$ (hence in $M$) such that

$$\gamma = (\lambda^+)^M$$

is not a cardinal in $M[G]$. Let $\lambda$ be the smallest such cardinal. Then

$$\lambda = \overline{\gamma}^{M[G]} < \gamma = (\lambda^+)^M.$$

Hence there exists $f \in M[G]$ with $f: \lambda \xrightarrow{\text{onto}} \gamma$. The function $f$ is denoted by a term $t$ of the ramified language, and there exists $p \in P$ with $p \in G$ such that

$$p \Vdash t: \lambda \xrightarrow{\text{onto}} \gamma.$$

Abbreviate $q \Vdash \langle \alpha, \beta \rangle \in t$ by $\varphi(q, \alpha, \beta)$. For each $\alpha < \lambda$, let $S_\alpha = \{\beta < \gamma \mid (\exists q \leq p)\,\varphi(q, \alpha, \beta)\}$. Since $f$ is onto $\gamma$, $\bigcup_{\alpha < \lambda} S_\alpha = \gamma$.

Let $S' = \{q_\alpha \mid \alpha < \lambda\}$ where each $q_\alpha$ witnesses some $\beta$. By thinning, we may assume that for distinct $\alpha, \alpha'$, the corresponding conditions are incompatible (otherwise two $\alpha$'s would force the same ordinal $\beta$). Let $S = \{[p]^{-0} \mid p \in S'\}$, where $[p]^{-0}$ denotes the regular open set generated by $p$. Then $S \subseteq B$ (the Boolean algebra of regular open sets of $P$) and any two elements of $S$ are disjoint, by the fact that

$$(\forall p, q \in P)\,[[p] \cap [q] = 0 \leftrightarrow [p]^{-0} \cap [q]^{-0} = 0].$$

Thus $S$ is an antichain in $B$ of size $\lambda$. However, the ccc on $P$ implies that every antichain in $B$ is countable, so $\lambda \leq \aleph_0$. But $\lambda$ was a cardinal, giving a contradiction (or showing that only $\aleph_0$ could be collapsed, and then $\aleph_1$ is preserved, and so on).

More precisely: if $\lambda > \aleph_0$, then $S$ would be an uncountable antichain, contradicting the ccc. Therefore no cardinal $\lambda > \aleph_0$ is collapsed. For $\lambda = \aleph_0$, the argument is similar but uses that $\aleph_0$ is absolute anyway. Thus all cardinals are preserved. $\square$
