Banach's theorem (1931) provides an elegant category-theoretic proof that nowhere differentiable continuous functions are not merely pathological curiosities — they are the generic case. Working in the complete metric space $C[0,1]$ with the uniform topology, Banach showed that the set of functions possessing even a finite one-sided derivative at a single point is of first category (a meager set). By the Baire Category Theorem, the complement — functions that are nowhere differentiable — forms a residual (comeager) set. The proof proceeds by defining nested sets $E_n$ capturing those functions with right difference quotients bounded by $n$, showing each $E_n$ is closed and nowhere dense via a clever perturbation with a rapidly oscillating saw-tooth function, and concluding by symmetry for left difference quotients.
