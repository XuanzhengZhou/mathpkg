Apply Theorem 17.2 in two settings: (1) With $\mathcal{S}$ being the $\sigma$-algebra of measurable sets and $\mathcal{I}$ the $\sigma$-ideal of nullsets -- since the measure is finite, any measurable wandering set is a nullset, so $T$ is nondissipative in the measure sense; (2) With $\mathcal{S}$ being the $\sigma$-algebra of sets having the property of Baire and $\mathcal{I}$ the $\sigma$-ideal of first-category sets -- by the Banach Category Theorem, there is a largest open set $H$ of first category; letting $Y = X - \bar{H}$, any non-empty open subset of $Y$ is of second category, and $Y$ is $T$-invariant. Any wandering set with the property of Baire decomposes as $G \triangle P$ with $G$ open and $P$ first category; the wandering condition forces $G$ to be empty in $Y$, so the wandering set is of first category. Thus $T$ is nondissipative in both senses. The combined result yields a single exceptional set $E$ (a countable union of wandering sets) that is both a nullset and of first category, outside of which every point is recurrent.
