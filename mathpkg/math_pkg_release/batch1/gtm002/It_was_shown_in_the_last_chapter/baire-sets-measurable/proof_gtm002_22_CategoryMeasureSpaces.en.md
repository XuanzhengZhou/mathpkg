First, suppose $A \in S$. By definition of the topology, any open set is of the form $\phi(B) - N$ for some $B \in S$ and $N \in \mathcal{N}$. Since $A$ is measurable, it can be expressed as a member of the $\sigma$-algebra generated by these open sets. In particular, $A$ can be written as $\phi(A) \triangle M$ for some $M \in \mathcal{N}$, where $\phi(A)$ is regular open in $\mathcal{T}$ (by Theorem 22.8 below) and $M$ is a nullset, hence nowhere dense by Theorem 22.6. Thus $A$ has the property of Baire.

Conversely, if $A$ has the property of Baire relative to $\mathcal{T}$, then by definition $A = G \triangle P$ where $G$ is open in $\mathcal{T}$ and $P$ is of first category. By Theorem 4.6, $A$ can be written as $A = [\phi(B) - N] \triangle M$ for some $B \in S$, $N \in \mathcal{N}$, and some set $M$ of first category. By Theorem 22.6, $M$ belongs to $\mathcal{N}$, and since $\phi(B) - N$ is in $S$ (because $\phi(B) \in S$ and $N \in \mathcal{N}$), the symmetric difference of two members of $S$ remains in $S$, so $A \in S$.
