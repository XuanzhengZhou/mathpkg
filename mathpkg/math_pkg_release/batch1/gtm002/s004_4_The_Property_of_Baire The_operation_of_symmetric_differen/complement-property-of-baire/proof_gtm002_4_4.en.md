If $A$ has the property of Baire, then by Theorem 4.1, $A = F \triangle Q$ where $F$ is closed and $Q$ is of first category. Taking complements,
$$A' = (F \triangle Q)' = F' \triangle Q,$$
since the complement of a symmetric difference $X \triangle Y$ is $X' \triangle Y = X \triangle Y'$ (because $X \triangle Y = (X \cup Y) - (X \cap Y)$, and complementing exchanges union and intersection). Here $F'$ is open, and $Q$ is of first category, so $A' = F' \triangle Q$ exhibits $A'$ as having the property of Baire by the original definition.
