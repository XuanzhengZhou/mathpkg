The Banach-Mazur game, invented by Stanislaw Mazur around 1928, is an infinite game played by two players alternately choosing nested closed subintervals of a given interval. Player A wins if the intersection point belongs to a prescribed set A, otherwise player B wins. This game provides a game-theoretic characterization of Baire category: player B has a winning strategy if and only if A is of first category, revealing a deep connection between determinacy and topological size.
