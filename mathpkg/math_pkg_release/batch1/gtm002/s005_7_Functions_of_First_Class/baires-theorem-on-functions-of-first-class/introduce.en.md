Baire's theorem on functions of first class (Theorem 7.3) states that the pointwise limit of an everywhere convergent sequence of continuous functions is continuous except on a set of first category. This was the original motivation for Baire's introduction of the notion of category. The proof shows that for any $\varepsilon > 0$, the set $\{x : \omega(x) \geq 5\varepsilon\}$ is nowhere dense, using nested intersections of sets where continuous approximants are uniformly close. The theorem yields immediate corollaries: an everywhere convergent trigonometric series cannot sum to an everywhere discontinuous function, and the derivative of a differentiable function cannot be everywhere discontinuous.
