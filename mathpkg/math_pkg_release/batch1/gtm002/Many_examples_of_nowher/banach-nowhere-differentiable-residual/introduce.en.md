Banach (1931) used the Baire category method to give one of the simplest proofs that "almost all" continuous functions are nowhere differentiable. The theorem states that the set of functions possessing a finite one-sided derivative at any point is of first category in $C[0,1]$. Its complement — a residual (comeager) set — therefore consists of functions that have no finite one-sided derivative anywhere. This result is striking: although classical examples like Weierstrass's function are crafted with care, the category argument shows that nowhere differentiability is the generic behavior for continuous functions.
