This proposition characterizes inconsistent sets of formulas in first-order logic. If a set $\mathcal{E}$ contains all tautologies of a certain basic type and derives both a formula $P$ and its negation, then $\mathcal{E}$ is \textit{inconsistent} — it can derive every formula whatsoever.

The equivalence shows that in classical logic (with tautologies of type $\text{B}_2$, which include the law of the excluded middle), a single contradiction is sufficient to trivialize the entire deductive system. This is the formal expression of the principle \textit{ex contradictione quodlibet} (from a contradiction, anything follows).

The result is fundamental to proof by contradiction: to show that a formula cannot be derived from a set of axioms, it suffices to exhibit a model in which the axioms are true but the formula is false.
