A deduction in the first-order language $\mathfrak{L}_1$ is the formal counterpart of a mathematical proof. It consists of a finite sequence of formulas ending with the target formula $P$, where each step is either an hypothesis from $\mathcal{E}$, a logical axiom, or follows from earlier steps by modus ponens or generalization.

Deductions serve as the rigorous foundation for the notion of provability: we write $\mathcal{E} \vdash P$ when such a deduction exists. This definition captures the idea that only true formulas can be deduced from true formulas, and it provides the framework for studying the relationship between syntax (deducibility) and semantics (truth).

The concept of deduction is central to the study of formal systems and is used throughout the book to analyze the provability of mathematical statements within specified axiomatic frameworks.
