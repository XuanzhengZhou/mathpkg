A \textbf{deduction} of a formula $P$ from a set of formulas $\mathcal{E}$ (in a language $L$ in $\mathfrak{L}_1$) is a finite sequence of formulas

$$P_1, P_2, \ldots, P_n = P$$

with the property that for each $i$, one of the following holds:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $P_i \in \mathcal{E}$ (an hypothesis);
\item $P_i$ is a logical axiom (a tautology or a quantifier axiom of $\text{Ax } L$);
\item $P_i$ follows from earlier formulas $P_j, P_k$ (with $j, k < i$) by the rule of modus ponens (MP);
\item $P_i = \forall x P_j$ follows from an earlier formula $P_j$ (with $j < i$) by the rule of generalization (Gen), provided $x$ does not occur freely in any formula of $\mathcal{E}$ used in deriving $P_j$.
\end{enumerate}

We write $\mathcal{E} \vdash P$ and say that $P$ is \textit{deducible} from $\mathcal{E}$.
