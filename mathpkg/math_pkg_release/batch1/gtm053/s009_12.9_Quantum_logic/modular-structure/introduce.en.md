A modular structure (modular lattice) is an algebraic structure introduced by G. Birkhoff and J. von Neumann in their 1936 paper on the logic of quantum mechanics. It consists of a set $L$ equipped with two binary operations $\wedge$ (meet) and $\vee$ (join) that are associative, commutative, and idempotent, and satisfy the modular law: if $b \leqslant a$ (i.e., $a \wedge b = b$), then $a \wedge (b \vee c) = b \vee (a \wedge c)$. The set $B(\mathcal{H})$ of closed subspaces of a Hilbert space, with intersection as $\wedge$ and closed linear sum as $\vee$, forms a modular structure (in fact, an orthomodular lattice once the orthogonal complement is included). Unlike Boolean algebras, the distributive law does not hold in general.
