The downward Löwenheim–Skolem theorem states that any first-order theory with a countable alphabet that has an infinite model also has a countable model. This is an immediate corollary of Proposition 7.3: take $M_0$ to be a countable subset of $N$ (e.g., the interpretations of the constant symbols, or simply a singleton), and apply the proposition with $\mathcal{E}$ being the set of all formulas of $L$ (which is countable when the alphabet is countable). The constructed submodel $M$ has cardinality at most $\aleph_0 + \aleph_0 + \aleph_0 = \aleph_0$, and all formulas are $(M,N)$-absolute, so in particular formulas true in $N$ remain true in $M$. This theorem reveals a fundamental limitation of first-order logic: it cannot distinguish between countable and uncountable models (the Skolem paradox).
