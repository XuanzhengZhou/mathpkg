Tarski's Undefinability Theorem states that the set of Gödel numbers of true formulas of arithmetic cannot be defined by any formula of arithmetic itself. In other words, arithmetical truth is not arithmetically definable. The proof is a formalization of the liar paradox using the self-reference mechanisms of SAr (or SELF): if a truth predicate $T(x)$ were definable, one could construct a formula that says "I am not true," leading to a contradiction. The theorem extends to any language of arithmetic $?Ar$ that extends SAr with the same class-term and satisfaction mechanisms. This result is foundational for modern logic: it shows the inherent limitations of formal systems and directly leads to Gödel's incompleteness theorems — if provability (which is definable) coincided with truth, truth would be definable, contradicting Tarski's theorem.
