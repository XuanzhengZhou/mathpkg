Gödel's First Incompleteness Theorem follows directly from Tarski's Undefinability Theorem. The key observation is that the set of provable formulas is definable in arithmetic (via Gödel numbering, the graph of "proof of" is recursive and hence arithmetic), but by Tarski's theorem, the set of true formulas is not definable. Therefore, these two sets cannot coincide. This means that in any consistent formal system for arithmetic strong enough to express its own syntax, there exist true but unprovable statements. Manin's presentation emphasizes that this form of the incompleteness theorem is independent of the specific formalization of provability: as long as provability is definable, truth and provability must diverge. Part III of the book provides more general formulations and detailed verifications.
