The arithmetic hierarchy classifies formulas and sets in the language of arithmetic according to the number of alternating unbounded quantifiers in their definitions. The base level $\Sigma_0 = \Pi_0$ consists of formulas with only bounded quantifiers. Each additional level adds one more quantifier alternation: $\Sigma_{n+1}$ formulas begin with an existential quantifier followed by a $\Pi_n$ formula, while $\Pi_{n+1}$ formulas begin with a universal quantifier followed by a $\Sigma_n$ formula. This hierarchy provides a precise measure of the logical complexity of arithmetical statements and is fundamental to computability theory and proof theory.
