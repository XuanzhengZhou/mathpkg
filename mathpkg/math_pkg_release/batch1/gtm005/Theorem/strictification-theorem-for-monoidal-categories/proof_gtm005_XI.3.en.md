The coherence theorem yields unique canonical maps between words; hence the plan of the proof is to embed the given category $M$ in a larger strict monoidal category $S$ consisting of iterated formal products (with all pairs of parentheses starting in front) of elements of $M$.

Let $S$ be the free monoid generated by the objects of $M$. Specifically, take the objects $s$ of $S$ to be all finite strings $s = [b_1, \ldots, b_k]$ of objects of $M$, including the empty string $\varnothing$. A product $s \otimes t$ of strings $s$ and $t$ is defined by concatenation of strings, as $s \cdot t$. This product is associative, so the associativity map $\alpha$ for $S$ can be the identity. Also, the empty string $\varnothing$ acts as a unit for this product, so the maps $\rho$ and $\lambda$ for $S$ can both be the identity. With these agreements, $S$ is an associative monoid with a unit, but not yet a monoidal category.

Define a map $F : S \rightarrow M$ on objects of $S$ by setting
$$F(\varnothing) = e,$$
$$F(s) = F[b_1, \ldots, b_k] = (\ldots(b_1 \otimes b_2) \otimes b_3) \ldots) \otimes b_k),$$
where on the right all pairs of parentheses begin in front. Now define the arrows $s \rightarrow t$ between strings $s$ and $t$ in $S$ to be exactly the arrows between the corresponding objects in $M$,
$$F(s) \rightarrow F(t),$$
with composition just as in $M$. This convention clearly makes $S$ into a category. Then the concatenation product $s \cdot u$ can be extended to a corresponding product $f \cdot g$ of arrows $f : s \rightarrow t$ and $g : u \rightarrow v$, where $u$ and $v$, like $s$ and $t$, are finite strings of objects of $M$. Specifically, $f \cdot g$ is defined as the following composite in $M$:
$$F(s \cdot u) \rightarrow F(s) \otimes F(u) \xrightarrow{f \otimes g} F(t) \otimes F(v) \rightarrow F(t \cdot v);$$
here the two outer arrows are the canonical maps derived from the coherence theorem.

The reverse functor $G : M \rightarrow S$ is defined on objects by
$$G(b) = [b],$$
on arrows by $G(f) = f$, with monoidal structure maps
$$G_0 = 1 : \varnothing \rightarrow [e],$$
$$G_2(b, c) = 1 : [b, c] \rightarrow [b \otimes c].$$
Here the last map is the identity because by the definition above, a map of strings $[b, c] \rightarrow [b \otimes c]$ is just a map $b \otimes c \rightarrow b \otimes c$ in $M$. The conditions (3), (4), and (4') of \S 2 on $G$ then follow. In the case of (3), observe that the map $G_2 \otimes 1$ is
$$(G(a) \cdot G(b)) \cdot G(c) \xrightarrow{G_2 \otimes 1} G(a \otimes b) \cdot G(c),$$
$$[a, b, c] \longmapsto [a \otimes b, c],$$
therefore, by the definition of maps in $S$, must be the map $\alpha : a \otimes (b \otimes c) \rightarrow (a \otimes b) \otimes c$ in $B$ as a map in $S$; this matches the map $G(\alpha)$ at the base of (3) of \S 2, while $\alpha' = 1$ is at the top.

The composite functor $F G : M \rightarrow M$ is the identity, while the composite $G F$ is naturally isomorphic to the identity. Hence, the monoidal category $M$ is indeed categorically equivalent (by monoidal functors) to the strict monoidal category $S$, as claimed.
