A \textbf{monad} $T = \langle T, \eta, \mu \rangle$ in a category $X$ consists of
a functor $T: X \to X$ and two natural transformations
$$\eta: I_X \to T, \qquad \mu: T^2 \to T$$
which make the following diagrams commute:

\[
\begin{CD}
T^3 @>{T\mu}>> T^2 \\
@V{\mu T}VV @VV{\mu}V \\
T^2 @>>{\mu}> T
\end{CD}
\qquad
\begin{CD}
T @>{T\eta}>> T^2 @<{\eta T}<< T \\
@| @VV{\mu}V @| \\
T @= T @= T
\end{CD}
\]

The natural transformation $\eta$ is called the \textbf{unit} and $\mu$ the \textbf{multiplication} of the monad.
The first diagram expresses the \textbf{associative law}, while the second diagram expresses the
\textbf{left and right unit laws}. All told, a monad in $X$ is a monoid in the category of endofunctors of $X$,
with the cartesian product $\times$ replaced by composition of endofunctors and the unit object replaced
by the identity endofunctor.
