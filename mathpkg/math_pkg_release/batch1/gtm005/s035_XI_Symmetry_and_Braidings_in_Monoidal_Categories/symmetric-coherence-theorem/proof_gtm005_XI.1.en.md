**Proof of the Symmetric Coherence Theorem.**

First, observe that the hexagonal identities (7) for the braiding $\gamma$ show that interchanging a single argument $a$ with a product of arguments (such as $b \square c$) can always be replaced by successive interchanges of individual arguments. More precisely, the hexagon

\[
\begin{array}{ccc}
a \square (b \square c) & \xrightarrow{\alpha} & (a \square b) \square c & \xrightarrow{\gamma} & c \square (a \square b) \\
& {}_{1 \square \gamma} \searrow & & \nearrow_{\alpha} \\
& & a \square (c \square b) & \xrightarrow{\alpha} & (a \square c) \square b
\end{array}
\]

expresses $\gamma_{a \square b, c}$ in terms of $\gamma_{a,c}$, $\gamma_{b,c}$, and instances of $\alpha$, while the second hexagon does the same for $\gamma_{a, b \square c}$. Therefore, any canonical map built from $\alpha$ and $\gamma$ can be factored into a sequence of elementary steps, each of which is an interchange of two adjacent arguments.

From the monoidal coherence theorem (Corollary of Theorem VII.2.1) for associativity alone, we already know that any two sequences of applications of $\alpha$ to get from a functor $(v\sigma)_M$ to a functor $(w\tau)_M$ will be equal. Hence, we may assume without loss of generality that the tensor product $\square$ is strictly associative and that the associativity isomorphism $\alpha$ is the identity.

Under the strict associativity assumption, the two hexagonal diagrams (7) reduce to two triangular identities:

\[
\gamma_{a \square b, c} = (\gamma_{a,c} \square 1_b) \circ (1_a \square \gamma_{b,c}),
\]
\[
\gamma_{a, b \square c} = (1_b \square \gamma_{a,c}) \circ (\gamma_{a,b} \square 1_c).
\]

These identities show that we need only consider successive steps which interchange two adjacent letters $a, b$.

Now, the symmetric group $S_n$ on $n$ letters is generated by the transpositions $\tau_i = (i, i+1)$ of successive letters, for $i = 1, \dots, n-1$. Any closed path consisting of such transpositions corresponds to a word in these generators that evaluates to the identity in $S_n$, i.e., a relation among the generators. It is a standard fact that all relations in $S_n$ are products of conjugates of the following defining relations:

\[
\tau_i^2 = 1, \qquad i = 1, \dots, n-1,
\]
\[
(\tau_i \tau_{i+1})^3 = 1, \qquad i = 1, \dots, n-2,
\]
\[
\tau_i \tau_j = \tau_j \tau_i, \qquad 1 \leq i < j-1 \leq n-2.
\]

Thus, to prove the coherence theorem, it suffices to verify that for each of these relations, the corresponding diagram of canonical maps is commutative.

1. **Relation $\tau_i^2 = 1$:** This corresponds to applying $\gamma_{a,b}$ followed by $\gamma_{b,a}$. The defining property of a symmetric monoidal category is precisely $\gamma_{b,a} \circ \gamma_{a,b} = 1_{a \square b}$. Hence this diagram commutes.

2. **Relation $\tau_i \tau_j = \tau_j \tau_i$ for $|i-j| \neq 1$:** When the two transpositions involve disjoint pairs of letters (e.g., swapping $a$ with $b$ and $c$ with $d$), the naturality of $\gamma$ with respect to the identity maps on the non-interchanged arguments ensures that the two operations commute. Formally, both sides compute the same natural transformation because $\gamma$ is natural in each argument separately.

3. **Relation $(\tau_i \tau_{i+1})^3 = 1$:** This is the braid relation involving three successive letters $a, b, c$. Under the strict associativity assumption, the triangular identities derived from the hexagons show that the composite $\gamma_{a,c \square b} \circ \gamma_{b, a \square c} \circ \gamma_{a \square b, c}$ (corresponding to $\tau_1 \tau_2 \tau_1$) equals $\gamma_{b \square c, a} \circ \gamma_{c, a \square b} \circ \gamma_{a \square c, b}$ (corresponding to $\tau_2 \tau_1 \tau_2$). This equality follows from the coherence of the hexagon axioms with the naturality of $\gamma$.

Since all three types of defining relations commute, any diagram built from instances of $\alpha$ and $\gamma$ must commute. Therefore, there exists exactly one canonical natural isomorphism $\operatorname{can}_M(v\sigma, w\tau)$ for each pair of permuted words, satisfying the required functoriality and product conditions.

The construction also extends to incorporate the unit maps $\lambda$ and $\rho$: the assumption that $\gamma$ is compatible with unit removal (via the coherence conditions with $\lambda$ and $\rho$) ensures that any unit $e$ can be removed before or after an application of $\gamma$ with the same result, exactly as in the original monoidal coherence theorem.
