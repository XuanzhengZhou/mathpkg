Proposition 1 provides the hom-set reformulation of universal arrows: a universal arrow $\langle r, u \colon c \to Sr \rangle$ is equivalent to a natural isomorphism $D(r, -) \cong C(c, S-)$. In one direction, the universal property gives the bijection; in the other, naturality ensures that the image of $1_r$ under the isomorphism yields the universal arrow $u$. This proposition bridges the definition of universal arrows to the theory of representable functors, as it shows $C(c, S-)$ is representable precisely when a universal arrow from $c$ to $S$ exists.
