The Freyd Adjoint Functor Theorem is the classic existence theorem for left adjoints. Given a continuous functor $G : A \rightarrow X$ from a small-complete category, it has a left adjoint precisely when for each $x \in X$ there is a small "weakly universal" family of arrows from $x$ into $G$. The proof reduces to the existence of an initial object in the comma category $(x \downarrow G)$, which is small-complete when $G$ preserves limits. This theorem explains why free algebraic structures exist: the forgetful functor creates limits and the solution set condition holds by cardinality bounds on generated subobjects.
