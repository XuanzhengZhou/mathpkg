The duality principle for categories is the fundamental metatheorem stating that if a statement is provable from the axioms of category theory, then its dual statement (obtained by reversing all arrows) is also provable. This is because each axiom of a category is self-dual: its dual is also an axiom. Consequently, replacing every statement in a proof by its dual yields a valid proof of the dual conclusion. The principle extends to settings with functors between multiple categories, since the functor axioms are also self-dual. In practice, this means that every categorical theorem comes with a ``free'' dual theorem, requiring no separate proof.
