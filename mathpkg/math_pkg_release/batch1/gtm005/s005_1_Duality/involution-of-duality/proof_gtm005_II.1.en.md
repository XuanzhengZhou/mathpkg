The dual statement $\Sigma^{*}$ is formed by reversing every arrow in $\Sigma$: interchanging domain and codomain in each atomic statement and reversing the order of composition. Applying this operation twice means reversing every arrow twice, which restores each arrow to its original direction. Formally, each atomic formula ``$a = \operatorname{dom} f$'' becomes ``$a = \operatorname{cod} f$'' after one dualization, and returns to ``$a = \operatorname{dom} f$'' after the second. Similarly, $h = g \circ f$ becomes $h = f \circ g$ after one dualization, and returns to $h = g \circ f$ after the second. Since the dualization operation acts independently on each atomic subformula and the propositional connectives and quantifiers are unchanged, applying dualization twice to the entire formula $\Sigma$ yields exactly $\Sigma$. Hence $\Sigma^{**} = \Sigma$.
