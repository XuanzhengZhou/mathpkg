The duality principle extends naturally to statements involving functors between categories. In the elementary theory of a functor $T: C \to B$, the atomic statements include those for both categories $C$ and $B$, as well as equations giving the object and arrow values of $T$. The dual of a statement is formed by simultaneously reversing arrows in all categories involved. Crucially, the functor axioms $T(g \circ f) = T(g) \circ T(f)$ and $T(1_a) = 1_{Ta}$ are self-dual, meaning the set of axioms remains closed under dualization. Consequently, any theorem about functors yields a dual theorem for free, just as in the single-category case.
