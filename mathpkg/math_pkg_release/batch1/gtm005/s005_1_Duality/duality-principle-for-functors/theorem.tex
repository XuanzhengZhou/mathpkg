The duality principle also applies to statements involving several categories and functors between them. For the elementary theory of one functor $T: C \to B$ between two categories $C$ and $B$, the dual of a statement is formed by simultaneously dualizing the atomic parts referring to $C$ and to $B$ (i.e., reversing arrows in $C$ and in $B$). Since the statement that $T$ is a functor is self-dual---the conditions $T(g \circ_C f) = T(g) \circ_B T(f)$ and $T(1_a) = 1_{Ta}$ remain valid after arrow reversal---the duality principle holds: if a statement $\Sigma$ about categories and functors is a consequence of the axioms, so is its dual $\Sigma^{*}$.
