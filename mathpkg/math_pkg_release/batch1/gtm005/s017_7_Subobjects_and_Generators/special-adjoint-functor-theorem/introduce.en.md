The Special Adjoint Functor Theorem (SAFT) provides sufficient conditions for a continuous functor $G: A \to X$ to possess a left adjoint. The hypotheses on the domain $A$ are: small-completeness, small hom-sets, the existence of a small cogenerating set, and being well-powered (every set of subobjects has a pullback, hence an intersection). The codomain $X$ need only have small hom-sets. The proof reduces the problem to showing that each comma category $(x \downarrow G)$ has an initial object, which follows from the previous theorem (initial object from a cogenerating set). Classical applications include the existence of the Stone–Čech compactification and Watts' representability theorem.
