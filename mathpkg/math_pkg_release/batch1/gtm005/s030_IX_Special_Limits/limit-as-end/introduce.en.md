Proposition 3 establishes the fundamental relationship between limits and ends: every ordinary limit can be expressed as an end over a dummy variable. Given a functor $T : C \to X$, one makes a bifunctor $S : C^{\mathrm{op}} \times C \to X$ that ignores its contravariant argument. Then a cone for $T$ (i.e., a natural transformation from a constant) is exactly a dinatural wedge for $S$, and universality is preserved. This result unifies the notation and calculus of limits and ends, allowing the powerful machinery of ends to be applied to ordinary limits.
