Proposition 1 demonstrates that ends are not fundamentally new limits but rather ordinary limits over a cleverly constructed indexing category -- the subdivision category $C^{\S}$. The subdivision category packages the dinaturality hexagons into a standard diagram: for each arrow $f : b \to c$, it contains a span $S(b,b) \to S(b,c) \leftarrow S(c,c)$, and a cone over this diagram is precisely a dinatural wedge. This construction shows that every complete category also has all ends, and provides a concrete method for computing ends when limits are understood.
