Proposition 2 shows that a universal element of a set-valued functor $K$ yields a representation of $K$. Specifically, a universal arrow $u: * \to Kr$ from a one-point set to $K$ (which is precisely a universal element $u(*) \in Kr$) induces a natural isomorphism $D(r, -) \cong K$ by sending $f': r \to d$ to $(Kf')(u(*))$. This formalizes the observation that the three notions --- universal arrow, universal element, and representable functor --- are essentially equivalent: each can be expressed in terms of the others.
