A universal arrow from an object $c$ to a functor $S: D \to C$ is a pair $\langle r, u \rangle$ where $r \in D$ and $u: c \to Sr$ such that every arrow $f: c \to Sd$ factors uniquely through $u$ as $f = Sf' \circ u$. This generalizes the construction of free algebraic structures: the free category on a graph, the free group on a set, the tensor algebra of a vector space, the completion of a metric space, and many others are all examples of universal arrows. Universal arrows are unique up to isomorphism and are equivalent to initial objects in comma categories. Together with their dual (universal arrows from a functor), they form the foundation for the theory of adjoint functors, limits, and colimits.
