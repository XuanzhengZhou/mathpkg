The canonical maps are constructed by induction on the structure of words. By the monoidal coherence theorem (Chapter VII), any two ways of reassociating a word via $\alpha$ yield the same composite isomorphism; hence we may assume the tensor product $\square$ is strictly associative and $\alpha$ is the identity. In this strict setting, the two hexagon axioms (7) reduce to two triangle identities (7a) which describe how to interchange a single argument with a product of arguments as a composition of interchanges of individual arguments.

Thus we need only consider successive steps that interchange two adjacent letters. The symmetric group $S_n$ is generated by the adjacent transpositions $\tau_i = (i, i+1)$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n-1$. Any closed path of such transpositions corresponds to a relation among these generators. A complete set of defining relations for $S_n$ is:
\[
\tau_i^2 = 1 \quad (i = 1, \ldots, n-1),
\]
\[
(\tau_i \tau_{i+1})^3 = 1 \quad (i = 1, \ldots, n-2),
\]
\[
\tau_i \tau_j = \tau_j \tau_i \quad (1 \leq i < j-1 \leq n-2).
\]

To prove coherence, it suffices to verify that for each of these relations, the corresponding diagram of canonical maps commutes.

\begin{enumerate}
\item The relation $\tau_i^2 = 1$ corresponds to the axiom $\gamma^2 = 1$ of a symmetric monoidal category (equation (8)).
\item The relation $(\tau_i \tau_{i+1})^3 = 1$ corresponds to the Yang-Baxter equation, which follows from the naturality of $\gamma$ and the hexagon axioms.
\item The relation $\tau_i \tau_j = \tau_j \tau_i$ for $|i-j| > 1$ follows from the naturality of $\gamma$, since the two transpositions act on disjoint pairs of indices.
\end{enumerate}

Since every relation in $S_n$ is a consequence of these defining relations, and each corresponds to a commutative diagram in $M$, it follows that any two paths from $(v\sigma)_M$ to $(w\tau)_M$ built from $\alpha$ and $\gamma$ are equal. Hence there is a unique canonical map between any two permuted word functors of the same length, satisfying the stated properties.
