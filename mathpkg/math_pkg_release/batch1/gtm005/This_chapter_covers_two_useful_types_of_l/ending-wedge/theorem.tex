The universal wedge $\omega: e \mathbin{\dot{\rightarrow}} S$ defining an end is called the **ending wedge** or **universal wedge**. Its components $\omega_c: e \rightarrow S(c, c)$ satisfy the dinaturality condition: for each $f: c \rightarrow c'$ in $C$, the square

$$\begin{array}{ccc}
e & \xrightarrow{\omega_c} & S(c, c) \\
\omega_{c'} \downarrow & & \downarrow S(1, f) \\
S(c', c') & \xrightarrow{S(f, 1)} & S(c, c')
\end{array}$$

commutes. The universal property of $\omega$ states that for any wedge $\tau: x \mathbin{\dot{\rightarrow}} S$, there is a unique arrow $h: x \rightarrow e$ such that $\omega_c \circ h = \tau_c$ for all $c \in C$.

If $\langle e, \omega \rangle$ and $\langle e', \omega' \rangle$ are two ends for $S$, there is a unique isomorphism $u: e \rightarrow e'$ with $\omega' \circ u = \omega$.
