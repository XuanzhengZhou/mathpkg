The end of a bifunctor $S: C^{\mathrm{op}} \times C \to X$ is a special and especially useful type of limit, written with integral notation $\int_c S(c,c)$. It is a universal wedge: an object $e$ equipped with arrows to each $S(c,c)$ satisfying a dinaturality condition. The variable $c$ is "bound" by the integral sign, so the result is independent of the choice of letter — analogous to a definite integral in calculus. Natural transformations between two functors provide a key example: $\int_c \hom_X(Uc, Vc) \cong \operatorname{Nat}(U, V)$.
